Title: Those mysterious colorful curbside markings
Post by: lmaniace on June 13, 2015, 02:50:46 PM
JHBG’s street-tree volunteers have worked on 180 trees on 37th Avenue since April, covering almost every block from 69th Street to Junction Boulevard. Today we focused on 80th to 84th Streets

The work entails loosening rock-hard soil to permit water to penetrate to roots, sometimes adding compost and then covering with wood-chip mulch, which keeps moisture in and weeds out.

And for those who did not make it out to the event, here’s the answer to those mysterious green, purple and orange scribblings along many curbs in Jackson Heights... They are markings for potential sites for BIOSWALES (little bits of of nature in NYC that absorb rainwater and dramatically reduce pollution in NYC’s rivers, bays and other bodies of water.

Check out this story on the city’s bioswale plans: http://gothamist.com/2014/11/10/bioswales.php

Title: Re: Those mysterious colorful curbside markings
Post by: lmaniace on June 14, 2015, 06:44:05 PM
Merchants along 37th Avenue can certainly help. Please water the existing trees near your shops.
Trees need about 17 gallons of water each week. Preferably delivered very slowly by hose or bucket so it doesn’t run off, twice a week. This is especially important for young trees, those planted in the thrtee-four years. These tree don’t have well established root systems to absorb enough water.

It’s often helpful to break up the soil so it will absorb the water faster, otherwise the water can runoff without reaching the roots.
Water that runs off doesn’t count toward the 17 gallons

Residents can do the same thing.

If you want to do it formally you can adopt a tree with NYC Parks. This does not carry any legal responsibility.

Title: Re: Bioswales in JH
Post by: lmaniace on August 11, 2015, 08:57:43 PM
The bioswales are probably going to be installed next year. They’ve been had those white machines that look like Zambonis all over JH the last few months drilling into the ground to determine how well the soil absorbs water. If it absorbs water well and the site is free of underground  infrastructure they most likely will install the bioswale. For those who don’t know, bioswales are a super-powered tree pit designed to take a lot of the rain water that otherwise goes down sewers often overloading sewage treatment plants resulting in untreated sewage going into nearby waterways. City is required by court order to do this because it doesnt’ comply with federal clean water rules. City opted for green infrastructure because it’s less expensive than sewage treatment plants which are very hard to find acceptable locations for. They are very cool and as Itsit and Sarah noted coming to JH in pretty good numbers.
