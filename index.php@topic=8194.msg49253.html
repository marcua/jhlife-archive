In lieu of green space, I would LOVE a whole bunch of public tennis courts here.

Hi, AmazingJason. Although I'm a lover of tennis myself, I think that a bunch of tennis courts are not what we need here. First, because I think that we could get more tennis courts by getting the city to open more school fields in the area to the public on weekends, for example. Also, because tennis courts are large spaces that can only be used by a maximum of four people at a time. Considering that we have very little space, considering how much cement we already have in this neighborhood, and considering the population density here, tennis courts would not be enough "use bang" for our "space buck."

I think it would be a better use to have more green space and kiosks to help pay for maintenance (more than one kiosk, to give park goers options and to give the kiosks motivation to keep up good services because of competition -- monopoly is not a good thing). I am in favor of at least some grass. But there are other options to grass, like threes, sitting areas and shrubs, that can make a nice area to take a walk and to relax.

Great discussion here.  Here's my two cents.   

The part of the park that seems to me like an overlooked opportunity is the small area inside the 77th Street entrance, between the lower basketball court and the handball courts.   This is a nice, shady spot, but has no particular use.   It suffers from poor drainage (which could easily be fixed) and is used to pile garbage.    With some social seating (picnic benches or game tables), this could become an attractive quiet refuge in the park.

As for the athletic field, it is often very crowded, and if we convert it to green space, I think it is important to develop a strategy to ensure we do not have a net loss of active recreation space in the neighborhood.  My kids (aged 6 and 10) often go to Travers wanting to use a basketball court or handball court, and more often than not have to give up after literally waiting for hours for a chance to use these facilities.   Putting the Garden School field to better use will help, but it's half the size of the Travers athletic play area.  Mayor Bloomberg's school playgrounds program, which has opened up PS69 and IS145 for recreational use, has been a big help, but still more is needed.   The Louis Armstrong school's playground, which has a bunch of basketball courts, seems like a great potential opportunity.

On a slightly different note, I've seen photos of what Travers Park looked like when Len and JHBG/FOTP were first getting started.   The play equipment was ugly and dangerous, and the park was a dump.   It's really quite remarkable what they've been able to achieve, and I have tremendous admiration and gratitude for the Herculean work that they have done over the years.
