I usually disregard mass e-mails that are circulated on the net, but the above e-mail does pose the interesting question of how safe are we on Facebook?  Keep in mind that many people have public pages.  If you're not a Facebook user, this means that anyone on the internet has access to  profile pages that may contain photos and personal information about its members.  The e-mail tells a brief story of a Mexican Facebook user that was the victim of a kidnapping.  Apparently, he frequently posted updates on family vacations, home photos, etc., allowing his kidnappers full access to private information that made the crime easier to commit.   The e-mail also implies that Facebook sells your private information to anyone/organization willing to pay the right price.  An example that's given involves employers that review the profile pages of it's job applicants in order to weed out those the company may deem unsuitable for a position.

The New York Times article seems to focus more on the intellectual property question of who really owns the information we post on our Facebook profiles.  The question here arises as the result of a recent change in the Terms of Service for Facebook users.  The popular consumer blog The Consumerist addressed the issue recently.

Facebook's New Terms Of Service: "We Can Do Anything We Want With Your Content. Forever."
