Regardless of any terms or buzzwords, America has seen a lot of small breweries come up, and a move away from the watered down swill that is often considered American beer.

What craft beer means to people who care about beer is basically any locally made beer (that is, not imported; made in the U.S.) that is not made in one of the mega factories (Bud/Inbev or SAB/Miller). It also tends to connote a certain style of beer -- hoppy ales -- but of course there are (a few) decent American craft lagers and other styles.

And, let's be real: the best American beers are "craft beers." Sure there's a lot of crap out there, but most of the really good stuff calls itself craft beer (and so we call it that too). It's marketing, sure. But sometimes marketing terms -- for instance: "organic" or "Bordeaux" -- are helpful to the consumer.

Also, I have to say: Goose Island, which is now owned by Budweiser and is thus sort of a fake craft beer, is still pretty good. I have mixed feelings about Bud, but its wonderful that you can now buy a decent IPA on an airplane. (Budweiser is bad at a lot of things, but distribution is not one of them.)
