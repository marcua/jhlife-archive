Prevention is obviously not a high priority (judging by the fact that Detective Johnson, the head of the crime prevention unit at our precinct, has yet to return a request for meeting placed a year ago), replaced instead by the assurance that there will be no punishment for criminals, because this is a high-crime area. Hi,

I'm going to ramble on a little.  If you want to read fine, if not don't.  But I need to share.

I am really concerned about these posts, especially when I hear about the "nonchalance" of the police officers posted by carrefour_ny and "good thing the police are busy checking seatbelts every day on bway... They are really cleaning up the streets!" posted by jeffsayes.  This last post is sarcastic in nature, but I hear you.

I'm sorry about everyone that has had a car break-in and that has endured something unpleasant while dealing with a police officer.  You can always take down the name of the office and contact the supervisor on duty, lieutenant and chief commander in writing.  Everyone that works in the Department has a file.

I have always been under the impression that police officers are out there putting their lives at risk, to protect us from "break-ins, muggings, rapes and murders".

Is it possible that a car break-in is considered low priority?  Is it possible that checking seatbelts is not a low priority?  In addition, to stopping those that are using a cell phone while driving.  I'm not an officer, but isn't there anything positive that we can say on this site that seems more appreciative of these men and women that are out there doing the best they can under much duress.

This is my vision of the day in the life of an officer:
20 calls during a tour and perhaps responding to 10 of them that are domestic violence calls, 5 that are stabbings and shootings after the bars have closed and 5 that are break-ins whether cars or residential or otherwise.  Don't forget the crank calls, the noise complaints, the heart attacks, etc.  Reminder:  A police car must escort an ambulance when the patients life is on the line.  That must be another call.

I put myself in the shoes of an officer and believe that must be quite a shift.  And to endure all that stress to put food on the table for the family.  Not an easy task, but that is what they are committed to doing.  And believe it or not many of our officers want to serve the community and make a difference.  Try doing that job 5 times a week, including overtime if you have an arrest to make, which could put you into overtime of 8 additional hours or more.  Thus returning to work without resting.  Not an easy task.

And perhaps you may be the first to arrive to a crime scene, and find a dead body of a little girl, who has been killed by her mother.  Not an easy task.  Or walk into a bodega while answering a call and get shot at instantly.  I don't know many humans that can take on that task, day in and day out, but some make our community proud when they risk their lives every night to protect and serve.  

And so as the work of a police officer may not be appreciated by some and perhaps the police officer was nonchalant and perhaps they are not cleaning up the streets the way they should or could, etc., can we show some appreciation, even if its just a little from time to time.

I always have to remind myself that we have 30,000 officers in New York and I believe our population is 800,000.

Not an easy task.  And, as it relates to Detective Johnson, our building placed a call and she responded immediately.  A meeting for all the shareholders was scheduled.  Please note that 15-20 shareholders were in attendance out of 268 residents.

The economic climate is tense and getting worse.  We may not become the next Bed-Stuy, but let us not forget Park and Fifth lots of crimes going on there too.

Best to all,

"Since when do individual cops decide policy and priorities?"

It is my understanding that their superiors decide policy and as well decide priorities.  And that radio that officers carry continuously guides them through places you never knew existed.  And guess who is on the other line?  Their superiors in many instances.  And they decide at any given time what is priority.  Don't put all the blame on the cops.  They have others that make the calls to them and decide for them, but unfortunately one never knows who is in charge at any given time at the precinct as there are many shifts and much movement.

I'm not defending any officer who has been condescending, rude or simply not helpful in any given case, but
those superiors will let them know that there is a "shot fire" call and to drop everything and move on to that job.  And sometimes it is an "officer needs assistance" call while perhaps another fellow officer's life is on the line.  

These jobs are dangerous, require immediate attention, and are truly gruesome.  And once you experience those jobs, perhaps back to back at any given shift, going to a break-in of a car may, at that moment in time, not be as important as it is to the person who has just experienced the break-in of his car.

Let us not forget for one moment that officers do serve, in many instances, as social workers as they are confronted with people arguing while sober or drunk, men beating on their wives as the wives decide not to press charges while informed to do so, and the worst is when someone has died and the officer has to arrive and comfort the nearest friends or relatives.

Cops are human beings, they are not robots.  Is there the need for consistent sensitivity training?  Yes.  Is there the need for consistent group counseling/open discussion during roll call daily?  Great idea.  Is there the need for required physical training.  Absolutely.  Is there the need for continuous job evaluations?  Indeed.  And is there enough support and money in NYC for that?  Or a brain storming group to make this happen.  I don't think so.

And so what is the solution.  I don't know, but I have a thought.

Perhaps what would work is to have a lower level/Tier of policing in our community which deals specifically with less challenging jobs and is committed to those calls that are less demanding but require personal attention.

As it stands, an officer must go to a crime scene, then a fire, then a missing person, then a double parked car with a drunk, then a break-in, then a noise complaint, then back to the break-in and perhaps back to the noise complaint, and the last job may be a domestic violence call. 

So here is my list of Tier 1 and Tier 2.

Tier 1
Murder
Rape
Child Missing/Adult Missing
Shot Fire
Officer in need of assistance
Fire
Domestic Violence
Accident with injuries
Robbery
etc,


Tier 2
Car Alarms
Car break-in
Noise Complaint
Apartment Breakin
Accident w/No injuries
etc,


To be continued . . .

I am the daughter of a police captain and have many other cops in my family.  My boyfriend is a detective (not in NYC).  So I have the utmost respect for the police.  It takes a lot for me to say that I believe our local precinct is not exactly a shining beacon of police work. 

The cops that handle missing persons/murder etc cases are not the same ones that handle break-ins.  The first responder might be the same, but thats all (in response to Marlene's Tier System).  The ones that handle break-ins, muggings, etc are the lowest on the totem pole and have to work their way up - I'm sure this is not news to anyone.  So if you are a lazy cop, you are going to be stuck answering these kind of calls because you are never going to get promoted.  The good ones don't last very long before they get promoted or wise up and leave NY for greener pastures/more money. So if you are a lazy cop, not getting promoted, bitter about it, sick of answering minor calls, then it doesn't surprise me that nothing is done when one calls about a minor crime.

It's simple.  If your car is broken into, the cop knows that chances are slim that the perp will be found and doesn't want to bother with the paperwork.  I completely agree with Jeff - checking seatbelts means tickets, which means $$.  Petit crime means annoying paperwork and follow-through.  Our precinct doesn't seem to care about the small stuff that affects our quality of life - noise pollution, trash, honking, small break-ins.  I'm not sure if it's because of a bad captain or if years of NYPD paying awful salaries and hiring less than desirable candidates has taken it's toll in our neighborhood. 

Why would anyone that would make a good cop accept a job in NYC when Nassau, Suffolk, Westchester all pay a lot more money?  Suffolk County PD is the highest paying police department in the entire country.  I know I'd make the long commute or move farther out for a starting salary that is $40,000 MORE than in NYC.  Not 40K total, 40K higher.  That's a big difference.
