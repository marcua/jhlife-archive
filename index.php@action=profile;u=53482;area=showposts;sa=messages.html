As for who pays for lawyers and engineers - any tenants happen to be lawyers or engineers? You're right EricaS, they don't come cheap. Maybe a call to Danny Dromm for some guidance would be in order - are there lawyers or engineers who offer this kind of work pro-bono to tenants associations?

How about any posters on here who lived through this (or are living through this) - any advice?

Whatever the case, I agree - I'm sure the sponsor/owner won't be paying -- it would be down to the TA to pay or get the services pro-bono.

I have a number of questions too - I'll post them here since there have been a ton of conversions in JH up and down 35th Avenue in the last 20 years (and more) maybe some posters who have first hand knowledge of the process can give us here some insight! 

1. Why convert to a co-op vs. a condo? Are there any benefits or downsides to the sponsor and/or tenants going with one over the other? I know the overwhelming majority of buildings in JH went co-op, but some on conversion did go condo (The Delano, The Colton). More recently, The Carlton on 85th went co-op while Stratford Hall on 35th and 80th went condo.  If I recall correctly, normally co-ops have a cheaper buy-in but higher monthlies, while the opposite is true in a condo. But that's about all I know - anyone have any insight?

2. How many seats does the sponsor retain on the Board and how do they lose them as more units are sold? I ask this as I have a friend who lived in a converted co-op a few blocks up on 35th where the sponsor held the majority of the seats for quite some time, meaning they could (and did) block a number of things the board/owners wanted to do with the co-op, determined which contractors got the jobs, which management company they employed, etc., and it was only recently (within the last 5 years) the owners made the majority on that board. Something to think about.

3. Can current tenants get out of their leases and get the insider prices if they want to purchase one of the vacant apartments on conversion? Maybe the tenant wants a better view, a quieter apartment, a bigger apartment, etc.. How about later on as tenants opt to move and more sponsor units go up for sale?

4. House Rules - if the current owners have been letting certain rules slide and not enforcing them (i.e.; cats, carpeting, etc.) since the co-op is technically a new owner, could non-compliance with these house rules be used as a reason not to renew leases?

5. Engineers Report - I was a bit unnerved by how often he used the term "fair" (windows, roof) and "acceptable" (elevators, window sills, skylights) in the report versus "good" when describing their condition. I also thought they were pretty vague on any pointing needs. These are all big ticket items and since the building is sold "As Is" it would fall to the co-op owners to pay for replacements/repairs. From my friends in the neighborhood, I know assessments to cover new windows or elevators can be $300 or so a month, on top on maintenance and mortgage payments. My last apartment I was subletting from a non-occuping owner - his maintenance was about $720 a month, my rent gave him double that - which covered his mortgage and gave him a few bucks too. The building had an assessment for pointing/roof repairs for a year and for that year, he made no money off my rent - I actually think he may have been slightly in the red. Imagine? Also, when co-ops need an influx of cash in cases like this, this is when they look to get rid of tenants in favor of buyers.

6. The Vacant Apartments - as they are owned by the sponsor, will they be counted towards the 15% needed to convert? If I counted correctly, there are 24 vacant apartments in WP so something to consider. That makes it so much easier to reach the 15%, right?

As for the basement access after 8PM, I figured it was a safety issue, which I totally understand and concur with. However, I would hope some compromise could be had - perhaps locking the outside doors at 8PM but allowing the elevators to go down to the basement until the doormen go off duty to give tenants access to laundry. Just my two cents on this issue.

I'm hoping some other JH posters who have experience with this whole conversion process will post -- or maybe we need a new thread asking for their help and insight?

Neighborhood Chat / Re: Washington Plaza going coop?
« on: April 23, 2014, 12:33:16 PM »
I'm with you Lilybell - got the prospectus last night and spent a good part of the evening going through it. It is a non-eviction conversion, thankfully. From what I've been able to find out, according to the NYS Attorney General's office, under a non-eviction conversion plan an outside purchaser or the sponsor of an occupied apartment may not evict you. You may continue in occupancy as a rent-stabilized or rent-controlled tenant, paying rent to the outside purchaser or the sponsor, who must provide all the services required under applicable laws. I do believe all the apts in Washington Plaza are either RS or RC. However, the tenant of course has to abide by all the terms of the lease -- even those that you may have been told are "unenforced". For example, when I moved in the super did tell me that while required to have X% of the apt carpeted, my neighbor downstairs was "good" so I shouldn't have any problems if I keep the hardwood. He was right - I kept the hardwood and haven't had any problems. Technically, this could be used as an excuse to not renew the lease, should the sponsor want to. So, if you want to stay as a renter, go through your lease and make sure you understand every term and that you as the tenant are abiding by every single one - don't give them any reason to not renew. Also, document everything - receipts for carpeting, air conditioner brackets, etc. as well as pictures of any damages from leaks, water, etc., broken appliances and the like and maintain a log of when you notified management of the damage and/or broken appliance. Make sure you return all those annoying forms the management has to send out about window guards, asbestos, etc. on time. We're supposed to be having new water supply pipes installed where they will need access to our apts in the coming month or two, you can be sure I will make sure there is carpet down by then and that the place is in pristine condition - again, the name of the game is to not give anyone any reason to not renew.

Having said all this, I was looking at the prices and I actually didn't think they were that bad. To be honest, I thought Washington Plaza was a co-op before I moved in because unlike too many rental buildings in the neighborhood, this place is extremely well kept inside and out. My "dream apt" to buy would have been in the Chateau, or the Elm Court, or the Hawthorne Court - but honestly, having friends in all three buildings, they are not all they're cracked up to be. They have fireplaces - which I love - but those big interior gardens? You can't go on the grass, lay out, let kids play, etc.. Some of the buildings in the Chateau and the Courts lock the front door to the lobby at 8 or 9PM (the apt bells are in the little vestibule) -- so anyone visiting or delivering to you after has to call you and you have to go down to let them in - hope this has changed, as it makes no sense. As all those buildings are self managed per address (i.e.; each entrance is its own co-op, it is not one big co-op) the boards are picky both in who they let in, what they require and in the house rules too. Also, some apt layouts are kind of boxy whereas others are nice and airy. And, with only 12 or so apts in the co-op, not only can assessments be steep, but everyone knows your business. So other than the interior gardens you really can't use and the fireplaces, what is the difference to here? Historic District? I believe there are plans in the works to expand it (someone on JHBG may know more?) which would encompass WP.

So, and this is just on one night's thoughts, if the plan gets accepted, I would definitely think about buying here in WP.

I have lived in Manhattan and in JH and I can tell you, this is the largest 1 bedroom apt I have ever had. The closet space alone would have be convinced to buy! LOL Aside from the large size of the apts here, the staff are fantastic, as I said, the building and gardens are so well kept and I've never (knock wood) had any problems with neighbors or the management company. Rare combinations in JH or anywhere in the city for that matter. While I don't think the proposed pricing and monthlies are out of this world, I do think there has to be some wiggle room too. From what I understand from others buying into conversions or converted co-ops, mortgage lenders have issues when it is not a majority owner-occupied co-op. I would guess the sponsor would know this (as would any realtors on here?) and I would think would be willing to work with you. I'm not saying they'll drop tens of thousands off the price, but they should be able to work with you I would think. For me, I am wondering if a tenant can buy another apt in the building that is empty under this offering? For example, I'm a one bedroom, I'd like a 2 bedroom. I saw in the prospectus there are a number of 2 bedrooms vacant under the plan -- could I buy one at the insider price? If so, what does that do to my lease? Would the sponsor work with me and let me out of my lease? Just wondering as ideally, if this goes through and if they would work with me, that is what I would think to do.

Anyway, it is still early days - I'd say we have a good 6 or so months before we hear anything about this. I see you said 80% of tenants need to agree to buy for this plan to go through, Lilybell - where did you see that? I read 15% of tenants need to agree to buy for the sponsor to enact the plan. Where did you hear about the tenant meeting - or did I miss that in the prospectus?

In any event, fingers crossed each of us get what we want - whether its staying as a renter or going in as a buyer :-)
