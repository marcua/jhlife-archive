Gentrification in JH?
« on: March 08, 2008, 11:40:10 AM »
With great trepidation, I am starting a new thread on gentrification, a topic that too often descends into anger and ideological finger-pointing.  Please, let's try to avoid that here.  But instead of small brushfires of gentrification debates cropping up in various threads around the board, it seems to make sense to have a central place to hash it out, so here it is.

Here is this urban planner's take on gentrification.

We live in a dynamic city, and our neighborhoods are prone to change.  All neighborhoods will, over time, see ethnic and socioeconomic shifts.  If a neighborhood moves up on the income scale, some people call that process gentrification.  But longtime neighborhood residents often resent whatever changes occur because it means that the image or character of the community they love is giving way to something else. 

But there's another face to gentrification that is less benign.  Sometimes, the pace of change can be very fast and disruptive.  Often, this is engineered by unscrupulous developers bent on speculative developments, with no real ties to the community in question.   Rents can rise dramatically, forcing many families from their homes and tearing the fabric of social ties that evolved over many years.  Or, in the case of the old practice of blockbusting, real estate interests have profited by driving property values down and sowing fear among the middle class.  Either way, this disruptive change can be very harmful to the community and the city as a whole.

Relative to many neighborhoods in Manhattan and Brooklyn, I think the pace of change in Jackson Heights has been very gradual, probably due to the high rate of home ownership.  Right now, we're on a gradual upswing, as is the city as a whole.  This creates economic opportunity for everyone, but we should recognize that it will bring economic pain to some families in the community.  We've always been a community the celebrates our diversity -- and in my mind, economic diversity is an important part of this.  So even though I like all our new amenities, I hope we are able to continue to remain affordable to the immigrant families that lend our community its wonderful character and flavor.

Anyway, that my two cents on gentrification in JH.  The soapbox is available for anybody else who wants to climb aboard.
