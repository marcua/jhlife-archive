"This neighborhood has changed" has become a clichéd "thing New Yorkers say" in 2016. The phrase echoes from Bushwick's graffiti-covered walls to the gallery-lined streets of Chinatown. But when we check out exhibitions and street art in these "up-and-coming" neighborhoods, we often fail to recognize an important part of the problem — "art washing." Coined by The Atlantic in 2014, the term refers to the superficial cultural narrative that new art spaces and non-native artists bring to gentrifying communities.

Ya gotta see it in context. The anxiety over 'gentrification' is not just about people feeling like they're being pushed out of their neighborhoods (and having to disrupt social connections, school assignments, routines, etc): it's about the people who are pushed out feeling like they have fewer and fewer options of places to move, like everywhere in NYC is becoming unaffordable. NYC and other cities are seeing a proliferation of low-wage service jobs, but a lack of new housing which people who have those jobs can afford. Meanwhile, NYC's status as a locus of global capital means that the elite will continue to buy apartments, while the people who serve them have nowhere to go (or just have to spend so much of their income on rent that they're barely surviving.) That is, if those elite property-owners even reside in their apartments; many luxury apartments in Manhattan and Flushing are kept empty, as they're basically safety-deposit boxes for foreign money, and therefore they suck the life out of neighborhoods.
