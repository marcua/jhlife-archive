Daily News
Park deal in Jackson Heights teetering as city won't offer Garden School any money up front

Read more: http://www.nydailynews.com/ny_local/queens/2011/02/11/2011-02-11_park_deal_in_jackson_heights_teetering_as_city_wont_offer_garden_school_any_mone.html#ixzz1Dh0zyF00

http://www.nydailynews.com/ny_local/queens/2011/02/11/2011-02-11_park_deal_in_jackson_heights_teetering_as_city_wont_offer_garden_school_any_mone.html

The city has upped its bid to purchase a rare chunk of potential park land from a Jackson Heights private school, the Daily News learned Thursday.

The city recently offered $6.8 million to the Garden School for its 29,000-square-foot yard - $2 million more than a bid last month, sources said.

But because the city isn't offering the cash-strapped nursery-through-12th grade school any money up front, school officials are still considering a deal with a developer to build residential housing, a school official said.

That's in spite of the fact locals have pledged nearly $250,000 to tide the 87-year-old institution over if it agrees to sell its yard to the city. A private foundation also is looking into possibly loaning the school up to $500,000, a source said.

"I remain hopeful that the deal will happen," said City Councilman Daniel Dromm (D-Jackson Heights), who has spearheaded the push to acquire the property.

"The city has given its word that this is extremely high-priority, and they have no intention of backing out of the deal."

But even the community's generosity may not be enough to sway school officials. It is in bad financial straits after its enrollment dropped with the economy.

"If the city came with the money tomorrow, then I'm selling them the property tomorrow," said Michael Rakosi, vice president of the school's board of trustees. But "there's no money down, there's no date of closing, there's no contract."

It could take up to two years for the city's check to clear due to a lengthy acquisition process. Rakosi said the city has not offered to rent the land in the meantime.

"Saying they're going to do it is different than going to contract, putting down a deposit, licensing the property and closing," Rakosi said. "That's what all the private developers promised, and they'll do it by putting up the money immediately."

The board is in talks with a handful of developers, he said, and is close to signing a deal.

Rakosi added that the board wasn't interested in accepting a community loan as a stopgap measure because the city could pull out at any time - leaving the school on the hook for paying back the loan.

But Edwin Westley, president of the Jackson Heights Beautification Group, wasn't buying that argument.

Westley said that Rakosi challenged him and other local activists at a December meeting to raise money for a loan. Now, after the community has raised about $250,000, the school refuses to meet with them, Westley said.

Rakosi, who previously told the Daily News that he didn't ask the community to raise the money, confirmed that he did, in fact, encourage the fund-raising.

"Things have changed since then," Rakosi said. "The offer did not extend to the end of time."

Nevertheless, Jackson Heights Green Alliance President Dudley Stewart, is still soliciting pledges.

Jackson Heights has one of the least amounts of park space per capita in the city, according to New Yorkers for Parks.

Stewart would like to link Garden's yard - more than half the size of a football field - to Travers Park by turning the summer-only 78th St. play street into a permanent pedestrian plaza.

"A number of parents of current students are going to withdraw their children from the school if the school sells to a developer," Stewart said.

"If an apartment building goes up, it's a death sentence for the school."

Daily News
Loan Sows Doubt Over Garden School
by Clare Trapasso 2/15/11

A JACKSON Heights private school may decide today whether to sell its prized field to the city for parkland or to a developer.

But one school official may have a vested interest in the sale, the Daily News has learned.

The Garden School took out two high-interest loans in 2009 for a total of $1 million from a now-defunct company run by the nephew of a member of the school's board of trustees.

Though the deal is legal, the link raised eyebrows among community members who have been rallying to help the school through its financial hardship.

"The board should avoid any appearance of a conflict of interest," said City Councilman Daniel Dromm (D-Jackson Heights), who vowed to look into the matter.

Michael Rakosi, vice president of the board of trustees, told The News that fellow board members were fully aware that his nephew, Mark Rosenbloom, was the CEO of Precision Financial.

Precision's loan, with a 12.99% interest rate, was the only loan the school could secure, Rakosi said. If Garden defaults on a payment, the interest rate jumps to 24%.

"The economy went bad," Rakosi said. "It was impossible to find an institutional financier. That's why the rates are so high."

The nursery-through-12th-grade school is about $2.5 million in debt, he said. He attributed the downward financial spiral to a drop in enrollment.

Board President Arthur Gruen said he wasn't sure if the board would decide the fate of the yard tonight. The board tried to get a loan from at least six banks before the Precision deal, he said, noting that Rakosi disclosed his family connection to the board.

The transaction was legal because the board was aware of that relationship and looked for loans from other lenders, said bankruptcy lawyer Neil Colmenares.

"If this was the only loan that a lender was willing to give the school, then the board members of the school need to do what's in the best interest of the school," he said. But, he added, the interest rate seemed a bit high.

Rosenbloom refused to comment on the specifics of the loan. He confirmed that Precision went under in 2009. It was unclear what entity succeeded Precision.

The city recently offered the school $6.8 million for its 29,000-square-foot yard to expand nearby Travers Park. But the city isn't offering Garden any money up front or guarantees it will buy the property due to its lengthy acquisition process.

The city isn't out of the running, Rakosi said, adding: "I'd be happy if the city sat down and rented the field or purchased the field."
