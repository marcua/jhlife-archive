dssjh: I wasn't insulted or talking offense to your question, I just don't understand its relevance to this proposed legislation. As I'm sure you've already gathered from other threads, I'm a stickler for playing by the rules and definitely a type-A personality.

I'm suspicious of this particular legislation because of the association pushing it (The Real Estate Board of New York), and because the original poster is a "tourist" to JHL whose hypotheticals sound a little far-fetched to me. If passed, coop boards will be more likely to be intimidated into accepting applicants they are unsure of, for fear of having to defend themselves against a lawsuit if the reason stated in their rejection letter fails to hold up to scrutiny. This will make NYC realtors (already near the bottom of the barrel in my book) even lazier than they already are, and NYC law firms will salivate over the potential litigation coming their way.

Oh, and who belongs to REBNY? -- Builders, brokers, managers, banks, insurance companies, pension funds, real estate investment trusts, utilities, attorneys, architects, marketing professionals and many other individuals and institutions involved in New York realty. (I don't know about you, but none of these institutions have my confidence or trust.)

IMO, the rights of current shareholders to protect their investment overrides the rights of prospective shareholders. When you buy into a co-op, you are entering into a business partnership, the success or failure of which has the potential to seriously affect your financial well-being. My concern is that passage of the bill would lead to co-ops only being able to consider the applicant's finances in making a decision, whereas in most cases we want to consider both finances and whether or not the applicant will be a responsible shareholder.
