While some inspectors may be fishing for a bribe to overlook a violation, the violations are real.

Inspectors aren't fishing for a bribe. They seem to have been trained or have their performance measured in a way that seems to overlooks the very training that DOH provides food handlers. An adversarial system has been created. The truth is, most small business owners just can't take a day off of work to challenge unfair violations (i.e. sticking a thermometer in lettuce. What are they sticking the thermometer in? It's not a leg of chicken!) in court, and the bad inspectors know that.

It's also worth remembering that this is supposed to be about *reducing* risk, not eliminating it. You can hard boil and egg, and if it was tainted with something that left a toxin in it, you're still gonna get sick. There's no way to know that, so you're taking a risk whenever you eat. What's the option? Not eating at all?

There's nothing ``shady'' about the city's restaurant inspections.

The folks who conduct these inspections are well schooled in sanitary guidelines.

But here is the reality. When De Blasio took over as mayor it was his view that the inspectors were too aggressive. This view was based on complaints from restaurant owners who didn't like the fines they received or being shut down. So now rather than automatically shut down a place the inspector might strip an eatery of its rating and allow them ample time to rectify matters, which is why you see so many "grade pending" signs. All that means is that these are places that probably would get a "D" or have been shut under the previous administration. But no longer.

That's the wrong tact to take I think.

I don't think is De Blasio, that is how health inspection works, not just in NYC but other cities as well.

Usually standard is one schedule inspection (as in they call the restaurant and tell them when they come by) and one random inspection (they just show up randomly) and from time to time they also do stand in (as in inspector just stand by the cook and watch them cook and check temperature of food being served.

Afterward, they will give you a timeframe to correct all the issues, and re-inspect the restaurant during that period is when they have Grade Pending sign. After the follow up if the issue is not corrected that is when they post the actual grade. You could have an A previously then get inspected with a C but then you corrected most of issues and end up with a B.

Some of the issue are not correctable probably due to aging of the restaurant or how the way kitchen layout not allow them to meet new standard. But yeah I won't go to any place that is C or less (that just mean the owner take no effort to correct their mistake) or if is a simple restaurant I expect an A, such as a sandwich shop (subway) or place sell few items and simple like chipotle or any other fast food.

There is also issue with other cultural food and language barrier issue so those I am okay with B. There is good article about roast duck hanging in Chinatown, and how the restaurants there hire an expert to tell health department it is safe:
http://opencitymag.aaww.org/the-roast-duck-bureaucracy/
