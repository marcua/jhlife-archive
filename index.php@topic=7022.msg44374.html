In our landmarked coop, tenants are required to hire only a licensed, insured contractor to do any sort of plumbing or electrical work, and notify the board that such work is being done. Further, if any structural changes are being made (adding or breaking through a wall or closet, relocating a sink or radiator, etc.), a plan must be submitted to the board and approved by the board officers before the work may proceed. Major renovations require the tenant to provide a $500 deposit against any incidental damage their contractors might cause to the entryway, halls or staircase.

I'm a partner in a  construction firm that actually specializes in coop work.  No, we no longer take individual "single" project apartment but have in the past in dozens of coops. While many Boards feel the need to be on every shareholders business, I can tell you what most "fair minded" and progressive Boards want to see before giving the OK to a renovation project.  I have also served on various Boards so I have been of both side of the table.

If the building doesn't have a standard Notification / Application of Construction activities "Form", then the shareholder should just call the Management and ask what is required.  Most likely the following will apply.

1) Scope of work (no, we don't mean blue prints or City forms unless city approval is required)
2) Copies of licences and Insurance of all the contractors.
3) Departmental approvals if required (DOB, DOL, NYCDEP etc)
4) After the green light is given they might ask for a letter with estimated start and completion dates.

Most work in Coop Units will not require City approvals and boards should back off pestering shareholders about little details of their renovations and upgrades unless there is clear evidence they are not using licenced contractors.  In my experience most of the process is relatively easy and handled by the management and monitored by the superintendent.  When a Board engages in systematic harassment of shareholders looking to upgrade their units, even when the shareholder has clearly complied with the "House Rules" , then the word gets around and it can only hurt the Building in general.

Hello jsnyc...all I can tell you is GOOD LUCK.  PLEASE..DO NOT involve your lawyer or these people will make your mom's life even harder.  Instead, try to reach out to one of the Board members and see if there is something they can help your mom out with.  If this is not possible, ask your contractor to see if their carrier is able to do something about upgrading his insurance for the project and if feasible, reimburse him for it.

On another note, a million $ insurance doesn't cut it in most places Ive done work with.  I now carry $12 million but that's because I also do Asbestos and Lead removal.  I no longer do individual or private work unless it is tied to the sale of that particular unit. In my experience, the A+ rated insurance is usually reserved for multimillion $$ projects and it is usually required in Government Jobs or government backed projects.  Your instincs are probably not fooling you and there might in fact be some sort of funny business if you get the feel that these people are pushing a contractor on you.  Please read the house rules for renovation in your complex.  If a rule is not in the house rules or Bylaws then you don't have to go beyond their written requirements and you must bring it up to the Board and management.  

On the other side of the coin, if it is in the house rules, then you really have to comply with them unless you can get a board member to advocate for you and the Board (unofficially) looks the other way.  A reminder about original COOPS (those that were built as original coops and weren't convertions), they will almost always be restrictive and intrusive.  They tend to have the same older Board year in and year out.  These members often like things the way they are...even if the wall paper in the lobby is from the 1960's  :2funny: .  They don't see any need to improve the overall presence of the building and beautifying the complex and / or increasing the value of the individual units is not in their bucket list

ON A FINAL NOTE...IF THE DRAMA GETS TOO CRAZY, THEN ASK THEIR CONTRACTOR TO GIVE YOU A PRICE. IF THE PRICE IS HIGHER THAN YOUR CONTRACTOR TELL THEM THAT YOU WILL ONLY GIVE IT TO HIM IF HE CAN MATCH THE PRICE!   SOMETIMES, YOU HAVE TO PICK YOUR FIGHTS CAREFULLY  :police:
