Hi all -

I'm in desperate need of recommendations for good, reliable and reasonable contractors in JH.

I have been trying to get my mother's kitchen and bathroom done over for nearly a year now. My mother lives in a co-op - Northridge II - and so far the bloody Board has rejected 5 different contractors. These contractors have all worked in JH in places like the Chateau, the Warwick, the Berkley, the Greystones and other Northridge sections. All came recommended from friends or on here (or both) and all were rejected for silly reasons - one was rejected because they thought his insurance coverage was "too convoluted", another because he wasn't a licensed plumber (the only one to get that reason even though all would have been installing a new dishwasher - didn't know a licensed plumber was needed for that!) and silly stuff like this. Needless to say, the contractors weren't happy and I'm fairly furious over it all. I also have to admit, I don't understand why the Board rejects them - these contractors have worked and done far more construction-like work (ie; moving walls, putting in pass thrus, combining closets to make one big one, etc.) in buildings that are, in fairness, much better than Northridge and sell for much more - all without any issue. I suppose when a co-op is self managed like Northridge II, this is what you get - Board members making decisions that they have no qualifications or clue about. Annoying nonetheless.

I need a contractor who carries all the normal licenses and insurance and who also has a $5 million dollar umbrella coverage.

The job, as it is in a co-op, is pretty simple:

1. Kitchen - remove the old cabinets, flooring and appliances; lay new floor, paint walls, install new cabinets and appliances

2. Bathroom - remove old vanity, medicine chest and light, paint and install new vanity, medicine chest and light

3. Painting - rest of the co-op needs to be painted - dining room, living room, foyer and two bedrooms. Removing old ceiling fans in the bedrooms and installing new ones as well as removing chandelier in DR and fixture in foyer and replacing.

As I said, fairly desperate to get this done as soon as possible - the mother is insisting it be done by Christmas - so any recommendations, advice, etc. would be greatly appreciated!!!


THANKS!

Sounds like the issue is not so much the contractor as the Board. I can recommend people I and my coop have worked with but it seems to me that you should get a clear idea of what the Board wants, and get it in writing. The umbrella policy numbers are a step in the right direction (though it does seem a bit high for the neighborhood; usually contractors here carry 1-2M). Any special requirements for the insurance? General contractors often subcontract (to plumbers, electricians, etc.). What does the Board want to see with regard to similar arrangements? Is it enough to present the licenses/insurance of the said special contractors? Do they want to see references? Finally, and with an abundance of caution and a grain of salt, is the Board willing to show you a list of "approved" vendors? I say with caution b/c this is a slippery slope for unkosher arrangements that probably overstep the boundaries of what a board should be allowed to oversee.

You do have to also understand the viewpoint of the board (and that's one I am very much conscious of as the president of a self-managed building) -- they have a legal and fiduciary responsibility to the building and the corporation, and they should not take it lightly. That's why I think it's best if you ask them what the corporation's renovation rules and policies are before you go forward.

Good luck.

Just replied offline with a couple of contractor recommendations.

Again, though, it's key to get to the bottom of why these contractors got rejected by the Board. Insurance is important on the contractor b/c/o agency/transfer of liability, i.e., homeowners often don't want to pay for damages contractors caused, so I think your board is not out of line on that. Licenses are also important b/c otherwise the client has no recourse should anything go wrong. With employees who work at the building the situation is slightly different, b/c they are usually covered by the coop's insurance and workers' comp, so the corporation willingly assumes the risk but of course the corp can't be expected to cover all its tenants. Either way, though, it does not sound like that's the main issue, which is why I say you've got to understand what their concerns are.

My personal stand is that renovation policies must be meaningful and useful for all parties involved b/c it is NOT in the coop's favor to discourage tenants from renovating and keeping up their apartments b/c the value of the WHOLE coop goes down. That's an argument to keep in mind.

Good luck.
