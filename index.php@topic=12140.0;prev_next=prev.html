I think their kickstarter page has an answer. The night market simply wants a variety of cheap food from first-timers who may not be able to pay a lot of fee or sure if they can sustain their businesses. I think directly supporting any of your favorite businesses is fine. If Jackson Heights Greenmarket was about to close and asked for donations, wouldn't it be fair for anyone who loved it so much to pay to keep it open?

This is basically a form of price discriminations. As a business, they should (legally) charge everyone as much as he/she can pay. Many restaurants do that in a subtle way such as providing happy hour or lunch menus at cheaper prices. Similarly, people who support the night market on kickstarter just pay slightly more per dish but that's for everyone's good time. If you are not against progressive tax rate, think about it that way.

Thanks for your response Puri, but I'm not convinced.

This is the second time round for this crew. In February, they went to Kickstarter asking for $100,000, also on the basis of subsidizing vendors, on the basis that that was the sum needed to make this market self-sustainable. They got $14,000 in pledges, which meant that the campaign failed, but apparently they got private funding. See https://www.kickstarter.com/projects/659750809/queens-international-night-market

Four months later they are back, this time asking Kickstarter for $50,000 (apparently the cost of becoming self-sustainable has dropped by 50% in the last four months), again on the basis that the vendors will be subsidized: https://www.kickstarter.com/projects/659750809/keep-the-queens-international-night-market-open

Where has the private funding gone?

Meanwhile, I don't see anyone starting a Kickstarter campaign asking for money so that he/she can subsidize the Roosevelt Avenue, 37th Avenue or 82nd Street vendors, or the Jackson Heights Greenmarket vendors.

To me, there's some explaining to do about this project, as well as some needed transparency, that is just not forthcoming.

It sure doesn't seem to be what Kickstarter was designed for, which might be why their original Kickstarter pitch raised 14% of what they were asking for.

I don't think you have to be worried about any shenanigans on the part of the organizers. As you can see on the page, the bulk of the cost is rent which goes to the Queens Museum. The rent price is about 10k a week. They need half as much because half the season is done.

This first year hopefully will give it support with politicians or whoever who have thusfar only lent emotional support - maybe it will open up some doors.
