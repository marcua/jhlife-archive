Board Approval policies

What are some general guidelines for what Boards want to see, in order to get approved? I've heard that 28% debt-to-income ratio is somewhat typical. A broker told me that Boards also generally want to see two years of reserve (maintenance and mortgage) in the buyer's bank account, as well. Is that typical? I understand that some buildings will be more lenient, and others less lenient.

I am a cash buyer, but two years additional reserve is not possible for me, even without the mortgage aspect (for the units I'm interested in). I was also told that in the case of a Sponsor sale, then this two year reserve is not applicable. Is this correct?

If I will need a two year cash reserve, then I need to lower my standards -- I've been looking at units that are priced at the top threshold of my ability to pay in cash. I may need to look at less expensive units, with the understanding that some of my cash will need to be held in reserve, to get Board approval.

Many coop boards will be extra diligent about checking into the background, employment history, and finances of someone offering cash for unit, and often for good reason. Many times the cash being used to purchase the unit is result of a one-time windfall (an inheritance, proceeds from a lawsuit or a divorce settlement, etc.) and so the ability to pay cash upfront does not necessarily guarantee the applicant's finances are on steady ground, and that they will have the adequate income and resources to pay any future maintenance increases or building assessments.

Yes, with sponsor sales, there is no board approval, so you wouldn't need any reserves.

With non-sponsor sales, I generally make sure that my clients will have at least 6 months in reserves after closing costs, which can be anywhere from 2-6% of the purchase price (depending on the type of apartment you're purchasing). There are certainly some boards that will require more than this. And like another poster said, they will be looking thoroughly through your employment history, tax returns and investments to make sure that you're going to be able to keep making the monthly maintenance payments in the future.

If you're going to have no reserves after the purchase, my sense is that a sponsor sale is the way to go for you.

Let me add this...Just because you have lots of $$$ left over and even if you are purchasing with cash, this doesn't mean the Board will approve an applicant either.  Know this, if an applicant qualifies on paper, they will likely be "interviewed" by the Board.  In fact, rule of thumb is that the Management is doing 100% of the  "financial"  due diligence and not the Board.  A board interview is simply an opportunity to "size up" the potential shareholder.  Only an irresponsible Board of Directors would interview a prospectus even when they don't qualify financially.  Having served in Boards before, our admissions committee would only be allowed to interview the applicant, ONLY after the management firm gave the buyer the "OK".  This is how we did it...

1- Application is filled and several copies were sent to the Management for review.  If anything was missing in the application, the entire package was returned to the applicant.  
2- If the application was presented as per instructions, then the Management would run the credit application and checked on references.
3- If the applicant did not meet the minimum standards set forth for shareholders, then the Management would simply print a letter of rejection and have the head of the admissions committee sign it.  If everything checked then a complete package along with the duplicates were personally presented to the Board during the monthly meeting.  
4- While the committee was headed by one Board member it was comprised of five shareholder.  Three shareholders had to be present on the interview and the Director of the Committee (A Board member)  did not have to be present.  We did not allow the management to be present on that meeting.  

After the meeting, the acceptance or denial was put to a vote right away unless a member wanted to do some background check before voting.  This rarely happened because management did a thorough checking before given them the OK.  It all came down to, "Do You Like them or Not"  or "Do they posses the personal traits" the Board is looking for in a shareholder at that time. Yes, on occasion, an exception was made to approve a "traditional unqualified" buyer if one of the members of the Board would advocate for the prospectus.  I could sit here writting chapters on the subject   (This is CHAPTER I)  :2funny: :2funny: :2funny:
