Weekday (7-9am): 2868 (counted on 5/11/16)
Weekday (4-7pm): 9190 (counted on 5/11/16)

Weekday (7-9am): 1739 (5/9/07)

That's roughly a two-fold increase in foot traffic in less than 10 years. Neighborhood Chat / Re: State of NYC's Neighborhoods
« on: May 17, 2016, 11:04:58 AM »
Some quick takeaways:

  • Over the last 14 years, from 2000 to 2014, Jackson Heights has become less white (down from 14% to 9%), less black (down from 10% to 5%), more Asian (up from 14% to 18%), and more Hispanic (up from 59% to 66%).
  • The percentage of higher income households (those earning $60k - $250k) has gone down while the percentage of low-income households has gone up. Households earning over $250k has remained steady at 1%.
  • Jackson Heights ranks number 1 (in Queens? in the entire city?) for severe crowding
  • 35.5% of households are severely rent-burdened (up from 28.8% in 2000); 54.5% of low-income households are severely rent-burdened (up from 43.4% in 2000)
  • Households with children under 18 years old fell from 45% in 2000 to 28% in 2014

If Jackson Heights is gentrifying, it's following a strange path. The poor are obviously being increasingly squeezed into unaffordable living conditions, but it doesn't seem like they're being displaced by high earners or whites or new families (or whatever the usual indicators of gentrification are).

I'm always amused by the bien pensant recoil at politicians who don’t have Kennedy School-level mastery of policy details. You’d think the last half-century of American politics hadn’t seen candidates like Jimmy Carter, Michael Dukakis, or Al Gore, wonks all who knew more about policy than your average PhD, yet whose intimacy with the arcana of state was somehow insufficient to propel them to—or keep them in—the White House. Or Ronald Reagan and George W. Maybe, just maybe, mastery of policy detail does not a successful political actor make.
