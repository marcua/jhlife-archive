Overheated Apartment?
« on: November 14, 2010, 11:08:04 AM »
Hello all,

I've been wanting to ask this group for a long time: how many of you live in buildings where the heat is kept uncomfortably high? My family lives in a 1930s-era apartment building in which the radiators turn off and on according to some centralized machine-- we don't even have the little knobs that you can use to adjust the level of heat coming from the floor unit. And it is often hotter 'n hell in here.

Now, my husband tells me I just have warm blood because I'm Irish and come from Boston. And it's true that when I was growing up my dad would turn the thermostat down to 55 degrees at night and we usually wore sweaters and thick socks to bed. But earlier this week when the weather was near freezing, I noticed lots of people in my building had their windows open, even on the top floors which supposedly are cooler. So I know it's not just me.

It seems illogical, what with the cost of heating oil and the fact that so many people don't have enough heat and global warming and all that, that local landlords wouldn't want to save some $$ by better regulating their thermostats. So what do you think is going on? And is there anything that we, as tenants or as a neighborhood, can do to encourage heat to be distributed a bit more rationally?

First, radiators and/or convection heaters are ON or OFF, the valve doesn't regulate the heat. Second, an older steam heat system does not deliver heat evenly throughout a large building, you can be sure that for every apartment like your that's overheated there's one that's underheated and has residents wondering why someone doesn't turn up the heat. It's not just your building/boiler/landlord/board. EVERY building that was constructed during this period has the same problem, that's why you can walk all over not just OUR neighborhood, but pretty much any neighborhood in any borough (with similar architecture) and see buildings with some windows open (to regulate heat) and some IN THE SAME BUILDING that are closed and have measures taken to prevent heat loss.
Our building installed a new computer sensor system that has cut our yearly bill by about $10K and has made the on/off cycling more "reasonable". But it's still heat on/heat off and we still have to worry about heat loss through old windows and doors etc. It will NEVER be perfect zoned temperature control unless the whole system is replaced.

Two possibilities:

1. Get a thermostatic radiator valve. They are costly ($75-$100 per radiator) but they can solve your problem. I just installed one on my radiator and now I am enjoying comfortable temperatures in my apartment. The top manufacturer of these thermostatic valves is Danfoss: http://www.danfoss.com/North_America/EnVisioneering/EnVisioneering/ProductGroups/TRVradiatorvalves.htm

2. Does your radiator have a little canister on the side of it that makes a loud hissing noise? If so, you might have a one-pipe steam system with old air vent valves that need to be replaced. My building went through and replaced all of the old air vent valves and it stopped a lot of apartments from getting overheated. As steam fills up the radiator, the air vent valve is supposed to stay open to let the cold air out. Once steam reaches the air vent valve, the valve is supposed to shut, keeping the hot steam in. But as they age, they get stuck in the open position, allowing lots and lots of steam to come out into your room. The air vent valves are relatively cheap to replace (less than $15)
