I think the "hipster" and "Brooklyn-like" labels were used for lack of anything more descriptive.  Maybe "stuff white people like"-esque?!  That doesn't even really mean white people, but more the 20's-30's middle / upper middle class with disposable income, the "creative class" that has increased in JH in recent years.

Of course dssjh is right that we are all incredibly spoiled and have many great things here.  In my post before I was trying to raise the question, however, of why the business community in JH has become so extremely conservative?

The businesses she lists above have all been here for years (except maybe Bhim's).  Every store that opens is a duplicate or quadruplicate of something else.  That leads to a situation where, for any type of store, you have a few stalwarts that are always open, and then a rotating cast of mediocre pretenders that open and close with the seasons.

It's similar to how Brooklyn neighborhoods now have a bunch of brunch places and bars that are all kind of the same.  We have overall diversity in businesses in JH, but it seems to have reached some kind of equilibrium or stasis.

So why not more risk-taking and creativity from the entrepreneurs?  Canelle is a great example of the kind of thing I'm talking about...someone with a great idea who took a chance on our neighborhood.

Can folks point to any new types of businesses that have opened in the last five years?  Maybe the Tibetan places would qualify.  The Tibetan restaurants / cafes are responding to a new market in JH, and I'm wondering why the business community hasn't caught up with the other new market we have been discussing here repeatedly.

Years ago I went from JH to Park Slope back to JH.  Back then, a "Brooklyn Bar" in Park Slope meant either a dyke hangout or an old school place, like Snooky's, or a combination of the two.  When the neighborhood started to change again, old bars became hipster hangouts.  My favorite place to drink was O'Conner's which was located on a very different 5th Avenue.  It was an old Irish place that was more or less the same since the 1930's.  The only things that changed were the jukebox selections and the clientele.

My point is, O'Conner's was not that much different than the Ready Penny.  I wouldn't walk into either place and order a sidecar, but both were/are bars that change a bit depending on their patrons, yet remain very true to themselves.  For me, a "hipster" bar doesn't have to be manufactured, and it doesn't have to displace what is already there.  I know there are a lot of people who don't like the Ready Penny, but in my humble opinion it strikes the right balance in that it's a throwback that blends all the different elements of our neighborhood.  You can have a shot and a beer, while a hipster reads a book at the bar, the old timers are searching for a cigarette, the South Asians are playing pool and the jukebox is blasting either an old Irish drinking song or something by Johnny Cash, which the hipsters see as cutting edge.  I love it.
