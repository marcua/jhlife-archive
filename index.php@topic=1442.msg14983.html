You also have weigh in the safety factor.  Exactly what is the condition of the elevators?  Keep in mind that resident safety is a major reason for elevator renovations.  Also consider the cost of having to pay for frequent elevator maintenance visits.  In the long run, it may be cheaper to renovate the elevators than to continue running the old ones.  There are new safety features available today that are not seen in older elevators, such as emergency call buttons that put residents in contact with elevator maintenance companies as well as the local fire department, systems that ensure leveled cab landings, etc.

As far as it being a tremendous inconvenience to residents, it certainly is.  When our co-op underwent elevator renovations, we were lucky enough to have many residents volunteer to help out with grocery shopping, picking-up meds, laundry help, etc.  Parents were given the opportunity to "park" baby strollers in a locked basement room for easy access.  Our Board coordinated the volunteer staff, and the 6 week renovation period for each elevator went by without a hitch.  What also helped is that some of our senior residents opted to stay with family rather than remain in the building.  I'm not sure how a plan like this would help in your situation because 6 months is quite a long time!

I think that the average elevator with a 7 stories elevation would be about $150k each for new ones.  That's what a building in Corona just paid about a year ago for each of the 2 brand new ones. The problem may be arising from the fact that MPJ is involved in the construction industry.  When this happens MGMT companies will often be too involved in bids and contracts.  I always recommend that the Board also bring in bids from outside sources and not simply the MGMT companies.  When you are dealing with small, everyday maintenance issues like painting, spot electrical and plumbing as well as groundskeeps and upkeep of mechanical systems.  When it comes to major capital improvement projects and expensive undertakings like elevators, boilers, roof replacements and such it is wise to involve a committee. 

In one of the buildings where I served on the board we created a beautification committee that also doubled as the construction committee whenever we had a major project coming up.  We also had an admitions committee and a groundskeep committee that was mostly active during the warmer months and during X-mas.  The trick was to have a Bd member head the committee but the remainder of the members would be shareholders that were not part of the Board thus ensuring that the responsibilities of the building are shared and spread out. The Board usually voted on the recommendations of that committee.  Not until I began dealing with other Coops I realized how well the buildings were I served and still own units were ran.  The truth is that most MGMT companies hate when the Boards get involved with the larger construction projects since this is one of the ways they make the extra $$$.
