This conflict over street vendors is as old as the city itself. For those opposed to this business model, I would note that the number of street vendors today is just a small fraction of what it used to be. Brick-and-mortar businesses, and eventually municipal government, have been hostile towards these vendors for centuries. This partnership has virtually wiped out this type of business, so what we see today is just a remnant of what used to be. That said, the concerns that some people have expressed about fraud are real. Food vendors need to get permits from a couple different agencies. Demand for these permits among immigrants is astronomically high, so it is common that people acquire these permits and licenses and lease them out to others at a ridiculous markup. We cannot tell which vendors have gotten their permits from the city, or which are leasing them. But the business is rough, and many of the food vendors around the city are forking over huge sums of profits to the official permit-holderswhich incidentally includes money to pay taxes--, are working twelve-hour shifts, make sub-poverty incomes, and have zero benefits or job security. As for effects on brick-and-mortar businesses, I understand the concern, but where in our competitive market-based system does it state that enterprises have to be affixed to the ground?
