I think the appropriate thing to do is to extend your left arm to indicate to any motorists behind you that you intend to turn left.  I usually point so there isn't any doubt.  When it is safe, take traffic lane, and stay there until it is safe to return to the bike lane.  You should never feel shy about following this procedure.  You have as much claim to the traffic lane as the cars do.

If you ever have to "take the lane" in heavier traffic (e.g. to pass a double-parked truck or deal with another unsafe situation along the curb lane), it is best to be polite but assertive.  Bike in the middle of the lane if that is what you need to do to clear the area safely, but give surrounding traffic ample warning of what you're about to do.   And don't stay there any longer than necessary.

Whatever you do, never swerve if you can avoid it.

Fortunately, there are bike lanes crossing from JH all the way across the QB Bridge. Once in Manhattan, crossing town isn't too bad - I usually go across 61st street to 5th Avenue, then across 59th street to Columbus Circle, then down the new bike lane on Broadway. I take 58th street to 1st avenue to get back on the bridge home. I have a little video of my commute in one morning here: http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=gfMenlZt2Q4

I stop at 54th street at my office, but when I do head further south, Times Square is crowded as the bike lane dissappears at the pedestrian zones, so I go down 7th, but generally traffic doesn't go too fast, then the bike lane picks up again south of 42nd street and runs along B'way to Union Square. There aren't any crosstown bike lanes between 20th and 120th streets, but most are only 1 lane of slow-moving traffic, with plenty of room along the side to ride. I avoid 42nd and 57th streets.  The new lanes on Manhattan avenues (1st & 2nd, 8th & 9th) south of 34th street are wonderful, and I hope they eventually make their way up through Midtown.
