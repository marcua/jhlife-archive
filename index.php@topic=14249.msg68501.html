I think it's too wordy. Why not try "is your restaurant wheelchair accessible?" Wait for yes. "How about the bathrooms?"

So, training on wheelchair accessibility is probably on that list.

I've tried everything you've all said. The question has yet to ever be understood. It's more than getting into a restaurant and a bathroom that makes for wheelchair accessibility. As per the Americans with Disabilities Act, a bathroom is wheelchair accessible when the door and accessible stall door are at least 34" wide. There are grab bars on at least two sides of the toilet. A wheelchair is able to make a 180 degree turn within the bathroom. A wheelchair can fit underneath the sink so hands can easily be washed. I realize that most employees won't know any of this, but they have yet to understand what I am asking when I explain it all.

To be sort of fair, there should be a way of certifying or marking a business as accessible using secret shoppers or something like that. But what I really think:

It isn't unreasonable to expect that a person in the United States can call any business and find out if it is accessible to a wheelchair. If you run a business you need to know about the ADA. The other comments make it seem like you're imposing on these businesses by wanting to spend money there. You are a potential customer and they can't even tell you whether you can physically enter their business.

It's possible that people are afraid to answer frankly because they're afraid to get in trouble somehow by not being compliant - but again, the owner of that business needs to take reasonable measures to make the business accessible. If it's not feasible to make that space accessible then the polite thing to do is explain that it couldn't be done. People should not be flummoxed by encountering a disabled person. Disabled people shouldn't need to spend their whole lives educating everyone else about the fact that they exist and eat food and spend money and live in NYC.

The other comments do not suggest even remotely that the OP is "imposing" on a business by wanting to spend money there. OP asked *why* a question was causing confusion, and the OP is getting answers as to *why* that may be the case.

If someone is using language like "Are the restaurant, and its associated bathroom(s), fully wheelchair accessible?" and "ADA compliant," with people who aren't the manager/owner and a good percentage of the folks answering the phone may speak English as second language (and that's very likely to be the case when calling a restaurant) that person faces a highly likelihood of talking with a confused worker. What's an associated bathroom? (As opposed to bathrooms that aren't associated with the restaurant?) What does it mean to be "fully" wheelchair accessible? (As opposed to being partially wheelchair accessible?) What's the likelihood of talking to someone who isn't fully aware of what ADA compliance looks like at a small business? (Very good).

Also, consider just how ADA compliant these businesses have to be. (Not very). The ADA places a higher burden on larger businesses than smaller ones. http://www.ada.gov/smbusgd.pdfBusinesses are expected to make (and pay for) changes like adding ramps, making restrooms accessible, etc. that won't do the business harm. Restaurants operate on a pretty razor thin margin, and that's why 20+ years after the ADA was passed you see restaurants in buildings built prior to 1992 with bathrooms in the basement, steps with no ramp, etc. because doing those things would bankrupt the business. So, considering it's an industry where many of the businesses operate out of a space that can't be made ADA compliant without destroying the business, I think it's fairly easy to understand why questions about accessibility aren't understood.

In general, I often find it difficult to communicate with restaurants by phone, so I agree with those advocating breaking the question down into simple parts.

But I think there's a possibility restaurants are hearing a more difficult question than you may be asking.  They may have found that some real-world customers have mobility limitations that are different than those anticipated by the ADA.  Consider a person capable of navigating a step or two, but who cannot walk long distances.  A large, wheelchair-accessible restaurant may still not be feasible for this potential customer, while a smaller restaurant may be a better fit.   I have a relative who fits this profile, and we try to scout out restaurants in advance when possible, because the suitability of the place often comes down to a judgement call that it's hard to make over the phone.

So restaurants may have learned from experience that different customers asking these questions have different requirements (notwithstanding the ADA's valiant efforts at standardization), and as a result they may find it difficult to answer these questions.  Alternatively, they may know that they're not ADA compliant by the letter of the law, but that they can meet the needs of some potential customers asking about accessibility.

In any case, I think the project you've undertaken is extremely useful and important, and I wish you continued success (and many delicious meals along the way!).  And thank you for sharing it with us at JH Life.
