Neighborhood Chat / Re: Local School Test Results
« on: August 12, 2013, 06:43:49 PM »
It's so hard to even reply to this thread in a way that makes sense because the whole thing really doesn't make sense.  In a nutshell, test scores mean something and they don't.  I'll just run down a list of things to remember when interpreting this data.

1.  The 3rd grade math test has 7th grade level algebra problems in it.
2.  In the ELA, 3rd graders had to cite text in their responses.

So... do I think this test actually tells us anything about where our 3rd and 4th graders are academically? My answer is:  very little. 

That said... some schools did drastically better than others.  What does this mean?  You really have to interpret the data differently for each school.  Here is why.

1.  English language learners have to take the test after only 10 months in the country.  10 months!  Imagine you come from another country and don't speak any English, but maybe have a strong academic background, you probably won't do so well after only 10 months.  Schools with high ELL populations and new arrivals tend to do worse on the test.  It doesn't mean they are bad schools.

2.  Our neighborhood has a lot of fantastic dual language programs.  Students in dual language programs do worse on tests in 3rd and 4th grade.  They don't reap the benefits of becoming literate in 2 language systems until 5-7 years in the program (that's 4th grade at the earliest for students that started DL in Kindergarten).  If you look at their performance in high school, they regularly outperform their monolingual counterparts in several longitudinal studies on the subject.  So, if the school has a dual language program, the students may score lower, but actually be performing on a higher academic level.

3.  The test was written by Pearson publishing.... Pearson publishing: a FOR PROFIT organization.

4.  The test is supposed to measure "college and career readiness" based on the Common Core Standards.  These have no research base... read the Diane Ravitch article above. 

5.  Does anyone remember the talking pineapple?  http://www.nydailynews.com/new-york/talking-pineapple-question-state-exam-stumps-article-1.1064657

I'm a teacher in NYC. I love my job.  I love my school.  I love my students.  I think a lot of schools are fantastic.  It's hard to interpret this data.  You know and love your children.  ADVOCATE for them!

Neighborhood Chat / Re: What's the average public schools rating in queens NY?
« on: July 29, 2013, 04:25:02 PM »
As a teacher AND a parent, all I can say is that the NYC public schools have tons of opportunities for students but you have to know how to navigate the system.  I'd say ask on the JH families yahoo group and find out about people's experiences.  School report cards honestly mean very little.  A school can get an A, but be a total nightmare, likewise, some of the highest performing schools have Cs because they don't meet AYP (annual yearly progress).  Schools get extra credit for servicing high-needs groups, and getting students to make more than a year's progress.  If students are already high performing and getting 4s on the tests, how can you prove more than a year's progress?  Also, the SQR (school quality review, available on the statistics link for each school) is a good resource, but again, not the only indicator.  It will have a narrative description of what the school does well and what it is working on.  Schools are scored with "well-developed, proficient, developing, etc."  While getting "well-developed" is a good sign, some "proficient" schools are excellent as well.  The parent and teacher surveys is also good indicators of the atmosphere at a school.  Bottom line:  visit your zoned school, enter your child into the lottery for every lottery school in the district and ones out of district that you would feel comfortable commuting to, get your child tested for gifted and talented (not because the programs are necessarily better, but because it may give you more choice of schools), and talk to other parents.  Good luck!
