There is a lot to unpack here.

First of all, pit bulls are not a breed, but rather a nebulous grouping of dogs based on certain shared physical characteristics. 

Pit bulls are NOT more aggressive than any other dogs. dogsbite is full of actual garbage "statistics" - basically they cull info from newspapers without realizing or caring that: (a) papers frequently misidentify the type of dog and have a bias to identifying almost any dog as a "pit bull" that bears even a slight resemblance to one, (b) when you take into account that the label "pit bull" comprises 4 or 5 breeds, they are as likely to attack as any other randomly grouped set of 4 or 5 breeds, and (3) that newspapers are significantly more likely to report on pit bull attacks than other type of dog attacks. Basically, it's all confirmation bias.

The notion that pit bulls are more dangerous than other types of dogs has been refuted by the ASPCA (https://www.aspca.org/about-us/aspca-policy-and-position-statements/position-statement-pit-bulls) and the Humane Society (https://blog.humanesociety.org/wayne/2014/07/pit-bulls.html), among other reputable groups.

If you are afraid of big dogs, just say that. Pit bulls are actually considered to have a temperament on par with labs. This is not to say that pit bulls never attack, but rather that labs DO sometimes attack - because they are animals. And animals are individuals.

This is a pretty good overview: https://www.huffingtonpost.com/steffen-baldwin/the-lies-damn-lies-and-st_b_8112394.html

The bigger problem is off-leash dogs, which is mostly an issue with small dog owners. That doesn't mean we should go ahead and ban all small dogs.

Basically, it's all confirmation bias.

I don't really have a dog in this fight (har har), but you're abusing statistics too, and so are the sources you shared. The number of breeds called "pit bull" is completely irrelevant; what matters is the number of dogs, relative to the number of incidents. If labrador retrievers are really as dangerous as pitbulls, then the number and severity of lab injuries vs. pit injuries should be in the same ratio as the number of labs vs. pits. But that's clearly not the case, right?

As for media bias, it might well be true that pit bull attacks are more likely to be reported (or it might not), but deaths are going to be reported no matter what, right? The CDC stats say 75% of fatal dog attacks were from "pit bulls." Are pit bulls 75% of dogs?

The better argument is that dogs in general are very rarely dangerous unless specifically trained to be, so the "most dangerous dog breed" is still not really anything to worry about. Personally I love those dogs and I agree they're mostly friendly goofballs, and I think it's heartbreaking how people don't want to adopt them. But I also think it's understandable that people are wary, when advocates do such a poor job of refuting the danger.

(Also, I agree leash laws are important, but I never see dogs off leash around JH.)
