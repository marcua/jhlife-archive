Neighborhood Chat / Yet another pedestrian killed on Northern Blvd.
« on: September 10, 2018, 09:09:11 PM »
Yet another fatality on Northern Blvd.   This time it was a 70-year-old man struck on Northern Blvd. and 108th Street. 

Over the past 12 months, in our area alone, there have been five pedestrians killed on Northern Blvd.
  • Jose Gomez, 71, killed September 13, 2017 on Northern Blvd. near 85th St.
  • Myriam Nino, 82, struck on January 5, 2018 at Northern Blvd. and 90th Street in Jackson Heights, died on January 20th
  • Giovanni Ampuero, 9, killed on April 28, 2018 on 70th Street and Northern Boulevard
  • Carlos Gavilanes, 47, killed on May 21, 2018 on Northern Boulevard between 100th and 101st Streets
  • Unidentified male, 70, killed on September 9, 2018 on Northenr Boulevard and 108th Street

Please read this important article in Streetsblog about the need for substantial changes on Northern Blvd to make it safer for everyone.

Currently, Jimmy Van Bramer is leading the charge for safety improvements on Northern Boulevard.  In the past, Daniel Dromm has also been a leader on this issue (and was responsible for many of the improvements that have already been put into place).  I hope he starts to speak out on this topic again soon. 

Jose Peralta, to his credit, has been a leader in Albany on efforts to pass legislation to pass legislation for traffic safety enforcement cameras. 

Don't be confused by the distractions being advanced by our other elected officials to hide their inaction.  Michael Den Dekker reliably shows up at press events when somebody is killed to advocate crossing guards or changes to traffic signals or repainting the crosswalks.  But he's silent on efforts strengthen or enforce our traffic laws to reduce reckless driving.

Francisco Moya also often shows up at press events to tout empty gestures on these issues.  But in his previous position as a state legislator, Francisco Moya was a consistent opponent of efforts to improve street safety.  Now that he is on the City Council, hopefully he will turn over a new leaf and encourage NYCDOT and NYPD to design streets for safety and enforce our traffic laws.

If you care about street safety and the quality of life in our neighborhood, please ask your elected officials and candidates for office to support a comprehensive safety redesign of Northern Boulevard, including measures to provide pedestrians with shorter crossing distances.  Please also ask them to support real traffic enforcement measures that penalize reckless drivers, and measures to bring New York State's backwards pedestrian right-of-way laws in line with other states.

« on: June 20, 2018, 09:09:58 PM »
Currently, the City of New York has about 145 public artworks depicting famous men in history, and only 5 depicting famous women. To address this imbalance, the city is seeking nominations of important women in the city's history, calling for "ideas for how to honor the inspiring, diverse people and events that comprise the history of New York women."   

See here for details

They will certainly get a lot of nominations, as there is no shortage of great women in New York City's history.  I'd love to see monuments to Emma Lazarus, Frances Perkins, Rose Schneiderman, Dorothy Day, Clara Lemlich, Nellie Bly, and many others.

But this is a rather Manhattan-centric list.  Were there women from the history of Queens that should be recognized as well?  Please add your ideas!

Louis Armstrong’s love of Corona will be explored at all 62 Queens Library branches



by Bill Parry

Jazz great Louis Armstrong’s love affair with Corona will be celebrated throughout May and June as Queens Library partners with the Kupferberg Center for the Arts at Queens College and the Louis Armstrong House Museum in Corona to bring events to all 62 of the system’s branches.

Free workshops, concerts, lectures, videos and surprise events will focus on the 50th anniversary of the release of Armstrong’s classic “What a Wonderful World,” which was inspired by the neighborhood around his home at 34-56 107th where he and his wife Lucille Wilson lived from 1943 to 1971.

Neighborhood Chat / Can we protect a space for civil dialogue?
« on: April 10, 2018, 08:30:54 AM »
Moderators Note:

Jackson Heights Life moderators and forum members work hard to keep this site going as a place where neighbors can exchange views and insights and ultimately help improve and strengthen the community.  We have no illusions that people will agree.  But we think that now more than ever, a space for this type of discussion is needed.  We depend on your help to maintain a community-minded discourse and prevent this site from slipping into flame wars as so many boards of this kind tend to do.

From our beginning over a decade ago, we have tried to maintain as light a touch on moderating as possible.  We put a lot of work into beating back the constant scourge of spam, bot accounts, and occasional hacker attacks. We maintain just a few rules.  Please review them.  We would welcome your participation and support in upholding these community standards.

If somebody posts something that you find distasteful, by all means tell them, but please try to rise to the challenge of doing so without calling them names or questioning their motives.  Assume they also want what's best for the community.

Please keep discussions on topic.

Please don't post here just to provoke.  That is trolling, and we won't tolerate it.

Don't feed the trolls or call them trolls.  If you think somebody is trolling, please send a note to the moderators using the "Report to Moderator" function.  If we take action, we will generally do so quietly.

Please help us keep JH Life going as a welcoming and useful community resource. 

Thank you very much,
The Moderators

Great new find in the NY Times...

An Ex-Bodega Takes a Star Turn at Cienega Las Tlayudas de Oaxaca
Hungry City
By LIGAYA MISHAN MARCH 15, 2018



The tortilla is monumental, stretched to its limits on the tin platter. It was made in Oaxaca, Mexico, of masa (a dough of dried corn steeped in slaked lime) pressed flat until kerchief-thin, then baked on the comal to an even crackle, as crisp at the circumference as at the center.

The corn is different there, starchier and stronger, able to sustain such breadth. It’s tricky to make these giant tortillas out of sweeter, softer American corn, which is why the stiff, pre-baked rounds, stacked like vintage records, are shipped from Mexico. Somehow they haven’t shattered on their long journey to Cienega Las Tlayudas de Oaxaca, a grocery turned restaurant in Corona, Queens....
