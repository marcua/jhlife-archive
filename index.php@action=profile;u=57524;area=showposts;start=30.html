Real Estate & Home Improvement / Re: Thoughts on Fildor Court anyone?
« on: May 04, 2018, 11:38:47 PM »
The best way to understand what issues are affecting the coop is to read the board minutes, run a search to see if there are any lawsuits pending, ask to see the financials so your accountant can take a look at them, and read the offering plan, bylaws, house rules, and proprietary lease; including any amendments. The reality is that most people will not be well versed on any of this stuff; hence, it is unlikely anybody will respond with any meaningful/substantive information.  If you like the place, make an offer otherwise chances are you will never see these documents.  If your offer is accepted, hire a lawyer who will arrange for you to have the opportunity to read the board minutes – I suggest going back at least 4 years.  You can learn a lot, i.e., any problems associated with tenants, apartments, structural integrity of the building, lawsuits, liabilities, loans, etc.  Moreover, hire a lawyer who read the corporate documents and go over them with you and explain what the nature of the relationship if you ultimately purchase the unit, i.e., you do not own the apartment and are a shareholder in a corporation that gives you the right to rent a particular unit. This will cost you more than the average RE lawyer because the average lawyer will not work that hard on individual cases because RE closings are generally a volume practice and most people do not ask questions.  Moreover, the average RE lawyer in Queens do not charge much; hence their fee does not contemplate the aforementioned.  In other words, a lot of lawyers who handle RE matters will not bill you much, but will literally show up at the closing and tell you where to sign.  On the other hand, you can hire one of those cheap lawyers and read the documents yourself – this option will be a nightmare.
