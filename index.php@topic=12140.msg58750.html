Is it the sugar? I know American Chinese food is sweetened up.

I think Thai food in the US tastes relatively sweeter because of less spiciness and sourness. Herbs and condiments taste different here too.

To me, American pad thai is generally bad because of poor stir-fry technique, incomplete ingredients (no hard tofu, dried shrimp, or banana flower), and substitutes for tamarind and palm sugar.

Oops, never mind, I got it to open again, at least enough to see the caption, which is "Shrimp Pad Thai" and a little bit of the article:

Kitchen 79 is on 79th Street in Jackson Heights, Queens, a neighborhood known for its ethnic diversity. And the restaurant fits right in: The cuisine is Thai.

From the minute you walk through the door, Kitchen 79 distinguishes itself. The décor is sleek—black and white chairs, black walls, a black-and-white-patterned floor and chandeliers.

The menu makes a mark, too. Diners get an appetizer and an entree, the lunch special, for one price: $7.50 for chicken, tofu or vegetable, and $8.50 for shrimp or beef.

Unlike many places that offer only soup or salad as lunch appetizers, Kitchen 79 offers seven—soup and salad, of course, but also fried tofu with sweet chili sauce; a pastry pocket stuffed with curried chicken, potato and onion; and steamed or fried dumplings filled with chicken, for example.

The traditional pad thai—rice noodles with bean sprouts, egg, scallion and peanuts—is the entree recommendation of co-owner Thawatchai Thadchan. The phad prik sod is made with chili, long hot pepper, onion and scallion. Other dishes include fried rice, green and red curries, and a couple of kinds of noodles.
