It seems to be a chicken and egg kind of thing. Do people come to a neighborhood because of the amenities or do the amenities come because of the people? it's probably both. Clearly the market can support a 99 cent store on every block. And those places are pretty full whenever I walk by. When the local market can't support those stores, they will go out of business and new ones will stop opening. The stores that take their place will cater to the population as it exists.

As for landlords, they charge a lot because they think they can get it. And obviously many businesses are paying those high rents. Whether those businesses are legitimate or fronts for something else is another story, of course. When store owners stop agreeing to those rents, then they'll come down. But it's a long process. Landlords will to leave a store vacant for months at a time if they think a willing tenant will eventually come along.

I don't mean to make this sound like Microeconomics 101. But this is what's happening, or at least an oversimplification of it.

I'd love a decent bar to watch American football. But that's only a little more likely than a bookstore.
