In my experience, it's definitely building-by-building.

For instance, my building has an extremely easy-going board - it's hard to get rejected - but a washer-dryer would be an absolute no because of our very old plumbing. (As Scotty said on Star Trek, "she canna handle the load, Cap'n!") Then again, I'm not in one of the fancy buildings you're describing with the wood-burning fireplaces.

I can't see a problem with you traveling for business as long as you swear up and down you won't do anything shady like rent the place out on AirBNB. That is a VERY hot topic in the city right now and an easy way to get boards to turn on flamethrowers. But an empty apartment wouldn't be an issue.

You will have to disclose every financial thing the board is even slightly curious about. No way around that. They need to make sure not only that you have money, but that the money has been acquired through legitimate means!

Realtor wise, Daniel Karatzas and Michael Carfagna seem to have a LOT of the listings in the specific buildings you're looking for. And they're super experienced, and fixtures in the neighborhood. They can really guide you through. There are other Realtors I'd recommend for other buildings, but for the places you're suggesting ...

Post by: ljr on March 28, 2015, 06:13:37 PM
The complexes you are looking at (except for the Towers, I think) are made up of small individual coops. So the issue of being somewhat of an absentee owner can be a problem, because a small coop needs all hands on deck to help run the building. I live in Hawthorne Court in a self-managed building of 10 apartments, and there really is a lot of work that goes into running the building. When several apartments are owned by people who don't really live here, it's hard to divvy up the work in a way that's fair. It's not board meetings so much as it is keeping the books, paying vendors, dealing with elevator maintenance issues, building plumbing issues, the laundry machines in the basement, various city agencies who change the rules and issue violations that have to be cleared, etc.

We once had a serious water leak on a major holiday (in an absentee owner's apartment) and someone (me, as it happened) had to stay home and deal with the plumber. Another time one of us had to go to some city agency to deal with a new plumbing requirement. It's a lot of work, but the upside is we have lower maintenance payments because in addition to having no underlying mortgage, we have no managing agent and no salaried staff (just a live-in part-time super who receives a free apartment in exchange for maintenance work, along with minor compensation.)

I just spoke to a neighbor in another HC building who said that they recently turned down an applicant, and one of the reasons was that the person traveled much of the time for work, and they want someone who will be there and be willing to pitch in and help run the place. I know our house feels the same way and when we interview an applicant, we make sure they know that we expect them to help with the shared "chores" of the building. I sometimes think that living in a bigger coop with professional management would be easier.  It's something to keep in mind. But we decided on this because of the lower cost, as well as the charm of the apartments and the convenient location. The downside is, we do have to put in time to help run the place.

I have to say, I am highly tempted to give you bullet point answers to your questions, but I feel you will likely get conflicting opinions on the same… From what I have read you may not have a “cookie cutter file”, yet possibly a very strong one. Coop deals in my experience value TRANSPARENCY above all things. (and all good business deals for that matter :)

My advice is usually to be open about the strengths AND weaknesses of any file. Sometimes determining what is strength or a weakness and in which order to address them may require some evaluation, in added effort to achieve maximum impact. For example your file's "strengths" are: 1. Strong assets 2. You have owned a coop before, etc.  “Weakness”  of your file may perhaps be that your income isn’t easy to verify, but if you have sustained comparable housing payments and perhaps even higher for the past 10 years, I don’t see why offering to escrow funds before being asked by the board to do so is necessary. That’s roughly how I would approach it. If the coop package is well structured you’d likely cut some time on processing and board review as well.
