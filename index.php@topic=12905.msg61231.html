Quote from: mchafkin on February 07, 2015, 04:49:59 PM
What are the average rents on 37th? Does anyone know with a bit more detail or are there any sources for this kind of info?

As far as I know, the only way to find out what the rents are on 37th is to ask. The rent number itself doesn't tell one enough, because the term of the lease, renewal options, annual increases, and right to assign or terminate on notice are equally important, especially for a tenant who is willing to invest significant amounts of money in the property. It's one thing to open a clothing store, it's another to open something like a restaurant that can involve hundreds of thousands of dollars in investment in the space. With that kind of investment, a five year lease, which seems preferred by many landlords here, doesn't work.

That said, I know of a space where the landlord is seeking rent comparable to what one would pay on Lexington above 60th, but there are also spaces where the rent is more in keeping with the area's economics. I also know of a situation that only makes sense to me if the tenant is happy to run his business at a loss. Interesting, but idle, to speculate on why that might be. And then there are situations that are just hard to fathom, like paying $7,500/month for 800 square feet to sell pizza by the slice. The owner of that business will tell anyone who asks that they can take over his space tomorrow.

In case anyone is interested, main floor commercial space in the Brunson Building is being quoted at $85 per square foot and $95 for street corner space.

Here's the short version of why I think we are not seeing smart bars and restaurants on 37th:

Many landlords are asking for rents, lease lengths and annual increases that discourage investment in commercial space. The result is establishments in which the tenant invests as little as possible.

Assuming that obstacle can be overcome, there is uncertainty for investors about community support.

The reason that there is uncertainty about community support is that nobody has tried to open a smart bar or restaurant, and they have not tried precisely because many landlords seem to want terms that discourage investment.

There are existing lease arrangements, resulting in landlord demands for higher rent from prospective new tenants, that are difficult to comprehend unless the tenant is happy to run his or her business at a loss. As we all know, in some situations it is quite rational to run a business at a loss.
