Impact of Subletting and Dog Policy on Apartment Values
« on: June 09, 2010, 10:37:41 AM »
I am wondering if there are any real estate experts out there that have an opinion on restrictive policies of co-op apartments, such as "no dogs" and "no subletting"
I live in a pre-war coop apartment building with over 100 units, elevators, garden in the back (no garage), near the subway, very spacious apartments, storage in the basement, laundry on premises, etc. bur we are seeing that apartments in the building are not selling for as much as comparable apartments in other buildings in the neighborhood. I'm wondering if my building's very strict "no dogs" and "no subletting" policies have an impact on the apartment values?
How much more can a nice 1 Bedroom or 2 Bedroom apartment in JH sell for if it has a policy that allows dogs compared with a similar apartment in a "no-dog" building?
How much more if the building permits subletting?
Is there an additional combined impact of permitting dogs and subletting?
I'm hoping the real estate gurus out there (or people looking to buy apartments) can weigh on the matter. Thanks!

When we looked for our coop in Jackson Heights, we only considered places that allowed pets in the coop rules. And "we don't really allow dogs, but a lot of people have them"  -- which we heard more than once -- wasn't good enough for us. That ruled out a number of buildings in this neighborhood that we would have otherwise seriously considered, but never looked at. That included buildings that had a pet-weight rule - we have a big, quiet, happy lab-mix who's over the weight limit of some neighborhood coops, so we never looked at them either. Moving here with a dog greatly limited the number of buildings we could even consider. There are a lot of dog owners in this neighborhood, and in the city - I wonder why anyone would want to immediately rule out a large percentage (20 percent?) of potential buyers.

Our board did have a "we must approve the dog" policy which we went through at our board interview, and our rules insist that pet owners make sure their dogs aren't unclean or a nuisance in public areas and don't bark all day, remain fully vaccinated and are responsible for any damage their pets may cause to the building.

For the record, our dog hasn't managed to damage our building yet. The cement/tile hall floors, wrought iron doors and brick walkways don't seem to damage too easily. Perhaps other buildings have more delicate brick?

Quote from: rhydewithdis on June 09, 2010, 10:47:53 AM
5% more for allowing pets

I've been talking about this subject for 2 or more years here.  Subletting is a big deal for long term investing.  I have said this before - unless this is your last coop or you got an amazing deal that would allow you a hefty profit in a relative short period i strongly recommend buying a COOP with liberal rules.  While the 30% in increase maybe a stretch is most areas in one particular building where I'm the sponsors broker I have sold units for as much as 33% more than buildings literally across the street that either did not allow subletting or made the subletting a temporary right.  Pet policy is actually one of those rules were most people do not take advantage of...if I am selling one of my units in a pet friendly Bldg I will exploit that when advertising & negotiating the price - pet lovers will pay up to 10% or more in a building that allows pets. 

There is absolutely minimal concern in having a pet inside an apartment...in a pet friendly building there will be specific rules about your pets but mostly common sense issues like picking up after your pet and some may have weight restrictions and in some occasions a "pet fee' of about $25 per month.
