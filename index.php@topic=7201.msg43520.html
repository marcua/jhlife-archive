Taggie --

You seem to be extremely confused. Or purposely distorting the facts. This is the unfortunate by-product of people using the internet anonymously. We don't know who you are and we can't verify exactly what you are saying and/or implying. But you succeed in misdirecting people's attention to things that are not accurate.

The Jackson Heights Green Alliance (JHGA) applied to make the 78th Street Play Street a permanent public plaza. This process is very transparent involving a minimum of 3 public meetings which the Community Board held over the last 24 months. In addition, the JHGA has held several additional public meetings (town halls, visioning workshops), in multiple languages, to get community input. The Community Board voted unanimously to support making 78th Street a permanent public plaza. Every elected official representing the area also supported JHGA's application for the public plaza including Mayor Bloomberg, Council Members Dromm and Ferreras, Borough President Marshall, Congressman Crowley, Senator Peralta and Assemblymen Moya and DenDekker.

The Department of Transportation (DOT) oversees all public plazas. DOT has allocated up to $2.5m in capital funding to renovate 78th Street into a public plaza. The Jackson Heights Green Alliance does NOT receive this money. The money will be spent directly on infrastructure improvements at the 78th Street Public Plaza.

From the DOT website:

78th Street between 34th Avenue and Northern Boulevard, Community Board 3

Jackson Heights is today considered to be one of the most diverse communities in the world. The dense neighborhood however is woefully lacking in public open space â€“ there is only one park, a heavily utilized, fully paved 1.92 acre playground named Travers Park. Before applying to the NYC Plaza Program, the Jackson Heights Green Alliance had already programmed a summer play street at the site for four years. This project will now formalize the space for the community by closing 78th Street between 34th Avenue and Northern Boulevard to through traffic in order to add approximately 10,000 square feet of open space for both active and passive recreation.

The outreach strategies have been so extensive and varied that I find it difficult to imagine that anybody with a passing interest in these issues has not encountered them.  Let's step back and look at the big picture.  Early on, when Travers Park expansion was just an impossible idea, Green Alliance and JHBG staffed information tables at the annual It's My Park Day event in Travers Park, explaining their vision and seeking volunteers.  These issues were discussed further through the Jackson Heights Green Agenda project, a tremendous organizing effort by Len Maniace/JHBG, Queens Community House, and the Pratt Center for Community Development, which engaged the community in an extended visioning process across language barriers and cultural divides.   The Play Street itself, since its beginning, has been staffed on weekends by Green Alliance and JHBG volunteers, who are generally easy to spot and always happy to talk to people about how to get involved.  And there have been many other meetings, publicized by email, flyers, social media, and word of mouth.

If you want to start getting involved immediately, why not help out JHBG with its 78th Street beautification effort on Saturday?  

The public process will continue.  When it does, if you're not hiding under a rock, you'll hear about it.  If you need a personal invitation, I suggest adding yourself to the Green Alliance email list (via their website at http://www.jhgreen.org/).
