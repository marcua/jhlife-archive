I moved into Southridge 1 in March. I am new to the Jackson Heights area.
There are pros & cons to Southridge, just like any other building.
Here's how I see it. If anyone thinks I'm wrong about something, please let me know, again, I am new to both Southridge and JH.

Pros:
Much cheaper (at least $100k) than similar properties 10 blocks west.
Good size apts., I believe they all have 1-1/2 baths.
Low maintenance.
Excellent staff. The hallways, garbage rooms, grounds, etc. are always spotless. Any problems with the apartment are almost immediately taken care of. There's an office on-site you can call with any problems. Maintenance guys are very friendly and nice.
Playground on grounds, interior play room. (I have kids.)
The screening process is stringent, which is good after you're accepted. Nobody there is rich, but everyone is solidly middle-class.
As far as I can tell, the area is safe. I walk home from the train after dark, and no one gives me a second glance.
I find the neighborhood very pretty, tree-lined 34Th Ave is very nice. 87Th Street between 34Th & 35Th is particularly beautiful.

Cons:
My biggest con is with the neighborhood. Not much to do in the immediate area. Some bars on Northern, past 90Th Street. I moved from Greenpoint, which, in my opinion, is a more vibrant neighborhood (if much uglier!)
Approx. 12 minute walk to 7 train (90Th Street.) I walk pretty fast. No problem when it's nice out, not so nice when it's cold/raining. About a $5 cab ride from 74Th Street station, which I only did once.
10 year + wait for parking spot in building, 7 year + wait for storage locker downstairs. Parking can be a pain after 6PM.
No sublets allowed. Good if you're not subletting, bad if you want to move and save some money while you're trying to sell.
I have heard that it's hard to sell, because they screen the buyers so stringently. Somebody else will have to verify this.
Some co-op rules seem a bit silly and arbitrary. I imagine this happens in a lot of co-ops.

My opinion as someone that bought in JH 11.5 months ago (  :smitten: love it here ).   

Southridge was appealing on price but just struck me as restrictive and inaccessible.  I'm in my 20s and don't want an inflexible housing arrangement, I mean yes, I may be here 30+ years but I also may not be and I don't want to lose everything I put into this if life gives me an interesting relocation opportunity in 3 years.  I looked for a building that had reasonable subletting rules, very accessible to the subway and in the historic district (but not necessarily landmarked itself).  I tried to mitigate commitment phobia by finding a place that I wouldn't feel trapped in and would be appealing to the masses if I need to leave for a couple years or if I need to sell outright.

I think I found a good buy.  I paid 190k for a one bedroom, top floor, elevator, laundry, face the courtyard, subletting allowed after 2 years, 79th and 37th. Its not the biggest 1 bedroom out there but its big enough, I even have a roommate and we live in it like two studios with a shared kitchen/bath/foyer at the front.

I think it really depends on what you're looking for in a home and in the investment. My building is really laid back and chill... but we also have lingering sponsor issues so there really is always something... I suggest being on the board wherever you land.

Roosevelt Terrace was built as an original coop (not a conversion). Yes, we have extensive house rules. However, they are not unreasonable and unbearable. I have lived her for more than 20 years and have never been "fined". There are 435 apartments and having fully explained rules help everyone understand everything. While there have been fines for some, it's not extensive.

One think to understand, as a coop. everyone is impacted by all actions of the shareholders/residents. No there isn't anyone running around with a clip board or inspecting apartments on the spot. However, when you take into account the following you might understand:
 
Utilities are included in the maintenance. There are fees for certain extras - such as air conditioners. If a shareholder installs an air conditioner and doesn't inform the office (and therefore doesn't pay the extra monthly fee) then all the other shareholders are actually paying the electric bill for that shareholder. 

NOT REALLY FAIR  :coolsmiley:. So, the rules are for all and a way of making things fair and safe for all.

Hope that helps understand what might otherwise sound hard or even untenable.
