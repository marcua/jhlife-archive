I also relocated to JH about a year and a half ago. Below are my answers to your questions, but if your motivation for moving here is mostly for social reasons, there's nothing to stop you from patronizing the local bars (The Music Box and Friends Tavern are two good places to start) and restaurants and getting a taste for the neighborhood before you start searching for a new home.

1. Yes, I feel safe here even when I am walking from the 74th Street Subway at 3AM. However, as with every place else I've ever lived in NYC, when I am walking home at that hour I chose my route wisely and am always aware of my surroundings. There is always a police presence on Roosevelt Avenue at that hour, for example.

2. Yes, but this is not Chelsea and most of the locals are a little more reserved about showing it in public, at least from my observations.

3. This is a very multicultural neighborhood. (Your opportunities double if you speak Spanish fluently... LOL.) I don't think it would be any more difficult to make friends here than in Forest Hills. And if you move into one of the many coops, you'll certainly get to know all your neighbors at the board meetings.

Good luck and happy hunting!

I've lived here for about 5 years and am a gay man in my late 20s...I like the area, though sometimes wonder what it's like to live in a more youthful area like Astoria. Now that I have a bike, I'm liking the area even more for its convenience... I can easily bike to Target, Home Depot, Bed Bath & Beyond, Flushing or to my friends house in Astoria. To answer some of your questions:

1) I always come home around 3-5am from my gay barhopping in Manhattan, and walk 12 blocks from the 74th street station (I live closer to the 82nd street 7 stop but it's not worth taking the 7 most nights). Like mentioned, the area is full of cops, so I walk all the way down Roosevelt amongst the drunk locals who filter out looking for late night eats around then. I've never felt unsafe, though I'm always aware of my surroundings. 

2) I notice a lot of gays in their 30s in the area, and some younger, and the area is full of gay Latins but it's not so obvious that the area is very gay friendly. It's very diverse and welcoming overall though, I never feel out of place in Jackson Heights.

3) I've never met friends in the area, but I wasn't really looking for them! It's funny how in NYC, people don't really base their location by where their friends live, everyone is so individual in their home process and then they just hang out in the city together.

I will say it's a very unique area, and whenever I host friends (or one night stands ) I always show them around to unique places and they get a slice of something they can't find anywhere else. I do wish more friends lived nearby, but at the same time, I like my privacy! And Queens pride is a lot of fun, def. check it out if you haven't already, it always marks the start of the summer for me.

We moved in February, and are very happy with our choice. There are a lot of gay people living here, but not much of a 'scene' other than the Roosevelt Avenue latin gay bars. We're not single or out looking - so we like that there are a few local bars if we just want to have a drink locally, and it's so convenient to Manhattan if we want to have a night out.

Also, the Queens Pride committee hosts a monthly happy-hour at a local (not exclusively gay) bar and we've met lots of our neighbors that way: http://queenspride.com/events.php.

I've never felt that there's been any safety issues here, nor have we ever felt unwelcome because we're gay - gay people are just part of the mix, and not really a big deal either way in this neighborhood.

Marcus beat me to my comment about the mixers

I've lived in JH my whole life but only rediscovered is as a gay community in my late twenties when I grew tired of the Manhattan scene. I was very happy to find a diverse group of people many of whom have become close friends. The neighborhood often gets pigeon holed in to being a Gay Latin area, and while there is a strong and very visible Latin presence in JH, it's not the only LGBTQ community that calls the area home.

As with any community you have to do a little work and invest a little involvement to get anything back. Go out to the bars, come to the Pride Committee Mixers, volunteer for the Queens Pride Parade and Festival (which takes place in JH on Sunday June 7th), stop in for a meeting of the Lesbian and Gay Democratic Club of Queens (also based in JH), or go to a workshop at Queens Pride House (right on 37th Ave.) There's something for just about everyone in JH and there's a million and one other tings to get involved with that aren't GLBTQ focused as well.

I think it's always easier, generally speaking, to meet people around a common activity.  So, if you're into local politics you could meet people through that (for example).  Or maybe if you're into gardening you try to buy into a coop with a private garden (there are many of these) where there is a garden committee.

If you go shopping for a new coop apt in JH, spring/summer is probably a good time to at least start going to open houses because you might be able to get a sense of the building's personality better if people are outside enjoying the gardens.

There are other neighborhoods in Queens that I like - such as Sunnyside, but I when I come back to JH I always enjoy the sense of activity on 37th Ave that seems to be missing in quieter neighborhoods like Sunnyside.  I am not such a huge fan of Roosevelt Ave because of the noise from the train, but I walk there sometimes for a change of pace and to learn a few new words in Spanish.
