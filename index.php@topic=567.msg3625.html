Hi everyone. Thought I'd chime in here. It's flattering that the map has sparked so many posts. Thanks to everyone who enjoyed the map. And the rest, you're certainly entitled to your opinion, though you may not realize that a casually thought out and hastily typed sentence can actually be hurtful to someone's feelings.

I was asked by the developer to do a cartoon map of the neighborhood. I did all the research myself. I posted to the parent's group message board and asked for recommendations, which boiled down the most unique and attractive (and yes, marketable) items into a somewhat exclusive list. This list is basically my own too. If I were to tell friends about how great the neighborhood is, which I do frequently, these are most of the things I'd want to include. They're the things that make my life here feel full. I'm not a big chowhound. I actually can't eat at most of the restaurants listed on the map because of food allergies. Still I tried to do the best research I could online and talking to neighbors. I think of it as a big love letter to JH, which has been my home (and wife and 2 young kids) for almost 6 years now. We have no intention of leaving. I knew that there'd be some people who would have strong reactions to what was and was not included for political reasons or otherwise. I really did try my best to give a snapshot of the incredible mix of people living here, a big reason I'm choosing to raise my kids here. If some of you think I failed at that, like I said, you're entitled to you opinion. Why do message boards bring out that persnickety, indignant tone in everyone, though?

As far as what I wanted to include which got cut... I had a box for local characters, including the "non-smoking guy," the whistling lady and the guy who rides around with his poodle. I also had a comic strip on the back about gentrification. 3 people sitting in the cafe arguing in circles. Too controversial for the marketing folks. I basically agree with everything Todd wrote about that topic so won't add anything here.

What can I say? This was one of the best commissioned jobs I've ever gotten. My sensibility was respected. I was paid well to research, write about and illustrate the neighborhood I love. I'm proud of what I pulled off.

By the way, the copyright remains with me, so once the Residences at JH marketing campaign is over I may do a 2009 version of the map. I'll consider some of the suggestions I've read  and will try to pack in more juicy details. The marketing folks over there wanted the list to be on the spare side (I was pushing it at 42 entries).

There. I've said my piece.

David,

I like the map ! It's great and I think does the best job it can and including as much as it can given its size and the particularities of your client. I'm also happy any time I see an artist get paid to do what he or she does well, and so much the better that you actually live in the neighborhood !

When I saw the map at 77 I initially thought it was a neighborhood map cooperatively produced by local merchants as a tool to stimulate business (perhaps this is a good idea for a future version of the map which I hope you create) it took a minute for me to realize that it was actually an advertisement for "The Residences At Jackson Heights". I guess I was a little disappointed when I realized I was not really part of the intended audience for this map, which then plays off feelings of being pushed / priced out of my own neighborhood by people who never gave JH a second thought before. Like many other residents and people who love Jackson Heights I grow concerned when developers move into a neighborhood and attempt to sell it off or change its image to appeal to a population that has no real connection to the area. Perhaps this may explain the reactions you've been seeing to the map. I do think the negative reaction is being misplaced, it should really be directed towards developers not artists.

It's interesting to note that "The Residences" otherwise known as Sheila Terrace are on 73rd st. between 37th ave. and 73rd Rd. perhaps one of the most congested, polluted, and certainly unpleasant streets in the whole neighborhood. All the marketing material I've seen for the place seems to focus on every aspect but the actual location of the buildings.  Marketing fascinates me

I just read Chuckster's earlier post and I agree with him. The map is basically a lie. It's like a big fat piece of fishing bait stuck on a hook to lure yuppies to Jackson Heights who will then start complaining about the 99 cent stores and eventually drive out lower income people through gentrification. "Oh no," readers will say. "Jackson Heights is too diverse to ever lose its multi-cultural flavor." But look at what happened to Columbus Avenue in Manhattan or Smith Street in Brooklyn where yuppies reign supreme to the exclusion of anyone else. The cute maps, real estate website and advertising posters like those at the Roosevelt Avenue subway station that show cartoon yuppies with inflated heads basically misrepresent our neighborhood, promising residential elegance and a environment filled with the kind of predictable signposts that say "we're building YOUR kind of neighborhood." Face it, most of the yuppie crowd that already came here from Park Slope and Williamsburg did so for primarily one reason: large, affordable housing. When they got here and began having to interact with the neighborhood, they suddenly began wanting to turn Jackson Heights into a mirror of where they had come from. Let's not be naive. While seemingly innocent, the map is another step towards eventually losing the dynamism of a neighborhood defined by minority cultures who need 99 cent stores to survive.
