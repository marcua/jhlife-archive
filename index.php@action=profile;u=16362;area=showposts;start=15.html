« on: December 17, 2013, 10:41:52 AM »
As outrageous as this all seems, and as bad as I feel for the workers and their families, this is actually pretty standard practice across retail. The thinking behind it is that if workers are notified in advance that they will be losing their jobs, they will loot the store for goods and cash before departing. It is crappy that they were given no severance and/or a deal was not worked out with the new owner to keep them on, but the lack of notification is a reality of the industry.

Neighborhood Chat / Re: 78-23 37th Ave previously NOVO?
« on: July 31, 2013, 05:30:07 PM »
I'm kind of in agreement with the folks that are saying that we, as a neighborhood, wouldn't quite be able to support a bistro, and throwing Starbucks into the conversation is comparing apples and oranges. As someone said in a previous post, Starbucks is a place that people will go to and read, work, stay a while; a brunch place or bistro wouldn't allow that, and would require a packed house and fast turn-over every night and weekend in order to make rent, let alone profit. Juju's, and even Alchemy, are the more apt comparisons for the kind of business a bistro would drum up, and these places aren't exactly bursting at the seams with patrons; luckily for them, they're not paying 37 Ave rents, and seem to be making do.

If you're longing for some different options/American food, I encourage you to try a burger at Cassidy's up on 31st Ave. It's a bit of a walk, but the burger is solid, and the regulars, while usually over-served, are friendly and entertaining. And if you want a Starbucks alternative, but would like to stay put longer than the E77 time limit, venture up to Coffeed on Northern Blvd. It's a couple miles (a good walk in this lovely fall weather we've been having), or a few stops on the Q66 bus or M/R (to 36). The coffee is fantastic, the food is good, and you can linger as long as you like. Oh, and the Brooklyn Grange farm is on the roof of the building, so you can go up and check that out too! It's pretty awesome.

I've done a little bit of research on Starbucks's leasing practices because, honestly, I found it hard to believe that a Landlord would give them this kind of power. As it turns out, they are being sued in a class-action suit in Seattle for this and other practices they utilize to wipe out competition. You can read about it here:
http://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/starbucks-accused-of-abusing-monopoly-power-in-violation-of-sherman-act-57142892.html

They have also fallen victim to the same practices used by McDonald's, which, for all we know, is where they got the idea. See details here:
http://articles.chicagotribune.com/2006-04-22/news/0604220176_1_lease-retailers-mcdonald

So, after all that, it is certainly plausible that they have a lease that restricts competition, and that competition is very broadly defined in their lease. Such a bummer for our neighborhood.

One final note (and possibly glimmer of hope)... I also did some digging around public records of some city agencies (Buildings and Finance, to be exact), and it appears that while this particular landlord owns this particular block, they don't appear to be affiliated with any other property in the area. That's not to say they're not operating under multiple names, but I would think that Starbucks's stranglehold is limited to buildings with the same lease ownership as their own, and is therefore limited to their current block. Here's to hoping, anyway.

Neighborhood Chat / Re: Carroll Gardens Blogger Gives up Her Neighborhood for…Jackson Heights, Queens
« on: July 25, 2013, 09:44:05 PM »
I do worry a little about the gentrification effect though. On the one hand, this is an established neighborhood that is largely owner-occupied. Gentrification tends to begin with rental prices and then spill over to selling prices, but Jackson Heights lacks and abundance of rentals (for a neighborhood so dense, anyway), and is therefore missing a somewhat critical stepping stone. The lack of certain amenities (a gym, for example) and retail diversity could further stall gentrification, maybe even squash it all together.

On the other hand, depending on how much press the neighborhood gets between her blog, brownstoner, and any other outlets that might jump on the bandwagon, there's potential for some opportunistic owners to put up a listing with a way-too-high asking price and see if they get any nibbles. And if they do, that might just set the tone for the next few years of real estate in Jackson Heights. And in that case, yes, people will be priced out of the neighborhood, specifically renters looking to buy and owners looking to upgrade. I'm in the former category, and my husband and I are hoping to buy two years from now. And right now I'm a little nervous.

Neighborhood Chat / Re: GNC on 37th
« on: July 15, 2013, 08:58:16 PM »
I don't think that's how right of first refusal works... Typically, when that is built into a contract, the owner is required to offer the lease proposal of the vacant space to the rights holder (in this case, Starbucks) prior to offering it to any other potential tenant; if Starbucks refuses the space, the landlord can do business with whomever he/she wants. Starbucks would not have a say unless they rented the space themselves.

They may have some co-tenancy/adjacency restrictions built into their lease, but having that kind of control worked into a lease is almost unheard of, and typically would need to be very specific (e.g. Dunkin Donuts, Panera Bread shall not be within 1/4 mile of premises).
