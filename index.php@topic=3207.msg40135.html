ideas about keeping co-ops bedbug-free
« on: August 22, 2009, 12:29:09 AM »
There are some new articles in the NY Times about real estate and bedbugs, including deals that are dead in the water when buyers do due diligence and find that there have been bedbugs in the building. 

Disgusting, Yes. But a Dealbreaker?
Buying and Selling in Bedbug City
Debugging a New Home

My question to the JHL Forum is:
How can we make sure our co-ops (for those of us who are owners) remain bedbug-free?  Or if they cannot remain bedbug-free due to widespread exposure to bedbugs everwhere from public transportation to schools to hospitals, then how can we ensure that our investment in our homes is protected?

Getting bedbugs is one nightmare.  Not being able to sell your home, or having your home's value drop because of bedbugs in the building, is another.

I'm going to think more about this, but my initial proposal would be that each co-op should have two owners who volunteer to educate themselves thoroughly about bedbugs by reading the resource sections on sites like Bedbug Beware, Bedbugger and New York vs Bed Bugs

Those people are then the building's advocates/educators on this issue.  They should get or find an accurate fact sheet and work with the board or the managing agent to set up a seminar for the residents where the fact sheets can be distributed.  Residents should be educated about how to do regular inspections and should perhaps be encouraged to buy the interceptors for beds such as http://www.insect-interceptor.com/ that would be an early warning, much along the same lines as a smoke detector.

Also, the co-op needs to make sure their pest control company is knowledgeable about bedbugs and that there is a plan in place including mandatory inspections in all adjoinging apartments (above, below, sides, diagonals) when bedbugs are discovered in one apartment.

Any other thoughts?

The link below is a terrific guide on the Dept of health's website.   I learned ways to make my apartment a bit safer.  There are still far too many people unfamiliar with how to spot and remove bed bugs.  If the different agencies are all saying this summer will be bad regarding bed bugs, then people need to know how to tell if they have them and then what steps to take to quickly rid them. 

I've been educating my Co-op residents for weeks now ever since we discovered them in our building.  Bed bugs are in many buildings in NY, never mind Jackson Heights,  so the best defense after hiring a Pest Control Company is to be educated about them. 

They are disgusting, but they are BUGS!  Not aliens from Mars.  The more aware people are, the quicker they take action.  The quicker the action, the more likely to rid them.   Although like roaches and mice, they'll always be part of the landscape.

So after a lot of time, energy and expense we got our bedbugs under control.  The good thing is that now most of the residents of the co-op have taken steps to detect bedbugs quicker from using the climb ups to covering their mattress/box springs with the protective encasements and many of them vacuumed every day with a bit of talc powder which is a great way to remove/kill bugs or eggs.

It's not easy because of the amount of time and energy you have to expend dealing with them but hiring a pest mgmt company that is experienced in the matter helped a lot.  When I hear someone say "oh we thought we had bed bugs but the Super came and checked and said we don't", I wanna cry for their naivete.  

Anyone on the Board of their Co-op should get the free copies, in English and Spanish, from the Dept of Health on bed bugs.  They're very well done.
