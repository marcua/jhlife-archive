Neighborhood Chat / Himalayan culture in JH
« on: December 06, 2007, 01:14:44 PM »
Although overshadowed by other cultures from the Subcontinent, a small but growing community of people from Nepal, Bhutan, Tibet, and northern India is taking hold in Jackson Heights.  Himalayan Yak is a wonderful restaurant, and recently has been joined by the popular Shangrila Express food cart.

Outside Magazine recently ran an article profiling former Himalayan Sherpas now working as cab drivers in NYC. 
It's a fascinating read.

As the blog Intelligent Travel points out, the article has this fascinating tidbit on Jackson Heights:

Quote
...If the center of worldwide Sherpa culture is the Solu Khumbu, in America it's Jackson Heights, the polyglot epicenter of Queens. At the corner of 37th Road and 74th Street, groups of women walk by in traditional Bhutanese robes, shopping bags from Bed Bath & Beyond in hand. Himalaya Video, at the corner of Broadway and 72nd, is the place to pick up a commemorative Everest T-shirt or the latest album by rapper Nurbu Sherpa, who recently moved to New York as well. "Representin' K.T.M.C." (short for Kathmandu City) is his big seller. Or how about a bootleg of Nhyu Bajracharya's single "Ma Sherpa Ko Chhoro," remixed with the sounds of an avalanche and grunting yaks?

Neighborhood Chat / Honorary Street Names
« on: December 01, 2007, 01:41:38 AM »
Several streets in Jackson Heights have received honorary names, a practice that is common throughout NYC.  I often wonder who these people are.  If you know about any of them, please tell us here!


Here's an article I found about Kalpana Chawla Way (74th Street, between 37rd Road and 37th Avenue)....


http://news.bbc.co.uk/2/hi/south_asia/3889605.stm

BBC News
Tuesday, 13 July, 2004, 12:31 GMT 13:31 UK

New York honours Indian astronaut
By Salim Rizvi

Almost one and a half years after the tragic disaster of the Nasa space shuttle Columbia, one of its crew, the late Indian-American astronaut Kalpana Chawla has been honoured by the New York City administration.

74th Street in the Jackson Heights area of the city has now been renamed 74 Street Kalpana Chawla Way.

Kalpana Chawla was one of the seven astronauts killed when the Columbia shuttle disintegrated in mid-air on 1 February, 2003.

The Mayor of New York City, Michael Bloomberg, officially unveiled the Kalpana Chawla Way on Sunday at a simple but well attended ceremony including hundreds of people from India, Pakistan and Bangladesh.

'Sacrificed her life'

The Jackson Heights area, also known as Little India, has a strong concentration of people of South Asian origin who strongly favoured the move to honour Chawla.

The community has been trying for some time to get the street named after her.

The president of Jackson Heights Merchants' Association, Shiv Dass, who is of Indian origin, says that his organisation has been trying to get the street name changed since the death of Kalpana Chawla last year.

They approached City Councilwoman Helen Sears, a Democrat, who got a bill passed to this effect.

"We are so proud to have the street named after our hero Kalpana Chawla, who sacrificed her life for this country. All of us from South Asia - Indians, Pakistanis, and Bangladeshis - we are all so happy to honour one of our own," Mr Dass said.

This Jackson Heights area also serves as a major shopping centre for the South Asian community in New York and surrounding areas.

It is a hub of commercial activity and also a major centre for regional cuisines from South Asia.

Pioneering spirit

Kalpana Chawla was a naturalised American citizen who lived in the Houston suburb of Texas state, about 10 miles (16km) from Johnson Space Center.

She was born in the town of Karnal in the northern Indian state of Haryana, 75 miles (120km) from Delhi.

After moving to the United States, she earned a master's degree and a doctorate in aeronautical engineering, and became a US citizen.

In 1994, she was selected by Nasa for a rigorous one-year training programme to serve as a mission specialist on board shuttle missions.

She said that that her inspiration to take up flying was JRD Tata, who flew the first mail flights in India.

Before and after her death, Kalpana Chawla's fan base was not just restricted to the South Asian community of the United States.

People from all walks of life remember the astronaut's warm smile and pioneering spirit.

A life that ended so abruptly will now be remembered for generations to come every time they visit Jackson Heights.

Quote
A chavurah or havurah (Hebrew: "fellowship", plural chavuroth) is a small group of like-minded Jews who assemble for the purposes of facilitating Shabbat and holiday prayer services, sharing communal experiences such as lifecycle events, and Jewish learning. Chavuroth usually provide autonomous alternatives to established Jewish institutions and Jewish denominations.

Most chavurot place an emphasis on egalitarianism in the broad sense (of which gender egalitarianism is one piece), depending on participation by (and welcoming of) the entire community rather than top-down direction by clergy.

Neighborhood Chat / PlaNYC, Congestion Pricing, Parking Permits
« on: November 30, 2007, 11:14:26 AM »
Mayor Bloomberg has proposed a broad array of policies to help NYC accommodate the projected 9,000,000 residents by 2030 while also improving environmental sustainability and quality of life.

See the PlaNYC website for details of these proposals.

The most controversial element in the plan is the proposal to charge vehicles a congestion toll in order to enter the Manhattan core -- or drive around within it -- between the hours of 6 am and 6 pm Monday through Friday.  Under the initial proposal, the toll would be set at $8 for cars and $21 for trucks, with lower rates for traffic that doesn't enter or leave the zone.  There are a lot of complex quirks: other bridge tolls would be reimbursable, certain classes of vehicles (including taxis) would be free, and the tolls would only apply if vehicles actually drive on Manhattan streets.  In any case, a commission is currently studying the proposal and is due to announce its recommendations in early 2008.

In the meantime, the city has published a "Request for Expressions of Interest" (RFEI), in which firms that may potentially bid on the contract to operate the Congestion Pricing Plan explain their overall options and proposals for how to make it work.  The NYC Economic Development Commission has published online 21 of the 30 responses it received to the RFEI.  These make fascinating reading if you're interested in the technical details of this stuff, and want to learn how other cities have made these types of systems work for them.  The documents are available for free at the EDC website, but first you need to jump through some hoops to get access to them (e.g. you need a non-Yahoo or Gmail email account).

Neighborhood Chat / NJ dealers park cars in boro
« on: November 29, 2007, 02:46:52 PM »
http://www.timesledger.com/site/news.cfm?newsid=19068860&BRD=2676&PAG=461

TimesLedger.com
NJ dealers park cars in boro
By Jeremy Walsh
11/29/2007

The next time you see a car for sale parked on a residential street in Jackson Heights or East Elmhurst, think twice about where it might have come from.

Police from the 115th Precinct said car dealerships in New Jersey have been parking their wares curbside and listing them under phone numbers with 718 area codes.

Out-of-state car dealers are forbidden from selling vehicles in New York. But Officer Edward Sawtell, of the precinct's traffic division, said in the past month police have towed 20 cars marked for sale by six dealers from New Jersey. He said police have convinced four of the six not to come back.

The dealers park the vehicles in areas of high pedestrian traffic. Unfortunately, that often equates to areas of high congestion, such as 82nd Street and Astoria Boulevard, 94th Street and 24th Avenue, 82nd Street and 35th Avenue, Junction Boulevard and 34th Avenue, 108th Street and 35th Avenue and on Northern Boulevard between 80th and 89th streets.

"Junction and 34th Avenue is a parking lot for Delaware [plates]," Sawtell said.

Local residents looking for parking places share the hassles of this arrangement with prospective car buyers. Sawtell said the dealers also advertise their vehicles in New Jersey newspapers. If a person agrees to buy the vehicle, he said, the dealer hands the buyer the keys and tells him the vehicle is in Jackson Heights.

Sawtell said the cars often are not insured and can incur any number of summonses due to parking violations or other issues- - all of which the buyer must pay before he can register the vehicle.

"It's buyer beware," he said. "If you buy a car, check the VIN."

Will Sweeney, co-founder of the anti-congestion civic group Western Jackson Heights Alliance, said he was glad the local precinct knew about the problem.

"It's good to know that they're going one step further and finding out who owns these cars," he said, noting one in four cars he passes during his morning exercise sport out-of-state license plates.

But Sweeney said the cause of the problem is the lack of parking regulation in an area that has one of the lowest proportions of vehicle ownership anywhere in Queens. Census data from the year 2000 showed 38 percent of Jackson Heights residents owned cars, compared to 62 percent boroughwide.

"We should have some sort of way to put a value on-street parking," he said, suggesting an experimental residential parking permit program. "There are people who just come and park their cars. It really becomes just car storage.
