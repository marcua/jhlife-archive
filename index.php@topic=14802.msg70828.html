An idea I've heard before is for the city to give some kind of financial incentive for businesses to allow for staggered starting times. Rush hour is horrendous because everyone working a 9-5 job is commuting at the same time, where 60 years ago New York workers were spread out more over three shifts (and we had a smaller population). Many businesses could operate just fine if they allowed desk jockeys some flexibility to work 8-4, 10-6, 11-7, or work from home a day or two a week.

I cannot imagine most NYC employers agreeing with you because people conduct business during standard business hours, i.e., 9-5. Moreover, a lot of people who work from home are not as productive at home as compared to when they at the office.  And, what kind of financial incentives should the government provide private sector employers to encourage them to stagger their employees'  work hours?  Public money? I do not think that many tax payers (those who actually pay into the system) would want their tax dollars being used to pay private employers to stagger their employees' schedules so that the poor workers are not too uncomfortable when commuting to and from work.

Telecommuting is common place enough it shouldn't cause this kind of confusion. Take this Gallup poll from last year, which shows that 37% of U.S. workers have telecommuted. http://www.gallup.com/poll/184649/telecommuting-work-climbs.aspx IT also shows that the majority of people surveyed believed telecommuters were just as productive as those who work in an office. And the entire point of telecommuting is that the job the person holds *can be done remotely during standard business hours, 9-5* No one suggests that bank tellers should be able to telecommute. Or nurses. Or receptionists. But plenty of white collar jobs require nothing more than a laptop, high speed internet, and a phone, to be done at home. There is a significant financial incentive as well for businesses in reduced office space requirements.

As for staggered start times, I am puzzled that you seem to believe so many jobs are customer facing. Plenty of workers can easily do their work in an office between 10-6, 11-7 or 8-4, especially white collar workers. Certainly business gets done between 9-5, yet somehow workers on the west coast who need to deal with colleagues and clients on the east coast somehow manage to get it done. Ditto for us on the east coast who have to wait until after lunch to connect with customers and clients on the west coast.

While I absolutely agree that not everyone is suited to work from home, largely I find this fear of non-productivity stems from lazy managers who don't know how to measure results, and instead would prefer to manage by counting hours an employees butt is spent in a chair.

You also don't seem to understand the societal benefit of what I've suggested. This isn't about "the poor workers not being too uncomfortable  when commuting to and from work." The suggestion is about making mass transit better for *everyone.* Do you see the difference?
