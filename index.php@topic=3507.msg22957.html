$230K for a one bedroom would be a very reasonable price for a one bedroom in many buildings.  However, there are way too many factors to say whether this is a good price.  Each building varies in terms of the prices it's able to command.  Every coop is a corporation with a certain degree of financial health (reserve funds, etc.) or un-health.  That's one factor.  Other factors could be flip taxes, how high are maintenance charges, and how recently was the unit renovated.

I don't think the first floor would have much to do with affecting the purchase price, as one thing I learned during my hunt is that some people find the first floor desirable because it means they will not have to use the elevator or stairs.

If you can find out what apartments in this building have been going for over the past year, that should help you somewhat.  Maybe the owner isn't budging because he/she knows what the apartment should be worth based on other sales in the building.  Also, if you haven't looked at many other places, maybe now is the time to be doing so to see what else is out there. If you think first floor would be annoying and that the apartment is over-priced, I am not sure why you don't just move on.

And I am curious about why you think the market for coops in this neighborhood has gone down so much.  It doesn't seem that way to me (based on observation) but then again, I am not in the real estate business.

If you haven't already done so, sign up for a free account on propertyshark.com.  The free accounts used to give you access to looking at 6 addresses per day, and you could then see the last several sale prices in a building.  I'm not sure if this is still the case. What this doesn't tell you is how large the apartment is or what kind of shape it's in.

Also look at lots of listings (local realtors) and try to go see a few more apartments.  The fact that you are out looking at other places may give you more negotiating power and I think you would also find that it will give you more information.

Hi,

I haven't read all the posts, but I can share some information that I have experienced:

1.  Some boards are not allowing seller's to go below a certain number, even though the market has changed.
 
2.  Some seller's will allow a seller's concession of a few thousand dollars.  Meaning, you can go ahead and sell/buy a unit at 230K, and at closing the seller will return a negotiated amount back to the buyer.  But the reflected sale price will show as $230K.

3. I do find it interesting that the asking price is $230K and the accepted offer is to be $230K.  Usually a seller will have the unit on the market for $245K, leaving some room for negotiation.
