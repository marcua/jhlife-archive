Cassi Alexandra for The New York TimesA woman walks her dog near a sign created by the Metropolitan
Etiquette Authority, on Ludlow Street. Jason Shelowitz has made it his mission to educate New
Yorkers on proper urban decorum. All he had to do wasbreak the law. Seventy times.
Beginning last month, Mr. Shelowitz, 31, a graphic designer and artist, has posted street signs across the city under theguise of the Metropolitan Etiquette Authority, a one-mancollective whose logo, printed on the bottom of each sign, borrows heavily from that of the Metropolitan Transportation Authority.
So far, about 70 signs, which cost Mr. Shelowitz $25 each to order, have been affixed onto
street-side posts in Manhattan and Brooklyn, in available slots beneath existing signage.
They convey four messages on city etiquette.
One encourages smokers not to litter. Another requests that walkers pay attention while
crossing the street. A third, Mr. Shelowitzs favorite, calls for police officers to pick up
after their horses. The last, which has become something of a viral sensation on
Facebook, reads simply: Pull Up Your Pants. No One Wants to See Your Underwear.
Next to the text is an image of an offending traveler, with a line through his semi-exposed
backside.Mr. Shelowitz said he planned to post additional signs in Queens and the Bronx planned
in the coming weeks. About half of the signs installed so far have been stolen by curious
passers-by, he estimated.
I tapped into something here, Mr. Shelowitz said in his West Village apartment last
month. People like it. If Larry David were a graphic designer, hed be me.
Perhaps. But Mr. Shelowitzs suggestions carry an added audacity: His signs, like the 250
posters on subway etiquette he installed in stations last year, are displayed illegally.
Theyre not bothering anybody, Mr. Shelowitz said. I put them up in front of police. If I
was covering stop signs or something, that would be a problem.

According to city officials, the signs may be a problem anyway, though not one they seem
particularly equipped to solve. A spokesman for the Department of Transportation said
that any unauthorized signs would be removed if discovered during scheduled
maintenance checks. But of course, the agency does not know the precise addresses of
Mr. Shelowitzs signs. (The Metropolitan Transportation Authority declined to comment
on the use of a logo similar to its on the signs.)
Mr. Shelowitzs ability to evade the authorities owes largely to his presentation. The signs
appear, at a glance, like any others in the city, with text written in black and red, against a
drab white backdrop, and people rendered in silhouette. Perhaps more important, Mr.
Shelowitz also dresses the part of a city employee or, rather, an artists in-the-flesh
imagining of one during the installations.
Hauling a white stepladder across Harlem one evening last month, Mr. Shelowitz wore an
orange construction vest and a tool belt for his supplies as he screwed in six signs along
125th Street.
The more conspicuous you are, he explained, the less conspicuous you are.
Each sign takes about a minute to install. Small crowds occasionally gather to watch Mr.
Shelowitz place the signs, though few observers have approached him during his routine:
open up the stepladder, insert the screws, bend them back with pliers, snap a picture.
And get lost.
Mr. Shelowitz said he tried to match the message of a sign to its location. For busy
thoroughfares, his statement on walker awareness Pay Attention While Walking. Your
Facebook Status Update Can Wait. finds particular resonance, he said. Areas with a
heavy police presence, like Times Square, are often best for his sign about horse
droppings.
Im doing community service here, he said, before fastening his sign about cigarette
butts onto a post outside the Apollo Theater. The proprietor of a clothing shop next door
walked over to inspect the blocks new addition. He grimaced, then returned indoors.
Hes going to love that, Mr. Shelowitz said.
