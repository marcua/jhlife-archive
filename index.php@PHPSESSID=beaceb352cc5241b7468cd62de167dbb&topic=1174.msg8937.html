I do not dispute that there are different social norms in other countries, some of which lead to cleaner streets than ours, and some of which lead to dirtier streets than ours.  However, I make the distinctly American demand that we treat all of our neighbors equally -- and not have special exceptions, explanations, or excuses for antisocial behavior within our own neighborhood based on ethnicity.

Can we have a free, diverse society and clean streets?  It's certainly a lot harder here than in countries where there is little diversity, or in countries where there is little liberty.  The only solution as I see it is to put in the hard work as a community to create a new social norm.  That means activating all of the various components of the community to participate in keeping it clean, including businesses, residents, enforcement agencies, community groups, etc.  It requires putting in the time to meet with people face to face, and to communicate your seriousness of purpose while demonstrating that you respect the people you're meeting with.  It really is hard work.  And that it why I have tremendous admiration for our emerging community leaders who are putting in the time and effort.  But every time somebody starts pointing at the supposed dirty habits of one or another ethnic group, or the neglectful behavior of one particular segment of the community, it makes it all that much harder to build the trust and working relationships they need to get this problem solved.  People are forced into a defensive position, and the litter once again becomes somebody else's problem rather than a shared problem.

I think the 73rd & 74th Street situation can change rapidly when we have elected officials willing to provide leadership.  Right now, neither our community board not our city councilwoman is willing to confront the issue.  Part of the reason they find it so difficult is that the issue is being framed in terms of race and ethnicity, which makes it risky and infertile terrain for politicians to invest time and effort.  I am optimistic that our current crop of candidates for City Council has a better understanding of how to address these issues, and a greater willingness to invest political capital in addressing them once in office.  We need to hold their feet to the fire, but we also need to help them by developing ideas and communication channels.

So, please, once again, can we discuss what can be done to begin addressing this problem?  'Cause I'm really sick of hearing people whine about the dirty foreigners.

having caught up on this thread finally, one thing  I've noticed is that we're getting race and culture confused. There's a big difference between the two. An Inuit raised in Japan form an early age will very likely behave culturally like a Japanese person as opposed to what his / her race might otherwise dictate. Understanding the various cultures that exist in our neighborhood is the first step to solving the problem of litter in the street. Once we know why people behave in certain way we can better address, communicate, and educate.

But, it's a lot harder to educate the masses then it is to deal with the commercial establishments on 73rd and 74th st. (there's certainly fewer businesses then there are people). Another complication is I'm reasonably certain most of the people who patronize these businesses especially on the weekends do not live in or even near Jackson Heights (as evidenced by the license plates from NJ, PA ,CT). So a lot of these people don't see the impact of their dropping a coffee cup here or there etc. as they don't have to walk that way to work on Monday morning.

82nd St. while busy and certainly not the perfect picture of cleanliness is still better off than 73rd and 74th streets partially because many of the people who use that street live in the area. Also, some years back 82nd St. was redesigned to allow for increased foot traffic, has more garbage cans, and the 82nd St. BID helps to keep the St. clean. I've also noticed a higher police presence on the street. Why the difference ? My theory, the merchants on 82nd St. realized that they would lose local business if they didn't clean up their act, The merchants on 74th st. are not as affected by members of the local community since most of their business comes form outside the community.   

Solutions ?

Some coordinated effort on the part of the JH Community as whole that reaches out to the Jackson Heights Merchants Associationhttps://a069-webapps3.nyc.gov/dsial/Agency_Detail.aspx?id=216, and the Bangladeshi Merchants Association (Sorry no URL), with petitions, letters, meetings and constant conversations asking these groups to take better care of their streets.

Step up and continue calls to 311 to report litter, vandalism, broken laws, etc. to the appropriate departments.

Constant communication with local elected officials highlighting these problems.

Scrutinize our local electeds and those running for office on their associations, donations and endorsements from groups or orgs. that do not contribute to the JH community in a positive way.

And as a last resort, boycott businesses that do not care about the cleanliness of their streets. This may be a challenge as I mentioned above most of the patrons come from outside JH, but with effective education and a visible protest maybe it might help.

In the end it comes down to money, the merchants on 73rd and 74th st. are not going to make any more money if their streets are clean, in fact they will lose money (as far as they are concerned) by investing in a BID.  But if their political power can be lessened, and they wind up getting more fines from Sanitation, etc. they might soon decide it's cheaper to invest in a BID.

That's my two cents.
