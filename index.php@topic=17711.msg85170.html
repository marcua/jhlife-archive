New report calls Jackson Heights a "food swamp"
« on: April 11, 2018, 01:35:50 PM »
Three Queens neighborhoods are being called “food swamps” because they’re rife with places selling unhealthy food sources, according to a report released on Wednesday.

Public Health Solutions, the largest public health nonprofit serving New York City, found that the large volume of food swamps in the borough resulted from the number of unhealthy food outlets outnumbering healthy alternatives. This means certain communities have limited access to healthy food choices, particularly in Jackson Heights, Corona and Jamaica.

From: http://qns.com/story/2018/04/11/report-points-certain-queens-neighborhoods-food-swamps-cheap-unhealthy-meal-options/

This seems so bizarre to me. I think of JH as a neighborhood with a plethora of wonderful food options—healthy, unhealthy, etc. We have multiple stores selling nothing but fresh produce, multiple supermarkets (of varying quality), a weekly farmer's market, an organic market, and many restaurants serving cuisines (Indian, Tibetan, Japanese) that are often quite healthy (depending on what you order).

There's a link to the full report here:
https://www.healthsolutions.org/blog/queens-neighborhoods-street-mapping-project/

Very interesting. I have not read the article yet, but I tend to agree that the cuisines most prominent around JH are in fact not very healthy. Rich, heavy, spicy sauces, lots of rice and noodles, and for the South American cuisines, lots of fatty meats, and meals that come with french fries and hot dogs AND rice.

I have trouble ordering simple, light meals in these places, which is why I am one of the people urgently awaiting new options that serve lighter, simpler food. The heavier meals disagree with me in addition to being high calorie-carb-fat.

For example, The Arepa Lady, which is moving into the old City Coffee space. I have looked at their present menu, and I really don't see what I could eat there without gaining weight and raising my cholesterol. It's heavy on the cheese and carbs, light on the vegetables.

I wish some of these places would think about offering lighter options. Thai food has so much sugar in the sauces--it's very hard to find anything that fits into my diet at any of these places.  If anyone can suggest what I could order at some of these places, I'd really be interested! Yes, I know we have a couple of Japanese restaurants, but I haven't been too happy with the quality, and I don't like sushi. I like Saw Shack for Chinese, but if you order the food without sauce it is really dull.

I order from Just Made 4 U and Ricky's mostly--Ricky's does nice salad and mixed veggies, and their chicken dishes and roasted salmon are both very good. That's the sort of thing I am looking for: grilled/roasted chicken/fish, well cooked vegetables, etc, and no heavy sauces. Frustrating that these seem to be hard to find in a bit more upscale, eat-in atmosphere in JH.

There's vegetables every other block. I don't understand what this study -wants- to have seen.

Their finding is based on the ratio of unhealthy food spots to grocery / produce stores, not absolute numbers.  The concept is that being surrounded by unhealthy food influences the choices that one makes. For example, there are 3 different Dunkin Donuts and 2 Wendy's within 5 blocks of me, plus countless other non-chains and carts serving food laden with salt, fat, and sugar.

Why does this matter? Rates of obesity, heart disease, and Type II diabetes have skyrocketed in NYC in recent decades. The overall city statistics are skewed by Manhattan (where obesity rates are very low compared to US averages), but much higher in the outer boroughs.  You  might have noticed that the NYC DOHMH has launched several initiatives to get people to choose meals at chains with fewer calories and less salt (educational campaigns, menu labeling, etc). They have been blocked by the soda industry from taxes on sugary drinks.
