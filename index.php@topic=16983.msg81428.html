Greetings, JH Lifers.  Just in case you missed it, this website will be closing down on December 31st.  It's been a great journey, but it is time for this site to walk off into the sunset (or whatever lies on the other side of the BQE).   Please see our recent announcement for more information.

Hopefully this is just the beginning of a new chapter in efforts to build community online and in real life.  Come join your neighbors at r/jacksonheights or r/queens on Reddit, Jackson Heights 11372 on Facebook, and the venerable JHfamilies group on Google.

Well in terms of the new building having parking, I think it does. Let's not forget that the number of spaces needed will exponentially grow. Many families usually have more than one car.  People have visitors, stores have workers, customers and deliveries. Look at 74 and 73 street. A paucity of  parking means double parked cars obstructing the streets, stopping the flow of buses, increased noise (i.e. "beeping, obnoxious volumes on radios) and  pollution (i.e. idling cars). What I mean by "refurbish" is to restore houses to fit the function for which they were in intended for. This has to do with zoning.   I ride my bike on Roosevelt and the side streets in the 90's and yes,  there are many "decrepit" structures that have been abandoned and  bordered up.  These homes once had backyards and garages,  some of which have now been turned into commercial  spaces.  Yesterday I counted six barber shops on the same block (this is not including the ones that operate before and after this location). Really?   To me, displacement of a saturated market hardly underserves its' residents.  That's like being upset if  a pharmacy/chicken place closes when there are about 20 others within a one block radius to go to.  In terms of city services, it doesn't necessarily follow that we'll get more.  At times the neighborhood is filthy, with garbage cans overflowing.  Have pick ups  increased?
