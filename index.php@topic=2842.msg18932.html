With respect to crime statistics, while any crime that victimizes someone is terrible, in some categories the overall numbers are so small that one or two offenses can translate into a disproportionate increase in the percentages.  For instance, if a precinct had two murders in 2008, and it now has three in 2009, the stats would show a 50% increase in murders for that precinct. That's a tragedy for the victim, but it is not necessarily indicative of a crimewave.

Don't forget that the sheets merely report numbers of incidents, not crime rates (which are a function of incidents divided by population). Here's a great map of the city with different neighborhood crime rates: http://media.nymag.com/docs/08/01/080114crimemaps.pdf

That said, here's an interesting article that asks the same question (broader, though, expanding to all of Queens - why are Rape reports rising in Queens at the same time that they are falling elsewhere in the city, and as other crimes remain low).

Part of the theory is that rape rates aren't going up, but more rapes are being reported - especially in communities where there may be a cultural stigma against reporting rape. The FBI estimates that only 10% of rape cases are ever reported.  Because of the diverse mix of JH/EastElmhurst/Corona (our police district) it's likely that many women don't necessarily feel they can report sexual violence, especially if language and/or cultural barriers make the process seem intimidating. As different outreach organizations are more successful in helping women come forward and report sexual violence, reported rapes are rising. Many of these outreach organizations are new, such as the one at Elmhurst Hospital:

Quote
Elmhurst Hospitalâ€™s program, started in 2000, recently received a â€œcenter of excellenceâ€ designation by the New York State Department of Health. The center has seen a slight rise in case numbers over the last year, approximately eight to 10 cases a month, up from six to eight in previous years, many of which are acquaintance or date rapes, according to the director, Rebecca Carmen.
Full article: http://www.columbiajournalist.org/rw1_gissler/2005/article.asp?subj=city&course=rw1_gissler&id=583

So the short answer may be that incidents of rape aren't rising in this neighborhood, merely that more of them are being reported.
