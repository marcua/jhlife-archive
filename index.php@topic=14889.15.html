I've been riding my bike over to LIC for the past few weeks and WOW!  With that said, these developments benefit no one else but the well to do  and the developers, and create little if any housing for the working class.  LIC is nice bc it has the water front, park, and the "hip" factor but I wouldn't trade it for my place here in Jackson Heights. It kinda makes me angry when I see the park and all the monies that went into keepimg it as pristine as it is.  We've been clamoring for years for additional park space and  get a play street instead.  Despite this, Jackson Heights remains one of the few "real" neighborhoods left.  Such high rise construction leaves little to be desired especially if you want to know your neighbors.  You get a pricey view of the city but your not in it. There are hardly any places left that have our sense of community and that's something you can't put a price tag on.

Yeah, when I looked at the census reports I didn't see a jump of 1 million. It was 7,322,564 in 1990 and 8,175,133 in 2010, for a gain of nearly 853,000. I did read that the figures are accelerating; I saw an estimate of around 8.5 million, too. Though a chunk of that increase was due to death rates in the city not keeping up with birth rates. Also, the population took a big nosedive before 1990 (from 7,894,862 in 1970 to 7,071,639 in 1980). You'd think that would have helped make more apartments available, but I'm pretty sure the vacancy rate still stayed below one percent. (Maybe because so many apartment buildings were torched and otherwise trashed by their owners.) 

For what it's worth, even though there are a lot of people flooding into the city, there are also a lot of people leaving. In the past few years domestic migration (people moving away from NYC to other parts of the U.S.) has increased by more than 400,000, while international migration (people moving to NYC from other countries) has increased by more than 452,000.
