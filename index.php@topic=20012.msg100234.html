Agree with alphabet person's post. It's a lifesaver to be able to go out to the garden and chat socially distanced with neighbors and get sun and fresh air on a regular basis. Our coop has struggled with how to adjust a bit so everyone's needs are taken into account. We established a no guests rule because we really can't have the space overrun with hordes of guests, whether adult or child. That impinges on the rights of residents who pay monthly garden dues and would like to enjoy the space quietly, or who need quiet in order to work from home. Certainly we are not allowing sports--that is a liability issue, for one thing. It's a garden, not a playground, and throwing balls of any kind is prohibited. We have elderly residents or ill residents who want to safely walk around the garden. I don't see how anyone would think playing sports should be allowed. We are now talking about whether we should relax the no guests rule for the summer--we have not done so yet and we may not. Of course, we have no power to control what residents do, and I'm sure there have been guests in the garden this summer, including kids. They make a lot of noise--the more kids the more noise. It's hard to make everyone happy.

Linden Court, 1919, is considered the first real garden apartment complex in Jackson Heights.

------------------------------------------------------------------

The Queensboro Corporation hired Thomas to build Linden Court in 1919, a transformative moment for the neighborhood. As the Landmarks Preservation Commission pointed out in their neighborhood report, the complex was innovative:

the buildings are grouped into attached pairs; the building wall on the periphery of the block is interrupted at regular intervals by open space. The interior of the block is an undivided landscaped space, held in common by means of easements and deed restrictions for the benefit of the residents. This type of plan creates cross ventilation, increased light, and views from the street to the landscaped garden, and encourages a sense of community.

Unlike Ebenezer Howard, whose Garden Cities removed people from the urban core, Thomas was building a Garden City-within-a-city, to balance the desire for open space with the needs of a New York commuter. The building was an instant hit, and over the next few years Wells and Thomas separately designed Hampton Court, Elm Court, Hawthorne Court, Laburnum Court, Cambridge Court, the Chateau, the Towers, and Spanish Gardens. Wells’s buildings, such as Hampton Court, tended to be more elaborate with grander facades; Thomas’s emphasized interior garden space.

Timeline – History of Jackson Heights
Prior to 1900. The area of mostly farms was known as the Trains Meadow section of Newtown (which was later called Elmhurst).
1900-1910. The decade preceding the opening of the Queensboro Bridge in 1909 was fraught with land speculation. The Queensboro Corporation, headed by Edward Archibald MacDougall, became the prime buyer of land in the area, and named it Jackson Heights.
1911. First buildings in Jackson Heights.
1914. Laurel Court completed, first apartment building in Jackson Heights. At four stories it was the tallest residential building in Queens.
1917. Roosevelt Avenue elevated subway completed, connecting Jackson Heights to Grand Central Station.
1917. First garden apartments built by Queensboro Corporation. Later renamed the Greystones.

The no guests rule has become extremely controversial in my coop. Some people insist such a rule is imperative to ensure safety for residents by barring outsiders, and others counter that such a rule is unenforceable, since we are made up of small individual coops, and do not have the legal authority to enforce rules affecting shareholders in all the buildings. All shareholders in all buildings here of course pay garden dues and are equally entitled to use of the shared garden. In addition, the "no guests" people want to keep residents from even strolling through the garden with family members who are not residents: grown children, grandchildren, siblings, parents. They go so far as to confront people they think are outsiders, and chastise the residents who have invited them. The opposition believes it is not only unenforceable to insist that someone cannot walk in the shared garden with a non-resident relative, but totally unfair to residents who have been isolated for months now, particularly those who live alone and just want to sit in the garden with their grown child or close friend. It's becoming a real problem and causing division when that's the last thing we need right now.
