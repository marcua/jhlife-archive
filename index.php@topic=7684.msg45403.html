amateur question: down payments
« on: August 20, 2012, 03:24:44 PM »
My partner and I, both lifelong renters, are beginning to think about buying a place of our own. I'm just getting started in learning about the homebuying process, and have also been poking around sites like trulia.com to get a better sense of what's attainable for around $200k. We'd need a 2-bedroom and would love to stay in Jackson Heights; Kew Gardens is another possibility.
One thing I'm wondering is what the typical down payment is for a co-op or condo in these areas. A lot of listings specify 20% down, which just isn't feasible for us (because the rent is too damn high!) Those of you who have recently bought a home, did you have to put down 20%? If not, how do you find places that will take 10% down or less?
Thanks for any tips-- I'm just trying to get my bearings at this point.

20% down is usually the minimum requirement established co-ops have. Co-ops that are not as stable financially can lower that amount, but then you end up having a hard time finding a bank that will finance the loan because of the co-ops financial instability.
 
Yes, your best bet is either looking for co-op sales by Sponsor's and/or condo's where it is up your credit worthiness and finding a bank willing to make the loan.

Sponsor Owned Units will be 10% Down but some sponsors will still want 15% down because a 10% down payment will likely force the bank to put the loan through a SONYMA loan.  Make sure the mortgage broker is straight with you about the 10% down requirements.  If the complex is already a SONYMA approved complex then this is a no sweat issue.  Sponsor sales is what I do 75% of the time and it's the best.  But remember that they account for a small percentage of the market and those units tend to be fully renovated.  Expect to pay a little more for those but it is well worth it when you take into account that you could easily spend 20k to 40k to fully renovate an apartment 

There are many things to consider when you buy a Coop.  If you can find  Condo at a low price you are better off, but they tend to be very pricey.  Your next choice is a sponsor owned Coop that allows subletting.  Be  careful because many Coops are very restrictive in their subletting policy and those units will keep the more savvy buyers away.  There is nothing wrong with "owner occupied Units", but remember that they are NOT the same as an investor friendly complex.  Please educate yourself as much as possible before you buy and feel free to email me about questions you may have about Coops, Condos, Investment Quality properties, steak cuts and wine  :2funny: :2funny: :2funny:

Best of luck in your search!!
