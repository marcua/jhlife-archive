My understanding is that the buyer compensates their agent, and the seller -- the real estate agent. I'm not sure I'd want them to depend on each other for money -- after all, they don't defend the same interests.

I didn't use an agent b/c I wanted to be on top of everything myself, it was just too important for me to delegate. I found the place myself after seeing maybe 40 other places. Friends of mine developed very good relationships with their real estate agent so that worked out fine for them. In my case, it happened so that my real estate was a dishonest and incompetent asshole so I relied more on my lawyer in negotiating with the seller and the bank.

Supposedly, having a buyers broker makes it easier for you because you only have to communicate with one person, who shares the burden of looking for an apt, communicating with others, etc. I think if I had already known someone I felt comfortable with and considered serious, dependable and who had my best interest in mind, I'd definitely have considered it. However, it didn't make sense for me to get someone I don't know any better than I do, say, the real estate agent to represent my interest. Bear in mind that many agents might ask for some sort of an agreement beforehand to be sure that they will get compensated for whatever work they do, so that's also a commitment you have to be comfortable with.

Good luck!

We did not have a buyer's agent.  We took a risk and it worked out for us.  However, looking back, there were a lot of things that we didn't look into that we could have or should have. 

Also, real estate agents will be able to compare comps better than you can and leverage that to save you money. A good agent will know which apartments were mint when sold and which ones were in estate condition whereas looking at a sale price registered with the New York Department of Finance will not tell you this. Also, if you go with a local real estate agent they are going to know the personality and amenities of each individual co-op from years of experience.   

It does seem crazy to me that some listing agents refuse to share their commission in this economy. 2-5% of 0 = $0. 

I am not in the real estate field but it would seem that in this economy a good buyer's agent would pay for themselves a few times over by getting you a good deal.

Just to be clear. A buyers agent contracted by the buyer to work SOLELY on the buyers behalf is an invaluable asset assuming they know what they are doing. It's especially helpful for first time homebuyers or buyers with little or no time to run around and ensure that every aspect of your transaction is covered.
1)Meet with you and explain in detail what they will do for you.
2)Sit down with you and review your financials and explain the process step by step.
3)Recommend a Loan Officer
4)Recommend an attorney such as Adam Sutton who knows a lot of the buildings in Jackson Heights
5)Negotiate the contract price to get you the best possible price.
6)Assist you in preparing the board package (Co-Ops & certain condos).
7)Give you tips on the board interview.
8)Recommend movers, contractors, etc.

Lastly a buyers agent WILL return your calls, because they know the buyer will be loyal as they have signed a buyers broker agreement. They will also preview properties for you and ensure it meets your criteria (otherwise you can fire them). if anyone would like a more in depth explanation please feel free to e-mail me.

Hope this helps.
