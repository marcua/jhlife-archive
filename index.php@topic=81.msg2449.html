Yes to all of the above!

We need more places to sit: well designed and well managed public spaces can work without being taken over by vagrants.

We need a real park.  We need a public green space for the community to gather and interact.  The asphalt landscape and scarce seating at Travers really are insufficient.

We need more active recreation space.  The two play areas at Travers don't really cut it for the older kids.  The large, flexible play area that the southern half of Travers provides is important.  But Travers is overcrowded and needs to expand.  I'd like to see more for the teenagers, like a skate park and/or more space for organized sports.

We need a dog park.  Our four-legged citizens need to run, too!

Perhaps it's because I'm new and naive, but I really don't think these goals are impossible.  Over the long run, if the community organizes and lobbies aggressively (and gets good political leadership), we can win some of these things for ourselves.  There no shortage of land around for it:

1. Queens Community Savings Bank and Queens Auto Mall/Rainbowland properties.  Of course I want the bank and Rainbowland to stay, but they could each benefit from new, reconfigured buildings financed by selling some of their land to the city for parks.

2. BQE Right-of-Way: suitable for a dog run (I think 69th street between 35th Ave and Broadway would be perfect).

3. Garden School yard.  Perhaps the city or community could pay for improvements or maintenance in exchange for greater access.

4. A property on the north side of 37th Road.  Despite my call for dense housing along 37th Road, I was sad about the new building currently going up at 75th Street because it is a perfect location for a pocket park.  The pedestrian densities are incredibly high in the area, and there's no place to sit.  Perhaps there's another location in the area, sheltered a bit from the subway noise, where something like that can be created.

I totally disagree with the view that benches should be limited/eliminated just because some people loiter on them "inappropriately."  While I get annoyed at kids trampling my front lawn or dog walkers allowing their dogs on it ... and a little less annoyed (but still annoyed) when I see young lovers necking on or in front of my stoop at night, I welcome the occasional elderly or physically disabled or just tired, package-bearing adult whom I find sitting on my stoop during daylight hours.  The person usually apologizes and offers to leave and I reply that he or she is welcome to stay and rest.  That is what a public bench is for, and I do not mind the occasional use of my private stoop for that purpose.  Where are those "quality of life" police officers to deal with bench denizens who are behaving inappropriately?  And when did snoozing on a bench become such an offense?  Other than the risk of having one's pocket picked, a nap on a bench in Central Park is one of the joys of city life.
