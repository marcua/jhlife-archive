Quote from: Rhino on September 05, 2013, 01:21:24 AM
They would never allow it on the upper east side,  so why is it allowed here?

These vendors are allowed on the Upper East Side. It's not a matter of allowance; it's a matter of demand. Save for Green Carts, which are reserved for food deserts, a merchant can basically set up shop wherever he wants. Louis Vuitton stores are allowed in Brownsville. Vendors selling plastic sunglasses are allowed on the Upper East Side. There's no statute saying where people are allowed to sell stuff. Concern over congestion is one thing, but it's not like the city issues permits to certain neighborhoods. Permits are issued to individuals who in turn locate and vend where there's demand for their goods.

Neighborhood Chat / Re: Halah meat wagon Gone
« on: September 05, 2013, 12:49:13 AM »
Quote from: jhlifer on September 04, 2013, 02:38:30 PM
I guess the end result would be empty storefronts and pushcarts lining the avenue. 

I'm sorry, but this doesn't even begin to make sense. In terms of street vendors posing a threat to storefront businesses, consider the numbers: In 1900, the city had a population of about 3.5 million. This was also the heyday of street vendors, who totaled about 25,000 in number. That's a ratio of 1 vendor per every 140 residents. Did this result in the collapse of the commercial economy? Did entire commercial strips hollow out and convert to pushcarts? Today, there are let's say 8 million residents of the city with vendors numbering around 12,000, at a ratio of about 1 vendor per 667 residents. This idea that street vendors threaten to upend the brick-and-mortar business might have been plausible a century ago, but it certainly didn't play out that way.  Claiming that they continue to be a threat to commercial corridors today just doesn't stack up against the historical record.

But even if they were a threat, isn't it true that every fruit vendor is a threat to every other fruit vendor? This is what competition is about. Permitted fruit vendors by definition have permission to compete with other fruit vendors. As for the halal vendor on 82nd and 37th, name me one business within a 5-block radius that he is even competing with.

Quote from: jhlifer on September 04, 2013, 02:38:30 PM
The brick-and-mortar businesses are more likely to pay sales taxes and by virtue of their investment in our community, more likely to be responsible local citizens.

I'm not sure which part of this statement to response to first. For starters, businesses do not pay sales tax; they collect them. Any business, whether storefront or not, must charge applicable sales tax. As for being good local citizens, let us not forget the Times Square t-shirt vendor who alerted police to a smoking car-bomb a couple years ago (http://www.nytimes.com/2010/05/02/nyregion/02timessquare.html?pagewanted=all). This is anecdotal of course, but it's not a law of nature that storefront businesses are more ethical than street vendors.

Basically, this backlash against street vendors is something of a canard, and a very very dated one at that. If we want to ensure that they're adhering to city regulations, that's one thing, and a very worthy cause in my mind. Attacking the entire business model as criminal or underhanded or economically destructive is absurd.

Neighborhood Chat / Re: Halah meat wagon Gone
« on: September 04, 2013, 12:52:40 AM »
This conflict over street vendors is as old as the city itself. For those opposed to this business model, I would note that the number of street vendors today is just a small fraction of what it used to be. Brick-and-mortar businesses, and eventually municipal government, have been hostile towards these vendors for centuries. This partnership has virtually wiped out this type of business, so what we see today is just a remnant of what used to be. That said, the concerns that some people have expressed about fraud are real. Food vendors need to get permits from a couple different agencies. Demand for these permits among immigrants is astronomically high, so it is common that people acquire these permits and licenses and lease them out to others at a ridiculous markup. We cannot tell which vendors have gotten their permits from the city, or which are leasing them. But the business is rough, and many of the food vendors around the city are forking over huge sums of profits to the official permit-holdersâ€”which incidentally includes money to pay taxes--, are working twelve-hour shifts, make sub-poverty incomes, and have zero benefits or job security. As for effects on brick-and-mortar businesses, I understand the concern, but where in our competitive market-based system does it state that enterprises have to be affixed to the ground?

Neighborhood Chat / Re: Diversity Plaza Upgrade?
« on: August 29, 2013, 11:42:28 PM »
We should not compare Diversity Plaza with other city plazas because it came about in a unique way. Normally, an established local organization puts in a request to DOT for a plaza (Jackson Heights Green Alliance and the 78th Street Playstreet; Queens Econonic Development Corporation and Corona Plaza). In the case of Diversity Plaza, this space was the result of a study DOT did a couple years ago in which they recommended the closure of this street to traffic (http://a841-tfpweb.nyc.gov/jackson-heights/recommendations-new-public-space/).

As Jeff said, SUKHI is just two people who are are busy running their businesses and were pro-active enough to take on this plaza when no one else would. We should not wait around for them to broadcast appeals for assistance. And I would not necessarily assume that they are members of this forum and hearing these concerns. So, instead, let's step up ourselves, pay them a visit next Wednesday at 7:30pm, get to know them, eat, hang out, and see what we can do to to help.

If anyone has unanswered questions, pressing concerns, or productive ideas, next Wednesday will be an opportunity to get them out into the open and actually be effectual.
