It's a nice place. Very quiet and suburban-feeling area. Totally different than JH but not in a bad way. The commute would be a bit of a pain since you'd have to take a (long) bus ride to the E train (there might be an express bus during the week as well).

Neighborhood Chat / Re: Our Park. Our Future.
« on: June 08, 2013, 09:50:17 AM »
When the decisions are being made by people like Bloomberg who get on a helicopter or private jet when they want to be in nature, this is what we get. Would it ever occur to Bloomberg or this oil sheik that when most of us wake up on a Saturday morning after a loud, jarring, fluorescent-lit week surrounded by screeching steel and concrete, there isn't a Gulfstream fueled up and ready to take us off to Martha's Vineyard for the afternoon? This was understood on a gut level even by the famously elitist Robert Moses - his visions for urban parkland, flawed as they were, showed some kind of compassion for the spiritual needs of regular working human beings, and are a great legacy for this city.

Today we have no such luck. Our city is run by people who only think about money generated per square foot, and as long as that's maximized, the city is being run "successfully." It must be nice to contemplate how successfully the city is being run when you are relaxing on your Hamptons estate on a cool summer evening. Of course the sweating masses wouldn't appreciate the subtlety of this sublime, relaxing place. They want their competitive sports and loud "active" enjoyment, so let's make sure they get it (especially if we make money in the process). If you want to sit on a bench, read a book, fly a kite, have a picnic, toss a Frisbee? Sorry, the corporate overlords say no. We'll take your tax money, thank you very much, now please get lost.

I am reminded of the very-much-in-vogue architectural drawings we see everywhere. The people are fuzzy, faceless computer-generated avatars whose only function is to provide an aesthetic accent on some plutocrat's dystopian architectural nightmare. This is exactly the way Bloomberg and Sheik Mansour see regular people like you and me - indistinct, computer-generated, part of the background, devoid of humanity.

Neighborhood Chat / Re: Proposed Stadiums, Mall, Garages at Flushing Meadows Park
« on: February 02, 2013, 09:53:31 AM »
I'm amazed that something like this is even on the table. As others have said this would never happen in Central Park, Prospect Park, or any park surrounded by people with social capital to burn. People who live near Flushing Meadows Corona Park - one of the city's most beautiful, under-appreciated parks - don't have the money or time to mobilize the way the Upper West Side moms did when Robert Moses tried to turn a Central Park playground into a parking lot back in the 60's. As a result, Bloomberg and his cronies figure they can just steamroll over any opposition. For wealthy Manhattanites, no harm done; for them, Flushing Meadows Park is a place to come to once a year for tennis, and the rest of the year, it might as well not exist.

Speaking of tennis, even the presence of the existing Tennis Center in the park is utterly egregious. Western Queens has just about zero parkland other than Flushing Meadows, so to restrict even a square inch to two-weeks-a-year paid admission is ridiculous.

And don't even get me started on a mall. As if the existing malls in Flushing and Rego Park aren't enough.

Memo to Bloomberg: NYC is not a corporation. Maximizing profits per square foot is not the appropriate function of a public servant.
