« on: June 26, 2009, 06:00:57 AM »
I've been discussing the topic of a new restaurant with two of my acquaintances who have opened several successful restaurants and bars in Manhattan and Brooklyn (separately). They have both offered to provide assistance in any way they can to help us open up something in our neighborhood. One is a restauranteur and the other is a contractor/developer.

There has been a lot of discussion on the topic of what is wanted or needed in the neighborhood, but are there any investors out there who are willing to put forth the start up cash to make something like this happen? I'm an architect and willing to provide all the necessary design and technical experience and I'm willing to start assembling a team of qualified individuals to consult on this, but what is really needed are investors who believe in our neighborhood and believe that something like this is feasible and profitable.

Let's start a discussion and make it really happen!

Someone that regularly goes out to eat good food will be happy to see a nice selection of beef cuts, poultry (not necessarily chicken 7 ways ) and maybe decent number of salads, soups and maybe some other type of proteins.  In an economy where many restaurant are struggling can anyone underestimate the importance of what a "favorite restaurant" stands for?   A mistake in strategy is to create a menu and atmosphere that is perceived as run-of -the-mill or where the surroundings are perceived as too lavish. To succeed a restaurant needs an identity and signature dishes that are well though out and expertly executed time in and time out.  Any restaurant that will survive the crisis will need to have a strong identity and quality service.

NYC Native-

I completely agree with the second paragraph of your comment. Identity and unique atmosphere are key. You are right on the money.

However, let me rephrase my earlier comment. I was not very clear, so I apologize. It is much too early in the process to be discussing what types of burgers and specific brand of beer the  proposed restaurant would provide. This is the time for conceptuals, not specifics. If we can't discuss and debate what the overall concept of the place might be, it will not matter if we have a "Zorba" burger or if we serve "Dogfishhead Ale". It could very well be decided that it should be a Belgian restaurant that serves mussels with fries and mayo and Peach Lambic.

The point is that the type of beer or burger will not matter if the concept  and atmosphere (as you have also stated) is not strong enough and never gets off the ground. So I'd like to keep the discussion to conceptuals as much as possible for now. There will be plenty of time to ponder the endless selection of beers, burgers and soups and salads later.

However, most importantly, since we seem to have gotten WAY off topic, is whether there are more interested parties who would be interested in investing in this opportunity in some form, by way of startup costs, donating expertise and management skills, small business knowledge, etcetera. Is there someone who seems to have a real professional keen sense of the neighborhood market and what types of business typically fail here and why they do? These are the types of discussions we should be having, so that we learn from the mistakes of former endeavors and do not venture down those paths.

Thanks everyone.
