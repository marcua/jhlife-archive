My friend's son is graduating from college this June and they currently live in the Philadelphia suburbs.  Their son would like to live in western Queens JH, Astoria Sunnyside etc..  He has an offer at a NYC accounting firm at 65K per year but he also has an offer in the Philly area for 58K per year.  My friend asked me if you can live on 65K in this area if you want to live on your own and not share an apartment. Since I am well established and no longer live in JH I'm not sure I can offer any solid guidance.  Is it possible to rent your own apartment and live on 65K per year in JH or is that a stretch.  Any advice would be appreciated.

There's a 40x rent rule that's commonly used by landlords in NYC. This means many landlords will want to see proof that the renter's gross income is at least 40x the monthly rent. $65,000 divided by 40 is $1625.  https://streeteasy.com/blog/how-to-afford-rent-nyc/

In Jackson Heights these days, $1625 is a studio price. So if he's ok living in a studio, then yes, I think he'd be ok. Of course he'd need to figure out his budget for all the other things that will come out of his paycheck. But in my opinion, $65,000 is a reasonable salary for a single person living alone who doesn't mind living in a studio.

It's also worth posing to your friend's son what his long term financial goals are. Something young people can overlook is just how much it costs year after year to pay the extra expense of living alone, not just in rent but in paying for 100% of high speed internet, cable, utilities, etc. Is that worth forking out an extra $7K a year that could be used to pay down student loans and/or save up money for a decent rainy day fund, and eventually the money needed for a down-payment if home ownership might be a goal?

Living alone is nice, but it's worth asking himself if he likes talking to people about his day, having roommates he might like to grab a drink with, etc. Roommates can come with their own set of challenges, but as long as he's careful about who he moves in with (or lets move in with him) and uses some common sense, most roommates are fine even if they aren't necessarily going to be your best buddy.
