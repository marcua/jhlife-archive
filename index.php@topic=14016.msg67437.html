They are common in all NYC co-ops, as one of the few legal ways a co-op can earn revenue that funds the reserve - money set aside for emergencies like roof repair that are outside of general operating costs.

In Jackson Heights the buildings that I have heard about charge much more than 2-3%. Some are as high as 50% of the profit (purchase price subtracted from sale price).

I think to properly answer your question Steven is to address the fact that Flip tax is not iposed but rather voted in (and sometimes out) by shareholders of the coop that a seller is part of. The seller is typically responsible for payment of the flip tax unless otherwise negotiated. It is for the need/purpose of adding to the building’s reserve and in some cases to discourage early resales.

I am active in both Manhattan and in Jackson Heights, and I have to say that Flip taxes and structure are different across the board. 2-3% is not typical, but rather a number indicative of the number that most mortgage lenders find acceptable. But neither in Manhattan or Jackson Heights there is a rule of thumb or any commonality. Remember, flip tax is instituted by some coops on AS NEEDED basis.

Now, calculations of flip tax can varie.

1.   Worth mentioning, many building have NO flip tax
2.   Another type of calculation is based on percentage of gross sale
3.   Percentage of GAIN made on the unit
4.   Dollar amount per share
5.   A flat transfer fee
6.   Some flip taxes are lateral. They start at a higher number for a set number of years, then drop to a lower number or be reduced to zero.

I don’t want to rattle off my knowledge of which Jackson Heights buildings do or don’t have a flip tax and of what type, even as an example, because they may vote one in or out just as I am writing this, rendering my information inaccurate 
My recommendation would be to study each building individually, and ultimately pick a corporation you feel comfortable and confident investing in. Flip tax is only one of many factors you must consider. I hope this helps.

If you are working with an experienced agent knowledgeable about the neighborhood, he or she should be able to give you the scoop. There have been fewer apartments on the market than compared to a year or two ago, so I'd guess if you have a price range in mind, it should be manageable to keep on top to find out if the building has a flip tax. If you aren't, the seller's agent should be able to fill you in. (However, I was surprised how many seller's agents didn't know if the building allowed pets and therefore failed to include that information in the MLS listing, or be able to answer the question at an open house, so maybe that's a pipe dream. I know if I were showing an apartment, I'd darn well make sure I knew about flip taxes, pets, and the sublet policy).

When we first began looking at apartments, we were wary of flip taxes. But, as we looked at little more closely, we realized a flip tax often meant a low monthly maintenance fee. I think if an owner isn't expecting to sell again in a few years, but instead plans to be somewhere for awhile, the benefits of the flip tax can be felt in the pocketbook by reduced monthly housing costs. It's also good for longtime residents on a fixed income (retirees) who can't afford maintenance hikes or assessments.
