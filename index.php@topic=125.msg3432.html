The Mayor's Office issued a press release about neighborhood parking permits today:

MAYOR BLOOMBERG AND COMMISSIONER SADIK-KHAN ANNOUNCE RESIDENTIAL PARKING PERMIT PROGRAM AS PART OF CONGESTION PRICING

Program developed to address community concerns about on-street parking availability arising from congestion pricing

Mayor Michael R. Bloomberg and Department of Transportation (DOT) Commissioner Janette Sadik-Khan today announced that the congestion pricing legislation that will be introduced in the City Council and State Legislature in the coming weeks will include a Residential Parking Permit (RPP) program.  The program is designed to give local residents priority for on-street parking in residential areas and to discourage park-and-ride activity by commuters.  It will be tailored by neighborhood to address specific needs, and restrictions will vary based on neighborhood parking patterns.  The announcement of the program follows a dozen community parking workshops held by DOT in 7 neighborhoods between November 2007 and February 2008. The announcement was made in Boerum Hill, a neighborhood where the program could hold great appeal to residents. All neighborhoods will have the opportunity to consider opting into the RPP program including communities where interest has already been expressed such as Brooklyn Heights, Long Island City, and the Upper East and West Sides of Manhattan. Joining the Mayor and the Commissioner at today's announcement were Councilmember David Yassky and President of the Boerum Hill Association, Sue Wolfe.

"This is a promising and proven parking management strategy that together with congestion pricing, will help us achieve one of the key goals of PlaNYC - cutting down on pollution-creating traffic and creating an environmentally sustainable transportation system for New York City," said Mayor Bloomberg.  "A number of other cities, including Washington D.C., Boston, Chicago, and San Francisco, have long had successful residential parking permit programs.  We are confident that it will succeed here too."

Well I say kudos to her for not caving on this. The MTA seems either hopelessly corrupt or inept or something, but it does a hell of a job making money disappear. Right now the Second Avenue subway the LIRR Grand Central connection and the Fulton Street Terminal are 1.5 billion dollars over budget.

There seems something truly wrongheaded about propping up an ailing MTA with a new bureaucracy in charge of generating revenue. It takes quite a bit of effort for me to stomach the idea the way this is being marketed as a sin tax for those who chose to commute by car. If revenue generation and a curbing of commuter traffic were the sole issue why not simply decrease parking availability and increase parking taxes? Why this new congestion policy?

Even the aims of the program -  which considers a 3% decrease in traffic to be a success - seem dubious. A 3% reduction in traffic doesn't seem to qualify as a success in changing air quality, nor does it grant a safer environment for bicycles. Overall the plan just seems lopsided in its intention of benefitting Manhattan and providing little more than window dressing for outer boroughs who will foot the bill.

JD - I'd say that you should feel thankful that bridge and tunnel tolls pay 13% of the MTA budget and state and local taxes kick in 22%. That $4 fare covers 58% of operating costs meaning that without outside help your train wouldn't run or you'd be paying maybe $7 each way. Keep in mind that cars are cheaper in the short term since ownership, depreciation and many other costs are covered by car owners not a large public organization like the MTA. Moreover automobiles fund mass transit, not the other way around.

At any rate, the "free" access to Manhattan doesn't seem to be the case unless you're in the rare position of getting a free parking spot once you reach Manhattan. Daily parking where I work is between twenty and thirty dollars a day. If I need to take the Midtown tunnel or the Tri-Borough that's another $8 each way.

As far as I know the City Department of Transportation runs the East River bridges and I'm not sure if the congestion pricing even applies to ongoing maintenance other than a mandatory bus lane on the Queensboro. On the other hand, if we took revenue from MTA bridges and tunnels and diverted it to the care of these bridges rather than to mass transit I'm sure they would be well funded.

I'm overstating my case but to insist that car drivers should be scapegoated for the sorry fiscal state of the MTA is a bit disingenuous. It completely turns a blind eye to New York City and State decreasing their contributions to public transportation.

As for your assurances that Congestion Pricing will be as successful as in London, may I remind you that "Spotted Dick", "Bangers and Mash" and "Pig and Whistle" are hugely successful in London. I would assume by your logic that they too would garner as much success here?
