Though recycling is great, I think it's more important reuse bags and reduce the amount of plastic introduced into the environment. The process of recycling isn't exactly clean. Recycling bags, rather than reusing them, is still wasteful.

As for whether or not to charge customers per bag: I think this might be necessary. Some people change their shopping habits because they're environmentally responsible. Others do it because it's fashionable (carrying around a cloth bag that declares, in bold letters, their environmental-friendliness to the world). Others, though, won't change a thing unless it directly affects them financially; they won't consider using a reusable bag until they see their total at the register has increased unnecessarily.

This reminds me that I still haven't made one of these bags.

I'm curious how this will play out logistically.  It seems to me that more often than not, the groceries are run through the scanner and you get your total and start paying while your groceries are being placed in bags.  If the cashier has to wait til the bagger is done bagging before he or she can tell you the amount you owe, and it's only at that point that you can run your credit card through or find the right change, won't this cause longer lines?  An important thing to think about in NYC.

I think implementation is an issue, but I've dealt with this before -

I lived and worked in Germany, and no supermarket ever supplies plastic shopping bags - everyone just brings their own (canvas bags, foldable nylon bags) or if need be, purchases one when they need one. The same doesn't hold true for other types of stores - I remember that other types of stores - The Body Shop and H&M clothing, for example -  did give out bags with purchases.

And don't forget - even here - at BJ's wholesale, they don't give out shopping bags either.

It just takes a little bit of an adjustment - but the intent is to work on a major environmental issue through small-scale taxes to change polluting behaviors - and a true case of "the polluter pays."

Mr. Bloomberg might get this one passed, and he might not - but I do applaud his efforts to make serious environmental changes in New York City.

(As for the comment about recycling metrocards- Bloomberg doesn't really have much say about that - that's all the MTA, which is not under city authority)
