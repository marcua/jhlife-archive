Wow.  Those 2007 rankings are brutal.  I admit that I am not a daily commuter... (actually my daily commute is a pleasant 12-14 minute walk thru JH) but at least 6-10 times a month I have a meeting in Manhattan and I take the 7 train in.   Usually in the mornings... usually late rush hour.

I think the 7 is generally a really pleasant ride. I NEVER get tired of the view.  And if I do, the people watching is exceptional.     I also have the advantage of usually getting on at 82nd street, standing one stop to 74th street, and then getting a seat when everyone gets off at 74th.  The trains are usually clean.  Many of the stations are quirky and interesting. 

My only complaints about the 7 were not even on the score list.  First of all, I wish that there were more stations with elevators.  Secondly, I find it really confusing trying to get a local back to JH in the afternoon/evening.  Sometimes the front of the train says local and the side of the train says express, or the other way around.  Or it looks like a local, and then as the doors are closing they tell you it is express.   

Oh.  And the announcements certainly need improvement.

But overall, the 7 is actually one of my favorite lines. 

What do others think?  Especially you daily rush hour crunch folks?

v70cat,

Don't be silly.  Nobody is picking on Queens.  Are you saying that somehow the Bronx is favored because it got new cars first?  All train lines are multi-borough.  And MTA allocates the new cars to lines that are best able to best able to take advantage of their new technology.

Upgrading the 7 line is actually one of MTA's highest priorities.  They are installing new signals on the line, which will eventually enable trains to run closer together than they are allowed to now.  By the time this project is completed, the 7 Line will have the new trains needed to take advantage of this technology.

Also, earlier this year, MTA completed a major project to add switches so that trains can change tracks at 74th Street.  This provides significant operational flexibility when there is construction or some other snafu on the line -- trains that otherwise would not have been able to stop at 74th Street can now do so.  I have benefited from this

As for notice, MTA posts scheduled construction information in its stations and website each week.  There are also unscheduled projects (broken rails, broken signals, etc.), but of course there's no advance notice for those.
