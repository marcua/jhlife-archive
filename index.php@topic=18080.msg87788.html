Should there be a cap on ride-hail vehicles?
« on: July 30, 2018, 10:51:21 AM »
New York City officials are moving to restrict the number of Uber and Lyft vehicles allowed on the road as part of a move to contain the massive growth in the for-hire vehicle industry that has been blamed for worsening congestion and low wages for drivers.

I'm all for less congestion on the roads, but I clearly remember a time when it was practically impossible to hail a yellow cab in the outerboroughs. The same applied when in Manhattan trying to get a yellow cab to take me home to Queens in the wee hours of the night. Companies like Uber and Lyft came around and made things easier for many. Now they say that there are just too many vehicles on the road, and the yellow cab industry is also suffering as a result.  Should there be a cap on ride-hail companies like Uber and Lyft in New York City? We're Fortunate in Jackson heights because we're well-served in terms of public transit, but what about the folks living in two-fare zones, or the disabled who are underserved by the MTA?

Uber and Lyft are facing a major crackdown in New York City

This place s most definitely a Manhattan-driven piece of legislation and I’m against it. I’ve always lived in Queens and I can’t imagine how having fewer ride hailing options, especially in areas that simply don’t have a plethora of options.

But specifically throttling the number of ride hailing cars that can serve people is the same as dismantling net neutrality and having fast lanes. Interesting follow-up from NY1 on conflicting perspectives:

From NY1:  https://www.ny1.com/nyc/all-boroughs/news/2018/08/08/city-council-vote-on-ride-share-cap

Who supports it?

Yellow taxi drivers have been pushing for this legislation because they say the rapid growth of ride-hailing services is destroying their industry.

The New York Taxi Workers Alliance sees it as an answer to the financial stress that they say companies like Uber and Lyft have put on drivers. They say six drivers have taken their own lives since November.

"No one is going to be destroyed by what happened today," Bhairavi Desai, the executive director of the New York Taxi Workers Alliance, said after the vote. "But had the City Council failed to act, hundreds of thousands of workers' lives would have been destroyed."

Who is against it?

In a statement issued after the vote, Uber said, in part, "The City's 12-month pause on new vehicle licenses will threaten one of the few reliable transportation options while doing nothing to fix the subways or ease congestion."

Minorities also praise the services because they say yellow cab drivers often refuse to pick them up or drive to their neighborhoods.
