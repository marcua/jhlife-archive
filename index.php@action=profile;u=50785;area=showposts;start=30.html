Only to clarify a couple points raised in this thread:

Please remember NY has a "Good Samaritan" law, so no one need fear the consequences of otherwise good intentions. The statute in full at: <http://la12.org/Portals/733/Web_content/files/NYSGoodSamaritanLaw.pdf>

Along those lines, the very sad story of our fellow Queensian Catherine Genovese was actually more complicated. As it turned out, the supposed non-responsive "bystander effect" (the subsequently so-called "Genovese syndrome") reported by the NYTimes was documented years later by that very paper of record as having been "grossly exaggerated." In point of fact, at least one neighbor heard Kitty's screams and came to her aid. See: <https://www.nytimes.com/2016/04/05/nyregion/winston-moseley-81-killer-of-kitty-genovese-dies-in-prison.html>

Having noted that historical discrepancy, it was once said you get more of a response yelling "Fire!" than "Help!" So there's that...

Neighborhood Chat / Re: Outdoor Seating
« on: July 31, 2016, 08:23:42 PM »
My thanks to Lalochezia and others for answering my initial response. I’m afraid the beautifully written “sophistry" has also led to some unfortunate, unwarranted conclusions.

That is especially to say that I never stated I did not want sidewalk cafés. Again, they’re a lovely idea in any practicable space, particularly for those entertaining Continental aspirations. Even so, JH is known to have one of the highest population densities in NYC, at least according to <https://www.arcgis.com/home/webmap/viewer.html?webmap=80f9b95a4ce0491091f1477710f6a91d> (as of 2015). Moreover, those areas with similar density (e.g., E.Vill. or the UES) have sidewalks along their avenues that are, generally speaking, actually wider (according to the NYC.gov map Lalochezia cited).

Though I post here on JHLife very infrequently, I do read the threads with regularity. As a result, I recognize Lalochezia among others on this forum as those who have often fought the good fight against entrenched NIMBY positions in this community. Yet not everyone here who disagrees with any one particular issue—certainly not I—always already supports those old-school attitudes, too. What I support above all is a measured, reasonable perspective that acknowledges, in this instance for example, the physical impact of density and infrastructure in this neighborhood, both of which make the import of any desired café culture into JH challenging to say the least.

That difficulty arises, I additionally argue, from the historical fact that—all pearl-clutching aside—the WASP who designed and developed this neighborhood, or the residents he once attracted, would not likely have ever conceived of such culture working here or fitting into his Scotch-plaid fabric. Jane Jacobs made her argument based on what was then still considered to be a working-class Greenwich (now West) Village, but not on the plans of a white-flight pandering, bougie developer like MacDougall.

As one can clearly see, then, these concrete features of JH I reference are not without merit when looking at, say, the installation of tables on sidewalks—certainly not any less merit than a similar uproar caused by the newest newstand. Furthermore, I should think the historical context also has merit, since it is primarily the Historic District we are arguing about, is it not?

In short, having now dispatched with the points raised, I am unsure whether Lalochezia et al. fully understand the advantageous use of what s/he calls “sophistry,” when it is instead the simple employment of, I hope, civil rhetoric. With the ad hominem assumptions made, however, Lalochezia has doubtless fulfilled the denotative criteria for his/her username. Kudos for consistency! And thanks again for your consideration!
