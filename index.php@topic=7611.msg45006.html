I think I may have genuine answer to this. I was born and raised in JH and went to Upstate NY for college than to LA for work. After ten years of being gone here are my thoughts:

- Early to mid 90's - I was in junior high at IS 145. It was a rude and quick awakening from elementary school. I disliked it a lot. I was in the honors program and I remember kids getting coats, book bags, bus passes and shoes stolen from them. I had a knife put to my face since the kids wanted my Jansport backpack. The school had minimal supervision when classes concluded. Myself and a few classmates were robbed more times than we liked. Keep in mind that these kids are the same age and attended the same school. My parents visited with the dean many times and nothing was really done. My friends and I also hated walking home from class since we didn't want any trouble. During this era there were a lot of kids hanging around Gianni's pizzeria since they had an arcade set up. These kids would always try to get quarters from other kids to continue playing the video game.

- Mid to late 90's - Went to HS in LIC and I loved it. The area and the student body was day and night compared to IS 145. I excelled and really enjoyed neighborhoods such as LIC, Astoria and Sunnyside. I got to experience something outside of JH since my HS friends lived all over. During this time I noticed that kids in JH (same kids that robbed others) started getting into graffiti and other escalated troubles. I remember them verbally abusing the former IS 145 vice principle who lived on 82nd street. They threw eggs at her home whenever they got a chance. The neighborhood shifted in a negative way in my opinion. The house right next to Ben's Barber Shop became a drug house that sold weed to everyone and a mom and pop video store on northern and 83rd was a front for a cocaine dealer. As I got older I noticed more and more negatives and how petty incidents became escalated.

- 2000's - Left JH for college in upstate ny and I loved it. The people, houses, neighborhoods was all well kept. It was the white picket fence community that I always wanted. After college I got a job in West LA. 1000x's better than JH. Got financially better paying job in the NYC. Decided to move into my investment property in JH that I obtained right after college.

- Present days - Community shock when I moved back. Some changed for the better and some for the worst. I enjoy seeing more attractive store fronts on Northern. It could be me living in West LA and Upstate that totaled 10 years but I feel that people lack respect here. I always say the true residents in JH are "good" it's just JH gets a lot of visitors that hang out here. I hate the illegal sub divided apartments since it brings in a certain class of people that cannot originally afford to live in JH. I feel terrible for the women that have to deal with all the whistling and rude comments they receive (especially on Roosevelt). I find it said how the public garbage bins are always filled with household trash.

Currently, I am looking for rent out my house once again and look for a new development condo in LIC. JH will always be part of me but I think it needs a lot of attention and TLC to make it better. It is too one dimensional. I noticed a lot of the senior homeowners are putting for sale signs up for the same reason. I applaud groups that are trying to make the area better but we are a small group. In my opinion the main obstacle are greedy landlords who sub divide apartments. I am looking to start a family soon to and I don't think JH will be ideal for me to do so. It is unfortunate because I grew up here and I don't want my kids to experience a knife to his/her face.

As a 20something woman I make it a point to exit off the elevated 7-train section of the station so that I'm deposited directly off of the 74th street section and I can bypass Roosevelt completely on my way home to the historic district. I was raised on the Lower East Side so I don't consider some guy whistling at me to offensive - but I've been followed for a few blocks home and had some very lewd things said to me.  To give you further context I'm a minority woman - and even though I'm Ivy League educated and work at a professional job in the city the sad truth is I don't get certain respect afforded to me because of my ethnicity.  Just the way it is for us and a sad state of the world.

That said, I don't think Jackson Heights is necessarily dangerous - again, I was a LES-er during the years that the area was known as the city's junkie hub.  I DO think that the density of the area is something that plays into the perceived crime rate - this is a VERY populous area, with very large buildings complexes that span a 10-20 block radius. So with that taken in account our crime rate is probably very low per capita. Those of us posting on this message board probably live in apartments with our spouses or 1-2 children while the immigrants in the neighborhood live with 8-10 of their relatives.  There is no other area in the city I can think of with the same type of density - not counting housing projects or new condo developments -  Brooklyn is mostly a mix of brownstones and 4/5 story walkups and single/multi-family houses; same with other parts of Queens; same with the Bronx and Manhattan.  Exacerbating the problem is the reticence of immigrants in reporting criminal activity and just this whole underworld infrastructure that exists (co-exists at times) with this city.  We just happen to live in the neighborhood where it all happens. 

I chose to live in Jackson Heights even though the majority of my peers live in Brooklyn or Astoria/LIC because I was raised in an immigrant family and I see a spark in this neighborhood that other neighborhoods are now sorely lacking thanks to Bloomberg.  Growing up I had a lot of friends in this neighborhood (also all immigrants w/ working class parents) and they are all very successful people now, who got to live in all senses of the world the American dream (great education, great jobs).  Queens also gave my immigrant parents their first taste of home ownership.  So I am committed to the neighborhood for the time being and hope that others like me (young, well-educated, diverse) boomerang back to the neighborhood to keep it a lovely middle-class neighborhood for new generations to grow up. I do think Roosevelt Ave needs a lot of TLC and there have been many positive changes over the years.  I've noticed on this board that a lot of times any words about improvement are automatically shot down as attempts at gentrification.  There is a difference between wanting fewer liquor licenses and stricter crackdowns on sex trafficking than wanting 20 hipster bars on your block. Immigrants come here because they DO want better for their children, and if we let the vice problem of JHeights overwhelm those desires - even they will leave for greener pastures.

I have lived in Jackson Heights since 1985, I have seen it all, the ups and downs, and the times when we are all in harmony.  The 80's were ok, the 90's were the days of high crime, and it was moderate as the new millenium began.

Right now Jackson Heights is relatively at a midpoint, but far from dangerous, I never have reservations walking down the street, not even a dimly lit one.

I have no problem telling the truth, so those who think it is dangerous here, let me enlighten you on the 90's:

1) Murders were at an all time high

2) The drug trade was flourishing

3) Prostitution on most of Roosevelt Avenue

4) Child molestors unphased, they stood right outside schools, even when their wanted posters were all over.

5) Burglary was at an all time high (my apartment was burglarized at least 3x during most years in the 1990's)


Why do I mention all of this negativity?  The key reason is that these activities are almost non-existant these days in JH.


No plans on leaving, Jackson Heights is the place to be, as for those who do not agree, why are you still here? (not to be too harsh)
