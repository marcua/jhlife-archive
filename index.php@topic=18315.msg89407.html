I'd like to expand on that (especially because I wrote a response before I saw yours, Lilybell!).

Most Washington Plaza tenants who rented pre-conversion are rent-stabilized. (I think there's at least one rent-controlled tenant.) But that doesn't mean their rents have stayed affordable. This is mostly due to the preferential rents Lilybell mentioned. With preferential rents, the landlord offers tenants a lease for somewhat less than what the law will allow (since the law might allow a higher rent than the landlord could get for the apartment). So there's the rent you pay, but there's also the higher rent the landlord could be charging you. Whenever you renew your lease, the landlord can raise you all the way up to the maximum rent allowed for your apartment. That can mean a sudden leap of hundreds of dollars.

One reason landlords have wanted to offer preferential rents is that the legal stabilized rents become inflated due to the landlords' practice of renovating kitchens and baths between tenants and then being allowed to increase the stabilized rent for doing it. Some landlords do this whenever an apartment is vacated in order to get a higher rent. The rate a landlord could legally charge might be $2400 but for that neighborhood and type of apartment, $1800 is the going rate, so the tenant gets a lease for $1800. But if the landlord wants to get rid of the tenant for some reason—say, for coop conversion, or because the tenant complains about repairs not being made, or lack of heat—whenever the lease comes up for renewal, the rent can legally be increased to $2400 (plus whatever yearly rent increases are allowable under stabilization).

Landlords can also make major capital improvements that hike up the rent. This has been done at Washington Plaza. The landlord has been doing massive work for the past few years, like redoing the water pipes, replacing all the windowsills, replacing the walkways in the courtyard, and so on. Most of these things are considered MCIs. We have already gotten one MCI for the repiping and windowsills. I'm sure there will be more coming for the courtyard walkways and other work that's in progress or planned, such as replacing fencing around the property and installing gates at the front of the courtyard.

These things all add up. And many people can't afford to pay $50 more a month, much less $100 or $200.

About the buyout offers, yes, a number of tenants took those. I'm sure some were happy to take them because they were thinking of leaving anyway. But some took them because they had no choice—they couldn't afford to stay. (Most of the buyouts were $25K-$35K.)
