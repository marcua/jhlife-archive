Post by: abee on February 14, 2008, 04:33:20 PM
Glass etching cream has a ph of less than 7 (I think the ph of Ammonium Biflouride, which is found in most commercially available glass etching creams, has a ph of ~2) which makes it an acid. When using glass etching cream, people should be in a well ventilated area, and avoid prolonged contact with the skin because you can get serious burns. This type of graffiti is destructive, and a common concern in urban areas, and there is really no need to be condescending.

Here's some more information:

Post by: Mr. C on February 19, 2008, 01:53:28 PM
I am a teacher who works with the junior high students you refer to in your posts, and I'd like to make a few points and suggest a possible solution.

First, the use of "these kids" and "they" implicitly demonizes a large group of children in our neighborhood who are at a very impressionable age--one that takes others' opinions very seriously.  Kids this age are constantly looking for recognition from their peers, as well as from adults; a suspicious look on the sidewalk or a suggestion that "they get jail time" sends a very clear message: our community does not care about you.  We teach children respect from kindergarten on though junior high, but they know that respect is conditional: we innately feel that we are obliged to give respect only when we receive respect in turn.  I suspect that building a stronger sense of community between our neighborhoodâ€™s children and adults would foster mutual respect and make some potential vandals think twice before defacing public and private property.  How to go about this, especially in a crowded neighborhood with very few facilities for community activities (youth sports, youth organizations, etc.) is a topic for another post.

Note that I stressed the word â€˜someâ€™ above.  We must realize that we cannot eliminate the graffiti problem altogether.  Young adolescents have been vandalizing community property since the Stone Age; no doubt some of the more lewd Mesozoic cave painting found in France were the work of angst-ridden teens.  While we can strive to provide alternative activities and a rich community life for teens, there will always be a few who do graffiti, egg houses, smash mail boxes, etc.  Teenage rebellion is not a modern problem, an urban problem, a gang problem, a racial/cultural problem, or a class problem; it is as close as we have to a sociological fact.

This brings me to my next point.  APG7714  seems to imply that our graffiti problem is imported from Corona (our poorer neighbors to the east), where many of the students that attend I.S. 145 (one of our neighborhoodâ€™s middle schools) live.  This is inaccurate.  Most of these young artists tend to paint and tag in their own neighborhoods where they can show off there work to their friends.  The motive is not to destroy property, but to gain respect and rebel status within their peer group, which would not happen if their peers did not see their work.  It is pure classism to suggest kids from another neighborhood are responsible for our graffiti because our teens happen to live in expensive co-ops.  Anecdotally, last year I happen to have taught one notorious vandal who left his â€œsemiâ€ tag on every above ground surface within a three block radius from his homeâ€”the doorman building on 76th Street between 34th and 35th.

I would also like to venture a suggestion to the community.  Rather than demonize â€œthemâ€ for the fact that less than one percent of their peers occasionally wield a can of spray paint, why donâ€™t we use the youthful energy and community spirit of the other ninety-nine percent to clean up?  When I polled my 7th grade students for potential community service projects, 75-percent of them recommended graffiti clean up.  So, if those of you who hate looking at graffiti on your way to work every morning can provide the necessary supplies and supervision, I can have 100+ pairs of tireless young hands ready to wield buckets and scrub brushes on any given weekend.  Not only would the kids love the opportunity to do something positive with their friends, but I think that you adults would benefit from a little personal interaction with a few of the thousands of the caring and vibrant young people in our community.

Sincerely and in defense of the hundreds of talented and caring 11-15 year olds I work with every day,

Title: Re: Graffiti
Post by: 80thstreet.observer on February 19, 2008, 04:12:58 PM
Mr. C brought up a good point.  Vandals who graffiti on private/public property in JH are not necessarily from poorer surrounding neighborhoods. 

However, I disagree with Mr. C on other points.

First, I don't agree that the fostering of mutual respect and sense of community is needed to "make some vandals think twice before defacing public and private property".  Some people in your community may not respect you because of your age, race, economic status, etc, but that hardly gives you any justification to run out and destroy public/private property. 

Second, I don't agree that some vandalism should just be attributed to "teenage rebellion" and that the resulting graffiti problem cannot be eliminated altogether, even if we provide "alternative activities and a rich community life".  When these vandals destroy property (to "gain respect and rebel status within their peer group" as Mr. C suggests), they don't stick around to proudly showcase their "art".  They run away.  Why?  I submit that they run away because they understand the difference between legal and illegal activity.  "Teenage rebellion" is not a defense to illegal destruction of property.  To allow "teenage rebellion" to be some kind of defense or excuse for engaging in illegal activity is to insult (and disrespect) the sensibility and intelligience of teenagers.  If caught in the act, the vandals (or their parents) should be made to pay for the clean up, not the community.  If appropriate, the vandals should be prosecuted to the fullest extent of the law.  Teenage rebellion may spur vandals to destroy property, but it is not a defense for their action, and the community should not sit idly by and just proclaim that we cannot eliminate graffiti problems altogether. 
Title: Re: Graffiti
Post by: Mr. C on February 19, 2008, 05:14:35 PM
80thstreet,

I did not mean to give the impression that I support, defend or find justification for the actions of the vandals.  Defacing property is certainly criminal, and those caught should face some penalty (although I would not go as far as saying that teenagers should be booked and jailed). 

I merely wanted to move the discussion away from what I interpreted as the demonization of an entire subpopulation of our neighborhood for the actions of a tiny number of vandals.  If this is a legitimate problem then we should be thinking of solutions, not simply complaining.

The fact that graffiti remains an issue after the NYPD has spent 30+ years hunting down graffiti artists, tells me that if we want to live in a graffiti free neighborhood we cannot rely on catching and penalizing the vandals. The only other option we have is to clean it as fast as they paint it. I'm offering one plausible way to get that done--enlisting some of the thousands of junior high students living here.  At the same time I guess I'm tentatively hoping that bringing kids in as part of the solution will create a certain amount of social pressure against graffiti, eventually reducing the amount of graffiti being produced.  That, however, may be no more than wishful thinking.

Post by: alleyinJH on February 19, 2008, 05:40:45 PM
I work in a large high school near here and tagging is rampant.  I've had many conversations with students who've been caught and they just don't get what's wrong about it.  They (meaning the students I've spoken to- not teens as a whole) don't understand the concept of respecting the property of others. What they're most upset about is having to take the time to go to family court.  But in most cases, it doesn't stop them from doing it again.  I've observed that kids are very quick to touch, throw, destroy, and in the worst cases, steal others property without a second thought.  They're even oblivious to personal space and are quick to touch each other.   One of my colleagues attributed this to the fact that many of the students in our school live in crowded households where they don't have any personal space of their own.  Often they have to share rooms with others, or even give up their rooms to elders.  I'm not sure I fully agree, but I can see how a kid so used to never having personal space or property wouldn't learn concept of respecting the property of others.  And while I advocate teaching our kids these lessons through service learning projects, these lessons must also come from the parents.  Most parents know where their kids are after school, but some don't, and it's likely that those kids are the ones tagging.  I think looking at a child's notebook (where they often practice their tags) isn't that hard.  I think everyone knows what a permanent marker and spray can look like, and that these items shouldn't be in a child's bookbag when he or she leaves for school.  And of course there are kids who get these items from their friends and whatnot, but if vigilance were increased, then perhaps there would be a decrease in graffiti.  It certainly couldn't hurt.

Alley, you hit the nail on the head when you state that parent involvement is critical.  Although from an entirely different era (early 70s), I am originally a child from the Kingsbridge section of the Bronx from the tender age of 2 until adolescence when we moved to Queens.  Graffiti and all sorts of vandalism were rampant throughout this section of the Bronx at the time.  I strongly believe my parents had a HUGE impact in why random acts of vandalism never crossed our minds.  We were taught the concept of pride and sharing and modesty, to respect our household and the property of others.  Your colleague's suggestion that lack of space may play a role in this is a little off mark.  I, along with my 3 siblings, grew up sharing 1 bedroom.  Talk about a lack of space!  Nonetheless, when my sisters were given permission by my parents to use makeup, I was tempted by the mere presence of lipsticks, eye shadows, etc. sitting atop the dresser.  I dared not touch, as I was taught to ask permission before taking.  Prior to getting married, I shared a bedroom with my younger teen brother.  No problems there either.  I credit my parents for teaching us these values.
