Reservations
« on: March 21, 2015, 08:54:19 PM »
The trend in New York is for restaurants to work on a first come, first serve basis and to refuse to accept reservations.

There are two reasons. One is that taking reservations costs money (staff paid to man phones, unless reservations are made online). The second is that no-shows and last-minute cancellations cost restaurants a great deal of money.

So here are my questions...

If a restaurant in Jackson Heights offered reservations but required all reservations to be made on-line, would you accept that?

Secondly, what would be your view if the restaurant required the person making the reservation to make a deposit via credit card (e.g. $20) that would be fully credited against the guests' bill, but forfeited if the reservation was not cancelled at least 48 hours in advance and the guests failed to show.

Third question, given the demographic of this area ... are the above questions consistent, or not, with the level of adoption of computers and smartphones in this area?

Actually, my post above was a bit misleading. Taking reservations on-line does cost money. Open Table, the most popular, charges restaurants about $1 per person plus a sign-up fee plus an annual fee, all of which is passed on to the guests.

Interesting questions...I think I would be inclined to look poorly on a nonrefundable deposit for a reservation at a restaurant in Jackson Heights.  I've eaten at a lot of restaurants around here, even during peak times, and usually you don't need a reservation. One of the good things about the neighborhood...so many great places and not too competitive. And a lot of us have little kids, which are certainly wildcards when it comes to our ability to go to restaurants at specific times.

The only places I can think of where I've made reservations were La Fusta, Uncle Peter's, and the late Sapori d'Ischia.  Those places tend to be busy enough on weekends that I felt like we needed a reservation, but they would have easily filled our table if we didn't show up.

Sapori d'Ischia might be a cautionary tale. They charged for bottled water and wouldn't offer tap water...judging by online reviews, a lot of people HATED being forced to pay for water as it just seemed like nickel and diming.

I would have no problem with this at an excellent, well-known Manhattan restaurant for which you needed reservations well in advance. That's a "scarce good" and the deposit which just be the price to have a chance to dine there.

Guess what I'm saying is, this place would have to be much better and more crowded than preexisting restaurants in the area before I wouldn't find this idea objectionable.

Quote from: redge on March 21, 2015, 08:54:19 PM
The trend in New York is for restaurants to work on a first come, first serve basis and to refuse to accept reservations.

I think that restaurants that do this, are in a very fortunate position in that their demand outstrips their capacity. As an example. look at Mu Ramen in LIC.
Unless you are in a position like that, I would advise against requesting a non-refundable fee for reservations b/c it would only discourage diners. Rather, what you could do is wait and see how well the place does. If you're product is great and there are times/days where your demand is totally overwhelming your ability accommodate all diners, then for those very peak times, why not add a small surcharge?
http://www.nytimes.com/2012/09/05/dining/restaurant-prices-can-vary-by-reservation-time.html

Again, I did you would need to be in a very fortunate position as a restaurateur to do this.

Best of luck.

I love Open Table, but I think the fee thing is a bad idea. For a neighborhood spot it sets the wrong tone, I think. You're hoping to have a relationship with your regulars, right? The hope, I assume, is to cultivate trust to the point that somebody wouldn't no-show, not because they're worried about losing $20, but because it's simply not cool. For me, the whole point of dining out is to be momentarily transported to another place; restaurants that seem to nickel and dime people on things like no-show fees (or to use NYC Peromyscus's example, bottle water), you break the illusion and ruin the moment. This is why using a Groupon in a restaurant is no fun as well.

Also, I'm not really sure what the point of a $20 fee is. It's not enough money to really compensate the restaurant for the loss of the table, and it's not enough to dissuade somebody from canceling if they have a good reason. Seems like it'll just make people feel bad and won't have much of a financial impact, except if it turns people off.
