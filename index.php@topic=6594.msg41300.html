Don Coqui is a real popular restaurant in New Rochelle.  Owner and infamous  Jimmy Rodriguez is the owner and the restaurant is known as a traditional Puerto Rican white cloth restaurant &  hang out joint.  Because these people have been doing this for 30 years, they know that you must have many American favorites like porterhouse steaks, clams, crab legs etc.  It's one of the legendary "have lots of food - drinks and lots of fun" that this crew have been doing since the days of "Jimmy's Bronx Cafe"  There have been a ton of people trying to do what this guys have been doing for years, but once you go to a place like Don Coqui's you realize that they have it down to a science.  It's a true NY style hang out with food many of us grew up with.  Unlike his other spots like Sofritos in Midtown and Sazon in Chelsea, Don Coqui is a little more tone down.  My concern is that because they don't take reservations in their city locales and Astoria is also very centrally located, it will become almost impossible to get a seat on the weekends, unless you are willing to wait 2 hours for a table.

The New Rochelle locale takes up an entire industrial building and it has been in the food network channels several times, thier set up has allowed for a kitchen stadium theme where they have competitions and cooking classes.  If you are not looking for rice, pork or the "Latin thing",    don't worry - you will find plenty of stuff in their menus.  Here is their link...   http://doncoqui.com/

dssjh...I have to ask you...what did you eat that you think was that bad.  Did you try the ox tail//Tostones, ?  Did  you try the shrimps?  What did you eat that you didn't like?  What did you think of the masitas  or chicharron?  I am asking because I can tell you what I believe was good at Don Coqui and what I didn't think was so great.  My biggest complaint with Don Coqui is that they are too careful in their "salt" content.  Unlike REAL PR food which is "mostly" identical to much of his menu.  For example...When we do a lobster we boil it with plenty of seasoning.  When we stuff it , we don't add any breading.  We just throw a garlic or sofrito based "cheap" seafood potpourri on top of a split lobster (that's usually for special occasions)

Their paella is not really a "Spaniard" paella.  Its simply Arroz con mariscos"  Which most people from the Caribbean prefer because many are not used to the "soupy" consistency that many paellas have.  Their Seafood rice is fluffier, drier and not as rich.  Hence, I don't know why they even call it paella (other than in OLD San Juan you will find Spaniard restaurants with two types of Paellas, traditional & Puerto Rican Paellas...which is very confussing because no one makes a PR paella the same way - maybe because WE DONT CALL IT PAELLA! )
I've been to Don Coqui in New Rochelle, Sofritos in Midtown, Sazon downtown.  These are hangouts that if you are smart, will order the "traditional" day to day food that grandma cooked.  If you try the fancier over the top dishes, most will be confused or even disappointed.  My advise to anyone going to any of these places is to order the "traditional" simple dishes.  Not only will you "not" be disappointed, but you will save some $$    :rockon:
