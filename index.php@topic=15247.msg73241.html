The market can correct some things, but it usually needs some goosing with public shame and bad publicity. Consumer Reports, the long-time non-profit based in Yonkers has a blog that takes companies to task. They have a staff that investigates, follows up, and usually gets, if not accountability, some answers. Their website is at www.consumerist.com . They have extensively followed and shamed the cable companies for ridiculous treatment of customers and have managed to get them to change some policies, particularly policies that affect subgroups of customers or localities. They have a tips link at tips@consumerist.com. They have shown an interest in their blog in helping out folks in NYC and Philly as well as nationally.

The New York Times also runs a consumer advocacy column that has investigated and followed up for individual consumers and class groups before as well. "The Haggler" is written by David Segal. The columns can be found at this link: http://www.nytimes.com/column/the-haggler. You could also suggest a tip and complaint to them and see what they could do. Sometimes a call from a media source is what is needed to loosen the gears and make change happen. At the end of a recent column he gives the following as contact info: "EMAIL: haggler@nytimes.com or tweet to @TheHagglerNYT. Keep it family-friendly and under 250 words, include your hometown and go easy on the caps-lock key. Letters may be edited for clarity and length."

I have a friend who does social media and has managed to shame companies (particularly airlines) by tweeting them out publicly. That certainly usually gets a phone call from a rep at least. The more technically inclined can set up a system that tweets from a range of selected messages that provide a complaint/description/call to action at a regular time period. Make sure that it is directly sent to the main TWC account and hashtagged so that it comes up with regular TWC tweets. Just be sure to take the call when they want to make it stop!

I believe there are consumer advocacy reporters for the local TV news stations "_____ on Your Side" kind of stuff. I don't watch TV though and can't name any off hand.

I don't live in the 'hood anymore but, after I moved to Columbia Cty., NY last year, I found a real problem  maintaining a consistent internet connection with TWC.  I polled our small (80 members or so) neighborhood listserv and found that my problem was shared by almost everyone.

After many calls to customer service, I got disgusted and wrote a  2-page letter to this guy:

Mr. Glenn A. Britt, CEO
Time Warner, Inc.
60 Columbus Circle
New York, NY  10023

I told him about the terrible service and the fact that most TWC customers had similar problems.  Within a week or so, I got a call from the head of "Northeast Operations", who sent a senior technician who not only solved our problem but he gave me his card and said he would personally come over if I had any further trouble.

I've found that writing to some big cheese at various companies always gets results.  Mr. Britt probably didn't have to do anything more than refer the problem to someone else but, when directives come from the top down, they're more apt to be heeded.  Also, the threat of several TWC customers seeking an alternative source of service should make them take notice.

Good luck with your problem and don't give up!
