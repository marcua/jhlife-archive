Jack.  I'll share with you an unbelievable experience I had selling my one bedroom cooperative in a 30 unit building in 2006 in Jackson Heights.  At the time, there were 10 seniors (65+) several with serious health & mental issues living in the building everyone of us owners.  The Board President (59 years old) told me, "We have too many seniors living in the building and we don't need anymore.  Make sure you sell to some under age 50 or your buyer will be rejected."  At the time, I was senior.  I couldn't give it to a Realtor, so I listed the unit in the NY Times in June 2006 (HOT Market) and sold it in just one week at full price to a divorced 40 year old male who was immediately embraced by the President.  I doubt if this building was the only one that discriminated against seniors and I'm certain they still do exist in 2018. 

I moved to a larger and much nicer apartment nearby and had no trouble since I was an entry level senior (not anymore).

I admire you and your siblings for caring so much about your Mom and wanting to make sacrifices but why a cooperative and not a rental.  With a rental its so incredible simple.  You pay first and last months rents, sign a lease and you're handed the keys.  Landlords who lease don't live on premises and don't care about renting to a senior.  So you have NO stress.  If for any reason your Mom at the end of the year doesn't like the building or apartment you look for another one.  So easy. 

Whatever your decision is, I wish you and Mom the very best.   If you go thru with purchasing consult with an Attorney in JH's who is a senior (60+) who specializes in Real Estate Closings.  The lawyer who just did my WILL in February is the person you need.  He not only represents buyers & sellers but is an attorney for many Cooperatives in Jackson Heights.  I would be happy to email you his name and telephone number.  His office is above Capital One Bank on 82nd Street & 37th Ave.   

Again, the very best.

What you're proposing is pretty complex and maybe the way to go is to have a real estate broker show you investment co-ops properties that you can rent out long-term. That would allow you and your siblings to be the owners and ``rent'' to your mother. The problem with doing this in non-investment co-ops is, first, you'd have to know whether the co-op even allows such arrangements *before* you even bid. My guess is many co-ops would probably discourage this arrangement for several reasons. The main issue in a co-op is who owns the shares and whose name is on the proprietary lease. In your case, I doubt they would allow your mother to be the ``owner'' given her resources and I doubt they would allow you just to put your name on the lease without residing there. But I do wonder whether you can as serve as her guarantor. To make that work you'd probably need a real estate attorney to draft a rider to the purchase contract that makes you the responsible party for the co-op. It's all very tricky and you'd want to start out with a broker who knows a lot about the buildings in the area. Brokers will know which boards are more lenient and/or flexible than others. Good luck.

Also, coops are not only concerned with owners being able to pay the maintenance (which can and does go up over time) but also with owners having sufficient resources to pay any assessments that might come up for needed repairs or building upgrades. That's why they look at your overall financial picture, assets, etc. I think they might shy away from a situation where several children are chipping in for a parent. The children would have to be the official owners, I think, and if there are several, that makes the situation more complicated in case there are assessments--who is responsible, and what if the children experience financial problems and the coop has trouble getting the money owed for an assessment? That's why coops are so fussy about the finances of buyers. If there is a problem, the coop is stuck, because it's a costly and lengthy process to evict someone who cannot pay what is required. And then the financial burden falls on the other owners. Condo's might be easier to get into on this basis--you should check into that. There are not very many condos in JH, but there are some, and there may be some in nearby neighborhoods as well. But the poster who mentioned investment properties is probably on the best track.

Dear all, the answer to this is quite simple actually. First I want to point out that age discrimination by board or a licensee is a Fair Housing Law violation, based on amendment passed in 1988. Therefore anyone of status, or licensee of the state pushing back based on age of an applicant is in violation of federal law.

Now to the matter at hand. Every applicant must meet basic income and credit requirements of a coop or a lender. Refusal based on income amount (not based on occupation ) is normal and logical. Therefore incomes and assets of siblings will play the necessary role. The ways you'd structure a deal like siblings buying for a parent are:

1. Co-purchase, where all parties are owners and proposed shareholders and parent is the main occupant and all are applicants

2. Siblings buy in their name only and name mother as authorized occupant, therefore only siblings are applicants

NOTE, you must check every coop's purchasing guidelines. More in my experience will allow co-purchase, less will allow option 2 of siblings buying in their name only with an authorized purchaser. If their rule guidelines specifically state that the occupant must be sole purchaser, then that makes it known to all across the board where the building stands.

I hope this is helpful.

A few of things:

1.  Co-ops are not required to give a reason for rejecting someone, and I know of none that do;

2.  Most Co-ops in the neighborhood do not permit subletting, and having children purchase an apartment for their parent, who will reside in the apartment, would be considered a sublet;

3.  While most Co-ops permit parents purchasing an apartment for their children (mine does not), the application typically lists only the children as shareholders, with the parents putting up the purchase money.  The Board then looks to see if the kids are able to afford the maintenance on their own -- without the parents' help.  This may be difficult in the case of your mother. 

4.  While a Co-op cannot discriminate based on age or source of income, the Board can decide that the arrangement proposed -- having all the kids act as guarantors -- is objectionable where the actual resident is unable to afford the maintenance.

It will be difficult to buy a cooperative apartment in the manner that you have proposed.  I know that condominiums are more expensive, but that may very well be your best bet.  Plus - if the apartment becomes vacant for any reason in a down market, you will be able to rent a condominium unit until the market swings back up, whereas you may be stuck with either a loss or years of carry charges for an unoccupied cooperative that does not permit sublets.

Last thought - have a well-funded trust created for your mother's benefit purchase the apartment.  The trust will be the shareholder and your mother will sign an occupancy agreement to occupy the apartment.  Manhattan buildings have been permitting this arrangement more and more, but not sure about this neighborhood.

Good luck and let us know what happens.
