Real Estate & Home Improvement / Re: seeking advice about buying in jackson heights
« on: May 07, 2014, 11:13:54 AM »
In general, a bank won't consider you for a mortgage with less than 20% down (unless you're willing to pay for private mortgage insurance, which is not a good road to go down), and most co-ops allow won't allow more than 80% financing, so what you have in the bank would need to be equal to 20% of the price of the home (+ another 2-3% for closing costs).

That said, money in the bank isn't the only thing considered when banks are evaluating a loan, and there are programs out there for folks with not a lot of $ to put down. If you have a good credit score and little to no debt, you could be an attractive borrower regardless of how much $ is currently in the bank. And given your income situation, you likely qualify for any number of programs that exist for first-time homebuyer assistance. See these links:
http://www.nyc.gov/html/hpd/html/buyers/downpayment.shtml
http://www.nyshcr.org/Topics/Home/Buyers/
There are additional programs out there if you are a military vet.

All that said, NYC co-ops tend to have stricter financial requirements than lenders, so even with assistance you may still have difficulty. You could look at condos, which cost more up front but tend to not scrutinize finances as heavily.

Another option is to look for city sponsored affordable condos/homes: http://www.nyc.gov/html/hpd/html/buyers/lotteries.shtml
Again, given your situation, you seem to qualify for this program, and if the Mayor's new housing plan is pushed forward, you could see a LOT of affordable condos popping up around town (perhaps not in JH, but certainly other parts of Queens). So there's some waiting involved, as well as some luck (if there are more qualified purchasers than units, there is usually a lottery), but something to put on your radar.

I'm not a realtor, lender, or government employee with any expert knowledge of any of the above, just someone who, like you, is wanting to purchase a home and see what options are out there. The above is a bit of what I've come across in my research so far.

Good luck!

« on: April 25, 2014, 01:57:49 PM »
ProdigalSon, I agree on the postings, I don't think the removal was deliberate. All notices were still up in my building yesterday evening and this morning.

Reading through your questions, I think a few are answered in the offering, but I don't have the book with me, so going from memory and subject to my interpretation and paraphrasing:

Quote from: ProdigalSon on April 25, 2014, 09:25:00 AM
3. Can current tenants get out of their leases and get the insider prices if they want to purchase one of the vacant apartments on conversion? Maybe the tenant wants a better view, a quieter apartment, a bigger apartment, etc.. How about later on as tenants opt to move and more sponsor units go up for sale?

Per the offering, during the 90 day window in which tenants have the right to purchase their apartments, they can make an offer on only their apartment. After the 90 days, current tenants can make an offer on any apartment, but would no longer receive the preferential pricing. Even if the tenant decides they want to purchase their current apartment, once the 90 days is up, so is the price. Not sure if they'd make exceptions, but I thought this part of the book was pretty clear.


Quote from: ProdigalSon on April 25, 2014, 09:25:00 AM
4. House Rules - if the current owners have been letting certain rules slide and not enforcing them (i.e.; cats, carpeting, etc.) since the co-op is technically a new owner, could non-compliance with these house rules be used as a reason not to renew leases?

This is a little tricky. It looks like the current managing agency will remain in place well into the process (not sure at what % sold the management/maintenance is handed over to the co-op, but I think it's much higher than 15%), and the only thing that would subject you to a new set of house rules would be if your unit was purchased; your landlord would then be the individual owner of your apartment, and you'd be subject to that individual's rules. There are City and State rules governing what the individual owner can do in terms of services and rent hikes, but I don't think there is anything governing what house rules they can impose/enforce.

Now, whether or not the current managing agency will continue to be relaxed in their enforcement is another issue, and one I'm not sure anyone can predict. Of note: Home Depot on Northern is having a tent sale on carpets in the parking lot.

Quote from: ProdigalSon on April 25, 2014, 09:25:00 AM
6. The Vacant Apartments - as they are owned by the sponsor, will they be counted towards the 15% needed to convert? If I counted correctly, there are 24 vacant apartments in WP so something to consider. That makes it so much easier to reach the 15%, right?

Where did you get that 24 number? Seems high. But from what I can decipher from the book, vacant apartments that are purchased by buyers that intend to occupy the apartment count toward the total. So the sponsor simply holding onto them doesn't make them necessarily count, they have to be sold first.

Neighborhood Chat / Re: Washington Plaza going coop?
« on: April 23, 2014, 01:31:04 PM »
ProdigalSon, my interpretation was same as yours re: 15% of tenants declaring intent to buy for the co-op plan to go into effect. I did some additional research on the NYSAG's website, and found that vacant apartments with buyers lined up would contribute to this 15%, and that apartments occupied by senior citizens or disabled person(s) would not. So, there are 192 apartments, and 15% would be 27 units. However (and if I'm reading correctly), any apartments in which the legal tenants are elderly or disabled don't count toward the total, so say there are 32 apartments that fall into that category... Now we're looking at 15% of 160 units, or 24 units.

Also, I think the number of vacancies right now is quite high, and I'm thinking that's probably deliberate on the part of the Sponsor/Owner so they can get to that 15% more easily, and without the buy-in of current residents.

The tenant meeting notice was posted in the elevators and lobbies this morning. It will be Monday at 7pm, somewhere in the building TBD.

All-in-all, I think this is a great opportunity to own property in the neighborhood if the majority of the residents are interested and financially capable, but otherwise, it's a sad loss for the neighborhood. It's one of the few rental-only buildings in the neighborhood, and it going co-op, combined with the rising housing prices that are well-documented in other threads on this site, are going to further limit options for middle class families. WP has a family-oriented, working-class vibe, and I think that will soon be a thing of the past. I imagine a lot of current residents will end up being displace by this, and that's a bummer.

Neighborhood Chat / Re: fios at 35th ave and 74th street? What are the laws about putting in a building?
« on: April 07, 2014, 09:43:27 PM »
I haven't looked into FIOS, but did ask my super about DirecTV last year. He explained that the building was open to it, but in order to get it we'd need to round up 35 units to commit - that's about 20% of the building's units. I imagine the building would have a similar policy for FIOS. It makes sense. There's a certain amount of building infrastructure that would need to be upgraded - cabling to all floors, ports on the roof, and for DirecTV they'd need to install and maintain the dishes as well. Seems like an expensive endeavor if only a handful of people are interested. The super also mentioned that he had tried to drum up interest a few years ago - he had wanted DirecTV to get some soccer channels - and he was only able to get 5 signatures. So we didn't bother. But I imagine this is a common policy.

Keep in mind, this is a rental building. I imagine co-ops have a lot more flexibility to bring in new technology if they can get the board to approve and if they're willing to shell out the $.
