We live in a building with lot's of teachers and lot's of kids. They go to a number of the schools you listed and as a previous
comment mentioned, it depends on the match for the particular child. The public elementary schools generally get good
ratings if the kids fit in socially and physically. Some are overwhelmed by the large school's population. We thought that
might be the case and are happy with the choice of Garden School. Some would be surprised by the small sizes of some
of the classes there but most kids thrive with more personal attention. They also have the kind of extras that a private
school provides. The Catholic schools, from what I hear, are a mix of both issues- sometimes larger classes and not as
much individual work. I really haven't heard from anyone that a school here in Jackson Heights wasn't at least good, if
not much better. I do think that the social aspects are special to each school and while parents might not be fully aware
of it, us teachers certainly see better "fits" in some schools than others. IS130 has changed it's population significantly
from the beginning and has a much larger Indian population than IS 145 which looks to be predominantly Latin just to
mention the cultural aspect too. I would ask parents in the park if you have the time. Good luck with the search.

 The P.S.222 story is something to remember when they talk about the evils of tenure! Sometimes it's about job security against someone in
a position of authority who dislikes you and I feel sorry for these teachers being jerked around like this. Anyway, I thought of a few other options. There is a charter school in Astoria called One World (I think) that people seem to like though it's a distance away and there is also an elementary school on the Queens College campus that our kids are eligible for as well as the gifted programs. Note that the gifted programs in District 30 are way under in number of seats available than many other areas. Some elementary schools in other districts have one in each school and here they are rare, maybe a handful in all of Western Queens, but apparently well-run.
 I do strongly advocate for a local school as I think the social aspects are rewarding, not to mention studying together and eventually, community
service activities and the families getting to know one another. It seems funny to me as I often see students ending up at the same colleges
whether they were on the highly competitive track or not which makes me wonder if the school choice madness is really needed. Just my opinion.
 Yes, the students locally do place in specialized high schools. A family member teaches many of them. The commute is far and am not sure why we don't have a top high school here in Queens, other than Thompson Harris near the Queens College campus, which by the way is completely subscribed by top students in the zoned priority area who train for the test and take almost all of the openings. A top high school in Western Queens should be on someones to do list. I do think the options for schooling are ultimately good here though they take a little digging to find out
about.

I recall a similar thing back in my day ... took a combination of trains (pre-the Lexington Avenue transfer for the EF to 456 no less) to get to Bronx Science.  And I had to school on Brooklyn-Queens Day!

Now there are many more options for high schoolers.

As for the local schools, I have mixed feelings about PS 69 ... it's a great school with great teachers, and a few bad ones as well (as any school of that size).  The assistant principal for the lower grades though was instrumnetal in helping me petition to create an alternate learning situation for my child with autism, as well as schooling his kindergarten teachers about his condition.  The principal though is not as schooled in special needs children, I had to get the District to do his IEP, as the school team was just not versed in autism.  On the other hand, his pre-K teacher was amazing ... as a special ed teacher myself, any teacher who calls in daily reports and coordinates with your child's EI support teacher is phenomonal in my book, especially when said support due to budget takes almost 4 months to be hired.

But to echo Todd's point, it is a big school .... so many classes that the first lunch periods start at 10:00 or so.  And in a big school, you get that whole cast of characters with disparate philosophies.
