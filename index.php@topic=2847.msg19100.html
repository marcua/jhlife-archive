The Daily News
Don't leave home without parking spot
BY NICHOLAS HIRSHON
DAILY NEWS WRITER
Tuesday, June 2nd 2009, 4:00 AM

A popular Web site that lets motorists plug in addresses to find local parking regulations - with photos of streets and signs to help plan trips - is expanding east from Manhattan into Queens.

Launched in 2007, PrimoSpot.com - which covers the city's most trafficked areas - recently added Astoria, Elmhurst, Long Island City and Woodside, and hopes to encompass the entire borough by year's end.
The next batch of neighborhoods - expected to go live within a month - will include Jackson Heights, Middle Village, Maspeth and Ridgewood, said site founder Mike Hill.

"We're just going to continue east out to Flushing Meadows-Corona Park and probably take a break there and see how user demand goes," said Hill, a Brooklyn computer programmer.

Hill's service, using Google Maps to show alternate-side rules and hours and rates at nearby parking garages, is also available as an application for the iPhone for a one-time $1.99 fee.

Community leaders hailed the innovative online database, which will soon offer a price-comparison tool for garages

I mentioned it in my post.  I know that permit parking was being considered when the congestion pricing plan was being considered.  What I don't know is how it was proposed to work here in NYC.

In many neighborhoods of Boston, and in some of the smaller cities outside of Boston such as Cambridge and Somerville, there are permit parking programs in effect.

How it works:
If you own a car and live in Cambridge, in order to park on any residential street in the city, you must have a Cambridge resident sticker on your car.  To get a sticker, you must prove your car is registered in Cambridge, and you must provide some evidence that you actually live at that address, such as two letters postmarked within a month of applying for the permit, or a utility bill.  Permits are cheap - maybe $8 a year.

Each household can also get one guest permit.  I believe guest permits can be used no more than 3 days within any 7 day period, but it is up to the discretion of the parking police if they think a permit is being "abused" then they will start ticketing the car every time it uses that guest permit.

Every residential street (or almost every street) in Cambridge has signs that say you must have a resident sticker to park.  In the commercial areas there are meters that allow you to park for up to two hours (no guest permit required).

So basically, if you are not a resident of Cambridge you cannot park for the day on a Cambridge street unless you are visiting someone and using their guest pass.  There's always the option of paying for parking at a lot or a garage.

I lived in Washington, DC for a number of years in a neighborhood with Residential Parking Permits (RPP)...they were instituted expressly to prevent commuters parking there during weekdays.

Basically, you get a sticker with a zone on it if you are a resident (you get it with your registration sticker), and then can park your car anywhere in the zone. 

Most of the RPP zones had 2 hour limits for non-residents.  However, these limits only seemed to be enforced if a car had been left somewhere for a few days.  In the end, there was never any parking in the neighborhood anyway!

Social networking to find parking?
« Reply #12 on: November 19, 2009, 12:36:16 PM »
I found the following New York Daily News article interesting and a bit amusing at the same time.  It's about a group of people in Park Slope, Brooklyn that started an online networking site called Roadify.  Roadify's purpose is to assist registered users in finding parking space in their neighborhood.  Apparently, parking space is limited in Park Slope and can take a good while to find.  Sound familiar?

The way Roadify works involves users sending text messages informing others of parking spaces that are about to become available.  The site just launched this past Monday, but criticism has already started.  Among complaints is that this parking system will promote texting while driving.

By the way, those commenting on the Daily News site are also critical of Park Slopers in general.  There are also the views pro and against car ownership.

I'm curious if a system like this can work.
