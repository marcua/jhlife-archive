Neighborhood Chat / Holiday Decorations at PS 69
« on: November 08, 2011, 03:34:50 PM »
When I went in to vote today at PS 69 I was met with the the traditional Thanksgiving holiday decorations - turkeys, cornucopias, leaves, and the like.  Along with these decorations was a stylized image of an Indian holding a basket of corn and wearing a headband with a feather - right next to the images of the turkey, etc.  I looked around and couldn't find anyone from the school to talk with so I took down the picture and brought it over to the security guard in the foyer and asked to speak with someone from the school.  Nobody was available so I left a note explaining that the context of this image is offensive and it should not be on display.

A few minutes later (as I was getting my ballot, no less) a woman who worked at the school rushed over to me, screaming, demanding to know why I took down the holiday decoration.  I calmly explained that this image should NOT be used in a holiday display and that I find it offensive.  This woman (I didn't get her name, unfortunately) said that I had no place to remove the image and that I have no say in what happens in the school because I don't work there and don't have a child in the school.  I explained that I'm a community member and I DO certainly have the right (and responsibility, IMO) to take part in ensuring that the children of our community are educated in a responsible, respectful manner.  This woman (wish I had her name!) said that they do teach about Indians as part of history and stormed off.

There are quite a few things that I found troubling in this exchange.
1) A stylized image of a nameless Native American should NOT be part of a holiday display which also includes images of a roasted turkey.
2) Teaching about Native Americans in the context of Thanksgiving - as part of history, a culture that existed only in the past - perpetuates the line of thinking that Native Americans are a historical, extinct culture which is not present in NYC. 
3) The principal/school leaders/PTA/etc. should welcome the participation of ALL members of the community (particularly since they are a publicly-funded institution).
4) The woman who yelled at me did so WHILE I WAS VOTING and caused quite a dramatic scene in front of other voters and poll workers. 

I doubt that the people who hung up these decorations intended to offend anyone; however, for an educator NOT to recognize why this image is offensive (or, seek to enter a discussion about WHY this image may be perceived as offensive) is very troubling.

Neighborhood Chat / Re: 75th street homeowners mighty annoyed at street changes
« on: September 17, 2011, 12:31:33 PM »
I also live on 75th St. and didn't know about the traffic change until a couple of days before it happened.  There were no signs up in our building, nothing sent through the mail, nothing.  We were out of town for about 4 days and came home to the giant signs on 37th Ave. saying that the traffic would change direction that week.  As a resident of 75th St. who finds myself reasonably aware of community issues I was shocked that there was no advance notice of this.  And I can definitely see how some folks would have been left out of the discussion. 

Also, there's the possibility that people didn't know that buses were going to be re-routed and that the only change would be the direction of traffic.  If you don't drive it probably was a non-issue until you found out that the buses were going to be re-routed.  There was very little, if any, notice from the MTA that they would actually re-route the buses.

There is a noticeable increase in traffic noise between the buses and cars honking.  We hear this in our apartment even though we don't face the street - I can only imagine how those people with apartments facing the street must be frustrated.

I was aware of the transit study (which was very well done, IMO); however, I only learned about it through this message board.

Oh, and we lost a parking spot on my street, too.  Just sayin'.
