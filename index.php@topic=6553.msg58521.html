Life long resident of 34 years. I stay away from the "hipster" term because I find it repulsive. The Lower East Side (where I spent most of my teenage and late 20's) was always interesting and creative, but, no, not hipsterish until around a decade ago...which kind of coincides with the after-birth of what is Williamsburg. Go figure.

All I'm trying to say is that creative and artistic are not the same as Hipster, neither is Bohemian for that matter. I'll never rally behind or associate anything redeemable with "Hipster."

What I will say is that I have always wished there were a bar with a more punk/hardcore/ska/reggae or bohemian flavor to it. Christ! We're the borough that produced the Ramones! And more immediately, the neighborhood that gave the world Johnny Thunders of the New York Dolls (like the Ramones, from Forest Hills) - not to mention Reagan Youth and Kraut and Murphy's Law.

People don't realize it, but when they talk about the sound of new york rock, they're referring to bands that came out of Queens!!!

I used to go consistently to Terraza a few years back. i found the vibe very cliquish. I'm of hispanic descent but there is a cultural difference when you're born and raised here (yet spanish speaking) than when you are born and raised in south America and you're not at your most comfortable speaking English.

To that end I would like to open a bar with the  vibe that would naturally result from having a jukebox (yes, a real jukebox matters because it tells you who frequents the place) of standard punk/roots rock/ska/reggae (and by standards I include latin american rock/punk/ska). It would also be great to have a place to watch a futbol (soccer) game with the sound ON for the english and the spanish leagues that is also capable of making a decent cocktail (terraza, you're terrible!!!)

This wouldn't be a place opened for "hipsters" or as a refuge for the recent arrival of non-hispanic transplants, but I'll bet money that's what it would become, and I see nothing wrong with that. It's always good to have an alternative to the mainstay. It's the bar I wish I would've had when I was a kid, before Giuliani made it impossible for teenagers to sneak into bars.

For the kind of bar some of you are looking for check out Terraza. It is the kind of place you find in the hip neighborhoods of Buenos Aires and Mexico City, and the LES. Yes, Spanish is the dominant language, but even if you have no Spanish, the young, artsy crowd speaks perfect English. It's easy to strike up conversations, enjoy the nightly music and pretty regular readings. Things really get going late, but I've had lots of fun here on the early side. I'm married, but I can also tell you that the women aren't shy if that's a factor for you.

What I love about this neighborhood is that it is definitely resistant to the kind of blanket gentrification that has gone down in Brooklyn, or even Astoria. Walk down 30th Avenue between Steinway and 31st Street and it looks like Bedford Avenue.

But I also agree that the demand is here for some of the types of places listed above, like Bare Burger or a exposed-wood wine bar. I don't want scores of these places, but one or two would do gangbusters.

It's EXTREMELY difficult to open new small businesses in Jackson Heights unless you're part of an existing ethnic business network, because otherwise the landlords demand extremely high (Bank/Walgreens/etc) level rents. Frankly, the best option is to find a Bangladeshi or Colombian enterpreneur who would be willing to open a place on Northern Boulevard or in one of the JBBA-protected spaces.
