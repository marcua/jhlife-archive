Attracting new business to the neighborhood
« on: May 08, 2013, 10:33:54 AM »
I really wanted hummus one night and didn't know of any place in JH with a good hummus. I stopped by a hole-in-the wall Mediterranean/Asian/Indian place (I know, weird combo) and decided to give them a try. It turns out that the place was new and the owner was very chatty so I started talking with him. I told him that I came from JH and it would be nice to have some more food options in the area. I asked if he had considered JH when he was looking for locations for his restaurant.  He said that he was very familiar with JH as he has family members that live there.  He decided against JH because he didn't think the neighborhood could support his restaurant.  His take was that JH has more families and working class people who don't have the discretionary income/won't go out as often. He felt that Astoria has more young professionals who are interested in eating out, so he had a greater chance for success in that neighborhood. 

I was kinda disheartened by that. I wonder how many other people decide not to open here because they feel the same way.  It appears that he might be right, just going by observation, but I haven't looked at the census info or anything to know for sure. Has anybody had a similar experience? Is there any way to change the perception.  Going by this forum, it seems like there are lots of people here who are interested in a wider variety of food options.

I apologize in advance if this topic has been covered to death and I missed it.

JuJu's has good homemade hummus, and there is no shortage of places in the neighborhood where you can buy garbanzos, tahini, lemon, etc. It's really easy to make hummus. Pro tip: soak your beans overnight in water that has a tablespoon of baking soda in it. 

My only real food-related complaint about the neighborhood is lack of a good burger (though I can get excellent ground beef at the farmers market). But in terms of the availability, and variety, of restaurants and markets, if you complain about access to food in Jackson Heights you're living in the wrong place. And as someone said, it is so easy to get to Astoria, LIC, Sunnyside should you want something like Salt & Fat.

It seems to me that there are plenty of people living here with discretionary income and that perceptions of Jackson Heights will change/are changing. I don't think this means it will become the next Park Slope -- that would be horrible -- but more and more people know about the great apartments, proximity to Manhattan, and amazing food. Just take a look at how the rental rates are going up.

Regarding discretionary income - Jackson Heights residents definitely have it.  You can't buy without being financially stable.

But, ironically if I knew nothing about Jackson Heights and stepped of the subway station at 74th I would not think that for a second.  Between the illegal ads haphazardly placed everywhere and random furniture sales and the gum splattered sidewalks and the pigeon dropping mountain. My impression of the neighborhood would be that it is a slum, quite frankly, and already filled with businesses that cater to a population that would not welcome new ones or have a need for them.  (Basically what that hummus business owner mentioned.)

Which leads to the quality of life problems that we are always mentioning. 

I think once those things are resolved, more entrepreneurs will be willing to take a chance on the neighborhood.  I won't lie - my mood goes down every time I approach Roosevelt to take the train. It's horrible. This morning when it was raining and people were pushing and everything was gray and dingy I wanted to scream and the first thing I did was look at real estate listings outside of J Heights at work. And then there are days when I run on 35th/34th avenue and am taken with how utterly gorgeous the neighborhood can be.  Environment matters. Once the environment is more welcoming, I do think things will come naturally. 

Also, an "us vs them" attitude is not going to work if we want to keep attracting people who care (like the people on the msg board) to the neighborhood.  There's a huge majority of the population in J Heights that I think will be migratory for the immediate future, so even if people are 'complaining' - about various things - it does mean that they are invested in this neighborhood.  I think unfortunately the large number of our problems stem from the impermanence of a large portion of the population who view the neighborhood as a launch off to the American dream, but don't imagine it as a place where they'd end up and therefore don't necessarily care about the state of things.  And I say this as a first-generation immigrant who does well for herself now.  My friends who grew up in Queens with me who are also on similar paths live in Manhattan or brownstone Brooklyn and think I'm a little odd for moving to Jackson Heights; my parents think I straight up regressed.  And I don't want them to be right, but who knows?

Thank you joopy86 for your thoughtful remarks.  As someone who grew up in the neighborhood, left after college, and then returned, there have always been pluses and minuses with regard to Jackson Heights.... and any community, for that matter.  I have always been attracted to the architecture, planning and scale of our community, and was certain - even during the "bad" years - that it would rebound.  And to a large degree it has. 

Jackson Heights has also been a tolerant, welcoming community, a far cry from its exculsionary origins.  We should all keep in mind while that things are not perfect, there are very few places not just in the country, but in this world, where so much ethnic, economic, and demographic diversity exists in relative harmony within just 100 city blocks.  Given that, it is unlikely there will ever be complete agreement on aesthetics, new construction, merchants, cleanliness, etc.  But when I compare it to more "perfect" towns - say Bronxville, Princeton - that have planned and pleasant downtowns, I cringe when I think about its impatient, monied and homogeneous population.  I worked on Wall Street for many years and don't necessarily want to live amongst them.  I think Jackson Heights is a good compromise, and much more affordable.   To the degree that if something is not available here, it is usually not far away, nor costly to get to.

So my solution is for everyone to do a little community service... or to take responsibility for their building, their block, etc.  Get involved with your building, house or worship, JHBG, or strike out on your own.  If 1% of us did 1 hour of community service a month, our world would be a better, far different, place.

I volunteer my time locally, and have met many great people... who are now friends... that I would have never encountered otherwise.  I am impressed by my neighbors.  There are many out there.  Go ahead, step out, and do something besides write on a blog.
