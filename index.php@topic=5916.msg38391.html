Den Dekker's Proposed Bike Registration Fees
« on: February 28, 2011, 10:11:42 AM »
This is impractical and an utter waste of time.


The New York Post
Bill for bike registration
By SALLY GOLDENBERG
Last Updated: 7:54 AM, February 28, 2011
Read more: http://www.nypost.com/p/news/local/bill_for_bike_registration_V0xn4OSEoMaCmen71bMK1J
A state lawmaker is trying to hit the brakes on rogue cyclists.

Assemblyman Michael DenDekker (D-Queens) has introduced a bill that would mandate registration -- much as for motor vehicles -- for every bicycle in the state, complete with license plates hanging from the rear of the seats.

He also proposed a separate measure that would force every commercial cyclist in the state to carry identification and have insurance.

If the bill passes, the license plates would cost $25 in the first year and $5 each additional year for recreational cyclists.

Commercial cyclists -- such as deliverymen -- would pay $50 for the registration plus the cost of insurance.

He introduced the bills last week in response to the major expansion of bike lanes throughout the city under Mayor Bloomberg and city Transportation Commissioner Janette Sadik-Khan.

I agree with toddg -- this seems incredibly misguided. What does Den Dekker hope to accomplish with bike registration? It seems like a complete waste of time. The part that is most troubling is how unfair the structure of the fee would be -- it makes deliverymen pay more??? This would be about the most regressive tax ever instituted in New York.

It is disappointing that this comes from someone representing our community which enjoys the 34th Avenue bike lane and has a significant population of people who use their bikes for work.

I hope that Den Dekker will quickly reconsider this proposal.

Here's more, and still more on today's news.

This was a bad proposal.   But I'd like to cautiously applaud the Assemblyman for recognizing the importance of increased traffic enforcement and the need for a different approach to the one initially proposed.

I liked the statement of his spokesperson: "The goal is making sure the public coexists together on public roads, which everybody pays for. So we have to listen to constituents who are concerned with reckless drivers, we have to listen to bicyclists concerned about reckless pedestrians, we have to do something to make sure everybody works together: bicycles, drivers, and pedestrians."

And I liked what DenDekker himself had to say: "The original intent of this bill was to enhance pedestrian and bicycle safety through increased accountability."

Great idea.  Here's my modest counterproposal.  Provide increased resources to the police for the purpose of improving traffic safety, and direct them to target enforcement efforts across all modes of travel to achieve real reductions in injuries and deaths on our city's streets.  Give the police the freedom to identify the most cost-effective measures to ensure this public safety improvement, including increased powers to use speed and red light enforcement cameras.  Yes, pay more attention to enforcing laws for bicyclists, too, but don't divert resources from the measures that are most effective at reducing the hundreds of deaths that happen on our streets annually due to automobile collisions.

If he can win passage of a traffic safety bill that targets additional resources where they can most effectively save lives, and make our streets safer for our kids, DenDekker will be a hero.
