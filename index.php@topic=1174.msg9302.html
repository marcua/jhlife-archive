MY two cents on this controversial issue.  74th Street is one of the main gateways to our community and it is a disgrace.   A friend of mine's boyfriend recently moved to JH on 74th Street and 34th Avenue.  Her first visit here was in the early evening and it was almost her last.  She couldn't believe the garbage and live vermin she saw.  She told her BF she didn't want to walk on 74th street again and asked him to pick her up.

I first came to JH in 1979 and used to frequent 74th Street but I now avoid it like the plague.  After reading this thread several times I decided to visit 74th street a few times in the last two weeks to take a closer look and I believe it is worse than ever before. 

I feel that the South Asian business owners and their patrons deserve some criticism for the state of the street since they occupy almost all of the stores.  In the late 70's and 80's before the street became predominately South Asian it was a busy commercial street but not the eyesore it is now.    I don't believe much else changed except the store owners and their patrons.  I know some people won't like my criticism but many others feel the same way.  I'm not picking on this particular community since I think many South Asians are probably disgusted by the street also.

I remember in the late 80's early 90's Bell Boulevard in Bayside became a hotbed of pubs and nightclubs.  The neighborhood was up in arms about loud cars, loud drunken behavior out 4 AM and people urinating on the lawns.  Most of these businesses were Irish owned and frequented by the Irish and other Caucasians.  The community pressured the local politicians and bars to clean up the area and restrictions of the zoning laws were put in place to  limit the number of bars.  As an American who is of 50% Irish descent I didn't feel the complaints were racist but justified.

I feel that many posters complaints here are also justified and not racist.  I'm sure others will strongly disagree but as someone with a longtime JH perspective I wanted to add my two cents.

Eddie, I don't remember what that area was like when I moved here in 1987.  I remember the neighborhood, as a whole, was not as nice as it is now.  I also remember how hard a group of us worked to get merchants to conform their store fronts and signs to be in accordance with the Historic Status of Jackson Heights.  It was not easy.  Back then the merchants that were ignoring the requests to work towards a prettier, cleaner community were mostly Hispanic.  So we, a group of Board Presidents from over a half dozen Co-ops along with single family home owners, (some of whom were Hispanic themselves) singled out these merchants with violations to try and get them to follow the rules of living in a Historic District.  The merchants said we were  racist and they complained bitterly. 

It's not easy getting people to change their ways.  When we were called "racists",  we responded by saying: 'we don't care who you are, where you're from, what your background is: these are the rules -- follow them or we will shop in stores that do.'  And then little by little, changes were made. 

I can remember our meetings and talking about the stores.  We honestly didn't know who some of the owners were.  We only knew some of the store fronts looked like crap.  And we wanted the main shopping avenue to look nicer. If many merchants could clean up and maintain their stores, why couldn't the rest?

What's wrong with that?  Why is that racist?  If you're FILL IN THE BLANK and I say JOHN DOE you have violations and you're not fixing the problem so I'm shopping elsewhere.... why is that racist?  I don't think it is.  But I'm sure some of you will think it is. 

Everyone has a responsibility to the community as a whole to uphold their end of being part of the community.  Everyone.  No matter where you're from, what sex you are, no matter your age.  Everyone is part of the community and everyone should work towards making it better.

74th St. Pre 1987 was cleaner and less busy largely because it wasn't as developed as it is today. There were a couple of 1 & 2 family houses on 74th closer to 37th Ave. that have since been torn down and replaced by commercial buildings. Several mid sized stores were chopped up in to smaller stores and every nook and cranny on the St. was filled with some one selling something. Even the tiny alley way between pizza boy and what used to be the Jackson Diner was turned in to a store !

The transformation of 74th St. from local shopping street to international center for all things South Asian brought a flood of visitors and traffic from a whole host of places. The street can not adequately support all the activity it is currently getting, wider sidewalks, more garbage cans etc. would help to alleviate a great deal of the congestion and littering issues on the Street. Coupled with stronger enforcement of litter and parking laws, and a strong effort from every level of the community to keep an eye on the street.

The problem with he tone of some of the posts here and some of the sentiments expressed by people who live in JH is they focus on the fact that the stores on 74th St. cater to a South Asian population and blame this population for the trash. The fact of the matter is, as Eddie pointed out in his example of the Irish pubs, the race or ethnicity of the commercial center that 74th st. has become has NOTHING to do with the problems the street is encountering. More people, regardless of race, more trash.

Any public effort to get the store owners and patrons of 74th St. to take more responsibility for the cleanliness of the St. has to be pitched away from ethnicity to be successful. Saying you have to clean up this street because you are a South Asian Business owner will certainly be met with resentment. Educating and encouraging businesses to do their part from a communal perspective is easier to push. Ultimately as is the case with any business, sometimes you have to hit them where it hurts to get a message across, fines, and boycotts can work wonders.

Buddy and Aronan -- Thank you for your thoughtful and insightful posts.  I agree with the approach you are advocating: shop owners who are neglecting their responsibilities should be approached about the need for them to take action.  The decisions about who to approach and how should be completely independent of the ethnicity of the shop owner or their customers.  And it should reach beyond the boundaries of one ethnicity's shopping district, so that nobody feels targeted because of who they are.  The keys to success are: (1) organization; (2) clarity about criteria and objectives (e.g. shop owners should be told specifically what is being asked of them); and (3) civility and respect. 

If anybody is planning to move ahead with this effort, I would recommend discussing it with the Western Jackson Heights Alliance or the Community Journal, since they have already initiated efforts to reach out to the merchants.  They may have some good advice.

Buddy, you write...
Quote
why is that racist?  I don't think it is.  But I'm sure some of you will think it is. 
I think this misreads the discussion we've been having on this board.  Several of us said it is harmful and counterproductive to demand cleaner streets while in the same breath generalizing about the ethnic groups of the people involved.  Some people who insist on pushing an ethnicity-focused approach have leaned heavily on straw man arguments and other diverting tactics that have confused this issue.  But nobody said it was racist to request that the shop owners do more to keep the street clean.
