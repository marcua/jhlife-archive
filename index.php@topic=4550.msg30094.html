The difference is psychological, obviously. Which is not to say it isn't valid.

But I'd advise against the word "authentic." I've got lots of Sicilian blood in me but am not as qualified to cook Sicilian or Italian food as someone who has NO relevant heritage but has spent time training in southern Italian kitchens or at least traveling extensively.

What constitutes "authentic" Chinese food? How many Chinese cook food from Szechuan and Beijing and Shanghai and Hong Kong and Taiwan? Should we throw in Tibet and Inner Mongolia too?

And, true, when going to certain places in Elmhurst or Flushing gets you something a lot closer to what might be served in certain parts of China, our Chinese take-out places serve food that has very, very little to do with China. I stop by Empire Chinese on 83rd from time to time, and while the service is pleasant and I have a couple of things I like to order, I'm under no illusions that the nice Cantonese people there are serving me anything like what they would be eating in Hong Kong.

A lot of sushi places in NY are staffed by Han Chinese. I have Thai friends who cook in Japanese restaurants too. One of the best Mexican chefs in the country is Rick Bayless, who is not Mexican.
 
As for Italian Farms, yeah I understand it's sad that the original owners are gone. But I'm pretty sure that the ravioli in the freezer doesn't taste any different because of the ethnicity of the person who hands me my change. I'd hate for the place to close altogether because of that kind of assumption. Of course it's not the same for prepared foods as places that SPECIALIZE in "authentic" prepared foods... but that's not what it IS. It seems pointless to complain about what it ISN'T.
