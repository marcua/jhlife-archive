Doing any kind of plumbing work in a condo or a co-op can really screw your neighbors up. My downstairs neighbor did some unlicensed work on their apartment and they ending up capping one of the water lines. I lived without hot water in my shower for months until it was discovered. And doing it on the sly is grounds (and deserving of) a big fine and order to remove the appliance.

You really need to speak to the property manager of your building before you attempt anything. If it can be done and/or it's allowable, they'll tell you.  Many buildings do not allow washers in units because they use more water than dishwashers, and there are new laws re backwashes that buildings have to comply with.

Your real estate agent should not be telling you what you can get away if unless they also tell you you need to get permission from the Board of your building. Just because someone puts something into they're apartment doesn't mean it belongs there. Perhaps it's allowable, in which case, everyone will be happy.  Living in a co-op means you have to co-operate.

For your own protection, do not assume that what the agent tells you is necessarily true.  A lot of agents will tell you anything to hook a sale.  (certainly not all agents are dishonest or willing to stretch the truth, but I've met more than a few).  and as I said, the presence of a machine doesn't mean it really belongs there.  People just get away with it or did it years ago before the Board was paying attention,

If it is so important that you need to install a washing wachine and it will make a difference as to whether you would make a bid, I would insist that the realtor speak to the property agent before intitiating a contract and get you an answer whether you can install a machine.

And those apartment washer/dryers without venting aren't that great.  To get one that's worthwhile you need to buy top of the line and because they don't really "dry" (they beat the moisture out of the clothese by rotation) it can take hours to dry a single load. I live in a co-op with lots of children, there are busy times to use the machines but there are also not busy times were the machines are free.   I honestly wouldn't get one of these machine to wash a lot of children's clothes, your electric bill will go throught the roof.  and the clothese aren't soft like heat dried clothese, they are "crunchy" and would need to be shook out and/or pressed.

If you live in a co-op, there are written Rules which will tell you whether you can install something that feeds into the plumbing line. You should take a look at them and/or speak to the Property Manager. Every building is different in terms of their policies on washers (dish and clothing) but if you do something that's against the rules in yours, you will get fined. You will have to pay the fine or you will not be able to sell your apartment when you want to move.  The simplest solution is to just look in the rules and if it's still not clear, speak to the property manager.   It may be perfectly fine  and no problem at all, but you need to be sure it's OK.  And as I mentioned there are issues re backwash regulations which may impact your building today, even if you know people who currently have  machines in the building (those machines may be grandfathered) or in other buildings.  Just ask or look in the Rules , then you will know.

« Reply #12 on: September 03, 2014, 01:02:32 PM »
when you attach a washer to the plumbing line (even a small one), you are not just turning water on INTO your apartment, you are also releasing waste back into the plumbing line (hence backwash regulations) . The building has to comply with strict DEP regulations as to what can be released back into the sewer system. So don't just think of it as a air conditioner or a refrigerator. It's not just your apartment that's effected by a washing machine (dish or clothes) it's the balance of the entire building. It may be OK, no big deal, but you need to find out what the building allows for the sake of the building.
