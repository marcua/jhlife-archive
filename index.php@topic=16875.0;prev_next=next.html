@jackinJH: to your questions:

No, resellers are not allowed to see at the Greenmarkets. The Greenmarkets only work with farmers and producers.

As to why it's more expensive, it isn't always, but sometimes there's a premium for fresh, local, organic, etc. Producers determine their own price, and their products aren't always more expensive or considerably more expensive. Whether or not you patronize Whole Foods, their 5 point rating system is a pretty good way to examine the meat you're eating. The difference in price is mainly due to the difference in production, if you don't care about that, then it really doesn't matter where you buy your meat. But the cheapest meat tends to be raised in not only the most inhumane conditions, but also the least healthy for humans as well. Flock raising (the number 5 on the WF scale) in outdoor, totally pastured conditions is much more expensive (fewer birds, more harvesting issues) than raising thousands (or tens of thousands) of birds in cages or having them spend their entire lives in a big building. Likewise whole scale smothering and bulk processing are cheaper than individual slaughter and small scale processing. So you're not paying for rent on a sidewalk stall when you buy a chicken or grass fed beef from Sawkill (for example), your paying for meat that's generally cleaner and in most cases healthier than factory farmed meat. Grazin'Angus Farms has a nice set of independent lab test results showing that their grass fed, pasture raised cows have, in their meat, Omega-3 fatty acids in the range of wild caught salmon.

So, just like the reason two different cars cost different amounts of money isn't because Bugatti's showroom costs more than Kia's, well you get where I'm going....

Well dig a little. Your Epicurean Farms chicken is a #2 on the scale, which is defined as "Enriched environment" with the expanded definition of "Step 1, Producers need to meet approximately 100 different standards to achieve a Step 1 certification for their birds – including providing good quality bedding (which promotes good health and welfare and allows birds to dustbathe), a maximum transport time of eight hours, and birds must not be given antibiotics or animal by-products in their feed. Each Step builds on the previous one. So birds in a Step 2 system are raised in similar conditions as Step 1 and additionally are provided with enrichments that encourage behavior that’s natural to them, such as pecking, perching and foraging. Our suppliers have found some innovative ways to do this, such as adding hay bales — it’s great to walk into a barn and see the birds pecking at the hay, standing on top of the bales, and pulling them apart."

A Sawkill bird is going to be Step 4 or Step 5 -"Step 4 is the first pasture-based Step. Birds at Step 4 live continuously on pasture or in foraging areas and are only housed at night or when seasonal conditions might put them at risk. Pasture is an area of grasses managed to provide nourishment as well as a mat of vegetation under their feet.  A foraging area doesn’t need to have grass but can include bushes and low trees that provide areas where the birds can nestle and not be visible to aerial predators. At Step 5, birds are bred to thrive in an outdoor environment and must be raised in small flocks. For the highest Step level – Step 5+ – birds are bred, hatched and raised on the same farm."

So again, it's cheaper to raise and slaughter and process a large quantity of birds in a Step 2 level than it is a smaller number in a Step 4 or 5 levels. Just like it's cheaper to manufacture a tens of thousands of cars in a large number of assembly plants using off the shelf parts manufactured in thousands of other plants than it is to hand build 300 or so cars with parts machined in house for precisely that vehicle.

It's not just the seats.
