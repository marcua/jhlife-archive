Hello

I was wondering if I could get some advice from the residents of Jackson Heights . I live in the Andrew Jackson condominium and my upstairs neighbors have two dog who constantly bark for hours when their owners are not there. Its frustrating and is affecting my quality of life . This has been going on for a year and a half.  On Christmas Eve, these dogs did not stop barking till 2:30 AM. I have done everything to ameliorate this situation , I have called 311 ,  spoke with the tenants, the super and the doorman . I have spoken several times with my management company regarding this and they have done nothing. Copper Square Realty , a company who prides themselves on professional services, has done nothing to address this situation. Is there company I can report my management company to for their failure of enforcing their policies ?

I do not know what to do and would appreciate any form of advice . THANK YOU

Some ideas:

Start a paper trail.  Document everything.  Buy a sound recording device for use in your own apartment, and when you use it, record the date and time when it is recording.  Write a certified letter to the dog owner and send a copy to the management. 

Also do some research on past precedents on this type of complaint in NYC.  Sometimes the NY Times has columns about this type of thing. 

A good way to limit the search in google if you want only NY Times results is:

Free mediation may also be an option, but I think the only way it works is if your neighbors respond to a letter sent by the mediation center, which seems kind of unlikely in this case.

Soothe your savage neighbor with free mediation

from the article
Ask any managing agent or real estate lawyer what apartment dwellers complain about most, and they will tell you neighbor noise--the kind produced by the neighbor's kids, contractors, subwoofers, weird exercise routines, etc.   

Too often, one neighbor's grievance triggers another's defenses, culminating in a feud instead of a solution.  But selling, suing or suffering aren't the only options: There is also third-party diplomacy offered by a non-profit mediation service.

The Safe Horizon Mediation Centers in Manhattan and Brooklyn are two community alternative dispute resolution programs that have mediated thousands of neighbor-on-neighbor disputes. More than a quarter involve noise, but the peacemakers at Safe Horizon have resolved everything from harassment complaints, to issues around pets, to co-op boards paralyzed by personality and communication issues.

Here is how it works:  You or any interested party (like a board member or managing agent) contacts the mediation center in your borough.  A case manager reaches out by phone or letter to the parties in conflict to arrange a meeting, which could last for 30 minutes to two hours. About 60 to 80 percent of conflicts reach some sort of resolution then. Everything is confidential and free.
