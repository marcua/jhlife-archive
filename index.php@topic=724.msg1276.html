Eye In The Sky' Sets Sights On Queens
By Liz Goff

The New York City Police Department launched its "Eye In The Sky" program in Queens in January, with the installation of security cameras along shopping strips in Astoria, Elmhurst, Jackson Heights and Flushing.

Dubbed "Operation Argus", the program, established by Police Commissioner Raymond Kelly, calls for the installation of 505 wireless cameras throughout the Big Apple at shopping strips, malls and other strategic, high-traffic locations, according to police officials.

The program was designed to help deter crime and nab the "bad guys" who prey on shoppers, pedestrians, local merchants and street vendors. Images caught by the cameras are fed back to a NYPD facility located at the borough command, where they are monitored and archived for future reference.

...

"Operation Argus" security cameras have been installed in Astoria at the intersection of Steinway Street and Broadway and Broadway at 37th Street and are currently keeping an eye on crime along the Roosevelt Avenue corridor in Jackson Heights, as well as outside the Queens Center Mall in Elmhurst, Roosevelt Avenue and Main Street in Flushing, along Liberty Avenue in Richmond Hill, and along Kissena Boulevard officials said.

The Times Ledger
Peralta wants crackdown on prostitution in Queens
By Jeremy Walsh
03/27/2008

The same vice that brought down former Gov. Eliot Spitzer is plaguing Jackson Heights and Corona, a state legislator and community groups said last week.

State Assemblyman Jose Peralta (D-Jackson Heights) said prostitution rings on Roosevelt Avenue are damaging the psyches of the women and children in the area with obscene promotional materials.

The leaders of these prostitution rings distribute business cards with glossy pictures of naked women, a phone number and, generally, the phrase "free delivery," Peralta said.

Calling out "Chicas, chicas, chicas," the men stand in areas of high pedestrian traffic, especially 83rd, 90th and 111th streets, Peralta said.

The behavior is particularly rampant on evenings and weekends, said Yonel Latellier, Peralta's chief-of-staff and president of the Hispanic Junior Chamber International. The men pass out the cards indiscriminately, often to women or children, he said.

I have also always felt safe on Roosevelt.  I remember about 15 years ago I was hesitant to walk north of 37th Ave late at night because the streets were dark and empty and just did not feel safe.  But Rooselvelt was bustling with life and activity.  Yeah, hustlers of both genders, offering sex and green cards and drugs and vice and dreams of opportunities, were there for those who were seeking that sort of thing.  But the streets were also full of people who just got off a late night shift and were stopping at the bodega to buy a mango or a quart of milk.  And couples holding hands and heading to or from a bar or restaurant.  And street vendors.  And people who live in small, hot apartments just sitting outside getting air and watching the world go by.  And people like me, who were just heading home after some late night fun. 

Roosevelt feels even cleaner and safer and more mainstream now than it did then.  And the cross streets and northern avenues feel much safer to me.  Do I want Roosevelt to feel like Times Square does today?  Ummmm.
