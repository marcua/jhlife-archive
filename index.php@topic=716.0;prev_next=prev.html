The researchers didn't do their homework.  The article neglected to study, and it boggles my mind that this didn't occur to the researchers, that many hundreds of outer borough folks commute into the city via the subway, just like Manhattanites who need to "...walk to the bus or subway. Walk up and down stairs to stations."  Basically their premise is that Manhattanites just have to walk more, getting more exercise than everyone else.  Well, I and everyone I know personally in the outer boroughs who work in Manhattan get just as much exercise commuting into Manhattan every day.  Why limit the so-called health benefits of commuting to Manhattanites?

I agree with you judibean.   I think most people in the outerboroughs do their fair share of walking on a daily basis, not only by walking to a subway or bus, but also by going for groceries, errands, visiting restaurants, etc.  This does not even include all those that incorporate daily exercise at local gyms or those that bike and run in Queens, Brooklyn, the Bronx and Staten Island.

This argument should focus more on why some people are getting heavier.  People need to learn to make wiser choices in terms of following a healthy and balanced diet and moderate the intake of sweets.  Some form of exercise is critical, and quality produce must be readily available and affordable for all to enjoy.  When I browse the supermarket aisles, it has occurred to me that eating processed food is sometimes cheaper than eating whole foods.  I remember a few years back when Waldbaum's offered doubling on coupons up to and including $1.00.  That meant that if something cost $2.00, you could get it for free.  Mind you, many of these coupons were for hamburger helper and similar products.  It was rare to come across a coupon for produce items.
