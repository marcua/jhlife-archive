Hey LookingInJH.  Jackson Heights is a wonderful neighborhood.  I moved here for the same reasons.  I think the long list of buildings being discussed shows that you have lots of good options.  Nothing is going to be perfect but you have to find the trade offs that work for you. 

I would say that the local brokers, who get most of the listings, prefer buyers to work directly with them and not through an outside broker (that way they get the full 6% commission).  This obviously has some risk as you would not have a broker working on your behalf.  But if you know the area and pricing well, which it sounds like you do, then it might open up some additional possibilities.

One reason for a large difference in price between apartments might be the condition of the apartment.  As these are older buildings, some apartments have been well maintained and upgraded and others have not.  Renovations can be very expensive and a big headache.  So if the apartment is larger and in better condition, I can see that type of price difference.  I don't know the listings you mentioned but that is one possibility. 

You are correct that the Towers and Fillmore are each a single coop and each building of Hawthorne, Elm, and Chateau are individual coops.  So each entrance is it's own coop.  I don't know about the Delano or Colonials.  So you would essentially be on the board in Hawthorne, Elm, and Chateau.  Especially with your travel schedule this might be something for you to consider.  Your travel might also be a negative during a board interview for these smaller coops. 

Lastly, I don't think anyone cares if you live in a large apartment by yourself.  As long as you have the financial ability to cover the maintenance, I don't see a problem.  They might make assumptions but I don't see it as a reason not to get the place you want!

Quote from: Cyril108 on September 28, 2016, 06:52:02 PM
I would say that the local brokers, who get most of the listings, prefer buyers to work directly with them and not through an outside broker (that way they get the full 6% commission).  This obviously has some risk as you would not have a broker working on your behalf.  But if you know the area and pricing well, which it sounds like you do, then it might open up some additional possibilities.

There is more than one local broker in Jackson Heights, so you'd only have a broker not solely working on your behalf if your broker is representing the seller too. In New York State, brokers are required by law to disclose this. It's not necessarily a bad thing. In those instances, the broker's fee is usually reduced a little bit (from 6% to 5%, good for the seller!) since the broker doesn't have to split the commission. And while your broker is also representing the seller's interests, your broker also knows a little more to get the deal done (what's the seller's real number to sell, and what's the buyer's real number go buy).

The benefit of the local broker is he or she is more likely to have insight in to a building's financials and know the board requirements (income requirements; debt to income ratio; financial reserves required after closing). I wasted time and money with my first broker when she steered me to an apartment in a building I later found out required buyers to have significantly more money in the bank after closing than I would have had (also suggesting to me problems down the road with big assessments). I made an offer on that apartment and paid a lawyer to review minutes for a building I simply didn't have enough money saved to pass the board requirements. My second broker got a solid sense of my financial temperament, and really knew a lot about how the building's were run, their financials, and board requirements. I'd be happy to give you a referral via PM is you're interested.

I believe that the 3-bedrooms with the sunrooms are really "classic six" apartment layout; a design that is more prevalent in pre-war buildings.  The "third" bedroom is really a maid's room, which is connected to the dining room or kitchen while the other bedrooms are on the other side of the apartment.  Moreover, the maid's room normally has its own bathroom and closet.  Hence, the layout is of a different era that does not make sense to us today, i.e., most people do not want their maid living in their 3 bedroom apartment when the maid can go home at night and report for duty the next day.  In any case, the prices seem to be dictated by what people are willing to pay for them.  With that being said, if I were willing to pay over $1,000,000, I would start my search in Manhattan!

With respect to brokers, local brokers can get you inside information to the extent that they have specialized in this neighborhood as professionals and live here as well.  They know the quirks associated with each individual coop, whether 14 different coops that make up the Hawthorne or a single large coop representing all the shareholders in the entire complex.  Local brokers can also set realistic expectations (i.e., the amount of time it takes to get a meeting with a coop board) and have more sway with the coop boards to the extent that they often have personal relationships with other members of the community.  And, as others have mentioned, the local brokers know which listings are coming up, i.e., they live in the neighborhood and hear of apartments becoming vacant as a result of estate sales - people do not tend to move out of the neighborhood voluntarily.  If a broker knows you are serious, that inside information can be invaluable and can mean the difference between getting or not getting the apartment you want. 

As to buildings in JH, the Towers is an awesome complex, if you do not have to take the train.  If you have to take the train, it is a bit of a hike to 74th Street (where all the express trains are) or 82nd Street (where the 7 Train runs).  If you do not have to take the train to work, I would say that the Towers is an awesome building - especially because there's tons of food options nearby on Northern Blvd.

That Hawthorne Court apartment with the sunroom made into a den was altered from the original Hawthorne Court layout. All the apartments in Hawthorne Court's 14 buildings have the same layout--as someone said, alternate buildings have either the larger size or the smaller size. The sunroom is the second bedroom in the smaller ones, and the third bedroom in the larger ones. Many apartments, though, have been altered in various ways over the years. The reason for the large price differential between the 3 bedrooms and the 2 bedrooms is simple: low supply and high demand for larger apartments. Prices are what the market will bear. If there are people willing to pay that much more for 3 bedrooms, those units will be priced that high. There is no real estate judge sitting on high somewhere deciding what's a fair price for a 3 bedroom in JH. Prices in these buildings have shot up just in the past year or so, I assume due to low supply and high demand. Lots of young couples priced out of Manhattan and Brooklyn who want a couple of kids and need that much space.

The thing about these small individual coops is--they are all different based on their individual histories (was the building well maintained over the decades, and well run?) which is the reason for the differing maintenance figures. Some of them have replaced their roofs and done other expensive work that has raised the maintenance, and others have not--which means that expense may be upcoming.

And who will your neighbors be--do they get along or are there difficult personalities in the building, making it hard for people to work well together to run the building? That is something that's impossible to know in advance--and it's a risk you take going into a small coop.

Do NOT go into a small coop if you are not willing to help run the building. If that's the case, go into one of the larger coops in JH. In a small coop, everyone must be willing to help--at least that's the way it should be. In reality, it's often just a few residents who do all the work, which is unfair and causes resentment.

If it sounds like I speak from experience--I do. I'm in Hawthorne Court, having arrived here 5 years ago. It's worked out well for us--we love the apartment and the garden--but it's definitely something to get used to, living in a small coop where you have to help manage things, and where there can be personality conflicts that can affect that process.

We came from a much bigger coop in Manhattan, with a management company and a unionized staff. A very different living situation. And yes, a small coop will want residents who are around most of the time for that very reason. To handle some of the responsibilities. That's the ideal, although in practice that's often not the case. In my coop we have two absentee owners, people who have owned apartments here for decades, but who now live elsewhere and are rarely on the premises. Subletting is not allowed, so those apartments are almost always empty. The good thing about that is we use less water in the building (lower maintenance), and there is less demand for the laundry machines and less use of the elevator. But the downside is that there are fewer people to help with building management. So on the rare occasions when an apartment does turn over, we really do stress to a potential owner that we want whoever buys the apartment to be willing to pitch in and help. Just to explain why it really is an issue, at least in the small coops.
