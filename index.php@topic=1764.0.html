Do authenticity or spiciness = good food?
« on: November 02, 2008, 10:27:08 PM »
Here I was thinking that I was the first one to think of this question, but I noticed that someone on Yelp beat me to it.  The question is, does food have to be authentic to be good?  And while we're at it, I'll throw in another question...does food have to be spicy to be good?

I ask because I've noticed that on some food blogs people criticize the lack of authenticity of food served at certain restaurants.  At the same time, I read many posts from people disappointed in the spice levels of dishes that are not usually known for spiciness.  I'm speaking in terms of heat, not flavor.

Re: Does authenticity or spiciness = good food?
« Reply #1 on: November 03, 2008, 01:47:54 PM »
Sorry if this is redundant to what's been said in Yelp- I haven't read the comments.

I think people are disappointed with "inauthentic" food when they come in with a certain level of expectation, maybe based on what they heard or having had some past experience with the cuisine.  It's a mindset thing, not necessarily that inauthentic automatically means bad food.  For example, if I went to Sushi of Gari with the expectation for old school "authentic" sushi, I would cry at the site of all the adulteration and interpretation that goes on with the sushi there.  And yet their sushi is awesome!  You need to always know what you're getting into- do your research beforehand.  Then you can go to a restaurant and appreciate it without prejudice. 

As for spiciness, doesn't that also come down to personal preference/expectation?  One person's tolerance for heat is different than another's.  If I get something at Sri and they spice it in a way that I don't like, too much or too little, then in my book, they haven't done a good job.  it doesn't matter what the person at the next table thinks of the same meal because I'm the judge of my own food.   

With food being such a subjective experience, "good food" is your own judgment call.   Yelp/Chowhound exists to encourage/discourage you to try something out.

« Reply #2 on: November 03, 2008, 08:13:49 PM »
Thanks for your reply Judibean.  I agree with your statement about knowing what you're getting into when visiting a restaurant and trying out new dishes.  This can basically apply to restaurants that serve food well-known for high levels of spice like Thai food.  My question focuses more on cuisines that are not generally known for high levels of heat.  To give an example, I remember reading a negative post on Chowhound for an Ecuadorian restaurant whereby the complaint was that the food was not spicy enough.  In my experience, Ecuadorean food is not necessarily spicy hot, but it is flavorful in terms of the ingredients that are used in the preparation of most dishes (fresh herbs, garlic, onions, corn...)  Of course hot sauce can be added to any dish, but traditionally, the focus is not on heat.

With respect to the authenticity issue, I have to admit that I'm no expert of any given cuisine.  If I think it tastes good, I'll eat it.  That said, I do tend to prefer foods prepared traditionally, perhaps because I grew up eating my mom's cooking and my dad would not have it any other way.
