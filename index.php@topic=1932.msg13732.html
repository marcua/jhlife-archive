There's a lot of room for reasonable people to reach a compromise on this issue.  Provide limited space for children.  Ban ball playing.  Set quiet hours. 

But in some buildings in the neighborhood, there doesn't seem to be an interest in reasonable accommodation.  They set rules to make courtyard space the private dominion of some groups, while excluding others.  In my building, there have been complaints when small groups of adults have sought to have social daytime gatherings in our courtyard, because of the noise.

Regardless of co-op rules, I don't think anybody who buys into one gives up the right to complain when the house rules enforce exclusion and intolerance.  It is within the rights of every shareholder to seek to change the rules so that they can participate in the enjoyment of the space that they own.  I know that change is uncomfortable for some people, but really that is a part of living in a vibrant city.

I don't think it's very useful or fair to frame this issue purely as the elderly vs. the young. Where conflict exists, it's true that it crops up between longer-term and newer residents who have different visions of what the gardens are for--and of course the people who have lived here longer tend to be older. But these gardens are also one of the few places in NYC where older and younger people can naturally mingle. We live in Hampton Court, where kids are allowed on the grass, and (with the exception of a few incidents), the older residents have been lovely and welcoming to my kids, including offering them a chance to do their own gardening. The lawn is closed for 6 weeks or so in the spring for reseeding and rejuvenation, and it seems to do fine the rest of the year. We have a few common-sense rules like no ball-playing, and children can be taught not to trample the flowers in the same way that they are taught any other social nicety--the Hampton Court parents are quite conscientous about teaching respect for the plants. I know that there have been rumblings of discontent the past few years as more and more children use the garden. I think that noise is actually a much more substantive issue than the potential destruction of the garden, which I think is kind of a straw man in a discussion that's really more about change and definitions of propriety. Perhaps this is an issue that has yet to come to a head, but so far things seem to be working out pretty peacefully.

I do think that it's a bit preposterous to say that because residents have become used to things a certain way, they must remain so. Especially since I don't believe the gardens were originally designed as cloisters--there's an early ad for JH in Daniel Karatzas's book showing a couple of small children frolicking on the grass (w/a hoop!) that to my eye looks suspiciously like running amok (p. 82 for those of you who have the book) And although I'm not sure if this is accurate, a friend of mine was once told by a long-time resident that many years ago there was a swingset right smack in the middle of our garden. As times change, standards change.

I didn't realize there was a "generational struggle" going on over the private gardens of Jackson Heights.
