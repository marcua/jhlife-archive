so, my co-op board mailed out our financials, along with a new offering amendment - standard stuff, nothing to see.

except:

for the first time, the board/management chose to individually list the remaining sponsor apartments, which are rented, and the precise rent of each, rather than simply lump them together as "rental income." there are only a few (there were about 15 or 18 when i purchased a sponsor apartment back in 2002) and all the tenants have been here for a really long time (and all pay a rent that's reasonably close to the maintenance the units would bring in).

i feel strange knowing the precise rent these longtime tenants - many of whom i am friendly with - and i wonder, does anyone else feel it's intrusive to give that intimate a look at the financials of individuals? i mean, does it actually matter that person A is contributing $1240 of the $7000 per month? isn't the total/bottom line the only thing that actually matters?

My two cents.

There's nothing "personal" about the rent that a stabilized or rent controlled tenant pays;  You can find out that information by simply asking HPD and they will provide you with the entire rent history for each regulated and controlled apartment going back several years.

The Co-op may be doing this to pressure the sponsor to sell apartments where their rental income exceeds the amount of maintenance - such as where a stabilized tenant moves out (or passes away) and the sponsor proceeds to rent to a market-rate tenant instead of selling the apartment and installing a resident tenant-shareholder.  Nothing illegal about a co-op applying such pressure to sell since the sponsor does not live in the apartment, as co-ops do it all the time with varying degrees of success.

According to http://www.nyshcr.org/Rent/tenantresources.htm


Access to Your Apartment's Rent History

Apartment rents are registered annually by building owners with DHCR's Office of Rent Administration (See: Rent Regulated Building Search). Under law, the rent information provided is confidential and can only be released to the apartment's legal tenant, the building owner, or their authorized representative.

You might want to ask a lawyer about the legality of public disclosure of the rent info.
