Broadband Internet Access Recommendations
« on: December 19, 2007, 10:46:44 AM »
Time Warner and Verizon charge around $60/month for broadband internet access in Jackson Heights.

I find this obscene.  The cost of the bandwidth is nowhere near this much and the community is so densely populated that the infrastructure costs per person are practically zero.  They could rip it out and re-install it every year and still turn a profit on us.  What we're doing is subsidizing the very sparsely populated portions of their networks (think suburbs) with our monthly fees.

Can we do this ourselves?  If building management bought a wholesale uplink to the internet and shared it between tenants I figure we could drive the monthly recurring costs down to $10.  This is not as hard as it sounds since one connection could be shared between buildings; even sold to neighboring private residences.

So what's saving $50/month worth?

Every commercial building I've worked in does this to some degree, but I've never seen a residential building do it.

Even a local business that did this for-profit could charge much less than Verizon/TWC.

I don't have a direct answer for your question, but I was reading recently that some communities are organizing themselves and setting up wireless internet networks.  This is not the same as centralized municipal wireless programs -- it involves getting a critical mass of people living in neighboring buildings to cooperate on forming a wireless mesh network.  One technology for doing this is Meraki.  San Francisco has a particularly ambitious effort to build a network.  Cambridge, MA is also doing it.  I don't think this neighborhood would have enough density of interest to replicate this at an areawide level, but it should be feasible for a single building to do it at reasonable cost, at least if everybody agrees to situate their computers near windows.
