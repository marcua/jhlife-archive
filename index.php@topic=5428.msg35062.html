Each of the boroughs has it's own distinction and flavor. Queens by and large was the last to be developed, having been the farm to the city. Still in all though Queens remains the largest borough in square miles, and a large population that is the most diverse. I think one can easily say that the "average NYer" likely works in Manhattan and lives in Queens.

As Manhattan becomes out of reach, run down and depressed neighborhoods throughout the city have been seeing regentrification for years now. Sometimes this is because of the present infrastructure or perhaps the area's accessability. Nothing in NYC stands still, it never really did.

Jackson Heights in and around the '70's was just about at rock bottom. That was also the same time that the major diversity  of the neighborhood began. It was also when the the great shift began from rental buildings to co-ops.

The co-ops are what kept this neighborhood from falling apart all together. And the original now.reputation of JH was also not only due to it's type of planning but also to home ownership.

So if anyone wants to complain about regentrification, consider what many of you did here - be it more recently or long ago. We've already seen it and been ahead of many other areas for a long time now.
