Neighborhood Chat / Re: Management company recommendations
« on: December 11, 2018, 12:20:19 PM »
How small is your building?

Have you considered having your current company take on some more responsibilities?

The reason I'm asking about the number of units and your current company is that luring a full time management company is very difficult for small co-cops. The cost can be prohibitive and, on the management end, there may not be enough money in it for them to take on the building.

The board needs to assess its needs and set a clear list of what they want the management company to tackle -- and what the expectations are. I have lived in several buildings where the board and the residents haven't thought this through. They hire a management company and expect the manager to be at their beck-and-call and they don't realize that for the company to keep afloat as a business they have to have a lot of buildings under management. So you have to be realistic about the service you expect. At a large management company, a building manager might have a half dozen or more buildings to run. At a small one they might have more.

If your building has a few big projects coming up perhaps it would be best to hire your current manager to take those on for a specific fee rather than hire a full time manager. We've done this and it has worked out well.

Neighborhood Chat / Re: Tipping Building Staff on Christmas
« on: December 10, 2018, 03:46:40 PM »
If there's a big staff the other rule of thumb would be to only give to those you really know and have interacted with or those who have assisted you in some particular way.

Most supers live rent free and get a salary. You still ought to tip them before anyobne as they're your go-to when problems arise.

I can't tell you how many times I've heard folks say: `I called my super five times about this issue and I can't get him to come.' Next question I ask: `Did you tip him at Christmas and do you ever tip him when he stops by to do something?' The answer is usually no.

I know that sounds harsh but the reality is the super is a service employee and no one gets rich doing the job. It's often dirty and thankless and not that all well paying. You're expected to be on call 24/7 and deal with all manner of people.

Giving the guy $75 or whatever is reasonable.

Think of tipping as insurance for good service next year.

$75 for the super seems reasonable but if you call on him regularly then maybe more. The super is essential to tip. When you have a flood in your apartment, or no heat, or some other issue and you have to call him at 1 a.m., you want him to remember that you were the very nice person who generously tipped. He will likely respond in a much more timely manner. Cynical, yes, but true.
