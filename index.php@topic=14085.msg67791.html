This is so sad.
http://www.nbcnewyork.com/news/local/Fatal-Hit-and-Run-Queens-Jackson-Heights-Police-Teen-Funeral-361200661.html

A 17-year-old boy walking home from a funeral was killed by a hit-and-run driver while crossing the street in Queens Thursday evening, authorities say. Ovidio Jaramillo was in a crosswalk at Northern and Junction boulevards in Jackson Heights just before 11 p.m. when he was hit by a black Toyota Camry that was headed west on Northern Boulevard. Cops say that the sedan kept going after the impact.

Jaramillo was taken to Elmhurst Hospital, where he was pronounced dead, authorities say.It's the ninth accident resulting in injury this year at the intersection, but the first instance where someone died.

Den Dekker, Peralta, and Moya like to hold these types of press conferences and feel-good public forums, even as they actively work to undermine the city's efforts to make streets safer.   These state legislators do nothing in Albany to reform our state's backwards laws, and won't even come to the support of their colleagues in the Queens delegation like Sen. Mike Gianaris and Assemblymember Margaret Markey, who are working hard to bring about more effective accountability for unlicensed drivers who injure or kill.

We are fortunate to have a progressive delegation in the City Council, including Danny Dromm, Julissa Ferreras, Jimmy Van Bramer, and Karen Koslowitz, all of whom have worked hard on behalf of streets that are safer for drivers, pedestrians, and cyclists alike.   But from Den Dekker and Moya all we get is posturing and obstructionism.  I find their hypocrisy disgusting.

Peralta, at least, has done some good work fighting for safety improvements near particular schools.   And he has supported the MoveNY toll reform proposal, which will improve safety on Northern Boulevard and help fund our beleaguered transit system.  But he has lent his name in opposition to common-sense safety improvements like NYCDOT's road diet proposal on 111th Street, and has not been a leader in Albany on urgently needed changes to state law to allow for more effective camera-based traffic enforecement.  His decisions to choose today to call for new mandates for pedestrian safety education, when NYCDOT already focuses heavily on that, is as empty a gesture as I can imagine.   He really can and should be pursuing more substantive legislative reforms.
