Jackson Heights of Yesteryear / Re: The Blue Haven
« on: March 07, 2018, 02:50:49 PM »
I had never seen the inside of the Blue Haven in the many times I walked past it on the way to my Boy Scout meetings in 1954. The smoked windows , the adults only atmosphere, the darkness made it something that you only peeked it to see but not for too long certainly not long enough to attract a bouncer's attention So it was an unexpected treat after all those long years to finally see what the place looked like and to even see some of the flyers for what must have been a great night out. I'd love to see the films that were made of those cigar fumed nights.
Thee was another night club in those days on Northern Boulevard which wasn't really my territory (excepting those times that I went to the Boulevard Theater to see a movie) that I think was owned by Sam the Taylor whose sax was a familiar break in the old  three minute Rock 'n' Roll songs. Does anyone remember that place?

Neighborhood Chat / D. Keith Mano and Jackson Heights
« on: October 28, 2016, 11:36:38 AM »
D. Keith Mano died recently. He was an author who I wasn't familiar with but the obituary piqued my interest so I read and recently completed one of his novels. The novel was called "Topless" and as in many of his novels it addresses Mano's rigorous Christianity but with a twist-the novel is about an Episcopal priest who inherits his brother's topless bar. 
The point of all this is that it takes place in Jackson Heights. The bar in this novel is called The Car and is on Northern Boulevard in 60's . It sounds like it could have been Jaxs, the diner, in some previous life or maybe it was simply invented as some of the things in the novel seem to be such as The Jackson Heights Free Press newspaper. Mano was from Queens -grew up in Whitestone and seemed to know Jackson Heights fairly well. The police in this novel are accurately portrayed as coming from the 115th Precinct. Anyone else familiar with his books or  perhaps even knew him?

Neighborhood Chat / Re: Construction @ 74th Street train station
« on: July 21, 2016, 09:40:57 PM »

Why does an important hub station like our own 74th Street seems to have no user group that regularly meets with the manager of the station or with the MTA. Certainly one basic issue might be the level of cleanliness on the subway tracks,platforms,tile walls, and the terminal itself. When you look down from the elevator on the Flushing side you see layers of filth encrusted on the lights. The elevator itself usually reeks of urine. This lack of care becomes self perpetuating as many users come to expect that this is just the way it is.
But beyond that base line issue are other things less critical but still an element in creating a positive quality of life for commuters. That might include more interesting wall displays for example.In the London Network there is video based advertising that offsets the cost of maintaining the system. Why are we stuck with billboards that reflects a hundred year old form and in any event just become the target for graffiti? Why is there no trafficator that shows all the bus lines and their arrival and departure times?

Whether or not any of these things are implementable is less of a question then why we don't have the possibility of meeting with the administrators and managers of a service we use and pay for.

Neighborhood Chat / Re: Famous JH Residents
« on: April 10, 2016, 12:08:11 PM »
Jackson Heights has had a number of residents whose celebrity we can all take pride in even if their time in our community was fleeting. The first is Orson Welles who in his early years in New York was involved in theatre both as a director and performer. In the book Young Orson by Patrick McGilligan there is a brief mention of him living in Jackson Heights. The book doesn't say where and if there are any known details it would be very useful to add that to the historical record of previous JH residents.

The other is the infamous Fritz Kuhn who was the head of the German-American Bund, a Nazi front organization that was once strong enough to hold a rally in Madison Square Garden where some twenty thousand brown shirted followers attended. Kuhn was imprisoned for fraud and embezzlement(he was a crook as well as being a fascist) and then deported to Germany where he died unmourned and unknown. He lived in Jackson Heights where his residence had a police guard.The newspaper archives always described him as living in Jackson Heights so he wasn't just a visitor in the community. Does anyone know where this infamous artifact of the 30's lived?
