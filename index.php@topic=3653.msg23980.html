I'm interested in getting some opinions on whether or not it's a good idea to create a website for a co-op.  If you think it's a good idea, where do you draw the line on information that may be uploaded to the site?  Let's go on the assumption that the site would be solely for registered users living in the co-op and not available to the general public online.  I assume that a private site would be the best route?

I know there are some websites that have been designed specifically to allow residents of buildings to communicate with one another.  I think the idea is nice as more and more services and communications are being moved online, but I would be extremely wary of having of posting any information about building finances or potentially negative building issues on any online site.  The truth is that even if you restrict access, it is not that hard for someone to give someone else they know the password... or for a former owner to continue to have access after they sell.  And what that means is it's not really truly a private site. 

I would say sure for certain things - like to have a building classifieds area where people can exchange services like babysitting, or to do a book exchange, and perhaps for the building's board to communicate about building-wide events like a garden activity or a lobby clean-up.  I think anything more is asking for trouble.

I think there are some buildings that do have restricted online sites.  If I were on a board and wondering whether I should create something like this, I would first see if there might be any legal problems that could result from having such a thing, and second of all, I would contact buildings that already have one in existence and see if they have encountered any problems.  (The legal issues may not have all revealed themselves yet, as the internet is really still in its early days.) One coop that I can think of that had one was on Ocean Parkway in Brooklyn and I believe it was called Caton Towers.  It may be worth googling that one.

I think a site can have the potential to create possible liability issues.  While site administrators can be careful about posting too much information, it may be difficult to control discussions by shareholders.  Someone may inadvertently post something that can put the co-op at risk for DOB fines, etc.

A friend recently told me of the fiasco that was her building's simple Yahoo group.  Residents were using the group primarily to post complaints about the Board and the building's staff.  This doesn't sound very productive to me.  I'd stick with a newsletter type format just to be on the safe side.

Still interested in reading some other opinions.  Anyone have any pros to share?

I like the idea of having a general website open to the public.  It is good way for prospective tenants looking into a building to learn things that they might have missed say at an open house or might add to an online property listing. 

I also like the idea of a private website for residents.  When I lived in Manhattan I rented an apartment owned by the Rose Group.  They have a completely electronic system for notifying you when you had a package, regularly scheduled maintenance, tracking of pending work orders etc....   

This system was great because there was a "paper" trail for everything.  When you put a work order in, it was tracked by the managing office, the super, the maintenance crew and the tenant so everyone was kept in the loop. It all really flowed seamlessly.  For example my super would add comments like "part ordered" "Part delivered" "please let me know when you are avail." 

In addition, tenants could post classifieds within the building through the system, set up play groups etc...

I wish my co-op could do something like this however, it largely has to do with the managing company.  Also, the software program may be cost prohibitive.  Finally you have the issue of a lot of senior citizens do not have access to the internet.  However, in my old building they could call management and they would manually enter the complaint into the system on their behalf.

That being said, I am extremely pleased with my super and managing company however, I prefer electronic communication due to my weird schedule and this system would be ideal for me.  Long term, also, I think it can be an effective way to track a building's / apartment's history.

We actually had two tries for a website in our building.  The first was through meettheneighbors.org.  I moderated that one and restricted it to residents in the building.  It was more an avenue for the Board to make announcements and alerts and for residents to ask questions that could be funneled to the Board or the super.  In the end, it got very little traffic.  More recently, I helped a Board member set up a second website that was just for our building (not through a third party provider).  Also private.  It seemed like more residents signed up for this one and we posted more documents on there, like house rules, but this one also fizzled.  My thinking is that our building doesn't have enough net-savvy folks...yet.
