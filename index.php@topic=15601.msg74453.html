The article's chart forgot to include an important detail.  Queens has a population density of about 21k p/sqm, trailing behind Brooklyn 37k p/sqm, the Bronx 33k p/sqm and Manhattan 61k psqm.  Transit and population density is a biggie and ought not be omitted, though I imagine such intentional.

Heavy rail on highway ROW's is a sticky wicket.  It looks good on paper and seems obvious, but it's never cheap and it's never simple.   Highways would have to be redesigned, graded and reconstructed, brick by brick, at staggering cost.  Just drive along a city highway and take note of what would go into putting a grade restricted heavy rail line down the center of it.  Those highways of the 40's/50's/60's resulted in low density land use.  That has to be fixed, else the MTA ends up with a millstone to match its Rockaway IND gem, for heavy rail needs high density.  That's more money and more political opposition (in addition to flipping car owners the collective bird).  Also, highways are not just for compensating SUV drivers.  They are critical to our city's economy (Caro wasn't unbiased and Moses wasn't all bad).  Yes, yes, traffic generation, but there does come a point where if traffic is constricted too much, real economic harm can be done.  Products do have to be moved and deliveries have to be made. 

Overall, while it certainly is an alternative (along the LIE, maybe the GCP.  On the BQE or Belt it would be a boondoggle), it's an expensive one and one of questionable return.  An LRT or BRT might be a better fit there.
https://pedestrianobservations.wordpress.com/2014/09/01/putting-rail-lines-in-highway-medians/

I'd like to see a subway on Northern Boulevard.  It's a density populated corridor, the wide boulevard lends itself to construction, it can plug into the underused 63rd Street Tunnel and Flushing is growing into a city of its own.  The Montauk Branch LIRR also has potential.  I think the pay off is less, but given its existing ROW, it has less financial and political cost, though that would rob our borough of an awesome rail trail.

This doesn't tend to work out too well.  See Javits Center.  It was intentionally designed with limited/no parking in the area and as a result was greatly underutilized due to both the pain in getting there and the lack of parking. 


Quote
There are so many expressways criss-crossing Queens. (BQE, Belt, LI, etc, etc)

The obvious and cheap solution is to convert a couple of the lanes in each of them to subways.

In most cases that would be far from cheap and practically impossible short of demolishing the existing roads and recreating them for dual use.  Turns and grades are designed for cars, not heavy rail.  Or even light rail.  Where do you put the stations and how do you connect them to the various neighborhoods?  What other infrastructure and transit connections are there in the area?  How much does this all cost?  Who will pay for it?  How long will it take, how long will residents and businesses along the way be disrupted?  If done, how much would it have to cost to ride so it breaks even every year?


Quote
Yes, the car owners will complain.

But tough.  New York is not for cars.  It's for people.

New York is for all of its people, both car owners and mass transit users.  I don't see a need to marginalize car owners in order to expand and improve mass transit.


Quote

Agreed.  Both should have been developed in parallel, but there's that adage about hindsight...

I like the way this conversation is going.  Yes, I do agree that NYC cannot sustain the number of cars it presently has and will continue to have.  We need to redefine our cities.  Just as in the 50s & 60s the urban planners redesigned our urban areas for cars (which pretty much destroyed many urban centers) we need redefine/redesign our cities for the future.  This will not happen overnight but is a gradual process as we slowly make the automobile obsolete in cities.  By doing this we will also recreate our city culture and create more viable and autonomous neighborhoods.  Suburbs are beginning to see a deterioration as the young, ambitious and creative move to urban areas.  Many of these are not as wedded to the car ideal as those who grew up in the 50s & 60s.  I would like to see the beginning of mass transit becoming the center of our infrastructure renewal.  The automobile as a machine requires an enormous amount of space compared with mass transit.  Cars require wide roads, parking areas etc.  This land could be better used for parks and dwellings.  Just look at GCP or the BQE and all the side street parking.  Imagine that space freed up for other use.
