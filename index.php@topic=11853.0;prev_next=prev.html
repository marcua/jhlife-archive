Celiac friendly Thai/Indian?
« on: January 23, 2014, 07:12:28 AM »
I posted this last year, but hope springs eternal. I love Thai and Indian food - it's one of the reasons I love living in Jackson Heights. Unfortunately, since being diagnosed with celiac disease last year, I haven't been able to eat in the neighborhood. The problem is just that the restaurants I've asked either don't understand what celiac or gluten-free is, or don't want to have to deal with it. There are many, many Thai and Indian dishes that are gluten free in their traditional preparations, I just need to be assured that there isn't secret soy sauce, wheat starch, or wheat flour being added. (That happens at restaurants sometimes.)

I walked around to several of the Thai places on Woodside Avenue and each time got a polite but sad, "we do not want you to eat here." This isn't the case in Manhattan, where I know several Thai and Indian places who at least understand what gluten-free is and can help guide me. Once again, I'm not asking them to change anything, just to know whether or not there's gluten in the dish as they usually prepare it.

Does anyone know any Thai or Indian places - hopefully that deliver! - in JH who can answer the  question, "what on your menu is gluten-free/celiac friendly?" THANK YOU.

By the way, shout-out to El Coyote on Northern Boulevard, which is totally awesome in this way.

Actually, the problem at Thai restaurants is the use of soy and/or fish sauces while cooking. Go look at the back of your bottle of soy sauce. What's the first ingredient? Wheat! Why? I have no idea. There are soy and fish sauces made without wheat (the word to look for in soy sauce is 'Tamari') but that's the point at which I'm usually kicked out of a Thai restaurant. (To be less flip, the answer I usually get is, 'we prepare our marinades in advance and have no idea what is in the commercial soy/fish sauce we use.')

My story: I was just fine. Then one day I went for a standard doctor's checkup and there was a problem with my liver. That could be 30 different things, so the doc tested for a bunch of them and got gluten antibodies. I went to a specialist, he put me under and took an intestinal biopsy and confirmed the situation. Since my only "symptom" is organ damage, I have to get re-tested for gluten every six months to find out whether I'm being a good boy and the organs aren't continuing to be damaged. FUN TIMES.

Different restriction but the same problem with getting a trustworthy answer about food ingredients: soy/legume sensitivity. Most Asian restaurants will use soy or peanut oil for cooking, which is understandable, although there may be curries that don't use the oil. Tortilleria Nixtamal at least makes their own old school tortillas, and since they're purists, use only corn oil, but try finding out which vegetable the "vegetable oil" is in most restaurants around here. Just about every commercial brand of tortillas--including those in the Mexican food stores--has soy in it.
