Coop Board Question
« on: November 04, 2016, 03:56:05 PM »
We just purchased a coop that needs a full gut. Everyone in the building (self managed 16 units total between the two buildings) is very aware of the condition of the apartment. Unlivable, even during construction. The coop does not allow subletting, but extended guests are allowed upon board approval. Our neighbor upstairs reached out and said that his unit is vacant, he moved out and is thinking of selling it next year. While we await for construction to be done downstairs, we could live in his place, pay his maintenance, to keep an eye on the construction and help speed it along, check in on it, etc. and not have to pay as high of rent from our current rental. And we just got word that the board said no and is now pissed at us. We're so confused. As current shareholders, we aren't just anyone...they've already approved us, we own shares in the building now, and they're our neighbors! Why do they care? And if this helps with the construction time, which it will because we can be there everyday in the event of questions, mistakes, etc. why wouldnt they want this for the themselves and the building as a whole?  Has anyone experienced something like this? Any recourse? It seems so spiteful for no reason. Welcome to the neighborhood

It is likely that one of your neighbors will read this.  You do not want to create a more awkward situation.  It may make sense to try to patch things up with your neighbors.  When something happens in the building, i.e., big water leak, lack of heat, broken laundry equipment, hiring/firing vendors, etc., you and your neighbors are the ones that will work together to solve the issue.  In other words, you do not want to have bad blood with your neighbors in a context where you all depend on one another to some degree.

Sometimes boards are sensitive to being accused of disparate treatment: "you wouldn't let me sublet my apartment but now you're letting him do it!"  It can weaken a coop's legal position if someone challenges them on a past or future denial.

On the other hand, under the business judgement rule, courts are reluctant to second guess businesses, including coops, on their decisions (as long as the decisions don't have a discriminatory effect).  You could argue that the unique circumstances of your request wouldn't open the door to a wave of sublets.
