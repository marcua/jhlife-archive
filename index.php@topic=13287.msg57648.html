For those worried the 82nd St. BID will cause commercial rents to rise, you might be relieved by the findings of this 2007 report from the Furman Center, which found that smaller BIDs made up primarily of retail spaces had little impact. (Bigger BIDs made up of office buildings did have an positive impact on property values, probably because those groups have a lot more money to spend.)

http://furmancenter.org/files/publications/ImpactofBIDcombined_000_1_1.pdf

In any case, I have never seen a clear case made for why commercial rents in our area of Queens are so outrageously high. I suspect it has little to do with the frequency of trash pick-up or holiday lights. It's the landlords, stupid, and the tax structure in which they operate.

Corinne Lestch was able to get a fake ID on Roosevelt Ave. in Queens.


It took me just an hour and a half to get a fake ID in New York City’s ground zero for the fraudulent document business — Roosevelt Ave. in Queens.

The Jackson Heights neighborhood is the epicenter of fake paper mills — rackets that fuel teenage drinking and identity theft and also create fake green cards and passports that can pose a serious security threat.

At first I was laughed at for looking like a cop. But when I swapped my leather jacket for a red hoodie, a guy carrying boxes was quick to call a friend who could help me out.

“You need a fake ID?” the man asked. While several cops roamed the gritty avenue, he led me into an electronics store to get my picture taken for $10.

I wrote down my information on a piece of paper, and then he told me the phony card would be ready in an hour. He took $100 from me and slipped away.

After 90 minutes, I was led into a narrow staircase in a seedy storefront where we made the exchange — my new fake ID in return for another $50.

I didn’t get a new high-tech driver’s license — those are much harder to reproduce — but the market for current licenses, which are still valid if they are issued before September 2013, is skyrocketing.

Law enforcement officials are worried that legislation in the City Council to create municipal ID cards for the undocumented would allow for more shady transactions to take place.

Council members and the de Blasio administration are still hammering out details of the bill, expected to be the subject of upcoming hearings. But Police Commissioner Bill Bratton expressed concern about the security implications of the program at an April 30 City Council hearing.

The new license hasn’t made a dent in the counterfeit market.

State Sen. Jose Peralta (D-Queens), whose district includes the Roosevelt Ave. area, is reintroducing legislation to crack down on the flourishing illegal industry.

“Sales of fake IDs are thriving,” said Peralta, who wants to raise document forgery from a Class D to a Class C felony.

City and state authorities lauded the rollout last September of the new licenses — polycarbonate cards that toned down color and enhanced security — saying they would be much more difficult to forge.

But Peralta said the fraudsters are undaunted. “The new license hasn’t made a dent in the counterfeit market,” he said.

Peralta introduced his bill, which doubles jail time for offenders caught selling the fake documents, in 2012, but it died in the Assembly. “If you crack down on the mills and increase penalties on these mill operators, it will put these guys out of business,” he said.

According to figures from the Queens district attorney’s office, there have already been 130 arrests this year in the borough for creating or possessing fraudulent documents, which include green cards, Social Security cards and passports.

Last year, there were 250 collars, and in 2012 there were 273, according to the DA’s office.

Identity theft is also a problem — there were 262 people charged with the crime in 2012, and 260 charged in 2013.

So far this year, 110 people in Queens have been charged.

Law enforcement sources said fraudulent document mills can be highly mobile and adaptable.

“A mill is a laptop computer with a printer and a laminator,” a source said. “It can move from one apartment to another apartment, from the back of a store to a basement.”

The source added that harsher penalties — for example, doubling jail time to 51/2 years from 21/2 years — would curtail the onslaught of illegal activity on Roosevelt Ave.

“The penalties for white-collar crimes in New York State are appallingly low,” said the source.

clestch@nydailynews.com
