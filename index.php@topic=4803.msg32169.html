Landmark Violation Questions

Hello,

My coop has begun discussions about replacing our windows. The issue has been put off for too long and the windows are now a potential hazard. The issue needs a speedy resolution. Now, enter Landmark. Here is our conundrum: we can do this in accordance with Landmark, where we will pay much more money (that we don't really have) and have to wait much longer, or we can do it without Landmark approval and get violations. In our minds, this is an issue of safety vs. being legit. Not to mention the significant cost increase for the 4-5 rounds of drawings and expensive windows Landmark will require. We have always seen safety the first priority in our building. We would rather do this with landmark approval, however time and money are not on our side. We also realize the coop should never had let the problem get to this level, but this problem started way before our time and has been inherited by the current board.

My question is this: What are the repercussions of having Landmark violations and how much does it cost to have these violations removed? Thank you in advance for constructive feedback to our situation.

I get the feeling that you are looking for some sort of tacit approval on this - I can't give you that, but I am writing this with no judgment and in the spirit of friendly advice from experience....

Why do you think that Landmarks will take more time?  If you follow their regulations and select windows accordingly, it shouldn't take long to get their approval.  I imagine that in this time of careful spending by co-ops, their paperwork load is lighter than it used to be and your application would get to the top of the pile pretty quickly.  I was on my building's board recently and we had no problem getting approvals in a timely fashion when we proposed things that were in accordance with their regs.

If you do not follow their regulations and install non-conforming windows, and are then cited for not following their rules, you will be on the hook for the penalty fee AND the cost of installing the right windows.  (this happened to my building a while ago when they made some roof repairs and used materials that were non-conforming - they ended up having to put in the right materials in the end and thus paid for the project twice!) So why bother putting in windows that you know are not approved and risk spending more of your co-op's money than you need to?  Windows are not a cheap item and I think you would be pennywise and pound-foolish to try to do this project in a roundabout way.

I know you raise concerns about saving time, but it sounds like the co-op has already waited a long time to do this - in the long run, and extra month or so probably won't make a huge difference.

You and your fellow tenants should be aware that you can file with the city for a J-51 tax abatement on landmark-compliant improvements/renovations to your property. This will help defer the cost of the renovations in future years, but you must comply and file the correct paperwork. More information can be found here:

I don't think you can assume that getting approval from Landmarks will necessarily take a long time or add huge additional costs.  The main thing landmarks will look at is if the windows in your building have some kind of unique architectural characteristic that adds to the buildings appearance.  Landmarks will likely insist you have divided lights (muttons and mullions).  But if your building has basic six over six or eight over eight double hung windows you will get approval fairly easily.  Now, if you have arched windows or casement windows or any other kind of unique feature it becomes a different ballgame.     

There are indeed repercussions associated with Landmarks violations.  As another poster mentioned, if you don't have your proper permits you can kiss your J-51 Tax Abatement goodbye.  That in and of itself would likely be thousands of dollars.  And I am not kidding when I say thousands of dollars.

There are also some banking considerations here as well.  If your cooperative corporation ever wants or needs to obtain financing from a financial institution a pesky violation could hamper your corporations ability to get the loan. 

One last thought.  Everyone loves elegant and genteel buildings that adorn the tree lined streets of the historic district.  Everybody loves it until it is their turn to comply with Landmarks rules, procedures, and occasionally, cost. If you support landmarks and the historic district you should do your best to comply.

All in all, I think you are better off getting the appropriate permits.  Best of luck.

I don't know the scope and complexity of the windows in your building, but if your window contractor typically has to revise drawings 4-5 times to get landmarks approval, he's doing something wrong.  A well prepared/researched landmark submission should go through on the first or second submission.  After the first submission you'll know what issues you have and should be able to correct them on the second go-round.

As mentioned before make sure the new windows match what is existing and there should be no issues.  There is a little leeway depending on the historical area. Some will require you to replace wood windows with wood, others will be ok with an aluminum or vinyl clad window as long as the color/style match.  When in doubt, consult the landmarks work guide: http://www.nyc.gov/html/lpc/downloads/pdf/pubs/workguide.pdf
