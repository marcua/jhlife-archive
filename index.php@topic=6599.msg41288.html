Maybe some people just can't handle the memories and realities of what happened to us that day. I say us because even if you didn't personally loose some one that day we as a nation and more directly, as a city, suffered deeply in ways that went to the very core of our bones.
 
We cannot afford to forget or sanitise no matter how painful or distasteful the realities. Our Jewish brothers and sisters already know the importance of keeping the horrific memories alive, lest they fade away and eventually become white washed or worse, entirely denied in future generations.
 
If you just can't handle the reality then come and let us comfort you. But we will never forget the day or the full, deep, and horrific reality.
 
For myself, I haven't been anywhere near the WTC since that day. Visitors come and ask me to take them there. I politely say no. I will give them directions there if that is what they want, but will not join them.
 
I joke here about channeling Gladys Gilbert but I am a sensitive, an empath. I know what my own experiences have been in places that hold the memories of things that have occurred there. And I am talking about things and places that are far less upsetting than the WTC site. But I also know that I must face this too. My promise to the memory, the folks who were lost, and to myself, was that I will go when the memorial is in place and to pray there. Now I have to decide if I should go at this time or wait for the finished memorial and museum. Perhaps both. I have no idea what might happen when I get there. I just know that sometimes certain things must be done in life unpleasant as they may be.
 
We all have to find our way. But we will never, ever forget that day. And I would urge everyone to make that respectful trip there. It is a duty.
