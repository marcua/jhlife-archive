Welcome to the neighborhood!  I hope your move goes smoothly.

Mapping ethnic businesses is one thing, but mapping where people live is quite another matter.  Often they don't match well at all.  For example, despite being home to the region's most celebrated Indian commercial district, Jackson Heights has very few Indian residents.  Most of our South Asian residents are from elsewhere on the Subcontinent.

Residentially, Jackson Heights is very diverse and very spatially intermixed.  I have some census data for the neighborhood that I'd be happy to share with you, but I think you'll find that it paints a murkier picture than the map you're trying to draw.

But we do have a number of fascinating commercial districts.  A Korean area along Broadway south of Roosevelt, a Filipino district just west of the BQE, and the possible beginnings of a Nepalese/Tibetan cluster around 74th & Roosevelt.  I don't know if anybody has tried to map the Mexican, Ecuadorian, Peruvian, and Colombian businesses, but that might reveal some additional clustering as well.

The Korean district starts at Unidentified Flying Chickens and Koryodang Express on Roosevelt near 71/72, and continues east onto Broadway and various side streets with probably two dozen businesses -- restaurants, beauty salons, video stores, a beer hall, several internet cafes, etc.

The "Himalayan district" is much smaller and more diffuse.  Currently, the most visible signs are the Shangrila Express cart on Broadway and Merit Farms on 74th Street.  Two restaurants -- Himalayan Yak and Om Tibet -- are currently closed but hopefully will reopen soon.  Here is some discussion.

Social Explorer

While the name Social Explorer may conjure up images for some of a new and powerful social networking site, this site is actually a splendid way to learn more about your friends and neighbors. Created by a team of demographers and GIS experts at Queens College in New York, the Social Explorer website offers access to dozens of different interactive data maps including time series maps that chart the ethnic transitions that have occurred throughout New York and Los Angeles over the past decades. Before delving into the maps any further, first-time visitors may want to go to the Getting Started section, where they can read a brief introductory essay about these data maps and their basic objectives. Finally, visitors can also use the site to generate their own specialized maps and reports.
