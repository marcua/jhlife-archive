I think the biggest problem is a lack of common knowledge of the traffic laws for bicycles. The really do occupy a unique space somewhere between motor vehicles and pedestrians, so it logically follows they should have a unique set of rules...and, in fact, they do.

The trouble is, these rules are not widely known, so it logically follows that they are not commonly obeyed, or enforced. Most people I speak to have no clue what cyclists are supposed to do (or not do) in any given scenario.  I'm talking about cyclists and non-cyclists. That's a recipe for failure, if not disaster, imho.

In my unscientific observation, as a life-long NY pedestrian, motorist since I was 17 - and bike commuter for over 30 years - cyclists are no more unlawful or hazardous than cars or pedestrians - they're merely the 'new kids on the block.' As the hot new, rapidly growing trend in urban planning, their presence (along with new bike infrastructure), for good or bad, is more noticeable today than the presence - and daily illegal behaviors - we're all accustomed to from pedestrians and motorists. 

I predict in 25-50 years bikes will be a much more widely used form of transit in NYC and most of the bugs will be worked out by then. Keep in mind, when cars were introduced here a century ago, their integration with foot and horse-drawn traffic was not an immediate success.

Putting in my 2 bits worth here.

Everyone is correct that jaywalking in NYC is an offense that could get you a ticket.  Also worthy of a fine is hanging your laundry in the back without a license, hanging around with two or more people wearing masks or dance for more than 8 hours without a break.  These laws are rarely, if ever, actually enforced.  I recall the Guliani once tried to enforce the jaywalking rules for a couple of days -- until his office heard from every single NYer, and every single one of his donors, to cut it out.  The police then stopped enforcement.

A pedestrian jaywalking is a fact of life that I accept, and that every NYer accepts.  Not so much in other cities (you will get ticketed in LA), but here that is the reality.

The other reality is that more and more people are riding bikes and those motorized scooters.  These "vehicles", for lack of a better word, have to obey the rules, just like cars do.  If NYC permits bicycle violations to become as unenforceable as jaywalking, our streets would be a disaster (I need to find a new word -- Trump has ruined that for me).

So yes, I agree.  Bikes must stop at a red light.  Bikes must wait for pedestrians to cross.  Bikes should not be on the sidewalk.  Where a bike lane exists, bikes must use it (and cars parked in bike lanes must receive heavy and increased parking fines).  This one is a pet peeve of mine since, as a rider, a car parked in the bike lane makes riding more dangerous and, as a driver, driving down NYC streets with bicyclists riding BOTH to my left and my right (when there's a perfectly good and entirely accessible bike lane on one side) is aggravating since a car can't move beyond them.

I would not give a car a license to stop at a red light and proceed if no one is coming, and I would not give a bike that right either.

wlirfan said, "Bikes must stop at a red light.  Bikes must wait for pedestrians to cross.  Bikes should not be on the sidewalk.  Where a bike lane exists, bikes must use it...."
