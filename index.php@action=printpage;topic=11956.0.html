Title: Re: Where would you live if not in Jackson Heights?
Post by: Palermo on May 14, 2014, 11:14:44 AM
If in the same situation, I would give Union City/West New York a look.  We have family over there and have been there quite a bit.

First, I like the feel of the neighborhood.  Its about a mile wide from the Hudson River to the edge of the Palisades, so you are never that far from the waterfront.  Its built to human scale, very walkable and pleasant.  The structures remind me a good deal of Williamsburg, most of it pre-war, though it doesn't have as much of an industrial feel to it.

Second, I like the commercial area, centered along Bergenline Avenue which runs up its spine.  I go there to buy my linen clothing and love eating in the restaurants there (for the most authentic Cuban pastels north of Miami, goto Grand Via on 48th Street.  Shamelessly cheap and shamelessly delicious).  The commercial feel represents the neighborhood, which is much more Spanish orientated than JH east of 76th Street.  A lot of shops do require a certain comfort with Spanish, which may be a sticking point for some, but for a silly monoglot like me, I sort of revel in it.

Third, I like the potential of the neighborhood.  It was once a huge Cuban community, but much of that has been replaced by folks from other remnants of the Spanish Empire.  That means that the housing prices are affordable (except along the water).  Given its proximity to NYC, I can see home values going up in price in the semi-near future.  Not having a direct subway connection makes this more difficult, but the bus service to the city is quick, cheap and frequent.  The Hudson Bergen Light Rail does connect you quickly to Hoboken and from there the PATH gets you into the city.  Overall, I can definitely see a good investment being made with a property purchase in the neighborhood. 

As for other things, like the schools and crime, I cannot speak with too much certainty.  My nephew went to the schools there and liked it.  Crime in West New York used to be problematic, but from what I understand, its much more under control, much as some NYC neighborhoods.

Either way, good luck with your searching.

Title: Re: Where would you live if not in Jackson Heights?
Post by: ljr on September 16, 2014, 02:33:29 PM
We bought in JH 3 years ago after searching pretty far and wide within NYC for affordability paired with an easy commute and good quality of life/good quality apartment. We wanted 2 bedrooms and 2 baths and the only place we could find an apartment that size plus all of the above for the $400s or lower (our price range) was in JH.

We wound up buying on the upper end of that price range, but we could have paid 100K less for an apartment only a half block from where we wound up. (We just liked the more expensive place much better for a number of reasons.)

Still--JH really was extremely affordable. In Brooklyn to get a place that size for that price would have entailed being much farther from the subway, having just one train line, etc. The only other area that was cheaper for that size apartment was Riverdale in the Bronx. You can get a lot of space up there for a very low price. ($200s-$300s for large 2 bed/2 bath.) However, the maintenance fees were generally a lot higher in Riverdale plus the commute much harder.

So we chose JH and are very glad we did. We did look in Montclair in a brief attempt to consider moving out of the city.  We were warned (by someone who reluctantly left Montclair for that reason) that taxes are very high in Montclair as in a lot of N Jersey, (see comment above: 17K vs 5K in JH) and when you add up higher commuting costs, the need for one or two cars, costs for lawn maintenance, roof and water heater repairs, etc, you don't really save money by moving to Montclair. And don't kid yourself, the schools in these suburbs are not always what they are cracked up to be—I have heard from friends and colleagues. When you stay in the city, you have more choices. In the 'burbs you are limited to whatever is there. I have friends who hightailed it back to NYC after several years in Hastings because they found that the schools there really did not suit their kids. Another friend wound up bussing her child back to a special school in NYC every day when the Chappaqua schools could not handle her special needs. So my friend was paying those enormous taxes and not even using the schools.

Basically you have to do a lot of research and figure out what your priorities are. If you want to pay a lot less for your apartment/house, you will probably be in a worse commuting situation. For example, we chose to pay 100K more to be 13 blocks closer to the express train than a beautiful, completely renovated apartment we also considered. To us, that was worth it. I also know people who stuck it out in their tiny Manhattan stabilized rental with 2 kids so they could go to great NYC public schools and be in walking distance. But really, bottom line, JH is still one of the more affordable areas in the city that is also very well connected via 5 subways lines. And here, there are some 1 bedrooms that really are large enough to make into 2 bedrooms. Plus maintenance fees tend to be on the low side compared to other neighborhoods. I'd say you should look very hard here before giving up on finding something that works.

Considering I take it the least out of the trains and I experience the most problems with it, I have to question Straphangers grading system. When the 7 train is not working, it is nice to be able to walk downstairs and take a different train.

Sunnyside is a nice neighborhood, but I still choose Jackson Heights.

One area that I've explored more this summer than in past years is the Rockaways from around the Cross Bay Bridge west to Ft. Tilden. There's a lot of nice things happening there with new and better restaurants and bars, etc. Very different than just a few years back. I'm not sure what it would be like to live there year round. It is definitely lacking many of the conveniences that we take for granted in JH, such as the wide variety of places to shop for groceries. Definitely a horrible commute if you have to get to Manhattan each day, but a great commute to the beach! I've definitely had the, "could I live here?" thought cross my mind while there, but this may be the result of too much sun after a day at the beach.

I'm happy to stay in JH for the foreseeable future.
