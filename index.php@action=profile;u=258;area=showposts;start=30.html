Neighborhood Chat / Re: A neighborhood with no green space - will I be happy?
« on: September 16, 2009, 01:59:17 PM »
I don't think any of us can tell you if you will be happy.   

But I will tell you why I am happy.

I love the mature trees in JH.  I love the little narrow gardens that are in front of os many of the buildings in the historic district, and the fact that 3/4's of the year I walk past a ever-changing display of flowers and flowering bushes.  I love the six story tree right outside my 6th floor window. I feel like I live in a tree house, with squirrels and pigeons living their lives oblivious to me.  I love the fact that when the leaves drop, and the tree is bare, I can peek past the bare branches and see the Empire State Building.  I love sitting in my building's lush garden.  I also love visiting some of the lovely gardens in the community's churches, and I do so rather frequently because my toddler/preschooler often asks if we can stop and visit a garden.

I love the fact that Central Park is 3 express stops away on the F train, with elevators in both directions, so I don't have to drag my daughter's stroller up and down steps.  I love afternoons at the Queens Zoo, in Flushing Meadow Park.  I love the playground at Travers Park, and I love the cultural events that take place there.

I don't like the fact that there is a clear divide in our community.  Some of us have access to beautiful private gardens.  Many of us don't.  And the fact that the most powerful/influential residents mostly live in buildings with private gardents, means that there has never been a successful push to create a public green space (outside of Travers Park and the churches) that the rest of the community can enjoy.  Oddly, I find that when I stop in the beautiful church gardens, most of the people enjoying the gardens are residents who live in garden buildings, and are just enjoying a different garden.

Neighborhood Chat / Re: Trash in JH
« on: September 14, 2009, 11:13:58 AM »
I'm joining this discussion late, but I have a couple of comments.

First of all, thank you Jeff for your photo study.  What stood out to me was the images of overflowing trash cans over and over again.  It seems like residents and visitors are often faced with two options.  They can walk over to an overflowing trashcan and try to balancing their  litter on top of the huge pile, before a wind picks it up and carries it away.  Or they can just drop their litter on the street.  Neither option is acceptable.  Clearly, we need more trash cans, and more trash pick ups.

My understanding is that a lot of city services (including garbage pick ups) are allocated based on census data.   Yet another good reason to promote having everyone counted in the upcoming census.  But that's not going to result in cleaner streets right now.

Can anyone explain the role of the Doe Foundation in Jackson Heights?  I am always delighted to see these guys out working in the community.  I attended an event recently that was catered by Doe participants, and I was exceptionally impressed with them.  I know that they provide temporary housing, job training and a variety of support services for formerly incarcerated individuals.  I think it is a great project.  But what are the implications of having them work in specific communities? 

Does the City say "well, Doe is in JH, so we can cut services there?"  I know that happened years ago with welfare reform, when public assistance recipients were forced to participate in cleaning up the subways and the parks and a variety of menial tasks in exchange for their welfare checks.  And if you did the math, their labor turned out to be significantly less than minimum wage. 

Lots of good City jobs with benefits and unions disappeared because the City decided that it was in their best interest to have welfare moms do the work for less than minimum wage, rather than maintaining employees with benefits and pensions.  Is that happening with Doe?  Or is it clear that Doe is supposed to be a supplement to City services, not a replacement for City services? 

Anyone know?

A fight erupted Saturday between Queens Council members John Liu and Helen Sears over whether he did or didn't endorse her for reelection.

At issue are mailers Sears is sending to voters in western Queens that feature Liu's photo and a letter signed by him that urges voters to support her campaign.

"Having served with Councilwoman Helen Sears for the last eight years, I have seen firsthand how deeply committed she is to the families of Queens," says the letter.

Liu, a candidate for city controller, says he hasn't endorsed Sears - and denies authorizing the use of his name, signature or image for Sears' campaign.

Asked what Sears meant by Liu's "memory issues" - an apparent reference to a News story that questioned Liu's claim of working in a sweatshop as a child - Joerss said, "I'll let your readers figure that out."

A spokesman for Danny Dromm, who is challenging Sears for the Council seat, which covers Jackson Heights, Rego Park, Woodside, Elmhurst and Corona, said, "False claims of endorsement are typical City Council shenanigans."

"They're attempting to use John Liu's name to gloss over her record," said Dromm spokesman Bryan Collinsworth.
