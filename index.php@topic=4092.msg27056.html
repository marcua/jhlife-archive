At a recent event hosted by Jose Peralta I believe it was a captain or high level officer form the 115th reported that the crime rate had dropped in the area over the past few months. Additionally a representative of the Queens DA's office reported that the illegal social security card business had seen a dip as well in this area and is now largely concentrated in The Bronx.

Clearly this doesn't mean Roosevelt has become the safest and cleanest Ave. in NYC, and I know data can be manipulated. But, sometimes it seems an area gets a reputation and keeps long after it has been cleaned up. Clearly, Roosevelt has a long way to go.  It would seem continued efforts on enforcement of existing laws and a regular police presence would help to continue reducing crime on the avenue.

As far as blaming all of Roosevelt's problems on undocumented immigrants, I'm not buying it. Legal or not there will always be people committing crime, running drug and prostitution rings etc. I think it's misguided to think that rounding up one specific group based on immigration status will solve any or all of the problems plaguing Roosevelt.

A multi-agency approach makes a great deal of sense. NYPD should focus on enforcing NYC laws, they should not (and are not) be in the business of enforcing Federal Laws. Let Homeland Security and ICE deal with immigration issues.  Sanitation, local politicians, and other agencies should focus on clean up and improvement.

Years ago, the then Community Board Chair asked the police department to place satellite precincts along Roosevelt Avenue.  They would have been modeled after the storefront precincts you find in places like Philadelphia, or even Tokyo.  Basically, you would take an empty storefront in a "hot spot" along Roosevelt, throw in desks and other equipment and deploy some officers.  You establish a police presence in an area where crime is a problem. The cops also establish relationships with the merchants and residents.  They become part of the community.

I know that some people resist the call for more police, but I never bought into the philosophy that you set up a fortress-like precinct in one part of a community and have all the law enforcement resources work out of there.  It isolates the officers from the community and the benefit of having the police close by is only shared by a few residents.  I wouldn't mind getting off the subway late at night and having a satellite precinct a few doors down.
