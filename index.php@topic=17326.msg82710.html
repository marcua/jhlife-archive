Obligations when one finds somebody collapsed on their front yard?
« on: December 01, 2017, 01:57:13 PM »
I found a guy collapsed on my front yard last night.

First I asked him if he was OK or needed help. He was conscious, gave a couple of muffled responses and could wriggle and move around a bit. I thought he was probably drunk, but how could I be sure?

I left him there, came inside, called 911 and told them the situation. I was asked by the 911 operator if I wished to request a police car or an ambulance. I was a bit surprised that they should be asking the informant that question. Shouldn't they, the emergency professional, take decision to send police or ambo based on the description given?

Anyway, I hesitated, but requested a police car. It definitely didn't come for at least an hour at which point I had to turn in. God forbid if the collapsed individual were not a drunk, and instead was seriously injured, violent or otherwise.

I woke up this morning and there's no sign of the guy but for his body's impression in the dirt where he'd been lying.

What are one's (moral and legal) obligations when one finds somebody collapsed like this (on their own property)?
Thanks

Only to clarify a couple points raised in this thread:

Please remember NY has a "Good Samaritan" law, so no one need fear the consequences of otherwise good intentions. The statute in full at: <http://la12.org/Portals/733/Web_content/files/NYSGoodSamaritanLaw.pdf>

Along those lines, the very sad story of our fellow Queensian Catherine Genovese was actually more complicated. As it turned out, the supposed non-responsive "bystander effect" (the subsequently so-called "Genovese syndrome") reported by the NYTimes was documented years later by that very paper of record as having been "grossly exaggerated." In point of fact, at least one neighbor heard Kitty's screams and came to her aid. See: <https://www.nytimes.com/2016/04/05/nyregion/winston-moseley-81-killer-of-kitty-genovese-dies-in-prison.html>

Having noted that historical discrepancy, it was once said you get more of a response yelling "Fire!" than "Help!" So there's that...
