Pets and Animals / Re: Where do you exercise/socialize your dog?
« on: April 21, 2008, 03:52:58 PM »
guvner, unfortunately I haven't been able to track down any (reliable) doggy day care facilities here in JH. I have to take my dog into Manhattan for doggy day care and boarding. I don't know what I would do if he was too big to get on the subway.

Re: dog runs... Luckily, my building has a dog run out back which is great for ball playing and off-leash milling around (and is one of the reasons why I moved in). However, it still doesn't offer the socialization factor of the public dog runs available in other neighborhoods -- ever since I moved in, I've never been in the dog run with anyone other than my dog. Again, I schlep my dog into Manhattan so that he can socialize at public dog runs.

I've also found that many people for whatever reason (unsocialized dogs, no time, etc.) don't like to stop and let their dogs "say hello" to each other. Many people that I've encountered with big dogs say that their dogs are "unfriendly" and have actually crossed the street to avoid letting their dog(s) near my dog. If I do say so myself, my dog is very well-behaved on walks and approaches other dogs with tentative caution (then warms up if the other dog is friendly), so it's not like they're trying to avoid a small, yappy, jumping dog. Point is, while you should always be cautious about approaching unknown dogs, it seems that you should take extra caution in JH if the other owners haven't been proactive about it already. Perhaps if there was a place for dogs to socialize (a dog run!),  there wouldn't be so many dogs in the neighborhood that haven't been properly socialized and this wouldn't be an issue. Don't mean to generalize here but it's just my experience walking my dog around the neighborhood anytime between 7pm and 10pm. There are plenty of wonderful friendly dogs and owners in JH too!

Neighborhood Chat / Re: Greener Living in Jackson Heights
« on: March 15, 2008, 11:37:01 PM »
Quote from: KC on March 15, 2008, 10:50:17 PM
Ok.  Serious question here folks.

I get plastic bags when I go to the supermarket.  I put them in a drawer, and I take them out one at a time to use as garbage bags.  I then toss my garbage down the chute in one of those supermarket bags.

If I didn't have supermarket bags, I would have to buy plastic bags to throw out my garbage.

Seriously.  For the readers here who are considering this bag, what do you do with your garbage?

KC:  I'm in the same position and have come to the following conclusion: plastic grocery bags, like many aspects of daily life in 21st century America, aren't good for the environment but are unavoidable -- an almost "necessary" evil.  If we can't cut these things out of our lives completely, though, one thing we can do to help the environment is to use less of them. 

I know people are going to say that those of us who use plastic grocery bags as trash bags should just dump our garbage into a large communal bin and to not use plastic shopping bags for trash at all -- but that just may not be feasible.  By reusing plastic grocery bags I get from stores for trash, I'm not buying and/or using large, kitchen-size garbage bags that use a lot more energy to produce and ship, use a lot more plastic (thicker bags, fancy pulls and ties), and have cardboard packaging with each box you buy. 

Over a year ago, I started carrying around two of those nylon shopping bags people have been talking about here and use them constantly.  It cut down on the number of plastic grocery bags I used so much (I rarely got them) that I eventually ran out and didn't have any for trash.  At my office of about 30 people, we all started saving our plastic grocery bags (from lunch, etc.) in a communal location.  These bags would have previously just been thrown away and not reused, but now I'm provided with free trash bags on a daily basis.

If I got plastic bags each and every time I was offered one, I'd be overwhelmed with unused plastic bags, more than I'd ever use for trash -- if you were like me, pre-nylon bags, you have too many to count in your drawer and add to your collection daily.  My recommendation is that while you/I/we will still need to use them, we just need to use less of them.  If you use less, stores will start carrying and buying less from manufacturers, and manufacturers will make less.  It's like changing out "regular" light bulbs for energy efficient bulbs, etc. -- still have to have them and use energy, but making small changes can make a big difference.
