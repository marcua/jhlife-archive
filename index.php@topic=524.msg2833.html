Encouraging creative entrepreneurship
« on: February 27, 2008, 11:06:16 AM »
So with all the news about pizza and chicken, I have to ask - are Rudy Volcano, Yogurberry, Inner Peace, Afzal and I the only ones crazy/brilliant/silly enough to open a new, nice, different place to eat or shop in the neighborhood? Apologies to fans of pizza bakeries, vitamin stores and chicken houses, but it makes me sad that a neighborhood with such great potential isn't developing more variety. 

Are there any other JH folks out there with an entrepreneurial yen to go out on a limb and open that cute boutique, yarn store, or restaurant with amazing brunch?  Would organizing a BID help?  Anyone know much about setting up one of those?  What can we do to encourage really good interesting businesses (big or small) to come here?  And I'm not talking about expensive "yuppie" businesses either - just shops that raise the bar on quality of product, environment and service.  For example - has anyone ever been to the Gowanus Yacht Club in Carroll Gardens?  Totally downmarket and totally great - a cool place to hang out.

We endured many bumps in the road before we opened e77 and it is a LOT of hard work, but we learned a lot and I would be happy to share our experience and advice with anyone else from the neighborhood who wants to investigate opportunities.

I would add Y Gallery to that list.  It's a great little space; lots of attention paid to making it look nice.  And in addition, the gallery's program is ambitious.

I have two ideas.

One- I have wondered if part of the problem is that Jackson Heights suffers from an image problem.  I've noticed that friends of mine, once they actually see the neighborhood, have a very different impression of it than they had before.

To address that, I was thinking how nice it would be to have a website where you could find really top-notch photos of the neighborhood that are outside the realm of what normally might be seen by doing a google image search.  I would have to think more about what images should be on there, but I do have some initial ideas (the interior of Espresso 77 would definitely be one!).  There's a great website hosting/image template site that lots of artists use called http://www.icompendium.com that costs $200 a year and it could easily be used for this purpose.  I'm sure there are other options as well.

The second idea is that I don't think the search for someone with an entrepreneurial spirit would have to be limited to someone who already lives in the neighborhood.  I know that through some NYC networking groups I am with, I have pretty easy access to more than 3000+ NYC professionals on email.  I would imagine that others have similar contacts and if a bunch of people really got together to work on this, they could really figure out how to best reach out to potential entrepreneurs.

Julesnyc, this is a good question, although I would expand your list a bit.   I think there are a great many businesses in our neighborhood striving in their own ways to provide unique, high-quality services and products.  I would add Rajbhog, the 74th Street jewelery & sari shops, D'Espana Foods, Jay Dines' forthcoming store, Farm Spot, Lety's, Happy Kitchen, Novo, La PorteÃ±a, La Nueva 2000, and countless other restaurants.  I think these businesses (and others I'm forgetting) add a lot of flavor to the neighborhood and deserve their due.

That said, I also crave novelty and variety, and certainly think it's healthy to see some ongoing turnover in the neighborhood's retail sector.  But I'd reframe your question with a slightly different one.

Since its earliest days, Jackson Heights has been a bedroom community for writers, actors, musicians, and other "creative types."   In the movie, The Wrong Man (1956), the lead character lives in Jackson Heights and works as a musician in Western Harlem -- incidentally, my exact commute! 

Yet, although we're as crowded with creative freelancers as Long Island City or any neighborhood in Brooklyn, we have never been a place where the creative community has come to work.  The second and third floor offices of our commercial buildings have not been colonized as artists' studios or as the offices for creative enterprises.  Nobody has tried to convert any of our funky commercial buildings (e.g. the strange building behind Tacolandia) into artists' workshops and black box performance spaces.  Why not? 

If we could transition from a creative bedroom community to more of a neighborhood where creative commerce happens, I think a more creative retail sector would follow.  What could be done to encourage this?
