Quote

You're right about the "luxury" condos in Astoria. The housing stock there is so different from in Jackson Heights. It's funny how neighborhoods will develop in different ways. For instance, though Astoria is filled with small buildings (two to six families), there are still a number of larger prewar buildings. But hardly any of them are coops. I'm sure there's a reason, but I don't know what it is. Established coops in Astoria are rare compared to Jackson Heights; if you want to buy an apartment there, most of the listings are for newer condos.

I haven't done much comparing of coop prices between Astoria and JH, but I wonder if the prices you're comparing are for apartments that are the same square footage, or just the same number of bedrooms. Astoria's prewar apartment buildings tend to be older than Jackson Heights', with smaller apartments with smaller, boxier rooms. When I was looking to rent a few years ago, what I could afford would have gotten me a small one-bedroom in Astoria, vs. a large one-bedroom or small two-bedroom in JH. (I used to live in Astoria, back in the '80s, but I got priced out because Astoria was being promoted as the next cool place to live in NYC.)

Hi all! Happy to contribute to this discussion.

Historically coops have held their value well, even during the downturn in the market, since most coop units are owner occupied and owners are 20 % or more (not to mention emotionally) invested in the properties. Though coop buyers are still susceptible to market markers such as interest rates, unemployment and inflation rates, rising demand for personal residences which require a complete board screening can hardly be classified as a “bubble” in my opinion.

Thank you Shelby for posting the recent resale information from our recent email blast:

35-21 79th Street, #6N, 2 Bed 1 Bath - Asked $598,500 - IN CONTRACT ABOVE ASK
76-10 34th Avenue, #1T, 1 Bed 1 Bath - $337,000 - IN CONTRACT ABOVE ORIGINAL ASK

I can assure you that final recorded prices won’t disappoint. We will release them as soon as they close.

Sectors of the marketplace that are being flooded with investor funds, foreign or domestic, some even institutional funds, can easily be called a bubble. Over the past few years we’ve seen a race to build properties into NYC airspace priced north of 10 million at an average price of $8,500 per square foot. This is a micro sector, which by all accounts is highly speculative.

I had written a recent article which brushes against this topic. http://www.jacksonheightslistings.com/hows-the-market/
It talks about the micro-markets of NYC. Jackson Heights Coops are a single sector of what I would classify as a micro-market, which by all indicators is a very healthy one

I think it's that there's a certain price that two earner families (with high income) can afford. Let's say it's $850k at current lender rates, just for sake of argument. They've finally saved up the deposit, but their desired neighborhood now exceeds the ceiling for what they can reasonably afford, so the prospective buyers cast around for other neighborhoods.

What makes a neighborhood worthy is whether or not people they know (or people like them, through word of mouth or featured in articles) are looking there or already bought there. Once a neighborhood gets a greenlight in people's perceptions, they all rush there to lock in a price before it climbs higher.

The prices will rise until they exceed the ceiling that a percentage of two earner families can afford. Then other underpriced neighborhoods will get their turn.

When I left Carroll Gardens, a 2BR was about 850k and you can still find them for that. 1BRs there have maxed out at about $500k - I just looked at my old building and nothing has moved for more than 18 mos. No one who wants to stay will sell because they can't buy in the same neighborhood. They stay put.
