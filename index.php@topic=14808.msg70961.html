The fact that the kid's graffiti was associated with gangs is irrelevant.  How can one accept that a basically non-violent quality of life incident can warrant 8 months in jail?  If the kid was convicted of a violent gang crime, it is ok to punish him.  Aside from this, the suggestion that it is ok to put a kid in jail for 8 months for using a marker to write on public property on two occasions is irrational - it is going to cost tax payers over $110k for his 8 month stay: http://www.nydailynews.com/new-york/cost-inmate-nyc-ivy-league-tuition-article-1.1471630. 

Jumping bail, I do not know the facts associated with that allegation - but I cannot help but feel that he would never receive a sentence of this kind if he were not a poor Hispanic with no money and a limited education.  It stinks of the built-in racism in our criminal justice system. 

People can try to justify it by pointing to the fact that the kid may have been associated with a gang.  However, if you are poor and you live in their (gangs) neighborhood, you may have to be friendly with them just to survive.  The fact that this kid was associated with a  gang does not mean that the kid was actively engaged in committing violent crimes and other serious crimes.  I guess my point is that I am appalled at the disproportionality between the crime and the punishment and the glaring fact that the kid is an economically disadvantaged simpleton, and I am surprised at the weak justifications in JHL, i.e., the kid missed some court appearances while on bail and his tag was associated with a gang.  My point is that it is going to cost all of us over $110k to punish this kid and he will come out a career criminal without any economic and/or professional opportunities - because he wrote on public property with a marker on two occasions!

I am not a champion for the downtrodden.  However, I am against cruel and unusual punishment.  The fact that I do not want a homeless population hanging around my neighborhood does not mean that I am for throwing them in prison. Moreover, I do not discriminate in my dislike for the homeless population - I do not like any of them, i.e., I dislike them all equally.  Here, I believe that the forces of discrimination at large, from the time the kid was born to the day he was put in jail, played a role in the circumstances that resulted. 

As for the Hispanic versus Black, it does not matter to the extent that men in both populations suffer from similar maladies, i.e., high unemployment, high criminal conviction rates, high recidivism rates, low income, low marriage rates, etc.  My point is that as a society, spending over $110k is not justified in this instance. 

While some people find it hard to sympathize with a person who commits a crime and is punished, taken to its logical extreme, this could be use to justify burning witches alive in Salem, Mass., or stoning Christians to death in Syria.  If one blindly accepts the crime punishment paradigm that you adhere to, the results will be ruinous to our society.  Luckily, we have the U.S. Constitution ban cruel and unusual punishment; although it was of little help to this kid.

Pointing out gang affiliation is completely irrelevant in my eyes.  The kid was not convicted or charged for committing gang related crimes. For all we know, he could be a poser, i.e., fake gangster.  To me it does not matter.  It is the crime that matters.  If everybody else got 8 months in jail for missing community service, then I would not voice any opposition to the sentence.  This is not the case.  This kid got an extra long punishment because he probably pissed the judge off, and the judge's poor decision will cost tax payers over $110k.  Ask yourself, could the judge had made his point without putting the kid in jail for 8 months?  Maybe a few weeks?  Maybe an alternative program for at risk youth?

Ultimately, I am annoyed about two aspects of this case.  The disproportionality between the punishment and the crime.  But mostly, I am upset at the frivolous manner in which the criminal justice spends our hard earned tax dollars.
