Quote
Is there a reason the Bronx part of the precinct couldn't be a separate precinct from the Queens part? (Sorry if this is something I missed in my long-ago civics class.)

I have a feeling you didn't miss it, because I don't recall getting this detailed in U.S. Civics (at least, not in high school).

The number of legislators in the House is capped at 435 representatives by law. The number of representatives per state is determined by the U.S. Census every 10 years, and redistricting happens when representatives to a state are added or taken away. Per the 2010 Census, each district is supposed to have approximately 711,000 residents. So, you can see from those two criteria how the Bronx can't be it's own separate district, and how complicated it can get when drawing the congressional districts if they are all supposed to have approximately the same number of residents.

Outside of redistricting related to the Census, the only other example I am familiar with is the redistricting that has taken place in Pennsylvania. In that instance, the Pennsylvania Supreme Court ruled in favor of those who sued on the grounds that the state congressional map violated the Pennsylvania constitution by discriminating against Democrats. The Pennsylvania Supreme Court redrew the Pennsylvania Congressional map. The only involvement of the federal courts there was to say they aren't getting involved. If there are actual instances of federal courts redrawing state congressional maps, I'm unaware of that.

http://www.pennlive.com/politics/index.ssf/2018/03/pennsylvania_elbows_to_center.html
