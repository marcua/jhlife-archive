But isn't the issue here not the missing soy sauce or even the messed up order, but the attitude of the person at Tomo on the phone ?

Even if / when the customer is wrong you still don't treat them in a sarcastic and snippy manner.  When you work in a service industry the customer may not always be right, but they are always the one paying. It's not good business to be snippy and sarcastic to your customers, eventually they won't come back.

Even if the request for a packet or two of soy sauce to be delivered was unreasonable (which I'm inclined to think it was) in the eye of the Tomo person, the answer was not to be rude about it. As I see it there were two viable options:

1) "I'm sorry but we can't send our delivery person out for such a small item, however, please come into the restaurant int he next week and I will be happy to give a you a free roll, desert, what have you to make up for our over sight."

2) "I'm sorry, We can't send our delivery person out right now, but I will have him stop by your apt. on his way to  his next delivery which I anticipate happening in the next X minutes" Then send along a certificate for 10% or 20% the next order as an apology.

I'm wiling to bet that if Ms.Tomo were nice from the first interaction and simply apologized for her computer problems this wouldn't have escalated  in the way that I did.

Sorry If I'm jumping on a thread that's been beaten to death here, but I can understand how frustrating it is when people who are preparing food or even something as simple as coffee simply don't listen and give you the wrong things. It's careless, and in some instances makes it impossible for the person who just paid money for something unable to eat it. For example as a vegetarian I get really upset when I get an egg and Cheese on a croissant, hop on the subway and find a chunk of bacon or sausage in there that I definitely did not ask for.

Yes, mistakes happen and we must take things in stride, but there's no excuse for carelessness.
