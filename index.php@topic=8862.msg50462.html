Here is the posting  to bring people up to date  as reported by one of the people involved in helping.On 9/4, around twelve residents of Jackson Heights met at a business run by the two core leaders of SUKHI. During this meeting, we discussed: how the 37th Road plaza came into being; the city's timeline for its development; community concerns over its current state; and proactive steps that local residents can take to improve it.

The 37th Road Plaza is unique in the way it came about. Typically, the DOT Plaza Program requires that an established local organization put in a request for a pedestrian plaza, and serves as a maintenance manager. This plaza, however, is the result of a DOT study conducted several years ago in Jackson Heights, in which the city recommended the closure of a couple blocks to traffic. One of these blocks was 37th Road, which was converted into a pedestrian plaza. This was met by strong opposition from local business-owners. After some time, SUKHI (Social Uplift Knowledge Help Initiative) was formed by a married couple that runs several businesses along the plaza, and committed themselves as city partners to maintain the space. This has been a significant challenge, as most surrounding businesses remain opposed to the plaza, SUKHI's resources are slim, and the residential community has remained largely uninvolved.

The plaza is still in an initial phase of implementation. A gravel street surface has been installed, and a number of tables, chairs, and potted plants have been provided. However, little in the way of official city planning or design has yet begun. Initial community outreach is expected to occur sometime in fall 2013, during which residents will be provided the opportunity to gather with DOT employees and offer their opinions on how the space should eventually look and function. At this point, the DOT drafts and finalizes design plans and contract out the construction. This process of outreach, design, and bidding is expected to take at least a year. This does not include construction and installation time.

Meanwhile, community concerns over the current state of the plaza revolve around its unfinished look, poor garbage management, and a number of substance abusers who frequent the area and at times harass other visitors. Overall, there is great concern that improvement of the plaza has been slow, and the community has been offered little means of engaging in the process or communicating with SUKHI.

Attendees of the 9/4 meeting proposed a number of actions that we as a community can take to bring progress to the plaza. First, some people proposed submitting petitions to Councilman Dromm's office, the DOT, and the 115th Precinct. Dromm's office, which has been instrumental in bringing signs of progress to the plaza, welcomes some pressure from the community, which they can use as leverage when speaking with DOT. Most attendees of the meeting agreed that we should call for the release of an official timeline from the city, outlining their expected phases, steps, and timeframes. We also agreed that the same petition could be submitted to the DOT directly. As for the 115th Precinct, many people indicated that the police should provide more frequent monitoring and/or nighttime lighting of the plaza to address the disturbances caused by local substance abusers. In addition to these petitions, most meeting participants expressed interest in assisting SUKHI in bringing maintenance and a stronger sense of community ownership to the plaza. People mentioned the possibility of forming volunteer cleanup crews to periodically maintain this space, which would exhibit public ownership. Another idea had to do with organizing low-cost, low-impact events at the plaza that would not require much or any capital, nor any permits from the city. Examples included yoga classes, book sales, or art fairs. This would draw a wider range of the neighborhood to this space, and advance it as a public space for the entire community.

Subsequent to this meeting, there will be persons in the neighborhood asking you to sign 2 petitions. One is to increase policing of this plaza and the second is to demand a timeline from Danny Dromm's office as to track development
