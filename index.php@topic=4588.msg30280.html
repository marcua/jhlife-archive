(WSJ) On the Grange, in Queens

By SUMATHI REDDY
The Brooklyn Grange, a 40,000-square-foot produce-growing rooftop farm, has finally found a home in the most unlikely of places. Queens.

The five partners in the projectâ€”which include the owners of Roberta's, the Brooklyn pizzeria hot spotâ€”have closed on deal to lease the rooftop of a six-story building on Northern Boulevard in Long Island City.
 
The Brooklyn Grange's new growing space will occupy a rooftop along Northern Boulevard in Queens. .
Next week, 1.2 million pounds of soil from Pennsylvania will be delivered by crane to the top of the building, owned by Acumen Capital Partners and housing a mix of businesses in the former Standard Motors manufacturing plant.

For months the five partners, all Brooklyn residents, searched for a space big enough to hold the city's largest rooftop farm, which is nearly an acre in size. "We had a number of options in Brooklyn and everything just kind of fell through," said Brandon Hoy, one of the owners of Roberta's in Bushwick.

The garden's main investors also pulled out in February, but then the group found the new location, though one outside the confines of Brooklyn.

"The fact that we're in Queens is just sort of another sort of ironic and unlikely aspect of the project," said Anastasia Cole, another of the partners. "I think we all find it pretty funny."

Ben Flanner, the full-time farmer and chief executive of the Brooklyn Grange, said the for-profit business has a budget of about $170,000, coming from a combination of community supporters, investors and personal loans.

The goal is to grow produce nine months out of the year and to sell it in markets in Brooklyn and Queens, as well as to local restaurants.

"The first things we're growing will be greens mixes, radishes and possibly peas," said Mr. Flanner, who last year launched a 6,000-square-foot rooftop farm in Greenpoint.

The Brooklyn upstarts say no name change is in the works. "We're not going to change our name," said Ms. Cole.

"We love Queens, we're excited to be there. But in the end of the day we're Brooklyn folks."

Cool, indeed.

I attended a talk by the guy behind Truck Farm, one of the documentary filmmakers behind King Corn. They're working on making a documentary about Truck Farm, and are interviewing others involved in creative urban farming. I believe Ben Flanner of Brooklyn Grange was in one of the clips he showed (this was the third episode, which does not appear to be on the website).

This reminded me of a thought I had earlier, back when someone on this forum stated that his or her co-op didn't allow residents to walk on the grass of the garden. If people aren't allowed to walk on the grass, why not replace it with a vegetable garden? It will add functionality (growing food) while preserving everything people currently like about their gardens (green, peaceful and quiet space). If two guys can grow a variety of vegetables in the back of a pick-up truck, certainly a wider variety of vegetables can be grown in the co-op gardens of Jackson Heights.

Granted, I don't live in one of these garden co-ops. Maybe some of them do grow vegetables. Or maybe there's a good reason they don't, one that is not immediately obvious to me.
