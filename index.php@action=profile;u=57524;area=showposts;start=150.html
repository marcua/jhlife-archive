The regulatory scheme directed at maintaining sanitary and safe conditions for employees and consumers who sell and/or purchase food from street vendors is directed at protecting the public.  Does it automatically make the food safer?  To some degree, yes.  Personally, I never eat street meat, arepas, or whatever because I never consider food being sold in this context to be completely sanitary.  Where is the food stored at refrigerated temperatures?  Where do they vendors wash their hands?  How about when workers want to use the bathroom? And the customer, I generally wash my hands before I eat - I hate those gels; how does that work with a street vendor?  And the vendors on Roosevelt under the train, YUK!  All sorts of stuff falls off the track on to the onto the street and vendors.  On the other hand, like my wife says when she happily eats street meat, "whatever doesn't kill you makes you stronger!"  With that said, I would rather see her eat from a licensed vendor than an unlicensed vendor.

Neighborhood Chat / Re: Proposed tolls at East River crossings
« on: June 12, 2015, 05:59:11 PM »
Tolls for east side crossings will be a burden on the working class who have to drive in and out of the city.  It is essentially a tax on driving disproportionately affecting those who make the least money in our microcosm (NYC).  Unfortunately, our government is inherently inefficient (by design) and the people working in agencies have an incentive not only to maintain the status quo in their agency (budget/workforce/statutory authority), but to increase its resources. To illustrate my point, imagine a bureaucrat advocating for the dissolution of his/her agency for the good of the public - can't do it, me neither. 

This feature of NYC government (and democracy generally) does not translate to better services for residents and in fact often creates more inefficiency, i.e., generally, the bigger the agency the more inefficient it is.

The point is, just because the City decides to increase taxes on those who drive in and out of the City does not mean that the additional revenue will address any of the underlying concerns used to justify the increase in the first place, i.e., maintenance, wages, etc.

On the other hand, taxing drivers for traveling in and out of the City in the form of bridge tolls may decrease traffic congestion and make Manhattan a nicer place for residents and workers. 

No matter how you cut it, NYC sure is an expensive place to live and it is not getting any cheaper. With that said, I would not be surprised if in my lifetime tolls are imposed on East River bridge crossings. When that happens I am sure that there will be corresponding spike in subway ridership.

Neighborhood Chat / Re: fruit vendors and sale of cars
« on: June 09, 2015, 10:23:05 PM »
You may want to direct your complaints about the vendors to The City of New York Department of Consumer Affairs (DCA).  The City Charter describes DCA's Commissioner's duties, in part, as follows:

The commissioner shall have cognizance and control of the granting, issuing, transferring, renewing, revoking, suspending and cancelling of all licenses and permits, except in the cases with respect to which and to the extent to which any of said powers are conferred on other persons or agency by laws, and shall collect all fees for licenses and permits the collection of which by some other person or agency is not authorized by law. (d) The commissioner shall enforce all laws relating to the advertising and offering for sale and the sale of all commodities, goods, wares and services; in addition he shall receive and evaluate complaints and initiate his own investigations relating to these matters and take appropriate action, including referral to a federal or state agency. (e) The commissioner, in the performance of said functions, shall be authorized to hold public and private hearings, administer oaths, take testimony, serve subpoenas, receive evidence, and to receive, administer, pay over and distribute monies collected in and as a result of actions brought for violations of laws relating to deceptive or unconscionable trade practices, or of related laws, and to promulgate, amend and modify rules and regulations necessary to carry out the powers and duties of the department. (f) The commissioner shall exercise the powers of a commissioner of public markets under the agriculture and markets law with respect to open air markets. - See more at: http://codes.lp.findlaw.com/nycode/NYC/64/2203#sthash.wrPMQhCA.dpuf-

I believe that subsection (d) and (f) may be right on point with respect to the Commissioner of DCA's jurisdiction over fruit vendors.  Moreover, under the Rules of the City of New York, DCA is explicitly charged with license enforcement.  6 R.N.Y.C. ยง ยง 1-01, et seq.  <http://rules.cityofnewyork.us/codified-rules?agency=DCA>.  And no matter how you cut it, the DCA has an incentive to issue fines and collect $ on behalf of the City with regards to fruit vendors - unlike any other City agency or politician. 

Assuming that the vendors you complain about are operating in violation of applicable rules and/or regulations, then you may be able to obtain better results by contacting the Department of Consumer Affairs, via 311 on the internet: http://www1.nyc.gov/311/.

My big gripe about the neighborhood fruit vendors relates to the quality and freshness of the oranges - it's like playing dice. 


Good luck.

Neighborhood Chat / Re: Jackson Heights featured in Today's New York Times
« on: May 23, 2015, 09:01:28 AM »
I recently moved to a 3 bedroom in JH.  We could have moved to any neighborhood in Queens, i.e., very large downpayment, very large household income, spouses both have advanced degrees, and no debt. We chose JH because it is way closer to the City than Forest Hills (five or six extra stops on the local line - sometimes the express goes local) and FH is too close to the Boulevard of Death (we have kids).  Bayside is nice, but then you have to buy a car and pay for the long Island Rail Road in addition to the MetroCard.  Additionally, JH offers far more dining in terms of ethnic restaurants and you don't need a car.  (Aside: we had to live in Queens because it was close to extended family who play a role in providing childcare.)

As compared to Astoria and LIC, JH is far less expensive/reasonable and the historic district's character makes it a special place.  I wanted a 3-bedroom, but did not want to spend over a million dollars.

LIC is pretty awesome - nice parks and cool bars - but the 3-bedrooms are ridiculously expensive.  Astoria is pretty nice, but the housing stock is disorganized and it only has access to local train lines.  Sunnyside is pretty awesome, but the 7 train is unreliable.

JH has the best options in terms of housing and transportation and most importantly offers the best bargain - at least for now.

JH's diversity is a big fat plus - it keeps life interesting in terms of people watching and exposure to different culture, i.e., food and entertainment.

On that note, I am not surprised the NY Times did an article on my news neighborhood.
