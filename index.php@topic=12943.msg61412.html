I don't think these numbers can give any indication of the price trend. Here are the details of these numbers
http://si.wsj.net/public/resources/images/NY-DL424A_NYMET_16U_20150213151208.jpg

For all of these neighborhoods, only the sale listed on streeteasy have been collected. Not many properties in Queens, Brooklyn and Bronx, Staten Island are listed on streeteasy, that is why they only had less than 100 sales in each neighborhood. Queens Douglaston has only 18 sales on streeteasy.

Second of all, the median sale price is not apple and apple comparisons. So if 2013 sale was about all big apartments while 2014 sale was only about studios and one bedroom, you will see median price decrease even though the same apartment has increased more than 20%.

To really understand the price trend, we really should focus on the manhanttan condo index http://streeteasy.com/nyc/market/condo_index, which is based on the sale history of the same apartment, so there is no problem of comparing apple and orange.

The condo index does not cover Jackson Heights or Queens. The best thing for Jackson Heights will be from http://www.jhrealestatereport.com/, collected by the knowledgeable local realtor Daniel Karatzas. He even collected the sale price in each well known buildings in Jackson Heights and divided it according to apartment size so one can easily spot the trend.

As far as I recall, the Karatzas report is not making any predictions.  They are just reports, but it doesn't mean they are useless. If someone reads it and sees that an apartment in the Towers sold for 10% more this year than it sold for last year, and believes that if they buy at this year's price that that would guarantee them a 10% increase the following year, then that's their mistake.  Real estate holds no guarantees.  I think everyone knows that, although some people tend to forget or ignore.

I happen to enjoy reading about real estate trends as long as the numbers are useful, and I feel the Karatzas' report is useful for seeing and understanding RE trends in the neighborhood, since it comes as close as possible to comparing apples to apples.  I do understand that a trend that shows an increase in prices isn't guaranteed to continue, just as a downward trend also is not guaranteed to continue.

I must admit I wasn't able to read the WSJ article because it was behind a pay wall, but I believe I got the gist of it from jhcoopbuyer's jpg and comments.
