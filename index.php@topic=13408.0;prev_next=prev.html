Before you come out swinging Rhyde (which is an altogether too common and unfortunate occurrence on this forum), it might benefit you to find out a little of the history of 5 Pointz.

The artwork at five pointz was originally created in partnership and with the full blessing of the building owners.    It's curator, Meres One,  attracted  artists, including the now famous Banksy, from all over the world to exhibit there. Artists had to submit their request and present examples of their work before being allowed to paint. Hardly the act of vandals wouldn't you say? In fact, one of the things that made this place unique was the fact that artists could take their time to render and create legally. Some graffiti artists saw this as a dilution of the art while others saw this as a chance to bring graffiti to a higher level.

Up until about five years ago, the interior was filled with low cost artist studios. It was a place flourishing with creativity. It also happened to be one of the largest tourist attractions in Queens,  drawing thousands of visitors each year.  Ever go to one of the open art events there? They were wonderful. Ever get a tour of the Exterior by one of the graffiti artists? I did about two years ago.   I was especially interested to learn about this one graffiti artist who, through quick sprays of paint,  utilized pointillism (yes, as in Seurat) to create extremely realistic pictures.  He refused to take money after spending an hour with me and others explaining the thought process of the artists. Hardly again an act typical of vandals.

In 2009 the main staircase collapsed. The building was for all purposes condemned. Only the exterior continued to be utilized as an art space by the graffiti artists. But it was at this point that the owners considered demolition to rebuild as high price residential units.  And so a legal battle began, and Queens lost one of its most prominent cultural landmarks.

Thanks for the history lesson. I went to several of their parties and had a great time. I get that they contributed a lot to what is now the burgeoning renaissance of Western Queens. What I still fail to understand is the gall to litigate against the building owners who gave them so much for so long (space, low cost artists studios, etc.) and the absurd sense of entitlement these artists have when they knew all along they were operating on unofficially borrowed land.

Sometimes you become a victim of your own success. Small business owners alike put blood, sweat and tears into their businesses in these neighborhoods they operated within. Their collective efforts have turned around Western Queens over time. The smart small business owners were prescient enough to buy the buildings they operated within or at the very least stay on top of negotiating favorable long-term leases with their landlords. The ones who couldn't or didn't become casualties. 

IF their work was as impactful as you make it out to be, in a city full of benevolent art patrons, how come no one  stepped up to buy the space or find them alternate space?
