Neighborhood Chat / New evidence on the broken windows theory
« on: February 24, 2009, 10:23:13 AM »
The Boston Globe says...

Researchers, working with police, identified 34 crime hot spots. In half of them, authorities set to work - clearing trash from the
sidewalks, fixing street lights, and sending loiterers scurrying. Abandoned buildings were secured, businesses forced to meet code, and more arrests made for misdemeanors. Mental health services and homeless aid referrals expanded.

The rest of the article is here
http://www.boston.com/news/local/massachusetts/articles/2009/02/08/breakthrough_on_broken_windows

I'm glad to see hard evidence on the topic has finally surfaced.  I had previously commented on the shortage of hard facts on the broken windows theory here
http://www.jacksonheightslife.com/community/index.php?topic=724.msg4283#msg4283

I would like to focus attention to this particular paragraph from the above article:

Many police departments across the country already use elements of the broken windows theory, or focus on crime hot spots. The Lowell experiment offers guidance on what seems to work best. Cleaning up the physical environment was very effective; misdemeanor arrests less so, and boosting social services had no apparent impact.

This means even the coldest-hearted Republican has a good reason to support the grass-eating hippies at the Jackson Heights Beautification Group (I say this with love). This isn't to say that the police are not essential, not at all.  Rather, if we want to further reduce crime in our community, the efforts of JHBG may go further than anything the police could bring to bear.

Neighborhood Chat / Entrepreneurs: build your book store, coffee shop, bar in Jackson Heights
« on: September 08, 2008, 08:44:56 PM »
To switch into hard sell mode for a second:

Jackson Heights is a major investment opportunity for community building oriented entrepreneurs.

Espresso 77, Terraza, The Ready Penney Inn,  Novo, Starbucks, Yogur|Berry, and others bustle with regulars.  Many of these places are less than two years old!  With several major subway stops around the corner, 37th avenue is a wall of people during the daytime and the channels which lead into the avenue are also heavily traveled.  Don't take my word for it, go check them out!

More community-oriented places like book stores, coffee shops, bars, pubs, beer gardens, etc. will immediately benefit from the draw of people towards the already established hangouts plus encourage more new foot traffic to come to the area.  Espresso 77 and Starbucks are practically next to one-another and both have steady business.  This is the very textbook example of the network effect in action.  Such venues have the opportunity of being viable and profitable for many years, not only surviving but flourishing in the coming upswing.

Sure, you have the option of coming in much later on when it's a safer bet, but safer bet is another way of saying lower returns.

Stake your claim now and earn the rewards that come with foresight.

Plus we'll help you promote the hell out of it.

Neighborhood Chat / Broadband Internet Access Recommendations
« on: December 19, 2007, 10:46:44 AM »
Time Warner and Verizon charge around $60/month for broadband internet access in Jackson Heights.

I find this obscene.  The cost of the bandwidth is nowhere near this much and the community is so densely populated that the infrastructure costs per person are practically zero.  They could rip it out and re-install it every year and still turn a profit on us.  What we're doing is subsidizing the very sparsely populated portions of their networks (think suburbs) with our monthly fees.

Can we do this ourselves?  If building management bought a wholesale uplink to the internet and shared it between tenants I figure we could drive the monthly recurring costs down to $10.  This is not as hard as it sounds since one connection could be shared between buildings; even sold to neighboring private residences.

So what's saving $50/month worth?

Every commercial building I've worked in does this to some degree, but I've never seen a residential building do it.

Even a local business that did this for-profit could charge much less than Verizon/TWC.
