Monday, October 19, Canadian Election
« on: October 18, 2015, 08:46:33 PM »
Canadian elections are normally a couple of months long. Not this one. The Prime Minister, Stephen Harper, called the election 11 weeks ago, which will make it the longest election in a century.

When it started, it looked like it was between Mr. Harper's Conservatives and the New Democratic Party, but it's now clear that 43 year old Justin Trudeau, leader of the Liberal Party, will be the new Prime Minister. The question is whether he will head a minority or majority government.

For those who are interested, this is going to be an interesting election to follow tomorrow night on TV, radio and social media.

Quote from: dssjh on October 19, 2015, 10:27:20 AM
i have a lot of friends scattered all around Canada -- to a person, they seem relieved at the apparent imminent defeat of Harper (i think it would be safe to call him the equivalent of a Tea Party guy) but just about all are disappointed in Trudeau, who some have called Harper-lite.

Supporters of the Tea Party would regard the Canadian Conservative Party as socialist if not communist. While I disagree with Mr. Harper on many issues, he well understood that ideas like dismantling universal health care, rolling back abortion rights, reintroducing capital punishment, attempting to curb gay marriage, etc, etc would result in a serious backlash at the polls.

One of the post-election points being made in the press today is that Mr. Harper got involved in politics in the first place because he disagreed deeply with the ideas of Justin Trudeau's father, and that losing to Justin Trudeau, who clearly shares many of his father's ideas, is particularly bitter. It's instructive to watch Justin Trudeau's victory speech last night, and then watch, on YouTube, the wonderful eulogy that he gave at his father's funeral in 2000.

Given that, the idea that he is Harper-lite is more than a little odd. Not to mention that he was the only one of the three major candidates to make rejection of austerity central to his platform. Followers of UK politics will know that Labour's Ed Miliband was recently crushed by the Conservatives after, among other things, essentially adopting Tory economic policy. In Cabada, the NDP's Mulcair, despite what happened to Miliband, did the same. Only Trudeau rejected austerity.

What happened is that Mr. Trudeau got his party's traditional centrist base to show up and vote, and in addition attracted a large number of moderate Conservatives and moderate members of the New Democratic Party.

Yes, he isn't his father - who was a truly brilliant, charismatic to the point of being electric, but austere man - but I believe that Justin Trudeau is what Canada needs right now. One thing that's clear, given the election result, is that he has an incredible amount of talent to draw on for his cabinet.

While I'm not keen on the headline, I think that Roger Cohen's analysis of why Justin Trudeau turned the country his way in the last few weeks is pretty accurate: http://mobile.nytimes.com/2015/10/23/opinion/canada-elections-justin-trudeau.html?referer=https://www.google.com/

This is a situation where both the Conservative Prime Minister and the Labor leader Mr. Mulcair thought that they could dismiss Mr. Trudeau, who is both young and was trailing, and found out on election day that the country wasn't impressed.

The big loser is the NDP/Labour party, which has made significant strides in the last few years (due, importantly, less to Mr. Mulcair than the previous leader who died of cancer), but is now back to its historic performance in national elections. Mr. Mulcair is finished as leader, in part because of the condescension with which he treated the new Prime Minister during the campaign, and the question is where the NDP goes from here.
