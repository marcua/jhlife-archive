Why do you live in Jackson Heights?
« on: February 04, 2008, 02:55:36 PM »
I came across an interesting thread from Trip Advisor whereby the original poster, an upstate NY resident, is seeking information on worthwhile points of interest to visit in Flushing and Jackson Heights.  The discussion veers off from the main topic, and two posters go on to describe their differing views of the Jackson Heights community.  This prompts me to ask the following question:

Why have you chosen to live in Jackson Heights?

Trip Advisisor link:
http://www.tripadvisor.com/ShowTopic-g60763-i5-k1682838-Exploring_Flushing_and_Jackson_Heights-New_York_City_New_York.html

To answer my own question, I'll state that Queens was a choice made initially by my parents.  Basically, I didn't have much say in the move 30 years ago, but I'm sure glad my parents made the choice they did.  As a teen, I lived in neighboring Woodside, but it was Jackson Heights, a couple of blocks away, where all the fun stuff was happening.  We had the record store, the now defunct Baskin Robbins, the diners, the pizza joints, the movie theaters, Flushing Meadows Park not so far off, etc.  Upon getting married, my husband and I decided to settle down in Jackson Heights...again, just a few blocks from my old stomping grounds of Woodside.  We both managed to practically steal a large and beautiful co-op apartment in the now Historic District back in the early 1990s, and are now basking in the glory of our purchase.  This was during a time when there was a new vision for a resurgence of the Jackson Heights of yore.

Over the years, I've enjoyed the changes I've seen in the community...the growth of varied immigrant groups and the businesses they've brought to our neighborhood.  These changes are what makes Jackson Heights so unique and diverse and attractive to people, not to mention the beautiful and historical architecture.  In my opinion, Jackson Heights has moved in an upward direction.  Would I want to see 37th Avenue lined with Gaps, Pottery Barns and like stores?  I'm not sure.  I'll chicken out of answering the question while waiting to see how we handle our newest entry, Starbucks!

Wow.  Great question Chuckster!!!

I love Jackson Heights because it is authentic.  Diverse.  Quirky.  Different from everywhere else on planet earth.   It is an urban, 24-hour neighborhood where I can walk out on the streets at any hour of the day or night and buy a mango or a carton of Ben and Jerry's.  It is a lush, green community.  Look up!  See how the brick on those apartment buildings juts in and out in funny ways?  Each one of those apartments up there was built for a maximum of light and air.  Intentionally.  Look up and down the business district avenues.  Nearly every storefront represents a family's dream.  Planning.  So much hard work.  A lot of risk.  But the dream of owning a business and not having a boss and making it all work.  A lot of dreams-come-true behind those apartment windows too.  Dreams of leaving economic, political or religious opression to seek out a new life in a new country. 

I joke that we have a parade every Sunday.  Not quite EVERY Sunday.  But it is close.  And most years those beautiful while lights go up for Diwali and stay up through Christmas and past Three Kings day.  For one week in the Spring, the trees on 82nd street go into a white bloom, and look like a canopy over the street.  I've mourned the death of many of those trees, but there are still enough around. 

I like to say that EVERYONE in Jackson Heights is a minority.  I was misquoted once as saying no one here is a minority.  Which is of course, the opposite of what I meant.  Each of us is a little humbled when we walk down the street and hear families bantering in unfamiliar tongues.  When the yummy smells of tonight's supper seeps through into the hallway from a neighbor's apartment, and the spices are unidentifiable.  When lots of people are running around on the street celebrating a holiday we did not even know was coming.  Or celebrating a sports victory in a game that we do not follow.  Or celebrating a political victory in a far away land. 

I live here because I love living in Jackson Heights.  Yeah, I will probably go to Starbucks from time to time.  But I mourn its arrival.  When the Gap and Pottery Barn line 37th Ave., I suspect I'll cash out my equity and move on.   But I will do so with profound sadness.

I am grateful to KC for providing such a beautiful answer so I don't have to.   I am also grateful to John Prester for a good laugh.  I wish I knew what sarcasm/honesty ratio to read into his post.

I grew up in beautiful Stony Brook, NY, and later lived in central NJ, Washington DC, and finally Oakland/Berkeley CA before moving to NYC.  As a fan of cities and a student of urban planning, I have spent countless days walking through neighborhoods admiring their architecture, tasting their noshables, observing their social and economic dynamics, and trying to interpret their physical histories.  Before I had kids, it was practically a hobby of mine. 

When I was moving to NYC from California, I made a number of trips to scout out neighborhoods.  Like John, I would have chosen Manhattan in a second, but knew I was priced out of that market.  I spent a great deal of time exploring Brooklyn, and I found that among the close-in areas I could possibly afford areas like Gowanus and industrial Williamsburg, which were not really neighborhoods at all.  I took the subway line further out into Brooklyn, looking for the idyllic multicultural neighborhood of my dreams, but never really found it.

Finally, I began exploring Queens.  I was familiar with the history of her idealistic early suburban settlements -- Sunnyside, Jackson Heights, Forest Hills -- and started exploring these on foot.  I liked them all, particularly JH for the whole mix of reasons KC cited.  My wife and I planned only to rent for a while, to get our NYC bearings while we found a neighborhood to call home for the long term.  BUt JH quickly grew on us, and now we have no plans to leave.

Years ago, for some reason, I was a passenger in a car driving the length of Roosevelt Avenue and Queens Blvd. from GCP to the Queensboro Bridge.  It was about 9:00 at night, and the person who was driving me commented that the area didn't look too safe.  I felt exactly the opposite: the street was crowded with people, shops of every nationality were open, young couples were pushing strollers.  Yes, the elevated subway added a certain unpleasantness to the scene, but all of these people chose to be here anyway.  To me it looked very safe and appearing.  I didn't know where I was at the time, and little did I suspect that years later, I'd be living there.
