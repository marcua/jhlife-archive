Greetings, JH Lifers.  Just in case you missed it, this website will be closing down on December 31st.  It's been a great journey, but it is time for this site to walk off into the sunset (or whatever lies on the other side of the BQE).   Please see our recent announcement for more information.

My responses to your thoughtful questions are interspersed.

Quote from: toddg on February 08, 2009, 12:53:09 PM
You've asked a great set of questions.  I'll give you my quick take on this...

If the top level domain administrator is the City of New York, then perhaps the community boards should take on responsibility for the neighborhood sites.  Or a single contractor for the entire city, like http://nyc.everyblock.com

If (hopefully) the domain administrator is some independent group, then I think there would be more freedom to choose locally-grown sites.


Connecting.nyc Inc. is an independent entity, a NYS not-for-profit (501c3 applicant) enthusiastically seeking public engagement and support for creation of locally controlled "Neighborhood Media Entities (NMEs)." We're developing the scope and accountability mechanisms for JacksonHeights.nyc (and 300 other neighborhood names - see http://www.openplans.org/projects/campaign-for.nyc/traditional-neighborhood-names in consultation with neighborhood residents. To our knowledge this is a first.

Quote
Many sites already offer demographics, schools, and other data (e.g. http://www.city-data.com/zips/11372.html and http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jackson_Heights,_Queens), so such information is hardly scarce.  JHLife tries to offer some of it here, but it would be hard to take on a mandate to maintain such information at an all-volunteer, non-revenue-generating site like this (just my view... I don't speak for this website).

Raw data sources might be made available on these sites, but it would be up to each neighborhood to decide on the content that meets its needs. Obviously the amalgamation of discussions from sites like this would be useful, as well, mechanisms to identify issues and opportunities and the organizing tools to facilitate their addressing, would make these civic instruments of the highest - bringing the Internet home. Revenue sources should also be made by individual neighborhoods.

Quote
On the other hand, in my view, only a two-way site that allows active participation would generate the traffic volumes needed to become a community's primary internet gateway, in practice, not just in name. 


I totally agree and suspect that if engaged residents, like those participating on this site, don't demand the best possible local communication tools, these neighborhood sites will be milquetoast, or possibly developed by a single outside entity with the ability to sell advertising on a broad scale. It's my belief that each neighborhood.nyc site provides an opportunity for someone to create "the" central site for neighborhood civic and cultural communication, with revenue from advertising and sponsorships. Economic viability will vary by neighborhood, and it might come come to shape neighborhood borders. I'd think a JacksonHeights.nyc site could make it financially if it has engaging local content. But this can only come from the residents. So calendars, discussions, pictures, videos, reports - crime, housing prices, health, education ... - and marketing information, supplied by residents (and suitably rigged site software), might provide enough eyes to feed a mouth or two. And if the neighborhood maintains some oversight of the site, we might create an even better neighborhood.

Quote
But will the "official" status conferred by .nyc participation allow a site to maintain the independence needed for freedom of speech?

We're hoping that civic involvement will create the opportunity to keep these under neighborhood control. There is nothing that makes them "official" sites other than resident demand. If residents don't stick a stake in these sites, they can remain idle (i.e., never used), be given/sold to an existing media company, be auctioned to the highest bidder - probably requiring that the site content be tweaked to pay off investors...

Quote
I suspect you've been thinking about these questions for a while, so I hope you'll share your views and insights with us as well!

This has been a passion of mine for some time. I hope Jackson Heights can take a lead in making neighborhood.nyc sites a reality. Our wiki at http://www.openplans.org/projects/campaign-for.nyc/traditional-neighborhood-names has more on this subject. But a lot more thinking needs to be done. I hope you'll join me.

Best,

Tom Lowenhaupt
