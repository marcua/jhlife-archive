With all due respect, I think you're wrong. You can't ignore the economic and social factors that fuel the growth of a market. There's a reason there's a market for store bought costumes, just like there's a reason there is a market for easy to prepare family meals. That reason is that most households don't have a full time homemaker spending her day running the household, preparing meals, making clothes for her kids, and Halloween costumes.

Looks like store bought costumes started appearing in the 1930s, and that kind of makes sense if you figure at least some part of the market had money to buy their kid a Count Dracula cape. Economic prosperity post WWII allowed many more families more disposable income, which would allow them to spend money on acquiring parts of, or an entire store bought costume if their kid wanted to dress up as a certain character, but it also allowed many families to live a comfortable middle class life on only one income -- freeing up parental resources to be spent on helping kids come up with a home made or partly home made costume.

Most families today don't have that luxury, and even if they did, many parents would lack some of the skills that their parents and grandparents had (how many 33 year old do you suppose know how to use a sewing machine today versus 50 years ago?)

Also, I'm not sure where you got the idea that childhood obesity is no longer a problem. It remains a problem.

I think the word "privilege" is one of the most misused words in our language. So many people take it to mean "anyone who had more money than I did growing up" or say things like "if you have enough money to eat and don't have 20 part-time jobs you are privileged".  It got skewed even more during the Wall Street protests.

Privilege is about power and the abuse of it. It's about using that wealth to gain more wealth while stepping on others to get it. It's about benefits given to some but not others. 

It's not about this petty crap on who has time to make a homemade costume or not and if having that time means you are privileged. It waters down the true meaning of the word how it is used in the US - to denote the 1% who have a disproportionate amount of power and wealth and use it to keep getting more power. It's something we should all remember during this frightening election cycle.
