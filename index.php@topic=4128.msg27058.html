Hello all,
We've been Astoria residents for eight years and are considering moving to JH.  I have some questions, any feedback is greatly appreciated:

First, are there any coops/condos to avoid? And if so, why? And any brokers you recommend (or don't recommend?)
Second, are there any coops or condos that are really terrific?
Third, how are the schools, esp. PS 69 and PS 212?
Fourth, how long is the average commute to midtown?
Finally, how  is the neighborhood for kids? Are there any parent groups?

And of course feel free to let me know anything else that might be useful to know?

Many thanks, Rachel

Hi Rachel,

You will see many units.

1.  Decide whether you want to live in a small building with ten to 15 shareholders or a larger building.

2.  The small buildings in Jackson Heights are the original co-ops buildings built on or about 1920's, a little earlier or a little later.  These buildings, although extremeley charming may not have an elevator, will have a garden, or may have an elevator that takes you directly to your unit.  The concern will be that there is little money in the reserve fund, and ultimately that will affect your budget as the monies have to be collected from 10 or 15 shareholders.
But many families love the buildings and I do too! 

The other alternative is to select a larger building with more money in the reserve so that if there are any major capital improvements it doesn't affect you as much.

3.  Decide whether you need a building with or without a garage.  The garage spaces are not plentiful, but there are a few units out there that you can purchase with the garage included. 

4.  Deciding whether you would like to purchase a condo at 3-5 % and no board approval or a co-op at 10-20% downpayment will depend on your budget.  There are some sponsor units in the 'hood that are 10% and no board approval required, and you may sublet asap.

5.  The option to sublet will be a decision that will influence your purchase.  Therefore, if you want to sublet some sponsor units will allow it immediately, some condos as well, but some co-ops will allow it after two years, five years, or never.  The never applies to the smaller buildings that I refer to in #2 above.

4.  There are many competent agents and brokers within the community and you just have to make some calls and get acquainted with some of them.  It is important for you to have a mortgage pre-approval from you bank so that you know what you can comfortably afford.  Therefore, when contacting an agent your price point, credit, downpayment, income details and wish list will be helpful.  Approximatley, 99% of the agents and brokers in Jackson Heights are Realtors and are members of the Multiple Listing Service.

5.  It's hard to recommend or not recommend a broker or agent as you may simply like the person that wasn't recommended.  Sometimes it's about chemistry as in any thing that is personal and emotional as is the purchase of your new home.

6.  There are terrific co-ops, but I feel that it is important for you to walk around with some agents and get a sense of what units are attractive to you and your family.  Then you can ask about the building's financials, which ultimately will be the most important part of your decision-making.

7.  P.S. 69 has been wonderful for me and my little ones, but I am as well home when the arrive so that I can assist them with their work, etc.  I as well found The Garden School to be pretty terrific.  I can't offer any feedback on P.S. 212.

8.  The commute is easy and quick.  With so many options you will be very happy that you can shift gears if needed.

9.  I have raised the four kids here and although we have enjoyed activities at the 92nd Street Y in the city, as we previously lived on 96 and Park.  We as well have enjoyed the Boys' Club of New York, which has a facility in Elmhurst, we have participated in many playgroups at St. Mark's, full time enrollment and summer camp at Garden School, The program at the Methodist church and from what I hear the parent groups for the little people are plentiful these days in the 'hood.

There is reading time in one of the coffee shops and plenty of things to do in the summer when the Farmers' Market and the 78th Playstreet opens.

The Hall of Science is my favorite place and you can take the 7 train and get off at 111th Street, many attend the Y's and Queens College for other activities.

There is soccer at P.S. 69, Garden School has extra-curricular activities, there is an Art Club, Music is available, spanish lessons available, after-school programs for those that have a later schedule and much more.

It's a great multi-culti community and there is a little bit for everyone.

Shopping is great and the majority of the properties are a block or two within walking distance to everything.

I'm happy and so is my family.

I wish you the best!   :smiley6600:

I don't know how old your children are but there seems to be a few "extra" child friendly buildings in the neighborhood.

Roosevelt Terrace has an awesome private playground.

My building, The Griswold, The Saybrook and I believe also one of the Berkeley Buildings have indoor children's playrooms which is great for little ones during the cold months.

Also, there is a listserve "Jackson Heights Families." I don't belong to it but many of my friends with children do.  

In our building alone, there was a baby boom last year and we have a dozen little ones.  Many, many young families moving to the neighborhood.

I hear wonderful things about the Renaissance Charter School as well as the Garden School.

Good luck with your search.
