Just a tid bit of info

NYS states that the seller is the client and the buyer is the customer.  This can change of course if you hire an agent to find you a property in which case your rep. is "buyers agent" and you will likely pay them.  An agent has a fiduciary duty to his client, 95% or more of the time this is the seller.  Dual agency will develop if you have a broker search for a property that meet your needs but you don't pay them and he/she will receive a split from the listing agent or from you and the seller.  This is totally legal as long as it is disclosed.  The danger exist that if the listing agents are not co-brokering or are paying an extremely low split (unfortunately very common in Queens) you will not find out about many wonderful properties since it won't be worth it for your agent to provide this info unless of course you agree to pay your broker an additional percentage of the sale upon closing. 

Most brokers have never done this and it is totally foreign to them but in "my experience"  the buyer will always get the best price when they hire and pay a broker to find them the best property for the best price.  A broker will negotiate the best price possible whenever they allow the broker to do their "thing".  Don't forget that full disclosure must be made and if you really want the best out there don't be penny wise and dollar foolish.  Listen to your Broker and you may find that you will save thousands of dollars.  You may not think about this but RE is just like other businesses, many of the brokers know each other and they all want to do the one thing they love the most, TO CLOSE!  Sometimes buyers get lucky and find a fresh listing with the right price but often this doesn't happen.  Last but not least...Don't Settle for less than you really want unless you are getting a super deal.

NO I DONT DO BUYERS AGENCY IN QUEENS.  SORRY. But there are some local agents out there that can help you.  Listen to the recommendations here.

Anyone that has the luxury of time to market and sell their property should.  But for those that have really busy lives and that luxury of time is one they don't have should of course hand it over to a broker.  Obviously a time frame is worked out and a seller should do their homework and negotiate with someone that will look out for their interest.  Another reason to work with a broker is the exclusive agency.  For those that do not know what it means is that in an exclusive agency the seller can continue to market their property and at the same time sign up with a broker.  If the seller finds a buyer then the seller owns nothing to the broker.  If the broker sells it or anyone other than the owner of the property sells it the listing agent will still get paid upon closing.  He may or may not have been co-brokering but that's another drama all together.

Title: Re: What type of realtor should I use?
Post by: jmp99 on May 27, 2008, 06:17:49 PM
Hello all,
I come from a real estate family and I wanted to give you, particularly sellers, my angle on Jackson Heights' realtors.  The local agencies - like MPC Properties (Carfagna), Beaudoin Realty, JH Landmark, JH Properties, 718 Realty - don't work w/ other real estate agents and keep the full commission amount that sellers pay them.  Larger real estate agencies - Prudential Douglas Elliman, Brown Harris Stevens, and Corcoran - always share the commission with cooperating agents.  When the broker that you hire works cooperatively with other agents (in other words, he/she shares the commission), all agents citywide with a Jackson Heights customer will bring their buyers to the property.  Go with the bigger agencies, folks - they offer an incentive to the city's real estate community to visit your property.  The more people that see your place, the more likely you are to sell... the more likely you are to see a bidding war... the more likely you are to sell for the highest price!  The smaller agencies limit exposure to your property by only showing your place to customers without brokers - in this case, you'd never know the highest attainable price because you've excluded every broker with a potential customer from the deal.  Remember, competition is a good thing - play it smart, and good luck.

With all due respect, a realtor does not have to be a "Biggie" to be a member of MLS.  It's relatively inexpensive to become a member since minimal requirements are needed to join.  I see nothing wrong with the MLS service since I am a member and I have benefited from it's membership.  What I am against is the falsehood and attempt to mystify membership to the MLS.  A member of MLS realtor is not more professional or better qualified than others since professionalism and efficiency has nothing to do with membership.  While I admit that many of the programs and services including specialized training for members are beneficial these training services are not a requirement for membership and are not free to members thus the vast majority of members will pass on the fancier training. 
 
The training is usually designed for special certification purposes and to "close the deal".   They are not necessarily structured to serve the general interest of buyers or sellers.  I have met  plenty of unqualified, uneducated and incapable realtors as members of the MLS but this doesn't mean I am going to generalized and attempt to point the finger at those brokers that depend strictly on MLS for their business.  When you place a property in the MLS system it's thrown into a pool of thousands of similar properties.  The property is seen by other agents that will be able to offer it to their customer hence the "best possible" scenario for the seller.  In "THEORY" on paper it works, but so does communism.  MLS is a tool and an option for realtors to help produce buyers for their listings.  On a sellers agency side the agent is able to show the prospective buyer any available property that has been listed.  Unfortunately many of the better properties don't hit the MLS.  The reason for that are many.  For one, many realtors use the system to show how much better their "in-house" listings are. 
 
A large number of realtors are uncaring to other agents time and waste it by bringing unqualified "lookers" or people posing as buyers.  It's so rampant that many listings agents will simply refuse to show the properties unless their client can show proof of funds or produce a current pre-approval.  On a number of occasions I found myself helping sellers and buyers with their coop needs by using the MLS systems.  I was able to help and co-broke these coop units but the listing brokers were so surprised they still call me today to help them with other properties.  MLS is specially great for other types of properties like houses, newly develop properties and of course the more difficult properties to sell.  To successfully market and sell Coops you need a neighborhood broker. Local brokers will have a better chance to know the building, they may know the Coop members, shareholders, the super or at least are close enough to the action that they know the intricate sociopolitical foundation of the particular coop board they may be working with.  That is something a Broker outside of the neighborhood circle cannot accomplish for you wether it's a 200 agent office or a one person team. 
 
So, I beg to differ when it comes to the local coop market and add that you are dead wrong, not about the MLS but about the so called "BIGGIE".   What you may not realize is that many local brokers, and you did take the liberty to name quite a few, do co-broke but on a local level, a sort of their own close knit mini MLS.  I sold a few properties that were with some very well known realtors in Flushing and Bayside for 6 Months with no takers.  The sellers had dealt with me on other matters and gave me a few properties to sell in JH, Corona and in Forest Hills.  Two weeks later I had offers for all units in JH, and in Corona.  Forest Hills was a bit tougher since it had the most available units in the market but 5 weeks later and lots of leg work produced an accepted offer. Choosing to be a member of MLS should be an automatic, deciding to keep the listing in-house is the brokers and sellers business.  The broker may know he can sell it within 21 days.  The likelihood of an "outside" broker knowing what the Coop Board is looking for is slim to none whereas the local broker will easily qualify a prospective buyer.  Why in the world would any one co-broke a listing that is a sure quick sale.  Now, if the property is on the market for 21 days with no serious buyer or the broker knows the property is not such an easy sale then absolutely, MLS from day one. If you choose to list your property with a broker you can request that they place it on MLS as a condition to the listing and most will.  If someone is not ready to buy and is just looking I recommend hitting the open houses and browse sites like realtor.com.  If you are looking to buy in a specific area or in a specific bloc or building avoid the MLS stigma and simply look into sites like Backpage and Craigslist or hit the streets and walk into several local RE offices and be honest.  Tell them that you are in the market, that you are ready and that you will visit other brokers in the area in your search.  On a final note, GET PRE-QUALIFIED before you start making appointments and be treated like royalty.

I met a possible buyer and the first thing she did is asked me for my pocket card.  :o  I wasn't carrying it and I apologized but assured her I was a licensed broker.  I even encouraged them to call the DOS and ask.  The person believed me and we continued to tour the unit but all the time I was very upset with myself thinking this individual was DOS checking up on people and I would probably get a reprimand or worse, a violation.  Well, it wasn't the DOS and the person admitted to me that she worked in the past as a real estate agent but never got licensed.  She told me she did it for 7 or 8 years before another "jealous" real estate agent began a which hunt and she just had to get out of the business.  I asked her why didn't she just get licensed and she decided to change the conversation.

This is a reminder, Real Estate Agents have to be licensed and they must carry their pocket cards.  Their licensed is held by their Broker in their office in a safe place.  If you feel that a Real Estate agent is not really an agent ask for their pocket card and if she or he cannot produce it then maybe you shouldn't do business with them.  Would you like to be driven in a cab by an unlicensed driver?  Real Estate agents have to go to school and get licensed.  The State has almost doubled the requirements for agents and brokers alike.  Even the appraisers now have to prove some college credits...I think this is all for the better.
