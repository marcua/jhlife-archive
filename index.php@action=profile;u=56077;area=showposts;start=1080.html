Real Estate & Home Improvement / Re: JH rents rising faster than other Queens neighborhoods - DNA article
« on: August 27, 2015, 12:01:05 PM »
I got a chuckle at what some listing agents tried to pass as an "eat in kitchen" when I was looking. Usually it would be a galley kitchen with appliances and counter on one wall, and the other wall left empty and a tiny table and stool good for a small child pushed against the wall.

Studios are tricky. Who is going to live in a studio? A two income couple? Not in Queens. I knew couples who bought studios in Manhattan, but they did so with an eye to rennovating, living in it (uncomfortably) for a year, and then renting it out. These were couples looking to invest in real estate. Obviously, you need a building that allows owners to sublet. An awful lot don't allow that in JH.

What always seemed tricky to me about studios I think they appeal to singles. But, if you're younger than say 40, aren't people still holding out hope they might couple up? So buying a studio in an 100% owner occupied building seems like a bad move if you hope to have a reason for some flexibility in the near future. For most young singletons (early-mid 20s) the savings needed is an issue, and I think people who have parents that will bankroll their first home purchase probably aren't looking to live in Queens. I can see a studio working for an older person looking to downsize after a partner's death - get the equity in your current place, get in to something smaller and reduce your monthly housing costs.

Real Estate & Home Improvement / Re: JH rents rising faster than other Queens neighborhoods - DNA article
« on: August 11, 2015, 07:37:06 PM »
When you look at what's on the market, and what has recently sold, $300K might be the target for an 800+ square foot pre-war building, with a top-notch renovation using high end materials and finishes, and maybe a full or part-time doorman. Keep in mind some of those comps are condos - which always cost more than a coop. I just hope the people who paid that amount bought in to a well-run coop. It would be a shame to pay such a premium if the building's financials weren't in good order.

For the rest of the housing stock, which includes post-war buildings, apartments <800 square feet, $300+K isn't here yet. Keep in mind how the facts presented by an interested party -- like someone who makes a living in real estate -- can be presented while ignoring the many one bedrooms that have sold in the last couple of months for less than $190K, some even as low as in the $120-130K range.
