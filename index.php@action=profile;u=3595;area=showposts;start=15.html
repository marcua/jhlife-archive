Has it occurred to anyone that the city is reluctant to invest resources (a.k.a taxpayer money) in to a process (ULURP) that may result in the school selling the land to a developer, which seems to be Mr. Rakosi's clear intention? If the school has cold feet about a deal with the city why would the "Cash-strapped" city invest the time and money in to a process that would merely result in a loss because of the Garden School's need for as much cash as they can get as soon as possible?

Again, I find it necessary to point out that the "Fiduciary Responsibility" of the board, seems to have not been too much of a concern to them in the years leading up to their current financial situation. Yet, all of a sudden it is key to why they can't sign a deal with the city.  

The school stated publicly that they would be open to some sort of loan or "option" in the amount of $500,000.00 to cover operating costs while the city completed the ULURP process. The community raised over $250,000.00 in response to that ask, and apparently other organizations have expressed a willingness to loan the money to the school as well. Even Mr. Rakosi admits that he was the one who supported the idea of a community based loan and apparently even offered his own money towards it. But, suddenly it's not enough. Now they need more money and an iron clad guarantee. The constantly changing story form the Garden School leads one to believe they were never negotiating in good faith to begin with. Has anyone done any research on this Mr. Rakosi and his dealings? I'm very , very skeptical.

The "cash strapped" city even came up with more money to offer the school. A big feat in our current economic climate. So I can't understand why people are questioning how serious the city is about buying this land ? Does it make sense to anyone out there that the politicians involved would risk their reputations and the city (which is ultimately the Mayor) would simply play a game to get us all riled up and never deliver ?

Further, if the School is so distrustful of the city, and their surrounding community who have raised an impressive amount in a short time, why have they not signed a deal with any one of the "several developers" they have an offer from? Why do they drag this out yet cry that they are in desperate need of funds ASAP? Seems to me like they could have signed a deal by now.

Maybe they should do us all a favor by signing a deal with a developer who will put up an 8 story monstrosity and let the lack of open space along with their poor reputation run the school in to the ground. Then the community can step in and buy the property an turn it in to community center that truly benefits the neighborhood.

It is very clear that the school's main market is not the immediate community, otherwise they would listen to the parents and people here who are willing to pitch in their hard earned money to help them stay afloat and develop a valuable community resource that would ensure their reputation and longevity in the neighborhood.

Neighborhood Chat / Re: Garden School Play Yard
« on: January 31, 2011, 10:21:28 PM »
There continues to be a lot of dis-information on this topic, and I can't quite tell where it's coming from.

I attended the community meeting the other night where it was disclosed, that only about 6% of the students at the school come from Jackson Heights, the rest are from various parts of Queens. So we're talking about maybe 12 to 15 students form the immediate area, based on an enrollment of about 240. Which means Jackson Heights Children are in a significant minority.  So, how can the innocent act of exercising one's first amendment rights be harming the "children of Jackson Heights"?

It was also disclosed at that same public meeting that the parents of the students who attend the school were not adequately informed about what was going on with the board's decision to reject the city's offer. Based on the fact that the overwhelming majority of families with children in the school are NOT from the immediate area, it seems only fitting to me that the community should inform these parents about the consequences of the board's choices.

Further, if there really were people blocking the entrance to the school or otherwise creating an obstruction, were the police called? Clearly if the children were being terrorized the responsible action would have been to inform the authorities. If not, then we have to assume the "threat" was minimal if it existed at all. Waiting an entire day to raise the concern without any real evidence on a local blog hardly seems like a responsible action to me.

There's a lot of spin here and what seems like an attempt to not vilify the school. While they may not be in position to wait for the city process to be complete they should be held responsible for the consequences of their actions.

There is a groundswell of support to make the deal with the city come to fruition. The community is willing to wok with the school, but from the negative comments and spin coming out form some people it's not so clear to me that the school is really willing to work with the community.

It's a shame because I really was considering sending my child to the Garden School when the time comes, but this whole experience has put a bad taste in my mouth.
