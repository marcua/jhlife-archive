If the owner wants to do an "as-of-right" development, there are really not many ways for the community to interject except through LPC. LPC has recently overturned numerous projects due to being "out of character" in Manhattan, not necessarily because they were out of the confines of the regulations. In addition, the DoB has a new formalized zoning objection process that any new building on this site would be subject to, but this only allows the public to object to legitimate zoning/massing issues.

This site has an FAR of anywhere between 1.06 and 4.8 (Site Area X FAR = maximum floor area). The site area is 10,000 square feet. This gives you a sense of how much the owner can build. I don't see any height limitations in the zoning resolution other than setback heights (which are at 60-85 feet above street level). If the owner provided a community facility use (a music conservatory, social services, etc), they would be allowed to build more area. If a park is on the wish list, the remaining portion of the site would likely have to be taller in order to offset the costs. If there were a 37th Avenue BID, they could help fund the upkeep of the park (hint, hint).

However, wouldn't it be interesting if the JHBG held a workshop/charette/competition to generate ideas and designs for the location and present those options to the owner? That would be a truly proactive and non-antagonistic approach and would involve the neighborhood in a positive way. If 37th Avenue had a BID, they could be involved as well. Perhaps this unfortunate event could be the catalyst for true change along this neighborhood retail street.

This is an excellent idea.  Twenty years ago architect, resident, and JHBG Board Member Fred Fox prepared a rendering of a proposed building to be built on the ashes of where 82-11 37th Avenue now stands.  The owner had planned for a lovely glass tower; note, this was before we were landmarked.  Ultimately, a building very similar to Fred's proposal rose on the site, even though it did not have to adhere to LPC rules because its foundation had been poured prior to our being designated.

So the call is out to local architects and interested parties to contact the JHBG and develop ideas and proposals that will enhance the character of our Historic District.  Danny Dromm has agreed to meet with us in the near future.  So if you have the interest, talent, and the time, go to www.jhbg.org and email us with your contact information.  I would also suggest that if we can get a half dozen interested people who can commit to making a difference, we can also start the process of expanding our historic district. NOW IS THE TIME!

I contacted the Landmarks Preservation Commission (LPC) and yes, they must and will opine and approve any proposed new building PRIOR to any Dept. of Buildings permits being issued.  Obviously, any new proposed construction on the scale of these blockfronts will require that the owner(s) follow all of the rules... and in our case the rules begin with those promulgated by the LPC.  So that is good news.

Demolishing an existing/still standing building in a historic disrict is far more complicated than building one on an empty lot.  But with that said, the LPC does does not have to approve something that the zoning allows. 

Again, if you are concerned about what happens in our community get involved.

« Reply #38 on: February 21, 2010, 04:34:10 PM »
Owner hires architect, engineers and landmark consultant.
Architect designs building.
Architect, owner and landmark consultant meet with LPC to discuss intended design.
LPC staff and landmark consultant assist Architect in "finessing" design to be more easily accepted by the commission and the community.
Architect formally submits design to LPC.
LPC decides whether the project requires a hearing (in this case, they most certainly will).
LPC holds a hearing (or is this at the Community Board?) and votes to approve or deny design.
If approved, Architect can then submit the permit drawings to DoB.
DoB reviews and approves or objects.
Once approved, Zoning drawings are posted on DoB website.
Local community has 1-2 months to review and object to building envelope.
Building is approved and work permit pulled by contractor.
Construction begins.
