The Green Agenda Hits the Streets!

Development of the Green Agenda for Jackson Heights brought this diverse community together across the divides of language, race and class for a community conversation to help create a greener and more inclusive Jackson Heights. From November 2009 to March 2010, 400 residents participated in three community workshops and fourteen smaller sessions that took place in living rooms, schools, churches, seniors centers, and at other community-based organizations.  This grassroots project, was led by the Jackson Heights Beautification Group/Friends of Travers Park and the Queens Community House.  



The result so far is an environmental sustainability plan released in May 2010 that contains 42 environmental recommendations, including increasing park and green space, supporting alternatives to private cars, and reducing toxic exposures in homes.

New York Daily News
Nabe gives green light - Jackson Hts. residents get say in project
BY Clare Trapasso

Tuesday, June 8th 2010, 9:43 AM

HUNDREDS OF Jackson Heights residents are taking their quest to become a more sustainable community into their own green-thumbed hands.

They plan to roll out a slew of environmentally-friendly initiatives in the neighborhood over the next few years - including hosting workshops this summer on composting and green roofs.

The efforts follow the release of the Jackson Heights Green Agenda last month. The plan was the result of a two-year project involving more than 400 residents who helped create a "greenprint" of what they would like their neighborhood to become.

"People were really into this," said Len Maniace, 58, a member of the Jackson Heights Beautification Group. Maniace conceived and spearheaded the project. "We hope this will generate enthusiasm in the entire community."

The Jackson Heights resident worked with about 15 community groups and churches in the area to devise the agenda. Spanish and Bengali translators were on-hand at some of the group's visioning sessions to ensure residents from different ethnicities were included in the process, Maniace said.

The goals the group came up with ranged from transforming an empty stretch of road beside Travers Park into a permanent park extension, to turning school playgrounds into parks after school and on weekends, and adding more bike lanes. They also called for more trees to be planted on the streets and in medians.
