The JHBG worked very long hard hours to get the landmarking status for this area as a means to preserved and enhance the neighborhood. Prior to landmarking, the store fronts were horrible. There were sites without windows and "junk" all over. There were large, ugly signs and loud music, flashing lights and dirty, old, broken things all over. While there are many restrictions involved in landmarking, the business district definitely has improved in looks and quality of merchants, merchandise and general feeling of the neighborhood.

For those of you who are new to the area, the JHBG and landmarking developed out of developers buying and destroying beautiful private homes to build flat, undistinguished multiple family (3 story) buildings. Also, there were suspicious fires that destroyed original taxpayers (store level buildings) on 82nd to building a large glass and mirror building. It was through the JHBG creating community outrage and holding meetings and pressuring that we were able to get the much more appropriate A & C Palace office building.

Sabini was involved as the Democrat leader and them the city councilman (and on land use committee at the time). He was responsive and responsible. Monsarrate was not involved in Jackson Heights then - or even now.

We need to remember who was there for us when we needed him. It will give us the idea who will be here for us in the future.

John is on the Transportation COmmittee (Ranking Minority Member). He has worked in many ways to help this area with it's transportation issues. Basic ways such as :

SENATOR SABINI HOSTS IMPORTANT #7 TRAIN FORUMS 

Monday, April 14, 2008


For the past two Wednesdays, State Senator John D. Sabini (D/I-Jackson Heights) hosted forums with Mr. Lou Brusati, new general manager of the #7 train, to address issues and questions from community residents.

Brusati's appointment as general manager of the #7 sparked a change in management that Sabini hopes will improve the line's service.

"This is an experiment in smaller management," Sabini said. "It used to be there were so many levels of bureaucracy in the MTA, you brought up a problem and it took months and months to solve it."

The #7 train is unique in that it carries a whopping 400,000 riders a day and offers few transfers.

"It's basically a shuttle from Times Square to Main Street," Sabini said. "If the #7 has problems, commuters don't have other options."

The senator said he spoke to Brusati recently about a hole in the staircase at 111 Street Station. Brusati walked over to the station himself, and a short time later, the problem was fixed.

Brusati fielded a wide array of questions ranging from the spacing of trains to annoyances involving pigeons.

"This is about empowering people and making sure their voices are heard," Sabini said.
