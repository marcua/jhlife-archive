I got a Zillow alert that a 2BR/1BA near us just sold for $570,000.

http://www.zillow.com/homedetails/8412-35th-Ave-APT-5N-Jackson-Heights-NY-11372/79509931_zpid/

I'd been following it and a few others that are similar to ours. Like this one that closed for $568,500 in March:

http://www.zillow.com/homedetails/8310-35th-Ave-APT-2R-Jackson-Heights-NY-11372/79509916_zpid/

And this one for $550,000 in January:

http://www.zillow.com/homedetails/8310-35th-Ave-APT-6U-Jackson-Heights-NY-11372/72649176_zpid/

At first I did a double-take, thinking maybe I'd saved one with two bathrooms or a terrace or something, but no, they are 2BR/1BAs. What they do all have in common are renovated baths and kitchens with pro stainless steel appliances and stone countertops. Nicely staged and photographed.

When we bought 3 years ago, I expected the homes in the area to increase, and of course I'm happy to see them increasing in value. But is anyone surprised at how fast it's moving?

When we bought, most 2BR/1BAs were about $320-$350k.

It took about 10 years in Carroll Gardens before we saw the same increase that it's taken 3 years to see here.

Are we in another bubble?

I know from my own experience looking at JH listings that prices have gone up pretty steeply over the past two-three years. I saw something on the NYT listings yesterday that I thought confirmed it for me. A graphic on the listing showed that prices for one-bedrooms in Jackson Heights had gone up 43.5% in the past five years, and when I looked at an Astoria listing, it showed that prices for one-bedrooms had gone up just 4.4% during the same time. I thought, WHOA!

But I just did some more poking around. For two-bedrooms, prices in JH went up 26.2%, and in Astoria they went up 55.7%!

I also checked Carroll Gardens: 39.4% for one-bedrooms, and 96.6% (!!!!) for two-bedrooms.

Since this is a formula the NYT devised, the figures may not necessarily jibe with other sites' stats. But also, I think various factors could skew the results.

As A2K noted, we are moored to the city as a whole and if its in a bubble, so are we.  That being said, neighborhoods perform differently from one another and asking if a particular area is in a bubble is not an indication of an island mentality.  Tenable arguments for bubbles in Brooklyn could certainly be cast.

I don't see JH in a bubble at all.  Its my opinion, and that is the reason we bought here, that the recent surges is the market correcting undervalued real estate more than anything else.  Its proximity to the city, along with its beautiful housing stock makes it an attractive place to live and its largely landmark status limits supply and the possibility of an UGM (ugly glass monster) being plopped next door.  Prices may plateau of course, but I wouldn't hover my finger near the trigger for fear of being mid bubble.

I think what the comment about JH being in a vacuum refers to is the sense that Queens in general has always been perceived as far removed from Manhattan (and now Brooklyn) in those boroughs' more upscale nature vs blue collar/middle class Queens, that Queens is like the store brand versus the high-priced brand and thus prices here should always remain lower, or just plain low. People don't perceive prices as being relative instead of fixed and they are shocked to see the current numbers, which are so much higher than only a few years ago. But when you see what is happening with prices in Manhattan and Brooklyn (something people probably are not so aware of unless they actively follow real estate trends) with studio apartments going for half a million or more, then the prices here do seem reasonable by comparison. But what goes up also comes down and it does seem kind of alarming that the ramp up in prices happened so fast.

I like the metaphors of bubbles and vacuums, but I think we should keep them separate.   (Google "vacuum bubble" to see what I mean.)

Jackson Heights seems to me a bit of a market inefficiency -- For whatever reason, the properties here are undervalued as compared to similar (prewar, spacious, etc) ones in other neighborhoods with similar commuting times to Manhattan. So, I think of people purchasing coops in JH as a little bit like Billy Beane selecting players with high on-base percentage for the Oakland A's when these players could be had more cheaply because other teams did not (yet) recognize their value.
