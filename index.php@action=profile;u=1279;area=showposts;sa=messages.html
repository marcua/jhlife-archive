Neighborhood Chat / Re: Why do you leave the neighborhood?
« on: February 17, 2015, 11:51:39 AM »
Quote from: dssjh on February 01, 2015, 06:17:35 PM
a Bareburger would be great.

but i wonder, how many of the folks here are native - or really long term - new yorkers. i ask, because back in the 70s, 80s and 90s (i am not old enough to go back further) and back then, neighborhoods actually differed from each other and you couldn't find every last thing in every last neighborhood. you could find great deli, cheap underwear and luggage and illicit drugs on the lower east side. you couldn't find a bistro or a high end wine shop. you could find great german, hungarian and czech food in yorkville, but not a children's gym or a gluten free sushi joint. astoria had amazing italian, later greek, but very little new american or hunan. greenpoint? an amazing live poultry (removed because of a new condo) but no gay nightlife.
why do we now feel the need to have everything within five blocks? it's a great city. with a subway. sorry for being the cranky, ancient 50 year old.
I don't see that it really matters how long someone has lived in NYC, we are all neighbors now and have a common interest in our neighborhood.  For the record, I'm a born and raised New Yorker and I have seen many neighborhoods change over time.  I do think JH has far more homogeneous retail offerings than many neighborhoods, both now and 35 years ago.  The neighborhood I grew up in has drastically changed from a funky old hood to an extremely wealthy one and obviously the retail has changed quite a bit over the years too.  BUT... 35 years ago there was still a reliable butcher and seafood shop, delis, clothing stores with a variety of price points, interesting restaurants  representing many different cultures and cuisines, a neighborhood coffee shop, etc.  I don't remember that my mother had to leave the immediate neighborhood to meet any of our day-to-day shopping needs.  And when she did want to socialize in our neighborhood, there were more than enough options for that as well. 

That's not to say that certain neighborhoods don't have their niches- yes, Yorkville for German, etc, but these neighborhoods have other offerings as well.  There's a reason why most neighborhoods do offer a variety of shopping/dining options and that is pure convenience.  It's not unreasonable to desire greater diversity and therefore convenience within our neighborhood.  I would also prefer to put my money into the neighborhood, though that is frequently not possible since there's not much I wish to buy in JH.  This is the first neighborhood I've lived in where I needed to shop in other areas so often.  I do most of my shopping in Manhattan, near my job, and I mostly shop for:  gifts, clothes, home decor/furniture, food (Fairway, TJ's or Fresh Direct mostly), bars and nightlife, restaurants of all kinds.
