There are good reasons to restrict the use of the garden. It's for all residents--not just children. And when kids are running around and screaming in the garden, guess what? It seriously diminishes the pleasure that others would like to have from sitting and reading in the garden, having a quiet walk around the garden, etc. It's a shared resource, therefore there have to be rules. For instance, playing with balls is not allowed in our garden. A few years back, a father sitting and holding his baby in the garden was hit in the head by a ball thrown by a child. It could have hit the baby. It's a liability issue--the coop could have been sued if there was a serious injury. Duh. It's not a playground. If there were an injury and a lawsuit, everyone living here might be seriously affected. I'm a parent, now an empty nester, and though I agree that everyone should be allowed to walk on the grass, I also feel there should be limits on how the garden is used to help protect the interests of everyone living here and sharing the garden. We collectively spend a lot of money maintaining the beautiful plantings, etc, which are sometimes trampled and damaged by children running around unsupervised. This garden in Manhattan does not sound like it had much in the way of landscaping or plantings the way ours does. Ours is a garden, not a backyard. There's a difference. We put huge effort, money and time into making the garden beautiful and don't want to see it damaged by kids running around, climbing trees, etc, or by adults throwing barbecue parties in the common areas.

Regarding the Hitchcock "Rear Window" connection--how interesting! I didn't know that Hitchcock lived here for a time. I wonder where that tidbit came from--that the production designers of Rear Window visited that particular complex in Manhattan. I'd never heard of it and thought it was really fascinating. Loved the part about the neighbors cutting down the "privacy hedge" that Richard Gere had put up.

Eddie--we don't want to appeal to "the masses," we want to protect the interests of the actual shareholders-- all of them, not just the parents and children. It's not a public park. It's private property, co-owned by all the shareholders. It's hard for me to understand why people don't understand that. If you were a shareholder in one of these garden complexes, would you really be okay with people doing whatever they wanted in the garden--throwing loud parties, barbecuing, large groups of kids playing soccer or whatever and screaming for hours right outside your windows...etc...seriously? There have to be limits, and rules. It can't be a free for all. As it stands, people here are in fact allowed on the grass, you can sit there with a blanket and have a picnic, etc, but no ball playing is allowed, no bicycles, no private parties except in the small "bays" that belong to each house, where the residents of that house can have parties if they like. The rules make sense. It's just silly to imagine that there should be no rules and everyone should be allowed to do whatever they want. It's unrealistic in a shared space.

Judging by the OP's article and my own observations, it seems like the uses of the greenspaces are converging. The riots of kids, green thumbs and flood of strollers are on decline between the village row homes and with the co-ops in JH are loosening their rules, they are on the uptick.  Maybe even one or two shareholders over here will admit to having sat through all of "Runaway Bride".

Differences may abound, but one thing is certain:  Lazing through a second drink, the city receding, swaddled in darkness and shuffling breezes, toes gnawing the grass, watching the fireflies dance, MacDougal Street or 84th Street, Sullivan or 76th, it's all wonderful.

Some folks here are engaging in what's called a "straw man argument."
