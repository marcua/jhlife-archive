In the original plan, the FDR Drive and West Side Highway would have been free, so a driver going over the Brooklyn Bridge, up the FDR drive, and up into the Bronx would not have paid a toll.  This had the advantage of not pushing additional traffic on the BQE, but also made the administration of the plan complex and costly.

The Legislature's Traffic Congestion Mitigation Commission's alternative proposal included the FDR Drive and West Side Highway in the charging zone.  The result of this is that drivers have a choice between a less-congested tolled route (FDR) and a more-congested free route (BQE).  But of course, anybody wanting to reach the Bronx via the BQE would have to pay tolls on the Triborough Bridge anyway, so I don't think there would be a big impact with regard to through traffic.

In other locations around the country that provide a choice between free and tolled alternatives (e.g. the California SR-91 Express Lanes), there has been some "sorting" as more time-sensitive drivers (e.g. transit, commuters, etc.) opt for the faster lanes and less time-sensitive drivers (e.g. shopping trips, leisure trips, etc.) opting for the free routes.  Interestingly, the lanes are popular among women and lower income drivers, who often face inflexible daily schedules and value the option to circumvent congestion when necessary.  While SR-91 is a very different context from NYC, if you're interested you can read more here.

But to answer your question more directly, Chuckster, I haven't seen any published figures specifically looking at impacts on the BQE.  Overall, the Commission's Plan would have reduced VMT in Western Queens by 6.1% and reduced the duration of congested conditions ("Level of Service F") by 38.6%.  (Interim Report to the Traffic Congestion Mitigation Commission, pp. 41-42). 

Hope this helps.
