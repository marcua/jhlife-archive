State Legislatures rescues Jackson Height's endangered plastic-bag trees
« on: February 07, 2017, 10:14:40 PM »
Yes, you read that right. Having nothing better to do the New York State Assembly joined the state Senate in delaying for one year a law designed to reduce litter in trees, sidewalks, waterways, as well as the mega-tonnage of plastic bags that end up in landfills.
Considering the mess in Albany, one would hope state government did not need to stick its nose in a much-debated NYC matter, but noooooooooooooo, as SNL's John Belushi used to say.
The bill, which imposes a 5-cent fee on plastic bags and was set to go into effect on Feb. 15, is similar to many successful laws in other cities.
The law is opposed by plastic bag makers (surprise,) but some state legislators seem clueless about the law itself, saying it is a tax on the poor. One problem with that, there are a number of exemptions including low income folks who use food stamps.
We hope to take a look at how legislators in Jackson Heights and neighboring communities voted. Many are hoping for Gov. Cuomo to veto this bill.

For background, this is not a new idea - there are already hundreds of municipalities with similar laws, so we already know what the effects would likely be. Washington D.C. enacted a nearly identical law in 2009, resulting in an immediate 80% reduction in plastic bag usage. Surveys show the overwhelming majority of business owners approved of the change. The funds raised, while not the point, were used for waterway cleanup.

A 5-cent fee is a negligible economic burden - these programs work not because people need to save money, but because it forces them to actually consider whether they want a bag instead of accepting one automatically, and usually (~80% of the time, apparently), they don't.

UPDATE -- How JH's state legislators voted on a bill to delay for at least one year NYC's law designed to reduce plastic-bag litter in trees, sidewalks and waterways.

The bill would impose a nickel fee on plastic bags distributed free in supermarkets, etc. Voting to delay the NYC law were Assembly members Michael DenDekker (D) and Francisco Moya  (D), as well as state Senator Jose Peralta, according to the New York Public Interest Research Group. (See attached.)

One of the leading reasons cited by opponents of the NYC law was that it would pose a financial hardship on the poor. The NYC law exempts people using food stamps from having to pay the fee. Food stamps, if you don't recall, is a program for low-income residents.

The fate of this bill is now before Gov. Andrew Cuomo, who can veto it, allowing the NYC law to take effect. See earlier post on how to let the governor know how you feel about this law.

Quote from: dssjh on February 10, 2017, 09:26:24 PM
i don't understand why paper bags are included in the same category, though. they're biodegradable and not environmentally unfriendly. is there something i am missing?

You might think so, but paper bags have their own problems. From an article comparing them:

Quote
Causes pollution: Paper production emits air pollution, specifically 70 percent more pollution than the production of plastic bags [source: Thompson]. According to certain studies, manufacturing paper emits 80 percent more greenhouse gases [source: Lilienfield]. And, consider that making paper uses trees that, instead, could be absorbing carbon dioxide. The paper bag making process also results in 50 times more water pollutants than making plastic bags [source: Thompson].
•Consumes energy: Even though petroleum goes into making plastic, it turns out that making a paper bag consumes four times as much energy as making a plastic bag, meaning making paper consumes a good deal of fuel [source: reusablebags.com].
•Consumes water: The production of paper bags uses three times the amount of water it takes to make plastic bags [source: Lilienfield].
•Inefficient recycling: The process of recycling paper can be inefficient -- often consuming more fuel than it would take to make a new bag [source: Milstein]. In addition, it takes about 91 percent more energy to recycle a pound of paper than a pound of plastic [source: reusablebags.com].
•Produces waste: According to some measures, paper bags generate 80 percent more solid waste [source: Lilienfield].
•Biodegrading difficulties: Surprisingly, the EPA has stated that in landfills, paper doesn't degrade all that much faster than plastics [source: Lilienfield].
