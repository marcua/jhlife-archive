Ha—thanks, I searched and found that after I posted.   Though I can't claim to have answers after looking it over. I suppose if I comb through the pages enough I might find out who the top person is. I did find a number of neighborhood rezoning proposals that sounded impressive, until I started to pick up on the fact that (duh) they're phrased to sound impressive. I mean, they're not going to say, "The Department wants to rezone XXX neighborhood to allow a lot more high-rise and commercial development than was allowed before," they're going to say "to preserve the existing scale and character of the area while allowing for a modest increase in residential and commercial density in appropriate limited locations." I think it's the kind of thing where you have to know enough to read between the lines.

It is very interesting reading, though. For instance, back in 2005, a rezoning proposal was passed that would restrict future residential construction in Cambria Heights to one- and two-family houses and even set regulations about floor area, front yard size, and height and setback measurements that would "more closely reflect the scale of surrounding homes." And other proposals have been passed since then that also set limits on the scale of new residential and commercial construction, with an eye toward maintaining the character of the neighborhood. So that has been going on around Queens. They even passed a rezoning proposal designed to restrict overdevelopment in Sunnyside and Woodside and impose restrictions on the construction of "unpredictable building types that can lead to out-of-character construction"! But the Sunnyside/Woodside proposal was also more complicated than others I saw, which seemed to be because they were attempting to embrace (or at least accept) a certain amount of growth and at the same time provide incentives for the construction of affordable housing.

Yeah, I know, this post is long and strays a bit off-topic. But the bottom line is, there's been rezoning all over Queens designed to preserve the character of existing neighborhoods. So sometimes that does happen. But other times, for whatever reason, it doesn't.

I see all these developments as collectively being a positive for Queens and NYC, with qualifications.   

On the upside, there is a huge, unmet need for housing and all these towers do help to some degree, although unfortunately only for a relatively small group of well-heeled buyers and renters. Also, having high-end, high density development clustered in LIC may reduce some of the density and gentrification pressure on older established neighborhoods like JH, Sunnyside, Woodside, etc. 

Plus, the area is served by multiple subway lines and is only a stop or two into Manhattan so the pressure on mass transit is less than would be the case if comparable developments were to spring up in other parts of Queens.  If we have to build up, up and up, this seems just about the best place to do so.

Aesthetically speaking, for me at least, there is a kind of glitzy appeal to seeing all these towers together, particularly as the 7 train threads its way through. (They already look much better than “luxury” towers from the 60s and 70s on the UES and elsewhere, which were horrible when built and have gotten worse with age.)     

It would be much better if the light manufacturing jobs that used to dominate that area were still plentiful providing working people with living wage jobs.  But given economic forces at large, those seem to unfortunately be disappearing (if not already all gone) irrespective of demand for residential units and office towers.  It would also be much better if these units were more affordable but for reasons that I do not fully understand it seems impossible to build units for middle/moderate income people in NYC without heavy subsidies.

You can look at how lowrise JH/Elmhurst/Corona is a thick yellow ball of "highest" density, while places with towers, the waterfront at LIC and Williamsburg, are in lower density brackets.  This is because the towers 1) tend to be more widely spaced out, 2) have a higher percentage of studio and 1 bedrooms, 3) are less likely to experience apartment overcrowding.  Of course if the towers are packed right on top of each other like in Manhattan, they can get higher densities, but I think when the dust settles, we won't see population densities higher than the back 7.

This follows the stray off the subject:  Our present zoning resolution was approved in 1961, with revisions since.  The man responsible for many of these revisions, in particularly the areas of air rights and inclusionary zoning which allow areas to experience growth while maintaining their historical character, was Norman Marcus, who I was fortunate enough to have as a prof.  He also helped save City Planning back when the Beame administration was considering canning it. 

The main purpose of zoning is maximize the investments of present and potential property owners.

Cambria Heights is a low density neighborhood and given its location and lack of rapid transit, it's well suited for it.  The benefits of a building out of character of the neighborhood would be less than the damage to investments of existing and potential property owners, so the zoning resolution is amended to preserve the character of the hood.  Alas, the old stomping grounds of 50 cent will live on as long as his art. 

But the city is a dynamic thing.  If one pines for a neighborhood (or population) to maintain a stasis ad infinitum then one is bound for disappointment.  It's been changing rapidly since the days of Walt Whitman and will continue to do so.  At times neighborhoods arrive at a place where a change in character will better the investments of present and potential property owners.  LIC's industrial patrimony, as Brycellen rightly observed, has ebbed to the point where such a change is warranted.  So the zoning resolution is amended to initiate change at the expense of the character of the hood.

Sunnyside/Woodside is more of a middle ground:  The role still fits, but there is warranted room for growth and such growth would be in the best interests of the present and potential property owners, so the zoning resolution is amended to encourage change while balanced with maintaining the character of the hood.  Such situations, along with politics, NIMBYism and sometimes flawed planning are why:

We all agree that such development will put more strain on the subways.  Will it turn into an L train scenario is the question.

In 20 years the city has grown by a million people.  That's 50,000 a year.  Have we been building that much housing per year?  These LIC towers will supply housing for about 50,000.  We need one of these each year, every year, to keep supply up with demand.  Since we clearly haven't, overcrowding and skyrocketing housing costs result.  Investment frenzies and greed are easy scapegoats, but it's simple market economics which steadily makes it less and less feasible for residents to stay on.

Our present zoning resolution is a citywide thing.  It differs from our first zoning resolution in 1916 because of its focus on FAR's rather than strict spatial requirements.  Zoning itself is a progressive era response to unchecked self destructive development.  It hinders development designed to make as much profit as quickly as possible, replacing it (hopefully!) with profit driven development which does not create a detriment to other citizens, property owners and the city as a whole.  It's not flawless of course, but it sure beats the alternatives.  40% of the existing buildings in Manhattan could not be built under our present zoning resolution. http://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2016/05/19/upshot/forty-percent-of-manhattans-buildings-could-not-be-built-today.html?_r=0

That last bit about being bothered over it.  Whenever there is change in a neighborhood, there is a general kneejerk response on how it will affect the people presently living there.  It's cold blooded not to strongly consider them of course, but some prefer to give due weight to the other stakeholders, including future residents, property owners, developers, NYC citizens and the city itself.   I don't think any one of them trumps anyone else, though I do think such stakeholders trump those outside the city.  So does the city of Berlin, who recently 86'ed airbnb because of its affect on its housing supply.  http://www.citylab.com/housing/2016/04/airbnb-rentals-berlin-vacation-apartment-law/480381/
