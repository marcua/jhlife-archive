Community Board Reform
« on: November 18, 2015, 02:32:34 PM »
On several threads, we've heard complaints about Community Board 3's poor communications about upcoming street closure events in the neighborhood, lack of notice about the agendas of upcoming meetings, and mishandling of liquor licenses by new businesses in the community.

What can be done about it?   How can the Community Board do a better job?   And if they won't do a better job, what are the options for reforming it from the outside?

Unfortunately, even reform proposals appear to continue lifetime tenure for current community board members.   The only person empowered to reform and revitalize our community board is our Borough President, Melinda Katz.   Given her job, she should be interested to hear about problems with our community board.   I would expect that she'd be particularly responsive to concerns from the business community.  But so far in her tenure, she hasn't shown much interest in shaking things up.

According to Stew's description, his interaction was with the District Manager. It's worth separating the role of the board and the district manager when considering them as a "unit".

Background on Community board district managers

From http://www.nyc.gov/html/qnscb1/html/explained/explained_manager.shtml

What is the role of the District Manager?
District Managers play many different roles. They are at once complaint takers, municipal managers, ombudspersons, information sources, community organizers, mediators, advocates and much more. They are above all public servants with the most direct ties to their local communities.

A District Manager is hired by the Community Board and serves at the Boardâ€™s pleasure. The District Manager establishes an office, hires staff and implements procedures to cut through red tape that impedes the delivery  of City services.
