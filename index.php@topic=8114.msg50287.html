The malling of Flushing Meadows and the hunger strike
« Reply #4 on: September 03, 2013, 06:44:29 PM »
By Len Maniace

New York City admitted today that it has little to show for its effort to relocate some 165 businesses from Willets Point, other than a hunger strike by angry shop owners.

The NYC Economic Development Commission has spent $700,000 to relocate only 10 of the businesses, the EDC’s President Kyle Kimball said at a City Hall hearing  on Sept. 3.

Kimball insisted his agency has been working hard on the effort but said the mostly auto-related businesses were not happy with the relocation sites.

A few hours later, business owners told City Council members why they were so angry that they started a hunger strike four days ago to protest what they said was no help from the EDC.

“They showed pretty pictures of the development, but no pictures of where we are going to work,” said one of the owners Marco Neira, through a translator.  It was Neira, a member of a group called the Sunrise Cooperative, who told of the hunger strike.

Kimball and Neira spoke at a public hearing to build a 1.4 million-square-foot shopping mall that would be the biggest mall in New York City on more than 30 acres in Flushing Meadow Corona Park that is leased to the New York Mets. The land serves as the Citi Field parking lot.

The proposal has been tacked on to a plan to build 5,500 housing units on the Willets Point business area that was approved in 2008. By describing the mall plan as a minor alteration to the earlier project, the developers Sterling and Related avoid undertaking a new environmental impact statement.  Sterling is owner by the Mets‘ owner, the Wilpon family.

Opponents of the mall plan outnumbered supporters 31 to 13 at the hearing. (Full disclosure, I read a statement from the Jackson Heights Beautification Group opposed to the plan.) Most of the opponents were members of the Save FMCP coalition, which includes JHBG, or members of the Willet’s Point businesses – many of them Latino immigrants.

In criticizing the city’s relocation efforts one business owner said the relocation sites shown to  shop owners far too expensive and nowhere near their current location, requiring them to reestablish their business, something that could take years, said one of the business, Jamie Sabeti.

The merchants also said they wanted to move as a group of about 60 businesses because their operations supported each other, something that was not possible with the sites they were shown.

Frankly, I don't mind so much that they turn a parking lot into a mall. Although I agree that we must ensure that every inch of park space remains a park, I consider that the Citi Field parking lot is a park space that has already been lost.

I would prefer to work the system in favor of the green areas that are still there: we should ensure that a very significant amount of the mall's tax revenue is earmarked for the maintenance of the remaining park-like areas.

We also should fight for a much larger amount of the revenue from the US Open to stay at Flushing Meadows Park.

Currently Flushing Meadows is not a fraction of what it could be. If we fight for the right thing -- that is: for the revenue to be used in the maintenance and improvement of the park -- the tennis center and the mall could generate a very solid financial base to create something similar to the "Central Park Conservancy" here.
