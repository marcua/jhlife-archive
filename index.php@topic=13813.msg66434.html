Crosswalks in Jackson Heights
« on: October 05, 2015, 05:20:02 PM »
I recently was ticketed for car that was just touching the white line of a crosswalk on a street off of 35th Avenue. A few days later I noticed another car similarly was ticketed.  In both cases the car was completely clear of the "crosswalk".

When is a crosswalk a crosswalk...then I noticed that the crosswalks on many streets in JH are extra wide (as compared to other neighborhoods) and are painted to extend well past the line of the building and the area of sidewalk for actually crossing the street.   In fact in this case the new line was about 1 1/2 feet wider than the previous line painted on the street.

I know this is revenue generation for the city and traffic cops, but there should be some standards on the area for cross-walks.   Appealed this and filing a complaint with the DOT.  Plus, there is so much that these cops should be policing that are actually causing traffic problems...double parking, intersection u-turns, incessant honking, parking really close to fire hydrants...

I drive too, and parking in the neighborhood can be a pain at times, but I never park near a hydrant or a cross walk.  The fire department should be able to access the hydrant in an emergency.  The yellow line that you sometimes see is not always indicative of whether you are far enough from a hydrant to legally park.  I've seen those lines painted in by owners of adjoining buildings, as opposed to the DOT.  You're supposed to be 15' away from the hydrant.  The traffic cops don't have a yard stick, but anyone can tell the difference between 10' and 15'.  As for cross walks, pedestrians shouldn't have top figure a way around a car that decides to double-park near a walk, stop in a walk due to traffic, or park too closely to a walk.  I get your point -- you were just touching the line, but to me, that's no justification.  If you were parked illegally, the parking cop will ticket you.

I can appreciate resentment at being issued parking ticket; however, I can not say that I am sympathetic.  While I own a car, I consider myself a pedestrian first and absolutely hate it when a driver violates any aspect of the vehicle and traffic law and/or related regulations.  I think it is proper to impose financial penalties, or revoke the privilege of driving altogether, on drivers who fail to adhere to the rules of the road.   If I had it my way, there would be cameras set up at every traffic light issuing tickets for speeding and running red lights.  The revenue generating aspect would be a secondary benefit unrelated to the goal of protecting the citizens of our city from drivers.

With regards to parking in JH, before my family purchased a car we made sure that we could afford to pay for a parking garage to avoid having to deal with all the related hassles of trying to find parking in an urban context. As far as going out, if there is paid parking, I will pay to avoid the hassle of looking for a parking spot.  My point is that driving in an urban context is a luxury and you will have to pay for it one way or another.  Getting a ticket every once in a while is certainly less costly than what I pay in one year for parking.  The upside is that you are ahead of the game when compared to me!

Here's that wonderful jingle.  It was recently re-recorded on a popular children's album by They Might Be Giants.   So it has not been forgotten. 

Every day I see a small minority of pedestrians and cyclists putting themselves and others in danger.   And I'm a driver, so I can attest to the tremendous stress that Shelby2 describes.  But every day I also witness outrageous recklessness and indifference of a small minority of drivers. 

We're a big, diverse city, and there's are countless forms of bizarre behavior on display in its daily spectacle.  But we don't get anywhere by standing in a circle and pointing fingers at each other.  We need to reengineer our streets to be safer for the most vulnerable users -- children and the elderly.   We need effectively written laws and strong enforcement to hold people accountable for unsafe actions.  And we need to deploy our resources effectively, to address the deadliest risks first.   And yes, we need more education for everybody.

This is pretty much what the city is trying to do with its Vision Zero initiative.  It's doing a better job at some of these things than at others.  But it is a serious, ambitious effort worthy of support from drivers, passengers, pedestrians, and cyclists alike.
