Neighborhood Chat / Safety pins worn by allies against Hate & Subway Signage for Sharing
« on: November 11, 2016, 11:23:23 AM »
HI JH Life,

So an idea is going around FB that I think would be particularly helpful in neighborhoods that are such hubs of diversity... anyone who is willing to be a safe stranger to stand with if being picked out of intimidation or hate speach.

http://www.vox.com/.../10/13586322/trump-brexit-safety-pin

But it occurred to me that many of the people likely to feel unsafe on the subway are not reading Facebook in English.... and that for this to work, lots of people need to know about it, wear it & be in the know to look out for it. So I just emailed city councilperson Daniel Dromm & state rep Jose Peralta the below letter to get this idea city wide visibility through our transit system.... maybe if others copy, paste & send, they'll give it some thought. It has the benefit of being non-partisan & low cost, so who knows???  If we get no official traction, I'd like to see about creating signage & gathering translations and at least making 74th street & key 7 stops hubs for information about this effort.  Welcome ideas & collaborators!

***

Dear Council Member Dromm,

I am writing today as the first initial shock of Tuesday's election gives way to grappling with the need to organize and for creative solutions to keep each other safe and supported through this time - especially in this beautiful, diverse city with such a long history of supporting each other through tough times.

I was struck by an idea that is going around social media, that is non-partisan, low effort, and encourages communities & individuals to be their best in this moment. The idea is simple - those willing to stand with someone being targeted for hate speech on our streets or transit wears a safety pin on their outer wear as a sign that they can be an ally in a moment of need. Bullies and hate-mongers target those who appear alone and vulnerable, especially in public places where they assume people will mind their own business (for a running list, see Shaun King's twitter feed right now - https://twitter.com/ShaunKing). Simply having someone to stand with, or someone who will pull out a cell phone to document or call for further help is the best and only immediate way of letting the bully know they do not have carte blanch in the situation. This is a simple model that has been used in Australia after a rise in Islamophobic attacks & the UK after Brexit.

http://www.vox.com/.../10/13586322/trump-brexit-safety-pin

However, it struck me too that the people most likely to see this on Facebook or Twitter may not be all the people that would need to know what it means, especially if it is only circulating in English. I wanted to humbly suggest that the MTA consider making this info available throughout the transit system in a "See Something, Say Something" fashion, in as many languages as are appropriate to each community. As City Councilman for one of the most diverse communities in the country, I encourage you to consider championing an effort that both symbolically and literally says we stand together as a city and state against bigotry, hate speech, and intimidation. If an MTA-wide effort is not possible, perhaps a City Council motion, or even a local effort can model this for other districts or neighborhoods concerned about public and personal safety for all.

Thank you for your consideration,
