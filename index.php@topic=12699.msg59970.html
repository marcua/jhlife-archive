Community garden?
« on: December 03, 2014, 01:14:06 PM »
Hey JH community, my wife and I are relative newcomers to Jackson Heights (Long Island transplants, been here for about a year and a half now, and loving it). I wonder, are there/have there ever been any plans for a community garden in the neighborhood? I've seen plenty of posts here lamenting the lack of public green space, and I have to say that really is my one gripe (if Flushing Meadows weren't so close, I don't know what I'd do).

I can't imagine this isn't something that JHBG or the Green Alliance haven't considered before; a small neighborhood garden with plots given out in a yearly lottery system, somewhere to grow vegetables, use all that compost JHBG collects every week down on 69th St., meet other local green thumbs? Our north-facing windowsill didn't cut it last spring, and unfortunately there's no access to our building's courtyard (though I doubt we could plant there even with access). Ironic to live in America's first Garden City, and not have any access to a garden!

Honestly, I have no idea where it could even go, but it would still be a nice asset. I've seen old photos of whole blocks on Northern Blvd. converted to victory gardens back in the '40s.... wouldn't that be nice?

 I guess the question is what kind of community garden would you like to see? If it's vegetables, there are certain
issues that aren't as relevant for flower gardens. Veggie gardens tend to be not so pretty and they are often
tucked away in areas that aren't well trafficked. The Queens Botanical Gardens has plots for members but
they want some work done in return and if you lose interest or aren't maintaining a schedule, they will take
it back as I understand. Vegetable gardens are fairly high maintainence in our main growing months of July,
August and September sometimes needing to be watered daily. Problems come up when you vacation and
things can dry up quickly. Containers are prettier and may be easier to get into a co-op area but need even
more watering. Flower gardens, on the other hand, can be much lower maintainance especially if you start
with good compost rich soil and note
if you need shade or sun loving plants. Volunteering is an easy way to garden if you don't have to have ownership.
 On the issue of green spaces, I wonder how much green you see on your Google map of the neigborhood?
I think that it's pretty green for a city area. Our guests always comment on this. The Million Trees program
has been adding street trees for a number of years now and we have JHBG to maintain them with a volunteer
crew.

 But here is an idea that seems to be politically charged here in Jackson Heights- what if more co-ops who
have lovely garden areas that some call "viewing gardens" actually change their policies to allow the
shareholders to use the gardens with care. Sit on the grass!! Put a few benches in for seniors and parents
and whoever wants/needs to sit for awhile. Let a child walk on the grass!!
 Let people garden smaller spaces in these areas if they keep them nicely. Maybe start with flowers. Who
knows but the solution may be right before our eyes if enough of the co-ops who are still heavily policing their grounds realize that some of the others who allow their shareholders to enjoy the property have a much higher quality of life for all residents. Radical, I know, but take the JHBG garden tour in the spring and see the happy
co-ops where there is a shared space mentality and the green spaces are actually lived in.
  The Berkeley co-op is a great example. They had dining tables in their center green lawn! A number of shareholders had their own garden plots. They seemed like a fun, caring and generous group with all ages
of people interacting. The Towers is another co-op that uses its space for gardens,composting, kids area,
etc. The Donnelly Gardens is another. The Windsor too. Am sure there are more...
  Could this be the way to more green space and garden possibilities in Jackson Heights? Or should the
arguments that have been in place for years in some groups continue even if new tenants promise to be
responsible for green maintainance and real estate values will go up not down for these apts. There is alot
of green space here but sadly much of it is much too private. St Marks Church gardens are one of the most
heavily uses spaces in the neighborhood and thankfully people have shown respect and appreciation even as
it is open for public use.
   Am curious if poster ever had a community garden plot before and what their experience was?

No, coming from the suburbs I was always able to plant out in the yard, so I've never been a member of a community garden, though I've visited plenty, notably out in East New York (lots of vacant lots converted to small gardens) and up in the Bronx, as well. I've also spent a lot of time at the Brooklyn Grange in LIC and the Brooklyn Navy Yard, though that's a business of course, not a public garden -- still, a great place to visit if you're interested in rooftop gardening, and the staff is incredibly friendly. I'm not an expert on how a typical community garden is run, but it seems as if most are administered by some umbrella organization like Grow NYC (or in the Bronx case, a neighborhood garden was overseen by the International Rescue Committee, as they ran agriculture programs for resettled refugees there). Although I'm sure there are plenty that are run by local neighborhood organizations.

I never meant to imply that JH was lacking in (private) green spaces -- believe me, I'm grateful for the abundance of trees and the wonderful courtyards, even if I can only appreciate those from the sidewalk.

 Cb88, the groups you cite are all wonderful and make NYC a better place whether they farm like the Grange
or the GrowNYC group who helps lot's of community groups. The idea of plots that could be given on a lottery
system is a beautiful concept but the reality of life for someone who gets attached to their garden giving it
up after a year seems painful. Consider too what usually happens where 3-4 people end up doing all the
grunt work to get the water, keep paths clean, deal with waste, bugs, possible rodents. It's not easy but if
you have the stomach for it then I would look for a spot and start organizing. I know that over by Landing
Lights park there was a couple trying to organize but the neighbors like the idea, just not right in front of
their house because they said they would rather look at the lawn than someone's personal vegetable garden
sectioned off.
      In my former community garden experiences, it takes the people who have lot's of flex time to be able
to keep it all together. As for land, the areas near highways or forlorn spots may be potential gardens.
As for roofs, this is a lively topic in JH and there are rooftop areas in a few co-ops now with solar panels,
yoga classes and lounge chairs. Reports are views are great but often hot or windy conditions exist. Our
building has pesky roof problems and I have suggested roof gardens but think it is unlikely.
  Superclam, this is great to hear that change is afoot. I hope it continues until many more JH'ers can
enjoy these big beautiful lawns that so many co-ops have in their interior spaces. It makes the co-op idea
of shared space with your neighbors a true community experience in a lovely environment outside of the
laundry room or the elevator. Hope it catches on.

A bit of history on why JH most likely won't have a community garden.   JH was originally set up so each apartment building would a  large backyards or gardens.  As I looked t the development of JH there was never any reason to plan a common area as each building has its own space.  ( how that is used since the 30's is another matter).   Unlike Manhattan, the Bronx, or Brooklyn, during the early 70's were there was abandoned buildings or houses, JH kept its population so there has never been a place where  revitalization could take placer like in those other boroughs.
At best you could contact the JHBG and ask how to assist in keeping plaaces like the Post Office looking good,  or mking Travis Park more green, but considering the land value here, any building up for sle won't go to a common area but to outside investments to build more apartment buildings- such as what happened on 85th Street.
