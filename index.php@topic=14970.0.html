Buying in the Warwick vs. Berkeley vs. Saybrook: Maintenance pricing.

Hello there terrific Jackson Heights community! Can you give us a little help? My husband and I would love some insights into a few buildings where we have liked recent listings. These are the Berkeley [Gardens built 1937?], the Saybrook [built 1936] and the Warwick [built 1940].

There are obvious differences between the buildings -- location, gardens, etc. But I'm interested in the relative value. The Warwick  has relatively high maintenances and I'd like to know more about why.

We have found the Warwick maintenance for one bedroom apartments to be roughly $1.03/sq ft whereas Berkeley Gardens (specifically) averages around $.85/sq ft & Saybrook averages $.62/sq ft.

Is it because the Warwick has done more improvements -- roof, elevator, etc. -- and is therefor in better physical shape than the other buildings? Is it because there are more renters in residence that predate their 1986 co-op conversion (that are not paying any maintenance) than in the other buildings? Anything else to consider?

More generally, I wonder if there is any way to calculate what are the trajectories of maintenance for these buildings? A good piece of information for us is if you have been in these three buildings for a while, what were the maintenance charges 5 years ago, and where are they expected to go 5 years from now?

We would really appreciate any insights and information you can share to help us in our first co-op buying experience!

Thank you so much!

Hello,

I'll preface my remarks with a disclaimer that I don't know the financial details on any of the coops you're mentioning. However, in general, one of the determining factors of maintenance levels in these buildings is the existence and terms of an underlying mortgage, i.e., the mortgage that the coop would have taken out to finance capital improvement projects. You can find this information in the financials of the building.

With regard to the trajectory of the maintenance, you can ask about the history of rate increases (e.g., some buildings review and increase maintenance nominally every year or two; others, particularly smaller buildings or those with a significant number of people on fixed income, are very cautious). You can also ask about the history of assessments -- some buildings might choose assessments over maintenance increases as a way of raising funds. It's also worth asking about future financial plans (e.g., is the building looking to raise additional funds, to refinance, to increase maintenance etc.). Recent minutes might include discussions of these questions, so be sure to review them.

Hope this helps. Good luck!

as a shareholder in the warwick, i can offer some insight, though i'd prefer to answer more specific questions in email if that's okay.

there are actually very few grandfathered-in rental units right now. when i purchased, about 15 years ago, the sponsor had enough inventory that there was a sign on the building soliciting buyers. now, there are a handful (less than 10, i'd guess).

maintenance has been relatively steady the past few years. right at the beginning of my residency, there were a couple of years with large increases -- 10 percent or so -- but over the past five years, we've had no increase at all in three years, and a small-ish one this past year.

for what it's worth, the building is very well taken care of. and that is worth a lot to me.

The underlying mortgage isn't just "the mortgage that the coop would have taken out to finance capital improvement projects", it also can be the initial mortgage for the land and building itself. Some co-ops pay off the original underlying mortgage and can take out loans with the land and building as collateral for capital improvements, others never pay off the original mortgage, but continue to re-finance it over the years. If the building has paid down its mortgage by several hundred thousand dollars and then refinances to the value of the property, it will then have access to funds for capital improvements without assessing or (particularly if interest rates have gone down since the last loan was taken out) raising maintenance costs.

Also, am I misunderstanding something? I was under the impression that the sponsor is a shareholder as well and not exempt from paying maintenance costs and/or assessments. So renters don't pay maintenance, but the sponsor who owns the shares to the apartment they are renting does. Is that incorrect?

While it is important to have an idea of maintenance, and how well a place is managed along with planned upcoming expenses, the old expression

"Past Performance is Not Necessarily Indicative of Future Results" most definitely applies. Management companies/boards change and get  better or worse, and in old buildings in NYC sometimes, sh*t happens!

In an ideal world, you should always buy with some sort of cushion for these kind of expenses. If you can't afford at a stretch,  a temporary assessment of, +20% of maintenance for a couple of years....then don't buy! In other words, since you have a choice,  don't buy at your monthly expenditure limit!

Bear in mind that a small co-op of 10-30 apartments repairing, for example, catastrophic roof damage will cost you MUCH more than a large co-op of 200-300 apartments, repairing the same kind of damage, even if the roof is bigger in the 2nd case.

If you bought a house of course, you would be open to MUCH greater risk for large unexpected expenses.  If your roof gets a hole then, then you'd have to pony up $5-25+k straight away.....
