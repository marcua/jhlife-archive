Maintenance fees...
« on: December 07, 2011, 11:52:38 AM »
First time buyer, looking at Jackson Heights for a one-bedroom (first post too).

How much can I expect my maintenance fees to increase each year, all things being equal? I understand that there are unforeseeable factors, like property tax rate increases, or necessary work on the building, so it's impossible to say for certain. But lets assume that the building is financially and structurally sound, and property tax increases are "average". Looking backwards can be an okay way to predict future conditions, so I'm asking those with a long history of ownership. My concern is that 20 years into the future, when I'm a senior, I lose the ability to pay the maintenance fees and then lose ownership of the apartment. What is reasonable to expect?

One reason I'm asking... I was looking at a unit, and the agent told me that the spec sheet was wrong -- maintenance was not 490, it was 550 -- an 11% increase since the unit had went on the market a few weeks before. (The unit had been fully renovated so maybe it was reassessed?)

Also, I'm looking at various units, and the range of maintenance can vary by 50% (I've seen some units under $500 dollars, and some that were over $700 dollars).

What can I expect as far as routine maintenance increases?

The maintenance fee is the revenue that supports the operation of the building - underlying mortgage, utilities, maintenance of the physical plant, staff salaries, management company, repair expenses, etc. Any major repairs will generally be handled by using the reserve fund or by a special assessment. The only thing that will significantly lower maintenance costs is having the underlying mortgage for the building paid off. Some buildings adjust their maintenance charges every year, the building I'm in only does so every few years, depending on how much utility and property tax expenses have gone up. Then it's usually just single digit percentages.

I'm a first-time buyer, looking for a one bedroom in JH, too! Don't go stealing my apartment! LOL

From what I've learned in my search, maintenance has everything to do with the size of the apartment and the financial status of the building. It's always ideal to buy in a building with a healthy reserve fund and no underlying mortgage. These buildings will not have frequent maintenance increases b/c they have the reserve funds to cover capital improvements or unexpected expenses. On the other hand, if you buy in a building that has a large underlying mortgage, has recently refinanced, and/or has low reserve funds, if anything goes wrong (unexpected roof damage, or any number of unexpected expenses, etc.), they'll either have to increase the maintenance or tack on a monthly assessment fee (worse case scenario, they can do both). So, before committing to an apartment, ask about the financial status of the building; also, have your attorney diligently review the building's financial statements; b/c this will be the biggest indicator of whether or not your maintenance will increase in the near future and, potentially, it may also indicate how often it will increase. Hope this helps! And g-luck!

Although I am not in Jackson Heights (Woodside resident), I have been living in a 250 unit co-op building for the past nine years and potential maintenance increases were certainly a concern of mine when I was looking to purchase.  In the nine years that I have lived here, our maintenance has increased twice -- the first time it was 8% the first year I moved in, and then again 4% in my fifth year.  Our building has a very healthy reserve, it also has a not so large underlying mortgage, ALL of our utilities are included and we are a self-managed building with a staff of seven not including the night guard who is is stationed from 8p-5a seven days a week (the guard services are hired through an outside company).

I would tend to say that my experience has been on the favorable side of the spectrum with only a handful of one-time operating assessments in recent years (due to rapidly rising electric & heating costs), but each time they were wholly offset by the annual NYC STAR rebate and tax assessment.  Of course if the city lets either rebate expire, then I would have to come out of pocket (the assessment for 2010/11 was $375).

Our building was also nice to enough to publish the the maintenance increases over the past 15 years on their board so residents have greater transparency over building finances and cost management.

Hope this helps.

Ask Management to give you the last three times the maintenance has gone up and make sure there is a healthy reserve.  Banks like to see about $1000 per unit but that might be way to small for a smaller building with large units and large property.  Make sure you look into the flip tax situation as well - This might be more inportant than a, slighly above average maintenance.  Make sure you also look into the assesments, many coops will make assesments only and as needed instead of increasing the maintenance every few years. 

Most converted coops will have an underlying mortgage, so finding one without one is not realistic...unless you are looking into original coops.  Those may have other issues with the by-laws, such as not being able to sublet and being quite intrusive.  These type of coops do offer the benefit of much lower asking prices and maintenance but I recommend looking at every aspect of what you are looking for in a real estate investment and not just the maintenance.
