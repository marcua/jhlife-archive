Unlike London and Tokyo, or Moscow and many cities outside the US, where subway construction is constant -- our subway system is not keep up with demand, very little has been built in the last 80 years. The new signals which allow trains to come more frequently are good, but Queens is a transit desert with vast areas underserved, and far too few tunnels into Manhattan - a major choke point.

Also, the new LIC construction is overwhelmingly residential which means a lot more crowding on trains (even with the ferries.) A mix of office and residential would mean some folks living in new housing would walk to work and some Queens residents would get off trains in LIC, making more room. Also the trains coming back to Queens from Manhattan are not crowded and easily could bring more workers in.

Of course this being Queens, LIC is springing up with little control, according to the instant market demand, and little attention to the future needs of the Queens, the city or involvement by government, whose attitude seems to be: "Forget it Jake; it's Queens."
