This is from the Dept of Energy's "ask a scientist" program:

Question -   My wife read an article that reported milk stored in
plastic containers is inferior to that of milk stored in paper
(cardboard) containers.  The reason stated was that plastic allows light
in and the light diminishes the nutritional quality of the milk.  Is this
"junk" science or true?

------------------------------------------------
While in principle, it is possible that milk exposed to light (which
wavelengths???) could be affected, as a practical matter exposure to light
is minimal. At the grocery store fluorescent lights, not sunlight, would be
the primary source of light, and if the milk were in glass, the glass would
absorb any radiation with a wavelength shorter than ~400nm. The absorption
of light in plastic containers is further minimized by the fact that the
plastic milk containers I have seen are transluscent, thus scattering incident
light, especially the shorter wavelengths. The residence time of milk on the
shelf is a matter of a week or so, at which time the grocer must remove the
milk. Milk itself scatters light strongly -- that is why it is white -- so
the penetration of light into the milk itself is very small, and there is
are not milk components that absorb a lot of visible light, otherwise the
milk would be colored.  In the home milk is stored in the refrigerator so
exposure to light there is minimal.

I think that the real enemy of milk is opening the container!!  When that
happens the milk is exposed to air, which can oxidize any number of
substances in the milk.  In addition. molds, bacteria, and the like can
enter the milk container and begin to grow.

A secondary concern for milk stored in either type container is leaching
components from the container. But even here, the FDA has regulations and
test procedures for extractable components in packaging that comes into food
contact.

V. Calder

http://www.newton.dep.anl.gov/askasci/gen01/gen01129.htm

The biggest factor affecting shelf life of milk is temperature. All dairy products should be stored below 40Â°F, even lower if possible. I used to buy a brand of milk that came in a carton that had a chart printed on one of the panels showing how each degree above 40 shortened the shelf life dramatically. This applies to all dairy products, by the way.

Also, just to clear up a possible misconception about the freshness date:
In general, the milk in the container is good for up to one week after the date if it's unopened. So those of you with young children may want to reconsider buying those gallon-sized jugs and going for two half-gallon containers instead. Also, don't leave the containers on the table while the kids are eating their cereal... Close and put them back in the fridge right away.

As for Trade Fair: It is unwise for them to not store the milk within refrigerated cases and just leave them in the aisle. If this is such an issue, I'd suggest buying milk across the street at MetFood where they store their milk in cases behind glass doors.
