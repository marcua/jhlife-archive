There are advantages and disadvantages to both. RE agents have access to a wider network of resources -- more clients, more showings, websites etc. They also can be helpful in pre-screening candidates, esp for board approval. Of course, it depends on the RE agents. Our neighborhood often works with exclusive agents; I find that those working through MLS are frankly a waste of time more often than not, and more of an inconvenience than anything because they don't understand how JH buildings operate. So if you go with an agent, I'd suggest getting some of the people who've been working in the neighborhood for some time and who are respected. It may also be faster to use an agent in this economic climate, though someone I know just signed a contract through a FSBO arrangement, and if you are not able to dedicate enough time to responding to and coordinating with interested parties. Plenty of people post on the local board and on other sites, too.

Where is Montclair Gardens?

Good luck!

If you have a place that is desirable and price it reasonably, you should be okay.

Advertising with your management office, on MLS, streeteasy, craigslist and NY Times should be enough to gain the notice of actively searching buyers.

Have all three years of your co-op's finanical's scanned/pdf'd and available to share, a pdf'd copy of the occupancy agreement, ensure you also have three years worth of any abatement's received (i.e. STAR rebates), assessments, etc.

Be totally clear about what the buyer needs to be able to pass board muster (i.e. down payment, gross salary expectations usually 48x monthly mortgage+maintenance, etc.).

FSBO's usually suffer from over-pricing b/c owners having inflated expectations of what their place is worth. Scan online listings of comparable apartments and also get a list from your management office of recent sales for apartments of similar size and number of shares.

Good luck!
