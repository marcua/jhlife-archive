I don't want to start another retail gentrification battle -- really! -- but I don't totally understand the need to have all my shopping needs met in my neighborhood. Grocery stores and pharmacies, sure. But the traditional urban model, before the advent of shopping malls, was to go downtown to buy clothes and other goods. It seems to work pretty well. We can be in Herald Square or SoHo via the subway pretty quickly and have a much wider selection of shops than any typical residential neighborhood could ever hope to have. I like the idea of neighborhoods each having a distinctive flavor and even occupying a specific retail niche that draws people from all over (like the dwindling ones in Manhattan for flowers, diamonds, perfume etc) or our own 74th Street.

I'll take this as a sign of better things to come- this is better than vacant storefronts. This is better IF Banana Republic allots budget to repair the old building façade while maintaining integrity. Commenting on the actual store is besides the point, it's what the profile of this store means in this setting (factory or no factory outlet).

The problem with "mom and pop" shops is not only rents which appear to be a big issue, it is also the city in general. Don't put this all on the landlord.....btw if you were the landlord of a commercial property, you would want to bring in the highest return on your investment too.

NYC makes all businesses pay UBT tax which is a slap in the face tax on top of all income taxes. The chunk of money small businesses and self employed individuals can put into building their business, paying their rent and hiring employees is significantly affected by this tax. It all stems back to the same root of all evil- millionaires not paying the proper percentage on their pied a terre investement properties- so NYC looks to the next victim- small businesses. It's a vicious cycle.

Like it or not "Mom and pop" shops are on their way out. Not just because of rents. NYC does not do enough for small businesses to give them a fair chance
