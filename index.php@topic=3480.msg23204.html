I doubt the bums have internet access and think that some newcomers are interested in the timeline and how the situation has gotten to its present state.  Is there another forum these things can be discussed in detail? (This is the only Jackson Heights forum I read, so I'm not sure what else is out there - no snark meant!)

And - to stay on topic - I've seen the liquor store open early as well.

The "bums" are human beings, and deserve the same respect any other human being on this planet does. Discussing the individual behaviors and personal histories of each one of these people does little to actually further the conversation on the bigger topic of homelessness and how it affects our community.

To help stay on topic I've heard a few things in this thread repeated a few times that may lend to some sort of solution.

1) Clearly the liquor stores are helping the problem grow by accommodating the less than healthy habits exhibited by some of the homeless people who have taken up residence on our streets. So it seems that putting pressure on the liquor stores to deny service to these individuals is one step towards reducing the problem. Some people have complained to the State Liquor Authority and 311, I would think continuing to make these complaints will help put pressure on the stores in the area.  Perhaps some one wants to take on the task of developing a petition that local residents could sign asking the local liquor stores to adjust their business practices ? If that attempt fails a small boycott could be very effective, there are certainly plenty of other liquor stores in the area that do not cater to this population so we could all take our business elsewhere until the problem is resolved.

2) We need to lobby our local elected officials to invest in outreach services that could have a more intense presence in the area to help convince some of these people to seek shelter and assistance.

3) Contacting the police when something crosses the line, the more reports on file at the precinct the higher the chances that action will be taken in some way shape or form.

It also occurs to me that many of us will give a homeless person money if asked, it would seem to be better to give food or clothing as money can be used to purchase alcohol / drugs.

The fact is homelessness is an issue that the entire city deals with. We will never be completely rid of our homeless population, at least not anytime soon. We can work to reduce it, and as I see it, that requires a compassionate approach combined with enforcement. These are people who for whatever reason find them selves in a situation no one ever wants to be in. If we dehumanize them or look at them as less than ourselves then we run the risk of dealing with the issue in a way that will only cause more problems down the road.

This discussion was not really about homelessness as a general societal issue, but rather about certain individuals and small groups that happen to be homeless and engage in behaviors that are simply unacceptable (and often illegal, but not bad enough to elicit strong action from the police).

It is helpful to discuss individuals, because it is possible for homeless to just leave people alone or at least engage with other people respectfully.  The problem right now are certain individuals, and how they get away with what they do is an excess of compassion that leads people to look the other way.  I can pretty much guarantee if the individuals committing these acts were routinely doing so within the historic district, or were taking crack instead of booze, or were black, then these things wouldn't be happening for very long (not that such biases are correct...they just represent reality).  Aronan makes some good suggestions, although those are more long-term and we could do without the browbeating.
