Restaurants & Food / Re: if you have own a store in JH - please ditch the credit minimum.
« on: March 25, 2017, 12:44:02 AM »
Quote from: mango on March 24, 2017, 10:55:15 AM
They are in business for my money
An oft noted consumer fallacy, but an understandable one, as we as a whole are generally more familiar with national chains than with independent stores, especially out in the burbs.  Chains have an inexhaustible product supply and they profit not by product quality or uniqueness, but rather proliferation and accessibility.  To establish market share they are hyper inclusive about their customer base, for the wares they vend are relatively homogenized in relation to those offered by their major competitors, differing primarily by highly touted superficialities. 

Despite the wide net chains cast, a more apt statement would be "they are in business to make money".  That's not petty semantics, especially when it comes to owner operated shops which establish market share not by brute numbers, but rather product uniqueness and quality.  Concerns of production limitations, product exclusiveness, limited advertising and diminishing returns inhibits them for being "in business for your money".  Instead they have to put their energies on their target consumer, the one who is their best fit.  It's of course impolitical to announce that "our shop may not be the best fit for you", but it sure is the case.  Especially in owner run shops like Juju's, 969 Cafe and the Arepa Lady, there are only so many hours in a day and so many things a Sammy, Oda or Maria can do in those hours.  What's the name of the guy who runs Starbucks?  If I spent $40 a week anywhere, you can bet me and the owner would be on a first name basis and I wouldn't need a rewards card to be appreciated come my birthday.

So with that, I'll readily acquiesce that walk-aways occur when there is a credit card minimum.  That doesn't directly translate to a loss in business though.  Walk-aways happen everyday for all sorts of reasons.  Owners try to mitigate them without incurring undue costs, sacrificing identity or causing other walk-aways.  Not having a credit card minimum has its benefits and its drawbacks and owners have to weigh the twain (credit card transactions take longer than cash, which can increase customer waiting times, which certainly does result in walk-aways, especially in grab and go situations and crush periods.  Not having a cc minimum may result in swapping walk-aways while making less profitable transactions).  There is no overriding right or wrong answer.

So while I think Mango's blanket statement is less concerned advice and more a moneyed demand, it is, much to my dismay, a growing one.  As short as 10 years ago, if someone were to push forward plastic on a $3 item at my shop, eye rolls would flutter on the customers waiting behind them.  Now, atleast once per day, sometimes more, a would be card user makes a 180, often in a huff and the eye rolls are rare enough that when I get them, it's like a warm secret between old friends.  Not nearly enough of a thing to justify giving 30 cents (that's 10%!) to credit card company executives, but it's on my radar.  Unfortunately, the future more likely belongs to Mango's ilk than mine and that sucks not just because I'm a tin hat wearing person who doesn't want businesses, hackers and the government to know how and where I spend my money, but because cashless benefits the chain, not the owner operated shop.  They'll still survive, but it will be harder and they will be fewer.  Till then, whenever I hear about an eatery that only takes cash, my ears will perk up, as there's usually a pretty good reason that they can survive without plastic.

Real Estate & Home Improvement / Re: Basement storage rooms
« on: March 21, 2017, 12:30:31 PM »
First, establish the existing situation.  Goto the basement and measure out a detailed layout.  Keep in mind things like emergency egress, fire codes and maintenance access to water pipes, electrical work and the boiler (these might be some of the reasons why it hasn't been done).  Then determine the number of apartments in the co-op, the number of storage units, the wait list, the size of the storage units and the amount of money being charged for their use.  Note the number of units which are barely being used or seem like they haven't been touched in ages.  Determine how they are being managed (if it self managed or managed by a company).

Second, compare your situation with other co-ops in the area.  See their fee structures and how they deal with demand.  When we were investigating bicycle storage, I took every opportunity to talk to shareholders at other co-ops (including this forum!) about how they dealt with bike storage.  In many cases, mine included, a free storage unit is assigned to each apartment, but there are plenty of others in JH which having waiting lists and fees.

Third, list the available actions.  Determine how many storage units can be built.  Get quotes from storage unit companies for all available scenarios (simple build, leasing, build/management schemes).  Weight that against self built ones.  Do a cost/revenue analysis.  Be creative with your list of available actions:  Perhaps a bike storage area would lessen the demand for storage units.  Maybe the storage fees are so low that people are careless about them and would move out should they be modernized.

Finally, sniff out a sympathetic shareholder.  See if it has been hashed out in the past (which it likely has) and see if your action list can avoid any pitfalls.  From there you either take it to an active board member or co-op meeting.  You major allies are going to be folks who are also on that waiting list as well as people who might be selling in the near future.

It's alot of work and it's much easier to just raise your hand at a co-op meeting, but it is important to you, it is best to be prepared, especially if it has already been addressed in the co-op.  Sometimes all it takes is someone to show some good initiative to get the ball rolling.  Good luck!!

Why are 1 bedroom and junior 1/2 bedroom apartments in the Berkeley sold at higher prices than other coops such as Dunolly Gardens or Warwick? What are the pros of the Berkeley buildings relative to the nearby buildings?

950 sq. ft is pretty big for a one bedroom/Jr4.  Some 2 bedrooms sold in JH don't have that much square footage (though as other posters noted, it may be a bit awkwardly laid out).  It helps that the Berkeley has a fantastic block long yard.  They are smart over there as well, investing in a website.  The $542 a sq/ft might not have been reached in the Warwick, a wonderful pre-war, but last October a cute 800 sq/ft one bedroom went for $375,000, which is $469 a sq/ft (and given how things are moving around here, I'd give odds that it would have sold north of $500 sq/ft if it closed last week).   
https://www.zillow.com/homedetails/7612-35th-Ave-APT-5O-Jackson-Heights-NY-11372/122587904_zpid/
 
f you really want to get a good feel for the differing co-ops, take the JHBG's tour this spring.  If you are lucky enough to walk with Daniel Karatzas, you will seriously learn everything you want about JH (he also has a book out, on sale at Espresso 77).  Also find out about building finances.  That's a major bender.

I had always thought that the mantra stated that if you were going to sell, just fix it up a bit and save the money, as you won't get a dollar for dollar price increase on the renovations you are making.  But I've noticed as of late, the well renovated, turn key flats are commanding prices that cover those renovations and then some.  Could be my population is just skewered though.
