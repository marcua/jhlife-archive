Sadly, Aronan is right about the underfunding of homeless and other social services in NYC.  The problem of street homeless (which is really qualitatively different than homeless families in shelters, of which there are many but relatively invisible) just gets shifted from neighborhood to neighborhood.  As posted above, Corona residents protested about a shelter, as have Elmhurst residents:

http://queenscrap.blogspot.com/2009/07/elmhurst-residents-rally-against.html

In my opinion, Jackson Heights bears a greater burden from street homeless because we are a major transit hub,  some business owners don't do anything about quality-of-life issues around their storefronts (for example, try getting Kabab King to clean up the debris of the shanty town that is erected daily outside their restaurant, or any other trash for that matter), and we are not as wealthy as many other NYC neighborhoods.  When Danny Dromm takes office, I hope he can help.

I am sympathetic, but where does that leave us as a community?  These do not look like the "newly" homeless.  They seem like people with long term mental and substance abuse issues.  I felt bad when I saw one early this morning huddled in a doorway trying to avoid the cold rain.  I didn't feel so bad when another homeless person harassed me for money this afternoon.

i'm a lot more concerned with the well-being of the men and women who live on the streets than i am with property values -- sue me, i am a bleeding heart commie pinko first and a homeowner second. but i do understand where some of these complaints/issues are coming from. i do not have people urinating in my vestibule now, but i have lived with urine, feces and dead junkies on my doorstep in the past. on one occasion, i attempted to have my then-local precinct come to investigate a dead body on the stoop, only to come back a full 18 hours later with the corpse still there -- one of my bad cop experiences from another thread. so i know what it's like.

i don't think the homeless have moved into jackson heights any more than they have into most inner ring neighborhoods -- astoria certainly has a goodly number, as do greenpoint, LIC, boerum hill, cobble hill and the south part of park slope). these are the areas i am more intimately acquainted with, and i won't attempt to address places i go on rare occasions.

Some serious addicts, when they hit absolute rock bottom, will then be amenable to help.  As much as I hate to say it, many of JH's addicts may be beyond rock bottom and too far gone for much help.  They have wrecked their bodies and minds to such an extent that I wonder how they are still alive when I see them (and I have seen family members die from alcoholism and decline from drugs, so know what it looks like).  The only help I see people giving them is money for booze (it was way worse during Ramadan, as folks were feeling more charitable!), and EMTs bringing them to the hospital when they are passed out on the sidewalk.

i don't know if forcible institutionalization/rehab is the answer for the folks with chronic substance abuse problems -- having also had family members with those, i've seen far more backsliding than successful recovery, and that is depressing as can be. there's also the question of human rights. being annoying and causing others to feel uncomfortable is not a crime, unless of course it escalates to the level of a threat. i have never seen or felt threats from any of the specific people we are talking about, although i certainly know that vibe from when i lived in astoria in the late '80s.

we have vagrancy laws, which could be implemented as a short term solution, although a sweep could also land me behind bars since i seldom carry ID -- technically a crime, thanks to Uncle Rudy's 'quality of life' blitz. we have outreach programs, but the truly hardcore don't often respond to those. i've begun responding in simpler ways, like giving some food or -- now that it's getting cold -- some worn warm clothing instead of cash. i know that a buttered roll can't be bartered for booze or crack.

sad to say, i agree with you that some of these folks may be beyond help. but i do appreciate having the discussion take this turn as opposed to simply 'how can we rid ourselves of this?'
