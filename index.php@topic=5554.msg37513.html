Dudley is working on creating a website that will track donations.  He looked into kickstarter.com, but they take a 6% fee of all money raised which is pretty hefty, considering the amount being raised. 

At the meeting, it was discussed that an account would be created (possibly by JHBG) where the funds would be held until enough was raised, approximately $500,000.  Once the money is raised, it will be offered to Garden School, who will be asked to sign a letter stating that they will sell the land to the city.  No money would be released to them without an assurance that they won't sell to a developer.  If Garden School won't agree to sell the land to the city, the money would be returned to the contributors.

Whether or not $500K is enough is up for debate.  That amount will get them through June, 2011.  What they will need for next school year is anybody's guess.  It will all depend upon how quickly the ULURP process goes.  If Garden School can get a signed contract by next December and enrollment doesn't drop any further, they could probably get a bridge loan to cover any deficit they might have for school year 2011-2012 until the sale goes through.

I'm sure more details will be released as they become available.  Until this meeting it seemed everyone involved was convinced that a sale to a developer was imminent.  It still may be, but Garden School gave the audience the impression that the land was still for sale until a contract was signed.

Here's a quick update:

We have raised over $100,000 in pledges so far! The response has been amazing.

You will be hearing from us as soon as we have worked out the details of the contract between people who have offered to lend/donate money and the fund that will hold the money for the community.

We are working with lawyers on a contract regarding the terms of this loan from the community to the Garden School. We will not release this money to the Garden School until we have a satisfactory legally  binding document.

Our goal is to obtain a contract explicitly stating:

a) That they will pursue the city's offer exclusively
b) That the first action they will take with the city's money when they receive it is to return this money to the fund so it can be redistributed back to the community.

We will be posting regular updates about this campaign on www.growapark.org (coming very soon!), facebook (http://www.facebook.com/group.php?gid=50572593804&v=info) and twitter (@JHGreenAlliance).

There continues to be a lot of dis-information on this topic, and I can't quite tell where it's coming from.

I attended the community meeting the other night where it was disclosed, that only about 6% of the students at the school come from Jackson Heights, the rest are from various parts of Queens. So we're talking about maybe 12 to 15 students form the immediate area, based on an enrollment of about 240. Which means Jackson Heights Children are in a significant minority.  So, how can the innocent act of exercising one's first amendment rights be harming the "children of Jackson Heights"?

It was also disclosed at that same public meeting that the parents of the students who attend the school were not adequately informed about what was going on with the board's decision to reject the city's offer. Based on the fact that the overwhelming majority of families with children in the school are NOT from the immediate area, it seems only fitting to me that the community should inform these parents about the consequences of the board's choices.

Further, if there really were people blocking the entrance to the school or otherwise creating an obstruction, were the police called? Clearly if the children were being terrorized the responsible action would have been to inform the authorities. If not, then we have to assume the "threat" was minimal if it existed at all. Waiting an entire day to raise the concern without any real evidence on a local blog hardly seems like a responsible action to me.

There's a lot of spin here and what seems like an attempt to not vilify the school. While they may not be in position to wait for the city process to be complete they should be held responsible for the consequences of their actions.

There is a groundswell of support to make the deal with the city come to fruition. The community is willing to wok with the school, but from the negative comments and spin coming out form some people it's not so clear to me that the school is really willing to work with the community.

It's a shame because I really was considering sending my child to the Garden School when the time comes, but this whole experience has put a bad taste in my mouth.
