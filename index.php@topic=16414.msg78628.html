Overpaying is a relative concept.  When we moved to the neighborhood, we paid pretty much at the top end for our unit (when compared to similar apartments in the same complex).  My wife was against the purchase because of this but when I pointed out that the mortgage/maintenance costs would be just a little more than what we were already paying for rent, what seemed like an overpriced unit was actually a good deal.  Hence, we overpaid in other people's eyes, yet we were not overpaying given our circumstances.  JH seems like a bargain when one considers RE costs for similarly situated neighborhoods, i.e., amenities, lots of train lines, and close proximity to Manhattan.  Maybe you are underestimating the true value of apartment units in the neighborhood and the rest of the City is catching on, which may explain the significant increase in RE values in the last few years.

On that note, welcome to the neighborhood Gentlemen!

Re: NYT Column 'The Hunt' on JH & Washington Plaza

It absolutely worked out less for us, but that's because my dad (who was retired at the time) was a professional carpenter all his life, so the only labor that we had to actually pay for was the plumber and the electrician.  He even built our bathroom cabinets as their size is far from standard.  That (along with smart buys, like purchasing tiles from some guy's basement in Flushing that obviously fell off the truck) totally saved us at least 50% of the renovation costs.

For us, doing it this way was worth it because we moved from a one bedroom apartment to a three bedroom apartment, so most of the apartment lacked furniture.  That allowed us to work room by room, and then move our stuff around so that we would always have a clean, non-construction impacted, bedroom and at least one working bathroom.  But the fact is that, for two years, we had rotating construction going on as our time and budget would allow, and towards the end, we were both so over the whole thing.  Not sure if we'd ever do it that way again.
