Without revealing my identity (ooh) let me just say I know more about this than most people.

First of all, your building is already full of Wi-Fi. I see 14 Wi-Fi networks from where I'm sitting. That battle is so far lost, that you are only shooting yourself in the foot. Also, all studies have shown that the consumer wireless technologies we use in homes can only affect you physically if you are within inches of the transmitter (and even then, they've generally shown those affects to be harmless - but even barring that conclusion, as I said, within inches of the transmitter.) All actual science has shown that people with "Wi-Fi allergies" tend to feel better when they are -told- there is no Wi-Fi around -even if it's still actually there.-

We're not going to get DAS units in old Jackson Heights buildings. They're expensive and the installation is invasive. Verizon's Network Extender is a lousy product, years old, but it will get you basic calling and texting functionality. It pipes your phone into your home Internet connection. A larger-scale option would be to go with a WeBoost [https://www.weboost.com/us/] but that may be overkill. Those are generally designed for large rural homes.

Your best bet, of course, is to change providers to one of the other three - AT&T has good coverage if you go outside the city a lot, and T-Mobile is great within the city - but I understand if that's a problem for you.

When I first moved into JH, I had Verizon, who I had service with from the first day I got a cell phone.  There were areas in the neighborhood where I had zero service, including inside my apartment.  Like you, I called to complain,  They sent technicians who confirmed what I was experiencing and said they'd do something.  Two months later, nothing changed.  Before I called to cancel my service, I borrowed a couple of phones from a cell phone store in the neighborhood.  The phones were hooked to different companies (T-Mobile, Sprint, AT&T and one more that no longer exists).  I just wanted to know which one worked in my home.  AT&T worked best; although not great, I did get a signal and could manage to make and receive calls.  You may want to do the same thing.

Also, if you have a smartphone, turn on WiFi calling.  When you are in an area where your phone signal is weak and you would get better service via WiFi, the phone automatically switches to WiFi.  AT&T just offered it last week;  the others have offered it for years.  It's free no matter who you choose as your carrier.

The most efficient solution would be to switch providers. Figure out either by testing other people's phones in your apartment or asking your neighbors for feedback on their providers.
