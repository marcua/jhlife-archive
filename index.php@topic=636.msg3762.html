Cheesesteak Shop Wins Discrimination Hearing

"The owner of a famous Philadelphia cheesesteak shop scored a victory in a racially charged debate over a sign he posted in 2005. The Commission on Human Relations panel found that two signs at Geno's Steaks telling customers, "This is America: WHEN ORDERING 'PLEASE SPEAK ENGLISH,'" do not violate the city's Fair Practices Ordinance." (from AOL News)

Since we live in a community where shops have signs up in their windows in languages other than English, I'm curious to hear peoples thoughts are on this ruling.

He's certainly within his right according to the U.S. Constitution (ever heard of that? - 1st Amendment?) to post the sign.  The whole "racially charged debate" is code speak for the typical readers of the NY Times. 

He didn't post a sign denying service to any particular class of people, just simply said to speak in English when ordering, and he was even polite about it - "Please".  He's never denied service to anyone for speaking languages other than English.

A common language is the glue keeping nations together.  Multilingualism only works for the most part in Switzerland (but Switzerland has STRICT border and immigration controls - oops don't want to touch that "third rail"!), and perhaps a few other European nations, but there are significant "separatist" movements in places such as Belgium, Scotland, Wales.  Closer to the U.S., just look north to Canada and the separatist movement in Quebec by the French speakers there a few years back. 

There are more important things that the useless bureaucrats at Philadelphia's "Commission on Human Relations" panel should be doing, rather than harassing business owners in a city with 406 murders in 2006.  Perhaps if the "Commission" was performing it's mission to improve "human relations", Philadelphia wouldn't have such a high murder rate.

I agree with Prester in that the cheese steak shop owner has a Constitutional right to post the sign he did.  However, I don't think his doing so makes practical business sense.  A sign such as this may be deemed offensive by potential customers, and can possibly alienate some of his regulars that may disagree with his political perspectives.

Although I feel comfortable with my knowledge of the English language, I tend to believe that if I encountered a similar sign in any of our neighborhood's shops, I would most likely steer clear.  Throughout my walks in Jackson Heights, I have yet to encounter anything like this where a proprietor endorses or suggests the use of a specific language.  As a matter of fact, I believe that many of the local merchants have made good efforts to learn different languages in order that they may accommodate a diverse clientele.

Todd, you just proved to be a great example of my earlier post on how these types of protests can alienate customers.  You find the sign offensive, and as a result, you refuse to patronize the business in question.  I'm sure there are many like you that will have similar thoughts on the subject.  By the way, I just read somewhere that the guy had over 20 employees working with him.  Surely, some of these have to have been able to assist some of the Spanish speaking customers?  I assume Spanish is the language at issue here because the shop owner speaks of the large Mexican influx in the community.

As a kid, I remember my parents taking us down to Delancey and Orchard Streets to shop for our shoes and clothing because the merchants spoke Spanish, and they were Jewish and Chinese Americans.  Because they chose to shop here didn't mean that they refused to speak or learn English.  It simply made the adjustment period easier for them.  Over the years, my parents have picked-up enough of the English language to be able to shop anywhere, but that overall sensation of sometimes feeling excluded unfortunately still rears its ugly head.  Certainly, signs like the one at the cheese steak shop don't do much in terms of helping to dissipate these negative feelings.
