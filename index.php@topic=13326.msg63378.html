The city proposed a one-way pair to help speed traffic, but the community understood that it wouldn't solve our traffic problems.
http://www.streetsblog.org/2007/06/19/jackson-heights-new-front-in-one-way-battle/
http://usa.streetsblog.org/2007/06/29/no-love-for-one-way-proposal-in-jackson-heights/

The major traffic problems in the neighborhood were southbound traffic congestion on 73rd and 76th Streets, which used to back up for a full block and cause problems on 35th and 37th avenues.  Especially at 73rd Street & 37th Avenue, the honking was incessant.  After the DOT started to embrace more progressive transportation engineering in the later Bloomberg years, they re-studied the issue in the JH Neighborhood Transportation Study and finally came to understand that fixing southbound circulation was the key to fixing the problem.   They "daylighted" intersections along 37th Avenue, added southbound turning lanes, reversed the direction of 75th Street, shifted bus routes, created Diversity Plaza, changed traffic signal timings, changed parking prices, added new parking areas, expanded loading zones, and widened crosswalks, and made traffic in the neighborhood flow immensely better.   There are still problems with double parking that need to be fixed (shifting more metered spaces to loading zones would help), and 37th/73rd still isn't great, but things are much better than they were a decade ago.

Meanwhile, there's a growing consensus that one-way multilane streets are terrible for public safety, e.g.:
http://www.washingtonpost.com/blogs/wonkblog/wp/2015/04/17/why-one-way-streets-really-are-the-worst/

I think there would be passionate opposition if the city were to revive the one-way pair proposal.
