Albany pols should not be the ones to judge Hiram Monserrate
ERROL LOUIS
Thursday, November 12th 2009, 4:00 AM

Don't be fooled by the moral posturing going on in Albany, where a "special committee of inquiry" of senators has begun investigating the domestic violence case of their colleague, Sen. Hiram Monserrate.
The panel is nothing more than a political kangaroo court, one that typifies the arrogance and conflicts of interest that are standard operating procedure in the state capital.
Don't get me wrong: Like most people, I get queasy thinking about Monserrate's slashing of his girlfriend last December (accidentally, both parties swear) and his manhandling of her to get her to the hospital. I'd be perfectly happy if he resigned from office.
I also suspect that Monserrate got a lucky break when a Queens court found him not guilty of felony assault. If found guilty of the felony, Monserrate would have been immediately removed from the Senate.
But that's not what happened. "Proof beyond a reasonable doubt is lacking," concluded Justice William Erlbaum.
Monserrate was convicted of misdemeanor assault and could spend up to a year in jail when he's sentenced next month. That should be the last word on the case, one that would give Queens voters something to think about when Monserrate stands for reelection in 2010.
Unfortunately, the Senate leadership is not content to let the court system and elections function as designed. Instead, they have cobbled together an inquiry process, which could lead to a vote to censure Monserrate or remove him from the Senate.
Every pol involved in this farce should be ashamed of themselves, particularly the senators who are lawyers.
Start with the fact that the whole ugly incident took place before Monserrate was sworn in as a state senator, raising the question of why the Senate has any jurisdiction over the matter at all.
Instead of activating the mostly dormant Senate Ethics Committee, or the Legislative Ethics Commission, the Senate concocted a new panel, one with no set rules of what evidence can be admitted and what procedures will be followed.
Unlike a court of law, there will be no neutral judge supervising the proceedings to make sure all constitutional rights are protected.
Instead, the Senate has hired attorney Dan Alonso to serve as special counsel to the committee. That creates a conflict for Alonso, who doubles as a member of the state's Commission on Public Integrity.
As a member of the integrity commission, Alonso oversees ethics rules surrounding lobbyists and campaign donors. Questions have been raised about Monserrate's legal defense fund, which may include money given by lobbyists and campaign donors.
So the man instructing the Senate's quasi-prosecution of Monserrate could very well end up investigating Monserrate's financial backers.
Several of the senators sitting in judgment over Monserrate have conflicts as well. One member of the committee, Sen. Toby Ann Stavisky, is the mother (and client) of Evan Stavisky, whose Parkside Group political consulting firm is working for Assemblyman JosÃ© Peralta, the pol running against Monserrate.
Another senator, Andrea Stewart-Cousins, has told the press that calls for Monserrate to resign were "appropriate," suggesting she's already made up her mind.
"It's breathtakingly stupid for these members to get involved in this kind of conflict of interest," says David Grandeau, a consultant who spent 13 years as top ethics watchdog as chairman of the Temporary Commission on Lobbying.
"This is all about perception; it's all about what they want it to look like," says Grandeau. "Anyone who thinks there's a watchdog looking out for ethics in New York should wake up. There's no one there."
Even worse than the shaky legal and ethics basis of the Monserrate committee is the precedent that would be set if pols start removing colleagues - in effect, canceling the will of the voters - simply because they don't like a particular person.
The ill-advised Senate committee should hold only one meeting, for the purpose of disbanding. Leave this matter to the court that tried Monserrate and to the voters who sent him to the state Senate.
