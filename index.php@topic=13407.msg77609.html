Quote from: NYC Peromyscus on February 02, 2017, 02:19:27 PM
I wonder if they are asking for exorbitant rents and nobody is biting.

We spoke with them and decided that it wouldn't work for us.

As you may know, there was an Italian restaurant on the main floor for many years. That restaurant moved after the fire.

The main floor rent for the new building is fairly high. From the point of view of opening a restaurant, a big part of the problem is that there is no basement, which means that a restaurant would have to pay full rent for its entire kitchen and storage area.

When we spoke with the Trust, it was not interested in accommodating this kind of issue. That was when they first asked for proposals, and were talking trendy grocery outlets and major fast food franchises. I don't know if the Trust has become more flexible since.

Unless the Trust changes its position on what kinds of tenants it wants, and its position on rent, I think that it is highly unlikely that an independent restaurant will open there, unless it is a fast food place that is part of a food court. Or a coffee place with a modest need for space.

Re: Bruson Building
« Reply #34 on: February 03, 2017, 07:50:06 PM »
Quote from: Benoit on February 03, 2017, 12:40:56 AM
And I would guess that corner location is zoned for outdoor seating? Perhaps the only space on the Ave that is. THAT would be the ideal local for a restaurant,  as our beloved Armondos proved to be.

I think that it's mostly a question of whether the tenant would be prepared to pay the rent that the city would require and, if so, whether the community/residential neighbors are on board.

But the fundamental problem is that a restaurant the size of Armando's serving labour-intensive food, would require kitchen and storage space that, if charged at the same rate as the Trust wants for its regular retail space, would be almost certainly uneconomic.

It's conceivable that an operation that does not require a large kitchen/storage area, such as Chipotle or similar, could take the space and offer outdoor seating. For such a tenant, it would become a matter of whether seasonal seating, and the costs that come with it, would make economic sense. I suppose that the space could also be partly enclosed (if I recall, that's how Armondo's did it, for at least part of the year).

It's admittedly speculation on my part, but I suspect that a chain like Chipotle would go into negotiations with the Trust on the premise that the Bruson building needs them more than they need the Bruson Building. If I'm right about that, the negotiation dynamic would be quite different from that with an independent restaurant.

Cheers
