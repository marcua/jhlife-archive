This building near Elmhurst Hospital has a bunch of coop units for sale for $585K to $795K, which sounds really high for a coop in Elmhurst.  (Corcoran is listing this Elmhurst location as Jackson Heights.)  I've noticed that realtors sometimes like to stretch neighborhood boundaries. 42-22 Ketcham St. $795,000

The six-story building at 42-22 Ketcham Street, between Baxter and Pettite avenues, contains 127 units.

RockFarmer bought 116 of those units. Most of the co-ops are currently rented out. George Michelis, a managing principal at RockFarmer, told The Real Deal the firm plans to renovate the lobby and backyard. “Over the next year, we want to financially stabilize the building and accent the prewar charm,” said Michelis, who worked with partner John Petras on the deal. “It is unusual to find such a large block of co-op shares on the market in Queens,” Michelis added." - See more at: http://therealdeal.com/blog/2014/02/07/rockfarmer-signs-paper-on-100000-sf-elmhurt-co-op-building/#sthash.JLy1N88Y.dpuf

Because I have the worst case of bathroom renovation hesitation, I check out open houses.  I went to the Berkshire Greens open house yesterday.

Overall I liked what I saw.  The prices were far beyond anything I could afford (studio from $225, 1 br for $315, 2br for $585, 3br $720).  They were well appointed.  Of the other mass sell offs I have seen in JH, when it comes to appointments and intangibles, if the Vin Chu Tower was embarrassing, Carlton House middling, this was comfortably above average.  The multi bedroom units were 1 br flats mashed together, so the layouts had a different, yet not unpleasant feel.  Most are single exposure (a 3br had 12 windows, all facing north!) but nonetheless felt airy and bright.  I thought the backyard very cute.

Maintenance seemed reasonable for the square footage and amenities (gym and rooftop not ready yet, TLC still needed throughout common areas and elevator), though given that the building was still overwhelmingly rentals, I don't know if that will have an affect in the future. 

According to the realtors, 15 units have been offered and 6 taken at or above asking price.  I originally thought given the location that there was a bit of b.s. with that, but one couple with a stroller actually wanted to make an offer on a 2br while I was milling about the lobby.  If it sells out, Brooklyn will have formally established a colony in the area.

One note, found it interesting that the marketers put together a glossy map of all the businesses of interest to perspective buyers and the vast majority of them (starbucks of course) were in JH rather than Elmhurst.

I went to the open house today.  The renovations seemed nice, but I just don't see how the price is worth it when you have lovely coops in garden complexes in Jackson Heights that are larger and selling for less.

Location is important, and the location of this coop is in an area that looks more run-down than any part of the JH historic district.  Also, many of the interesting area amenities that I think would appeal to people who can pay this kind of price for an apartment are located in Jackson Heights and not Elmhurst.  Another negative thing I noticed was that the floors were really sloping.  The studio floorplan was very small, although the studio we were shown did have nice light.

The ground floor had been renovated to include a lobby area with a fireplace and a room called a "library" which I found to be tacky, as it had old books stacked in patterns on the tables.  They were obviously there as a decoration.  I think it's not right to call something a library when the books are there just to look pretty.

There was an area in the back that had been renovated to be a garden space.  It looked nice, but I wonder who is going to maintain the garden areas.  You'd need shareholders who are willing to do that.

The broker said all the first phase apartments were in contract.  I believe this was around 25 apartments.  She also said that closings would not happen for another 6 to 9 months, but I didn't catch the reason for that.  All apartments are sponsor units, so no board approval is required.  I am not sure how many other units are included in the second phase.

Elmhurst has one thing going for it that Jackson Heights does not -- a rapidly increasing presence of Asians (especially Chinese), who have money to spend. Now that a critical mass has been established including grocery stores, restaurants, etc. we are seeing the beginning of the snowball effect.  These apartments are priced competitively against what similar places would cost in areas like Flushing or Sunset Park in Brooklyn. I am speaking generally here, but for the most part, there is far less importance placed on the historical significance of a building and much greater attention paid to the convenience of amenities locally and being able to interact socially with others.

Another very important factor driving these prices is that these are sponsor units so they will be much easier to transact -- about as easy as purchasing a condo. The provenance of where the funds are coming will be far less scrutinized.

We purchased in this building in June, and I think the "6-9 months to close" was a misunderstanding. The SECOND wave of apartments currently for sale would take that long to close, but the first wave (which has sold out) will begin closing finished apartments in the next two months.

We weighed going with a historic apartment in Jackson Heights v. Berkshire Green and ultimately our decision was based on a few factors:

1) The renovations of Berkshire Green are gorgeous, but also thorough. Yes, the finishes are truly lovely (and nicer than we would have picked if we renovated a historic apartment) but the electric and plumbing were also done (something I'm not sure we could have afforded to do in a historic apartment.
2) As first time home buyers, the idea of renovating an apartment was daunting. This was already done for us, and we wouldn't be the ones dealing with any surprises. The costs are fixed this way, and they wouldn't be if we renovated on our own.
3) Even the loveliest historic apartments don't have amenities. Berkshire Green has a laundry room, playroom, gym, backyard, roof deck. They aren't finished yet, though.

I, too, was concerned about renters but when I did more research it seems that the developer bought almost every single apartment in the building (roughly 130 apartments). If you're closing 25 at a time, just a few months apart from each other, it really isn't too long.

So far everyone has been a pleasure to deal with, the mortgage process was demanding - but generally friendly. The junior realtors showing the place are generally useless and can't even answer an email, but the main realtor is terrific.

Hi All! New to the board here. My husband and I just bought a 2 bedroom condo here from phase 2. Thought I'd jump in with a little more info.

We've been told closing will be in 6-9 months, and that the rest of the amenities including the roof deck should be completed by January 2017.

The reason buyers have to use their mortgage broker is because typical when selling units in a building, sellers are not allowed to close until 51% of the until of the units are sold. Since these units are being sold as they are completed, most mortgage lenders will not underwrite the mortgage. The sellers worked out a deal with the this bank that allows them to this. For the typical buyer to get that deal would take 9 months - not practical. Sterling National bank appears to be very reputable, and so far we aren't concerned.

As for "falling out", you really can't break the contract - though we did have our attorney add a clause that if the unit was not ready to close on within X amount of time we could break the deal.

As for reasons why we bought in this building:
* The units we were seeing in the JH Historic District were significantly more for the same amount of space - and required renovation. (Let alone apartments in Sunnyside, Astoria, Woodside and Brooklyn - the other areas we looked in.) They are tastefully done (most of it is what we would have picked had we done the renovations ourselves).
* The building will have a lot of amenities that other buildings don't have - the playroom, the multiple outdoor spaces, in unit laundry + laundry room - without a crazy high maintenance fee. In fact, the fee is comparable to that of some JH apartments that had zero fees.
* And then really specifically, we liked that the units had a lot of light, plenty of closet space, and we were big fans of the open concept layout of the living room / kitchen area.
* It's a relatively short walk to the express train stop + the 7 train.
* Lot's of good food in Elmhurst

Things we are concerned about:
* Lack of parking
* Timeline being pushed back on amenities
* Neighborhood not being totally safe

Hope this helps!
