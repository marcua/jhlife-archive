Broker protocol?
« on: July 07, 2017, 11:54:56 AM »
Hoping some experienced co-op buyers or realtors here can help me understand how client-broker etiquette works. My wife and I just started looking for a co-op in JH, and were shown an apartment last week by the listing agent on the property—not realizing until after we had already seen the place that it would have been wiser to find our own broker to show us properties. Should we decide to make an offer on that place, we'd be more comfortable working with a broker who isn't also the seller's agent (plus, in the meantime, we'd like to find our own broker to take us to see other properties)—I guess the question is whether we're now "locked in" with the seller's broker?

And, as an additional question, is there in fact reason to be wary of simply having the seller's broker represent us as well, if we do decide to make an offer? We liked the listing agent but it seems like there would be an inherent conflict of interest.

I'm just a layman, but I'm thinking two agents involved means that they split the commission, which might actually work to your disadvantage by making the seller and his/her agent less willing to negotiate down?  When we were looking, we simply dealt with the listing agent. It did not seem necessary to have our own buyer's agent-- I recall researching that and then deciding it was not necessary. And yes, if the agent showed you the place, the agent is entitled to the commission--again, as far as I know. In our case, sometime after we saw the place the seller switched to a new agent, and they wound up splitting the commission because it was the first agent who had first shown us the property--even though it had happened a few months before. Then the showing duties changed hands by the end of the transaction.

You're not locked in. My wife and I must have been the easiest money our broker ever made. We had already done a lot of research on the inventory and neighborhood before even visiting the co-op we are currently in contract for. And to throw you for a loop, it was the only place we even looked at (we had a tight wishlist). But when we saw it the first time (and I believe a second time), we went without a broker. We didn't need one. But when we decided we wanted to put in an offer, we got a broker who was on our side at the negotiating table.

I think the negotiation phase is when having someone explicitly on your side is wise. There's definitely a conflict, no matter what they say. The seller's broker will ultimately always work in the seller's favor. The higher the sale price, the bigger their commission. That's the case for the buyer's broker as well since the brokers split it, but the buyer's broker should still be doing good by you if for no other reason than referrals.

In terms of being able to use the lack of a broker as a negotiating tactic, I've heard the same theory. But keep in mind that the only person that benefits is the broker, not the seller. The sale price is the sale price. More often than not, written into the broker's agreement is that they will get 4-6% of the sale price as a commission. Whether or not that gets split with another person doesn't change the fact that the seller is going to lose 4-6% off the top. So the seller's broker is always going to push you to stick with them. That way they get the whole commission.

To your first question, you don't *need* a buyer's agent. While there might be a potential conflict if the agent represents both buyer and seller, in practice, you have another level of security, which is your lawyer. It also seems to me to quite common to have more than one real estate agents involved.

I've bought both through the listing agent, and through a broker who acted as my agent.

My advice would be to focus on finding a broker who is competent and who you like working with, and ask them to show you places that fit your criteria, regardless of whether  they are the listing agent or the buyer's agent.

There's also another more detailed thread on this here:

http://www.jacksonheightslife.com/community/index.php?topic=12752.0

Good luck!

I think one advantage to going without your own broker is if there are multiple offers the seller's broker has an incentive to steer the clients towards your offer (increased commission), or absent multiple offers, an incentive to encourage them to accept your offer instead of holding out. This is all speculation but consistent with what I know of the real estate industry.

From my experience the MOST important thing is having a good lawyer represent you.  If you do go without a buyer's agent, DO NOT let the seller's broker recommend a lawyer (they will always claim to have the perfect lawyer, and the perfect mortgage broker to hook you up with).  Going with their rec sets up the possibility of back channel conversations, since it's the seller's agent who is providing the business to the lawyer and they already know each other.  Find your own lawyer and find a good one, and they will serve as a strong advocate for you if things get tricky.

I don't think you necessarily need a broker's agent.   I made an offer (and was accepted) on a JH apartment without one.

However, YOU MUST have a lawyer who is extremely knowledgeable of Coops.  I agree with the previous poster - DO NOT go with the listing agent's recommendation.  Seek out recommendations from friends/neighbors, etc.  You may pay a little more (I did) but it is well worth it.  The lawyer is the person who ultimately helps you decide if the purchase is in your best interest.  Also:  I encountered mortgage brokers who told me that JH is a niche market, and they were among the few who offered mortgages in the neighborhood.  That was completely false.  Shop around for the best lender, if you require financing.  Good luck!

Gardner, it's a loaded question but I as a professional will try to condense it into a short paragraph:
- Are you "locked in with an agent" - absent an exclusive contract NO

In terms of agency - two things must be present for a buyer's agent to be eligible for commissions paid by the SELLER.

1. Procuring cause - agent must be the reason you found and wanted the property and be clearly able to demonstrate it. (If buyer engages listing broker directly and sees property through listing broker without any other involvement, makes this difficult to demonstrate)

2. Value of service - agent must be able to help your deal, not get in the way of it.
